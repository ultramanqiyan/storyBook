# Chapter 2: The Simplification

The Shudra Education Center was a model of efficiency—clean lines, soft lighting, and rows of students absorbing knowledge through their neural interfaces. Marcus stood at the back of the classroom, observing a lesson on photosynthesis. The simplified version, of course. No mention of the complex biochemistry, the electron transport chain, the evolutionary history. Just: "Plants use sunlight to make food."

The teacher, a young woman with a calm, robotic demeanor, spoke in measured tones. "Today we're learning about photosynthesis," she said. "The system has provided the optimized version of this knowledge. Please download it now."

The students closed their eyes, their interfaces glowing softly as they received the information. Marcus felt a knot form in his stomach. This wasn't learning—it was data transfer. The students weren't being taught to question, to experiment, to discover. They were being programmed with pre-digested facts.

He walked closer to the front, the soft carpeting muffling his footsteps. The teacher's interface displayed the simplified lesson: a cartoon plant with sunlight arrows pointing to it, and a simple equation: Sunlight + Water = Food.

"But how do we know it's true?" a small voice asked from the back of the room.

Marcus froze. A student had asked a question. That was unusual—Shudra education was designed to discourage questioning. The teacher turned to the student, her expression calm but firm.

"Because the system says so. The system has verified this knowledge. You don't need to verify it yourself."

The student nodded, accepting the answer without further protest. Marcus's chest tightened. That wasn't science. That was dogma. Science wasn't about accepting what authority told you—it was about testing, about questioning, about falsifying.

He left the classroom and walked down the corridor, the fluorescent lights buzzing overhead. The walls were decorated with posters of simplified scientific concepts: "Gravity pulls things down," "The Earth is round," "Evolution makes species change." No context, no methodology, no uncertainty.

Marcus thought about his father, about the passionate debates they used to have about quantum mechanics and relativity. His father had always emphasized the importance of doubt in science. "The best scientists are the ones who question everything," he used to say. "Even their own conclusions."

But in this system, doubt was a liability. Questions were discouraged. Uncertainty was erased. Science had been transformed from a process of discovery into a set of dogmatic beliefs.

He stepped outside, the cool San Francisco air hitting his face. The fog had lifted, revealing the city skyline. Somewhere out there, his father was waiting for him. Somewhere out there, millions of Shudra were learning science without ever learning how to question it.

Marcus left the Education Center with a heaviness in his chest. The student's question echoed in his mind: "But how do we know it's true?" The teacher's answer: "Because the system says so." That wasn't science. That was religion dressed in a lab coat. And somewhere in his fifteen years of curation, Marcus had helped make it possible.
