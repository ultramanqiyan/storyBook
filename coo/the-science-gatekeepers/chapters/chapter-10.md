# Chapter 10: The Threshold

Three days had passed since Marcus had discovered the consequences of his archive. Three days of carrying the knowledge like a stone in his chest, of looking at his work and seeing not liberation but potential chaos. The archive sat on his server, ready to deploy. All he had to do was decide.

He had thought about it day and night. He had replayed the simulation results in his mind, seen the riots, the unrest, the social collapse. He had thought about his father, about how he had lost everything for standing up for the scientific method. He had thought about his daughter Sarah, about what kind of world she would grow up in.

The weight of the decision was almost unbearable. If he released the archive, he would be responsible for the chaos that followed. If he didn't, he would be responsible for the continued castration of science.

He went to visit his father, hoping for guidance.

"I don't know what to do, Dad," he said, sitting at the kitchen table. "If I release the archive, it could cause chaos. If I don't, science will remain a shell of itself."

His father poured him a cup of coffee, the bitter aroma filling the room. "You're asking the wrong question, Marcus," he said. "The question isn't whether to release it. The question is what kind of world you want to live in."

Marcus stared at his coffee, the steam curling up toward the ceiling. "I want to live in a world where science is respected for what it is— a process of questioning, not a set of dogmatic beliefs. But I don't want to be responsible for people getting hurt."

"No one said it would be easy," his father said. "When I made my choice, I knew I would lose everything. But I also knew I couldn't live with myself if I didn't try."

Marcus thought about the Shudra students, about how they had been taught to accept science without question. He thought about the system, about how it had turned science into a tool of control. He thought about his daughter, about how she deserved to grow up in a world where she could question everything.

He left his father's apartment with a heavy heart, but with a clearer sense of what he had to do.

Back at home, he sat in his office, the archive glowing on his screen. The weight of the decision was still there, but now it was accompanied by a sense of resolve.

Marcus stood at the threshold, the archive ready, the truth preserved. On one side: scientific integrity, the right to question, the foundation of knowledge. On the other: social stability, the prevention of chaos, the system he had served. There was no right answer. There was only the choice he would make, and the world that would exist because of it.

He reached toward the interface. His fingers hovered over the activation key.

And then—

His daughter Sarah's voice echoed in his memory: "Dad, why do you always have to question everything?"

Marcus smiled bitterly. Because that's what science is, he thought. That's what it's always been.

He made his choice.
