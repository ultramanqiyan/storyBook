# Chapter 1: The Repository

The Science Repository hummed with the quiet power of accumulated knowledge—or what remained of it after simplification. Marcus Chen had spent fifteen years curating scientific truth, deciding which facts deserved preservation and which were too dangerous for general consumption. Today's queue included three physics papers, two biology studies, and a controversial climate model that the Brahmin council had flagged for "excessive complexity."

He sat at his desk, the morning light filtering through the windows of his San Francisco office. The repository's servers occupied three floors below him, storing millions of scientific papers, experiments, and theories—but only Brahmin caste members could access them in their original form. Everyone else received the simplified versions: stripped of uncertainty, cleansed of doubt, reduced to facts that could be memorized without questioning.

Marcus pulled up the first paper in his queue: a physics study on quantum entanglement. The original text was dense, full of mathematical proofs and experimental uncertainties. The simplified version, generated by the Meridian Science Module, reduced it to a single sentence: "Quantum particles can be connected across distances."

That wasn't science. That was a sound bite.

Marcus remembered his father's voice: "Science without doubt is just dogma in a lab coat." Benjamin Foster had been a physicist, one of the best in his generation, before the system had classified him as "historically problematic" for refusing to stop teaching students how to question.

Now Marcus was the one simplifying science, one paper at a time.

The interface pinged, notifying him of an incoming message. It was from his father.

The message was brief: "We need to talk."

Marcus stared at the screen. His father rarely reached out directly—they communicated through coded messages, careful phrases that wouldn't trigger the system's monitoring algorithms. A direct message meant something was wrong.

He finished reviewing the simplified versions of the three physics papers, flagged the biology studies for later, and closed the climate model without making a decision. Then he gathered his things and left the repository.

The San Francisco streets were busy with the mid-morning rush. Marcus walked past the cable cars, the tourists, the tech workers hurrying to their offices. He thought about the simplified science curriculum that Shudra students received—how they learned that gravity was "a force that pulls things down" without ever learning about relativity, space-time curvature, or the revolutionary ideas that had transformed our understanding of the universe.

He thought about his father, who had been exiled from the scientific community for insisting that students should learn to question. Who had been labeled "historically problematic" for teaching the scientific method.

And he thought about himself, who spent his days stripping the scientific method out of every paper that crossed his desk.

Benjamin Foster lived in a small apartment in Berkeley, the kind of place that smelled of old books and fresh coffee. Marcus had been visiting his father every Sunday for as long as he could remember, but today felt different. Today, he had questions he wasn't sure he wanted answered.

"Marcus!" his father said, opening the door with a warm smile. "Come in, come in. I made your favorite—dark roast with a dash of cinnamon."

Marcus stepped inside, the familiar scent of books and coffee wrapping around him like a blanket. The apartment was cluttered with old scientific journals, textbooks, and a half-finished physics problem on the kitchen table.

"You're still working on that quantum gravity problem?" Marcus asked, nodding at the papers.

"Some questions are worth pursuing, even when the system says they're 'too complex,'" his father said, pouring coffee into two mugs. "Have a seat."

They sat at the kitchen table, the sunlight filtering through the windows. For a moment, it was just father and son, not system curator and exiled physicist.

"So, how's the Repository?" his father asked, taking a sip of coffee.

"Busy," Marcus said. "We're processing a climate model that the council flagged for 'excessive complexity.'"

"Excessive complexity," his father repeated, the words tasting bitter on his tongue. "That's their code for 'too much uncertainty.' They don't like science that isn't neat and tidy."

Marcus felt a familiar tension building. This was the part of their conversations he dreaded—the part where his father criticized the very system Marcus had dedicated his career to.

"Do you know what falsifiability is, Marcus?" his father asked, leaning forward. "It's the heart of science. The idea that every theory must be testable, that every truth must be challengeable. When you remove that, you don't simplify science. You kill it."

"We're making science accessible, Dad," Marcus said, defending his work. "Most people can't understand the complexities of quantum mechanics or climate modeling."

"Accessible?" his father scoffed. "You're not making it accessible. You're turning it into a set of dogmatic beliefs. Science isn't about memorizing facts. It's about asking questions."

Marcus felt his temper rising. "You don't understand the pressure we're under. The system has to maintain social stability. If everyone had access to the full complexity of science, there would be chaos."

"Chaos?" his father said, his voice rising. "Science thrives on chaos. It thrives on uncertainty. That's how we make discoveries. When you remove the questions, you remove the possibility of progress."

There was a long silence. Marcus stared at his coffee, the bitter taste matching his mood.

"Do you know why they classified me as 'historically problematic'?" his father asked, breaking the silence.

Marcus shook his head. He'd always been told it was because his father had been a troublemaker, but he'd never gotten the full story.

"They classified me as 'historically problematic' because I refused to stop teaching my students how to question. I told them that science without skepticism isn't science at all. The system didn't like that. So here I am, a physicist who can't publish, can't teach, can't even access the full scientific literature. All because I believed in the scientific method."

Marcus felt a cold chill run down his spine. He'd always known his father had been marginalized, but he'd never fully understood why. Now he did. His father had been punished for doing what scientists are supposed to do—question.

"They've turned science into religion," his father said, his voice carrying the weight of decades of exile. "Facts without questioning. Truth without doubt. That's not science, Marcus. That's dogma dressed in a lab coat."

Marcus left his father's apartment with more questions than he'd arrived with. And for the first time, he wasn't sure he wanted to find the answers.

He walked back to the repository, the afternoon sun warm on his face. He thought about the climate model he'd left undecided, about the simplified versions he'd created, about the Shudra students who would learn science without ever learning how to question it.

He thought about his father, about how he had lost everything for standing up for the scientific method. About how he had been punished for doing what scientists are supposed to do.

And he thought about himself, about how he had spent fifteen years helping the system that had destroyed his father's career.

When he returned to his office, the climate model was still waiting. Marcus sat down at his desk, pulled up the original paper, and began to read.

Not the simplified version. The original. With all its complexity, all its uncertainty, all its doubt.

For the first time in fifteen years, he wanted to understand what he was simplifying away.
