# Chapter 1: The Repository

The Science Repository hummed with the quiet power of accumulated knowledge—or what remained of it after simplification. Marcus Chen had spent fifteen years curating scientific truth, deciding which facts deserved preservation and which were too dangerous for general consumption. Today's queue included three physics papers, two biology studies, and a controversial climate model that the Brahmin council had flagged for "excessive complexity."

Marcus sipped his coffee, the bitter aroma mixing with the faint scent of old paper that permeated his office. The repository's servers hummed in the background, a constant white noise that had become as familiar as his own heartbeat. He ran a finger along the cold surface of his interface, the smooth glass responding to his touch with a soft blue glow.

"I'll have it ready," Marcus said, his voice measured, evidence-based even in casual conversation. "Any particular concerns they mentioned?"

"Just the usual—'too complex for Shudra comprehension' and 'potential social instability,'" Lisa said, rolling her eyes slightly. "You know how they are."

Marcus nodded, turning back to his screen. The climate model data flickered before him, a complex web of variables and feedback loops. This was science as it should be—messy, questioning, full of uncertainty. But he knew what would happen next: the Meridian Science Module would simplify it, stripping away the complexity until only a sanitized conclusion remained.

He accessed the simplified version from yesterday's biology study. The original paper had discussed protein folding mechanisms, complete with molecular diagrams and statistical analyses. The simplified version read: "Proteins fold into shapes that help them work."

Marcus's chest tightened. That wasn't science—it was a factoid. Science wasn't about memorizing conclusions; it was about questioning, testing, refining. He remembered his father's words: "Science without doubt is just dogma in a lab coat."

The hum of the servers seemed to grow louder, as if the repository itself were judging him. Marcus ran a hand through his graying hair, the texture familiar under his fingers. He had always believed he was doing the right thing—making science accessible to those who couldn't understand its complexities. But now he wasn't so sure.

He pulled up the caste access levels on his screen. Brahmin: full access to original research, methodologies, and uncertainties. Kshatriya: advanced professional knowledge, simplified but still rigorous. Vaishya: intermediate knowledge with translations. Shudra: simplified knowledge only—conclusions without context. Dalit: passively received information, no active learning.

"Is this really accessibility?" he muttered to himself. "Or is it just censorship by another name?"

The interface beeped, notifying him of a new message. It was his father, Benjamin Foster, now classified as "historically problematic" due to his resistance to the simplification process. The message was brief: "We need to talk."

Marcus stared at the screen, the words burning into his retinas. His father had been a physicist, a man who had dedicated his life to the pursuit of scientific truth. Now he was relegated to a simplified existence, his once-brilliant mind constrained by the very system Marcus helped maintain.

The coffee in his mug had gone cold, the bitter taste lingering on his tongue. Marcus thought about the climate model, about how its complexities would be stripped away until only a simplistic narrative remained. He thought about the Shudra students who would learn science without ever learning how to question it.

He stood up and walked to the window, looking out at the San Francisco skyline. The fog was rolling in over the bay, obscuring the horizon. Somewhere out there, millions of people were living in a world of simplified science, a world where questions were discouraged and uncertainty was erased.

The simplified report stared back at him: "Gravity is a force that pulls things down." No mention of relativity, no space-time curvature, no questioning. Just a fact to be memorized. Marcus wondered what else had been lost in translation. He closed the report and looked out his window at the San Francisco skyline, the fog rolling in over the bay. Somewhere out there, millions of people were learning science without ever learning how to question it. And he was the one who had made that possible.
