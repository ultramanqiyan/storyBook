# Chapter 9: The Discovery

The archive was complete. Marcus stared at the encrypted files, the culmination of months of secret work. Thousands of scientific papers, preserved in their original form, ready to be released to anyone who wanted to see them. All he had to do was activate the distribution protocol.

But before he did, he wanted to understand the potential consequences. He created a simulation, modeling what would happen if the archive was released to the public.

The initial results were promising. The simulation showed a wave of scientific curiosity spreading through society, with people eager to learn about the real scientific method.

But as the simulation progressed, the results changed. People who had been taught that science was absolute truth began to experience cognitive dissonance. They couldn't reconcile the dogmatic science they had learned with the provisional, questioning science in the archive.

The simulation showed protests, riots, and social unrest. Institutions built on "settled science" were questioned. Teachers were unable to answer students' questions. The social order began to unravel.

Marcus felt a cold chill run down his spine. He had thought he was doing the right thing, preserving science for future generations. But now he realized he might be unleashing chaos.

He ran the simulation again, with different parameters, but the results were the same. The sudden introduction of scientific uncertainty would destabilize society. People weren't ready for the truth.

Marcus thought about his father, about how he had lost everything for standing up for the scientific method. He thought about the Shudra students, about how they had been taught to accept science without question. He thought about the system, about how it had been designed to maintain social stability at all costs.

The system might have been wrong, but it had been designed with a purpose. And now Marcus was about to undo that purpose, with potentially catastrophic consequences.

He sat in the darkness of his home office, the simulation results glowing on his screen. The numbers were clear: if he released the archive, the sudden introduction of scientific uncertainty would destabilize society. People who had been taught that science was absolute truth would suddenly discover that everything they knew was provisional, questionable, uncertain. The chaos would be incalculable.

There was no good choice. There was only the choice he would have to live with.
