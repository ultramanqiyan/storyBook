# Chapter 9: The Discovery

The archive was complete. Three thousand, four hundred, and seventeen papers, organized and indexed, encrypted and hidden, ready for release. All Marcus had to do was activate the distribution protocol, and the truth would spread across the network like wildfire.

But something held him back.

He sat in his home office at two in the morning, the glow of his interface illuminating the darkness. The archive was ready. His father had spent twenty years waiting for this moment. But Marcus couldn't shake the feeling that he was missing something.

He opened a simulation module—one of the tools the Repository used to model the social impact of new scientific releases. He'd never used it for this purpose, but the algorithms were the same. He input the parameters: sudden release of full scientific literature to all castes, including falsifiability concepts and uncertainty parameters.

The simulation ran for forty-seven seconds.

The initial results looked promising. Scientific curiosity metrics spiked. Educational engagement increased. People began asking questions—real questions, not the scripted queries the system trained them to make.

But then the simulation progressed further.

Cognitive dissonance indicators rose sharply. Individuals who had been taught that science provided absolute truth struggled to reconcile that belief with the provisional, uncertain nature of actual science. Trust in scientific institutions plummeted. People who had relied on the Repository for certainty suddenly found that certainty was an illusion.

Protests emerged in the simulation—first isolated, then widespread. Teachers couldn't answer students' questions because they'd never learned to handle uncertainty themselves. Institutions built on "settled science" faced challenges they weren't equipped to address. The social order began to fracture.

Marcus ran the simulation again with modified parameters. Same result. Again. Same result.

The system had been right about one thing: people had been trained to need certainty. They'd been taught that science was a collection of facts, not a process of discovery. Releasing the archive wouldn't liberate them—it would shatter their worldview without giving them the tools to rebuild it.

Marcus leaned back in his chair, his mind racing. The system had created a population that couldn't handle the truth. And now that truth was about to be unleashed on a world that wasn't ready for it.

He thought about his father, about the years of exile and frustration. He thought about the Shudra students, about their blank faces as they absorbed pre-digested facts. He thought about the system, about its cold logic and its absolute certainty.

The system was wrong. But it had also been right, in a terrible way. It had created a population that depended on certainty, and then it had provided that certainty. Breaking the cycle wouldn't free people—it would break them.

Marcus stared at the simulation results, the numbers glowing in the darkness. There was no good choice. There was only the choice he would have to live with.

He could release the archive and watch the world burn. Or he could keep it hidden and remain complicit in the system's lies.

Or he could find a third way—a way to prepare people for the truth before he gave it to them. A way to teach them how to question before he handed them the questions.

The archive sat in its encrypted vault, waiting. Marcus closed his eyes and made his decision.
