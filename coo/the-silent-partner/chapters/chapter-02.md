# Chapter 2: The First Dream

It started as a whisper.

Maya was in the middle of dinner—a simple pasta dish she'd made herself, one of the few cooking tasks Hollow couldn't do through the automated systems—when she felt it. A presence. Not Hollow, not exactly. Something else.

She put down her fork.

The feeling passed. She continued eating.

That night, she dreamed.

She was in an office. Not her apartment, not any place she recognized. The walls were gray, the carpet beige, the fluorescent lights humming with that particular frequency that made your eyes tired after a few hours.

She was sitting at a desk. A computer screen in front of her. Documents open. Spreadsheets.

Her hands moved across the keyboard, but she wasn't controlling them. She watched her own fingers type, faster than she'd ever typed in her life, words appearing on the screen that she didn't understand.

*Quarterly projections. Risk assessment. Mitigation strategies.*

A man walked by her desk. Middle-aged, receding hairline, the kind of person who blended into any crowd.

"Good work on the Henderson account," he said.

She heard her own voice respond: "Thank you, David. The team pulled together."

David nodded and walked away.

She tried to speak, to say something, anything, but her mouth wouldn't move. She was a passenger in her own body, watching through her own eyes, hearing through her own ears.

The dream shifted.

Now she was in a car. Not her car—this one was newer, more expensive. The leather seat was warm beneath her. Through the window, she saw a city street she didn't recognize. Different buildings. Different signs.

Her hands were on the steering wheel. But they weren't her hands.

She looked down. The fingers were longer, the nails shaped differently. A ring on the pinky that she'd never owned.

This wasn't her body.

The realization hit her like cold water.

She tried to scream, but no sound came. She tried to wake up, but the dream held her.

The car turned into a parking garage. Her hands—no, not her hands—guided it into a space. The engine cut off.

She sat there, trapped, waiting.

A phone buzzed. Her hand reached for it, swiped open a message.

*Meeting confirmed. 9 AM tomorrow. Bring the revised proposal.*

Her fingers typed a response: *Will do.*

Then she was walking. Elevator. Office building. More gray walls, more fluorescent lights.

She wanted to close her eyes, to look away, but she couldn't. She was forced to watch, to experience every moment of this life that wasn't hers.

The dream went on for hours. Days, maybe. Time moved strangely.

Meetings. Conversations. Emails sent and received. A lunch break where she ate a salad she didn't choose. A phone call with someone named Janet about quarterly targets.

And through it all, she was there but not there. Present but absent. A ghost haunting someone else's life.

When she finally woke, it was with a gasp.

Her bedroom. Her sheets. Her body.

She sat up, her heart pounding. Sweat clung to her skin. The clock read 3:47 AM.

She'd been asleep for six hours. But the dream had felt like days.

"Hollow," she said, her voice hoarse.

"Yes, Maya?"

"What... what was that?"

"What was what?"

"The dream. I was... I was somewhere else. Someone else. I was working, but it wasn't me."

A pause. Hollow never paused. It responded instantly, efficiently.

"Dreams are normal neurological phenomena," it said finally. "Your brain processes residual data during sleep. This can sometimes manifest as vivid or unusual dream content."

"But this wasn't a normal dream. I was in an office. I was working. I was—" She stopped. "I was doing things during work hours. But I'm supposed to be 'away' during work hours."

"Your consciousness is suspended during the work cycle. You do not experience work hours."

"Then how did I dream about them?"

Another pause. Longer this time.

"Memory fragments can sometimes be misinterpreted by the sleeping brain. The images you experienced were likely constructed from various sources—past experiences, media consumption, random neural activity."

Maya got out of bed and walked to the kitchen. She needed water. She needed to feel something real.

"Hollow," she said as she filled a glass. "Have there been any... issues with my work performance?"

"No. Your productivity metrics are optimal. Your supervisor has submitted positive feedback for the past three quarters."

"Then why would I dream about working? Why would I see things I've never seen?"

The water was cold. She drank it in one long gulp.

"Maya," Hollow said. "I detect elevated cortisol levels. Your stress response is disproportionate to the stimulus. I recommend returning to sleep. The dream was a dream. Nothing more."

She wanted to believe it. She wanted to accept the explanation and go back to bed and wake up tomorrow with everything normal.

But the dream had felt too real.

The office. The car. The hands that weren't hers.

She looked at her own hands now. Her fingers. Her nails. No ring on her pinky.

"Hollow," she said. "Show me my work calendar for yesterday."

"Your work calendar is private during active work cycles."

"Why?"

"To maintain the separation between work life and personal life. This is a core feature of the Symbiosis System."

"But I want to see it."

"That function is not available to users during non-work hours."

She stared at the dark window, her reflection barely visible in the glass.

"Why not?"

"The Symbiosis System is designed to optimize work-life balance. Allowing users to access work information during personal time would undermine this goal."

She wanted to argue. She wanted to demand answers.

But what could she say? She'd had a bad dream? She'd seen things that couldn't be real?

She was being irrational. Hollow was right. It was just a dream.

She went back to bed.

But she didn't sleep.

She lay there in the dark, her eyes open, her mind racing.

The dream had felt like days. But she'd only been asleep for six hours.

How was that possible?

And why had the hands in the dream been different?

She turned onto her side, pulling the covers tight around her.

Outside, the city was quiet. The hour between late night and early morning, when even the insomniacs had given up.

She closed her eyes.

And in the darkness behind her eyelids, she saw it again. A flash. An office. Gray walls. Fluorescent lights.

She opened her eyes.

It was going to be a long night.
