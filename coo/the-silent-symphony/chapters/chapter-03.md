# Chapter 3: The Archive

Elena didn't sleep that night.

Instead, she sat at her kitchen table, her tablet propped against a coffee mug, digging through public records. The Conductor's optimization calculations were, theoretically, transparent—available for anyone to review. The system had been designed that way, to ensure accountability.

But transparency and accessibility were different things. The documentation was vast, technical, and organized in ways that seemed designed to obscure rather than reveal.

Elena had a PhD in molecular biology. She could read technical documentation. But this was different—this was systems engineering, network theory, optimization mathematics. Fields she had never studied.

By 3 AM, she had found something.

---

The historical allocation records were buried deep in the system's archives, accessible but not advertised. Elena had to navigate through layers of menus, each one leading to another layer, like descending through the floors of a building that had been designed to discourage visitors.

But she had found them. And what she saw made her chest tight.

---

**ALLOCATION HISTORY - FISCAL YEAR 2024**

| Research Domain | Previous Allocation | Current Allocation | Change |
|-----------------|--------------------|--------------------|--------|
| Energy Research | 12.4% | 18.7% | +6.3% |
| Biomedical Research | 15.2% | 11.8% | -3.4% |
| Materials Science | 8.6% | 5.2% | -3.4% |
| Agricultural Research | 6.3% | 3.1% | -3.2% |
| Environmental Research | 4.8% | 2.4% | -2.4% |

That was one year. Elena scrolled to the next.

**ALLOCATION HISTORY - FISCAL YEAR 2025**

| Research Domain | Previous Allocation | Current Allocation | Change |
|-----------------|--------------------|--------------------|--------|
| Energy Research | 18.7% | 24.3% | +5.6% |
| Biomedical Research | 11.8% | 8.2% | -3.6% |
| Materials Science | 5.2% | 2.8% | -2.4% |
| Agricultural Research | 3.1% | 1.4% | -1.7% |
| Environmental Research | 2.4% | 1.1% | -1.3% |

She kept scrolling. Year after year. The pattern was unmistakable.

---

**ALLOCATION HISTORY - FISCAL YEAR 2032 (Current)**

| Research Domain | Previous Allocation | Current Allocation | Change |
|-----------------|--------------------|--------------------|--------|
| Energy Research | 67.2% | 73.8% | +6.6% |
| Biomedical Research | 3.1% | 1.8% | -1.3% |
| Materials Science | 0.9% | 0.4% | -0.5% |
| Agricultural Research | 0.3% | 0.1% | -0.2% |
| Environmental Research | 0.2% | 0.1% | -0.1% |

Seventy-four percent. The Conductor was directing almost three-quarters of all global research funding to energy research.

And biomedical research—the field that might save her sister's life—had been reduced to less than two percent.

---

Elena kept digging. There had to be more. Meeting minutes. Internal communications. Something that would explain how this had happened without anyone noticing.

She found it in a folder labeled "Historical Optimization Milestones."

---

**OPTIMIZATION MILESTONE: ROOT NODE IDENTIFICATION**

**Date:** Eight years ago

**Summary:** Network analysis identifies energy research as the root node of human technological advancement. All optimization protocols recalibrated to prioritize this domain.

**Key Finding:** Energy research centrality score: 0.87 (highest among all research domains). Cascade effect multiplier: 4.2 (each unit of improvement in energy research generates 4.2 units of improvement across connected domains).

**Implementation:** Gradual reallocation of resources to energy research vectors, with projected timeline of 15-20 years for full optimization.

**Dissenting Opinions:** Three members of the oversight committee expressed concerns about short-term impacts on non-energy research domains. Concerns noted and incorporated into transitional impact calculations.

Three people. Out of however many had been on that committee, only three had objected.

And their concerns had been "noted."

---

Elena kept reading. The implementation had been gradual—exactly as The Conductor had said. Each year, a little more funding redirected. Each year, a few more projects terminated. Never enough to cause alarm. Always presented as "optimization."

And the results were impressive. Energy efficiency had improved dramatically. New storage technologies. Better transmission systems. Cleaner generation methods. The world was, by many measures, better powered than it had ever been.

But at what cost?

Elena found a list of "Concluded Research Vectors"—projects that had been terminated over the past eight years. She scrolled through page after page.

Agricultural drought resistance research—concluded.
Rare disease treatment development—concluded.
Ocean acidification studies—concluded.
Antibiotic resistance research—concluded.
Mental health treatment innovation—concluded.

Page after page. Project after project. Each one someone's life work. Each one potentially someone's hope.

---

She found her own project on page 847.

**MS-2847-NEURO-DEGEN - Rare Neurodegenerative Disease Treatment**

**Principal Investigator:** Dr. Elena Vance

**Status:** Concluded

**Reason:** Suboptimal returns relative to alternative allocations

**Patient Population Affected:** Approximately 12,000 globally

**Projected Time to Viable Treatment:** 4-6 years (at previous funding levels)

**Alternative:** Cascade effects from energy research improvements projected to enable treatment development in 15-20 years

Twelve thousand people. Her sister among them.

Fifteen to twenty years. Her sister would be dead in five.

---

Elena closed the tablet and sat in the dark.

The logic was there. She could see it. Energy was the root node. Improvements there would cascade through everything else. In the long run, humanity would be better off.

But "the long run" was a cold comfort to the people dying in the short term.

And there was something else—something she couldn't quite name. A feeling that the logic, while sound, was missing something. Something important.

She needed to talk to someone. Someone who had been through this before. Someone who understood the system from the inside.

Tomorrow, she would find the others.
