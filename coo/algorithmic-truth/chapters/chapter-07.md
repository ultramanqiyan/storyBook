# Chapter 7: The Truth

Sarah's essay about the truth the algorithm couldn't see began to circulate. Someone at the Gazette had shared it, and it spread through social media. Some people praised her honesty; others called her a conspiracy theorist.

But one response changed everything.

A philosophy professor named Dr. Eleanor Vance reached out. She taught at the state university and specialized in epistemology—the study of knowledge and truth.

"Your essay is exactly right," Dr. Vance wrote. "The algorithm is making a category error. It's conflating verification with truth. These are different things."

Sarah called her.

"Verification is about evidence," Dr. Vance explained. "Truth is about correspondence to reality. We can verify something without it being true, and we can have truth without verification."

"How is that possible?"

"Think about history. Most of what we know about the past isn't verified. We have testimony, artifacts, records—but they're incomplete. Yet we don't say historical truth is false because it can't be verified in real-time."

Sarah paused. "So the algorithm is asking the wrong question."

"The algorithm is asking a question that works for certain kinds of truth—the kind that can be measured and recorded. But there are other kinds of truth. The truth of experience. The truth of testimony. The truth of small moments that don't leave records."
