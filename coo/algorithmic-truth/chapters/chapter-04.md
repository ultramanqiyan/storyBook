# Chapter 4: The Evidence

Sarah couldn't let the story go. The "LIKELY FALSE" label burned in her mind.

She started digging deeper. The man who collapsed had left with a woman—maybe his wife. If she could find him, she'd have the evidence the algorithm demanded.

---

Three days of searching. Sarah visited every church, community center, and coffee shop in town. She showed the description to anyone who would listen: "Man, sixties, gray hair, collapsed at Harmon's Grocery on March 12th."

On the fourth day, a breakthrough.

A woman at the senior center tilted her head. "That sounds like Harold Brennan. He had a heart scare a few weeks back. Said some woman brought him back. His wife was with him—Dorothy."

"Where can I find them?"

"They winter in Arizona. Won't be back until May."

---

Sarah called Arizona. The number was listed under H. Brennan.

"Hello?"

"Mr. Brennan? My name is Sarah Mitchell. I'm a reporter with the Iowa Valley Gazette—"

"I don't want to talk to reporters."

"Please, sir. I wrote a story about what happened at Harmon's Grocery. The woman who helped you—"

"I don't know what you're talking about."

The line went dead.

---

Sarah called again. No answer. She called a third time. Voicemail.

She left a message: "Mr. Brennan, I know you were at Harmon's on March 12th. I know a woman helped you. My story about that incident has been labeled false by an AI fact-checking system. I'm not trying to cause trouble. I just want to verify that it happened. Please call me back."

He didn't call back.

---

But Sarah had a name now: Harold Brennan. And names could be traced.

She searched property records. Harold and Dorothy Brennan owned a house on Oak Street. She searched voter registration. Harold Brennan, born 1948. She searched social media. Nothing.

Then she tried something else. Hospital records weren't public, but she knew people who knew people.

A friend in billing at Iowa Valley Hospital texted back: *Harold Brennan came in for a checkup two days after March 12. Routine follow-up. No admission.*

It wasn't much. But it was something. Harold Brennan existed. He'd had a medical event around the right time. And he'd refused to talk about it.

---

Sarah wrote it all down. The evidence was circumstantial, but it was evidence. An algorithm might not accept it, but a human would understand.

She submitted a third dispute to FactCheck AI, attaching her documentation:

*1. Witness Martha Chen's testimony (recorded interview attached)*
*2. Confirmation from Harmon's manager Tom Wilson that an incident occurred*
*3. Identification of the individual who collapsed: Harold Brennan*
*4. Medical record confirmation that Harold Brennan sought follow-up care two days after the incident*
*5. Identification of the responder: Elena Vasquez, RN, Iowa Valley Hospital*

She hit send.

---

The response came the next morning.

*Dear Sarah Mitchell,*

*Your third dispute has been reviewed. While the additional documentation is noted, our verification system has determined that it does not meet the threshold for removing the "LIKELY FALSE" label.*

*Specifically:*
- *Witness testimony remains uncorroborated*
- *Store manager confirmation is secondhand*
- *Individual identified has not confirmed the incident*
- *Medical record shows follow-up care but does not confirm the described event*
- *Identified responder has not confirmed participation*

*Our verification system requires direct confirmation from involved parties. Without such confirmation, the label will remain.*

*This decision is final.*

Sarah stared at the screen. The algorithm wanted Harold Brennan or Elena Vasquez to confirm what had happened. But they wouldn't.

The truth existed. But it couldn't be verified.
