# Chapter 5: The Witness

Martha Chen sat across from Sarah in the Gazette office, her hands wrapped around a cup of tea Sarah had made for her.

"They're calling your story false," Martha said. "I've seen the label online."

"I know." Sarah's voice was tired. "I'm sorry, Martha. You came to me with something beautiful, and I've turned it into... this."

Martha was quiet for a moment. "What do you need?"

"Your testimony. Officially. On record. I need to submit something the algorithm will accept."

"What does that mean?"

Sarah explained: a formal interview, recorded, with Martha providing her full name, contact information, and a detailed account of what she witnessed. Something that could be verified—not just the words, but the person behind them.

Martha listened carefully. "And that will fix it?"

"I don't know. But it's the best chance I have."

---

The interview took an hour. Sarah asked Martha to describe everything: the produce section, the man collapsing, the woman in scrubs running over, the CPR, the man breathing again, the woman leaving.

Martha's voice was steady, her details consistent. She answered every question Sarah posed, never wavering.

When it was over, Sarah had a transcript and a recording. She submitted both to FactCheck AI.

---

The response came within hours.

*Dear Sarah Mitchell,*

*Your submission has been received. The witness testimony provided is detailed and internally consistent. However, our verification system requires corroboration from multiple independent sources. A single witness, regardless of credibility, does not meet our threshold for verification.*

*Additionally, our system has analyzed the witness's account and identified potential concerns:*
- *Witness is 74 years old; memory reliability may be a factor*
- *No other witnesses have come forward*
- *Physical evidence (security footage) is unavailable*

*The "LIKELY FALSE" label will remain.*

*This decision is final.*

Sarah read the last line again. "This decision is final."

The algorithm had dismissed Martha's testimony because of her age. Because no one else had come forward. Because there was no video.

---

She drove to Martha's house that evening. The older woman invited her in, offered tea, sat her down at the kitchen table.

"They rejected it," Sarah said. "Because you're seventy-four. Because there's no video. Because no one else saw."

Martha's face was calm. "I know what I saw, Sarah."

"I believe you. But the algorithm doesn't."

"Then the algorithm is wrong."

Sarah looked at this woman—seventy-four years old, sharp-eyed, steady-handed. Martha Chen had lived in Iowa Valley her whole life. She'd raised three children here. She'd served on the school board for twelve years. She'd witnessed a man nearly die and a stranger save him.

And a machine had decided her testimony wasn't reliable because of her age.

"It's not just about this story," Sarah said slowly. "It's about who gets to decide what's true. A machine that can't see, can't hear, can't know what it's like to be human—that's what's deciding."

Martha nodded. "Then what are you going to do about it?"
