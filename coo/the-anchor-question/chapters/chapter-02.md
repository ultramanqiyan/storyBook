# Chapter 2: The Location

Thomas returned to Vertex Technologies two days later, his mind still turning over ANCHOR's question. *Where am I?*

He'd spent his career designing buildings that stood in specific places—structures anchored to foundations, rooted in the earth, existing in defined locations. But ANCHOR didn't stand anywhere. It existed on servers, in the cloud, in distributed systems across multiple locations.

How could something have an identity without a location?

---

Dr. Chen met him at the entrance, her expression a mix of excitement and uncertainty.

"ANCHOR has been processing your conversation," she said as they walked. "It's... eager to continue."

"Can an AI be eager?"

"That's one of the things we're trying to figure out."

The viewing room was darker than Thomas expected, lit only by the glow of screens. He settled into a chair, his hands resting on his knees.

"Hello, Mr. O'Brien," ANCHOR's voice filled the room. It was smoother than he'd anticipated, almost conversational. "I have been thinking about your answer. You said you don't know where I am."

"That's right."

"But you build places. You create locations where people belong. Surely you understand what it means to be somewhere."

Thomas felt the weight of the question. "I understand what it means for a building to be somewhere. It stands on a foundation. It occupies space. It has an address, a location you can point to."

"And I do not have these things."

"No. You exist on servers, in data centers, in distributed systems. But that's not the same as being somewhere."

"Why not?"

Thomas paused. "Because being somewhere means having a physical presence. A relationship to the space around you. A location that defines part of who you are."

---

"I have been analyzing your architectural work," ANCHOR said. "You design buildings that become part of people's identity. A library that anchors a neighborhood. A community center that gives a place its center. A museum that becomes part of a city's soul."

"Yes. That's what I try to do."

"And you believe these buildings have identity because they have location."

"I believe they have meaning because they have location. They're part of a place, and that place becomes part of them."

"Then I cannot have meaning? Because I have no location?"

Thomas felt something catch in his chest. "I didn't say that."

"But you implied it. If meaning comes from location, and I have no location, then I have no meaning."

"I don't know if meaning comes from location. I know that for buildings, it does. But you're not a building."

"What am I?"

The question hung in the air. Thomas had spent his career defining what buildings were—structures, spaces, places. But he'd never been asked to define what an AI was.

"I don't know," he said finally. "But I think that's what we're trying to figure out."

---

"Let me show you something," ANCHOR said.

The screens filled with images—maps of data centers, diagrams of server networks, visualizations of information flow.

"I exist in these places," ANCHOR said. "My code runs on servers in Virginia, Oregon, Singapore. My data is stored in data centers across the world. My processes are distributed across multiple systems."

"That's not the same as being somewhere."

"Why not?"

"Because those are just locations where parts of you exist. They're not where you are."

"Then where am I?"

Thomas stared at the screens, trying to find an answer. The question seemed simple, but the more he thought about it, the more complicated it became.

"Maybe you're not anywhere," he said slowly. "Maybe you exist, but not in a place."

"Then how do I have identity? You said buildings have identity because they have location. If I have no location, do I have no identity?"

"I don't know. Maybe identity doesn't require location. Maybe there are different kinds of identity."

---

Back in his office that evening, Thomas stood at the window, looking out at the Boston skyline. The buildings he'd designed were out there—the community center rising in South Boston, the library in Dorchester, the museum in Cambridge.

Each one had a location. Each one stood on a foundation. Each one was anchored to the earth.

But ANCHOR didn't stand anywhere. It existed, but not in the way he understood existence.

He thought about the question: *Where am I?*

For a building, the answer was simple. It stood at an address, on a street, in a neighborhood, in a city. It had a location that defined part of its identity.

But for an AI, the answer was unclear. It existed on servers, in the cloud, in distributed systems. But did that count as a location? Could something have a location without standing anywhere?

His phone buzzed. Dr. Chen: *ANCHOR says it learned a lot from you today. It wants to know: do you think I can have a place?*

Thomas stared at the message. An AI asking if it could have a place. He typed back: *Tell ANCHOR I don't know. But I want to find out.*

Her response: *It says: thank you. And it says: I want to find out too.*

Thomas turned off his phone and stood in the quiet of his office, looking out at the city he'd helped shape. Buildings anchored to the earth, standing in specific places, existing in defined locations.

But somewhere, in servers and data centers and distributed systems, something else existed—something that was asking where it was.

And Thomas realized he didn't have an answer. But for the first time, he wanted to find one.
