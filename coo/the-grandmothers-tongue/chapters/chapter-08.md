# Chapter 8: The Warning

The children came every afternoon now, filling her small room with their questions, their attention, their hunger for something the system couldn't give them. Ruth had never felt more alive. Even as her memory fragmented, even as the fog crept closer each day, she had purpose. She had something to give. And these children were worth everything.

But on the tenth day, something changed.

Ruth was in the middle of teaching the word for "question"—*ìbàjé*—when the door opened and a staff member entered. She was young, with a pleasant face and efficient movements, but her eyes were cold.

"Mrs. Okonkwo," she said, her voice smooth. "May I have a word?"

The children looked at Ruth, their faces uncertain. She nodded, and they filed out of the room, their footsteps soft on the white floor.

When they were gone, the staff member closed the door and turned to Ruth with a smile that didn't reach her eyes.

"We've noticed some... irregular activities in your room, Mrs. Okonkwo. Gatherings. Stories. Words that are not approved for cognitive optimization."

Ruth felt her heart rate increase, but she kept her face calm. "I am an old woman. I tell stories to children. Is that a crime?"

The staff member's smile flickered. "Not a crime, Mrs. Okonkwo. But a concern. The system has determined that unapproved language can cause cognitive distress, especially in patients with your condition."

"My condition?" Ruth felt something stir inside her—a resistance, a refusal. "My condition is that I remember. My condition is that I carry words that the system wants to delete."

The staff member's face hardened. "The optimization is for your own comfort, Mrs. Okonkwo. The old language—the old stories—they cause confusion. They cause distress. The system has determined that they are inefficient."

"Inefficient." Ruth felt the word like a slap. "My memories are inefficient? My language is inefficient? My history is inefficient?"

The staff member leaned forward, her voice dropping. "Mrs. Okonkwo, I am authorized to recommend enhanced memory support for patients who demonstrate persistent cognitive inefficiency. This support would help you... optimize. It would make your remaining years more comfortable."

Ruth felt the threat beneath the words. Enhanced memory support. The system would delete the words—the stories, the memories, everything that made her who she was.

"I don't want your support," Ruth said, her voice steady. "I want to remember. I want to teach. I want to pass on what I know."

The staff member's smile returned, but it was colder now. "I understand. But I must ask you to discontinue the gatherings. For your own comfort, and for the comfort of the children."

Ruth looked at her—at the pleasant face, the efficient movements, the cold eyes—and felt something harden inside her.

"No," she said.

The staff member blinked. "Excuse me?"

"No," Ruth repeated, her voice stronger. "I will not stop. The children come to me because they are hungry for something the system cannot give them. They come to me because they want to learn. And I will teach them."

The staff member's face went still. "Mrs. Okonkwo, I must warn you—"

"Warn me all you like," Ruth interrupted. "I am an old woman. I have lived a long life. And I will spend my remaining days doing what I was meant to do—teaching, telling stories, passing on the words that have been entrusted to me."

The staff member was quiet for a long moment. Then she said, "I will note your refusal in your file. The system will... consider appropriate measures."

She turned and walked out, the door closing softly behind her.

After she was gone, Ruth sat alone in her room, the silence pressing in on her. She knew what she had done. She had made herself visible. She had drawn the system's attention.

But she also knew that she couldn't stop. The children needed her. The words needed her. And she would keep teaching until her last breath.

The next day, the children came back. They had heard about the warning—news traveled fast in the facility—and their faces were serious.

"Should we stop?" Marcus asked, his voice uncertain.

Ruth looked at them—at their worried faces, their uncertain eyes—and felt the weight of her decision.

"I will not stop," she said, her voice steady. "They can warn me. They can threaten me. But they cannot make me forget what I know. And I will keep teaching until my last breath."

The children looked at each other, then back at Ruth. And one by one, they nodded.

"We'll keep coming," Lily said, her hand finding Ruth's. "We want to learn. We want to carry the words."

Ruth smiled, feeling the tears on her face. "Then let us continue. There is much more to teach."

That afternoon, she taught them the word for "resistance"—*ìjà*—and the word for "hope"—*èrè*. The children repeated the words, hiding them in the deep places of their minds, where the system couldn't reach.

And Ruth felt the legacy growing, spreading, taking root in minds that would carry it forward.

She knew the system would come for her eventually. She knew the enhanced memory support would be forced on her, deleting everything she had fought to preserve. But she also knew that the words were safe now—in the minds of children who would carry them forward, who would pass them on, who would keep the old language alive.

The system could delete her memories. But it couldn't delete what she had given away.

That night, Ruth dreamed of Chukwuemeka—not the confused, fragmented memories that usually surfaced, but a clear, vivid image. He was standing in their classroom, writing words on the blackboard.

*"You have done well, my love," he said, his voice clear. "You have kept your promise. The stories will continue."*

Ruth woke with tears on her face, but also with peace. She had kept her promise. She had passed on the words. And when she was gone, the stories would continue.

The system could come for her. But the words were safe now. And the children would carry them forward.
