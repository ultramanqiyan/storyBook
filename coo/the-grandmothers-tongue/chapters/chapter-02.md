# Chapter 2: The Visitor

The caregiver's voice was gentle. "Ruth, you have a visitor. Your daughter is here."

A daughter. The word surfaced slowly, bringing with it a flood of half-formed memories—a small hand in hers, a voice calling "Mama," a face that was both familiar and strange. She had a daughter. She remembered that much.

The door opened, and a woman entered. She was tall, with dark skin and hair pulled back in a neat bun. Her face was kind but careful, her movements efficient and precise. She wore the gray and white uniform of the optimized world—the world outside these white walls.

"Mama." The woman's voice was warm but measured. "How are you today?"

Ruth looked at her, searching for recognition. The face was familiar—the shape of the eyes, the curve of the smile. But the name wouldn't come.

"I'm..." Ruth paused, trying to find the words. "I don't remember your name."

The woman's smile flickered, just for a moment. "I'm Adanna. Your daughter."

Adanna. The name settled into Ruth's memory, bringing with it more fragments—a baby's cry, a child's laughter, a young woman's graduation. She had watched this woman grow. She had loved this woman her whole life.

"Adanna," Ruth said, the name feeling both familiar and foreign on her tongue. "My daughter."

"Yes, Mama." Adanna sat in the chair beside the bed, her hands folded in her lap. "I came to visit. I try to come every week."

Every week. Ruth tried to remember the last visit, but the memory slipped away. The days blurred together in this white room, one flowing into another without distinction.

"They tell me you're doing well," Adanna said, her voice warm but careful. "Your optimization scores are good. The caregivers say you're comfortable."

Comfortable. The word felt wrong. Ruth wasn't comfortable. She was confused, lost, adrift in a sea of white. But she didn't know how to say that in the language Adanna was speaking—the optimized language of efficiency and progress.

"I had a dream," Ruth said, the words coming without thought. "I dreamed of Nigeria. Of the rain. Of my mother singing."

Adanna's face changed, just for a moment—a flicker of something that might have been pain, or fear, or longing. Then it smoothed back into the pleasant, efficient expression that seemed to be her default.

"Dreams are normal for your condition, Mama. The system can help with that. Have you been using the memory support?"

"I don't want the memory support." Ruth's voice was stronger than she expected. "I want to remember."

Adanna was quiet for a moment. Then she said, "Remembering can be painful, Mama. The system can help you... optimize. Make things easier."

"Make what easier?" Ruth felt something stir inside her—a resistance, a refusal. "Make forgetting easier?"

Adanna's smile didn't waver, but something hardened in her eyes. "The optimization is for your own comfort, Mama. The old memories—the old language—they cause distress. The system has determined that they're inefficient."

Inefficient. The word felt like a slap. Ruth's memories, her language, her history—they were inefficient?

"Nne m," Ruth said, the Yoruba words coming without thought. "Kíni tó n ṣe e?"

My mother, what is troubling you?

But Adanna just looked at her, confusion in her eyes. "I don't understand, Mama. What language is that?"

The grief rose like a tide. Ruth's own daughter couldn't understand her. Couldn't hear the words that had been passed down through generations of women—the words that carried love, and wisdom, and the weight of history.

"It's Yoruba," Ruth said, her voice thick. "Your grandmother's language. My mother's language."

Adanna's face went still. "The old language is not... recommended, Mama. The system has determined that it causes cognitive inefficiency. It's better to speak Optima."

Better. Ruth wanted to laugh, or cry, or scream. Better to forget? Better to lose the words that carried her history, her identity, her self?

"I don't want to forget," Ruth said, her voice steady despite the grief. "I want to remember. I want to speak the words my mother taught me."

Adanna reached out and took Ruth's hand, her grip warm but impersonal. "Mama, I know this is hard. But the system knows what's best. The optimization is for your own good."

Ruth looked at her daughter—at the face that was so familiar, so loved—and felt the gap between them like a chasm. Adanna spoke a language that had no room for the word "mother" as Ruth understood it. Adanna lived in a world that had deleted the very concept of what Ruth was trying to pass on.

"I love you," Ruth said, the words inadequate but true. "I have always loved you. Even when I can't remember your name, I love you."

Adanna's smile softened, becoming almost genuine. "I love you too, Mama. That's why I want you to be comfortable. That's why I want you to use the memory support."

But Ruth shook her head. "I don't want to be comfortable. I want to be... myself. Even if myself is confused. Even if myself is lost."

Adanna was quiet for a long moment. Then she stood, her movements efficient and precise. "I should go. I have work at the medical center. But I'll come back next week."

"Next week," Ruth repeated, the words feeling hollow.

Adanna leaned down and kissed Ruth's forehead—a brief, impersonal touch. "Take care, Mama. Let the system help you."

And then she was gone, the door closing softly behind her.

After Adanna left, Ruth sat alone in her white room, the silence pressing in on her. She had a daughter who loved her, who came to visit, who held her hand and spoke kind words. But the words were wrong—optimized, efficient, empty of the things that mattered.

Her daughter spoke a language that had no room for the word "mother" as Ruth understood it. And Ruth spoke a language her daughter couldn't hear.

She looked at the photograph on the shelf—the young woman with the bright smile, the man beside her, the children at their feet. That woman had a name. That woman had a story. And that woman had passed on something that her daughter couldn't receive.

Ruth closed her eyes and reached for the fragments—the smell of palm oil, the sound of rain, her mother's voice singing in Yoruba. The words were still there, hidden somewhere the system couldn't reach.

*"Nitori omo ni n se baba, nitori omo ni n se iya..."*

Because of the child, one becomes a father. Because of the child, one becomes a mother.

She had been a mother. She was still a mother. And even if her daughter couldn't hear her, Ruth would keep speaking. She would keep remembering. She would keep carrying the words that the system wanted to delete.

Because somewhere, there had to be someone who could hear.
