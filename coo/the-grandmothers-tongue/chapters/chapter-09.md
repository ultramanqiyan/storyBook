# Chapter 9: The Passing

Ruth could feel herself fading. The fog was thicker now, pressing in from all sides, stealing memories before she could catch them. But some things remained clear—Lily's face, the words she had to pass on, the stories that had to survive. She didn't have much time. She had to make sure the legacy was secure.

Lily came every day now, sitting by Ruth's bed, holding her hand, listening to the fragments that surfaced. And Ruth taught her everything she could—the words, the stories, the techniques for hiding them in the deep places of the mind.

"There are five words you must carry," Ruth said one afternoon, her voice weak but clear. "Five words that hold everything."

Lily leaned forward, her eyes bright with attention.

"Ìmìnira," Ruth said, feeling the weight of the word. "Freedom. The ability to choose. To be the author of your own story."

"Ìmìnira," Lily repeated, her voice steady.

"Ayafe," Ruth continued. "Beloved. The word for love that goes beyond affection—the love that carries responsibility, that binds generations together."

"Ayafe," Lily echoed.

"Omoluwabi," Ruth said, her voice growing softer. "A person of good character. Someone who does what is right, even when no one is watching. Someone who carries the weight of their community."

"Omoluwabi," Lily repeated, her hand tightening on Ruth's.

"Ìbàjé," Ruth said, her voice barely a whisper. "To question. To ask why something is the way it is. This is the most dangerous word of all, because once you ask why, you can never go back."

"Ìbàjé," Lily said, the word hanging in the air between them.

"And finally," Ruth said, her voice growing faint, "Ìtàn. Story. The thread that connects us to the past and the future. The thing that survives when everything else is gone."

"Ìtàn," Lily repeated, tears streaming down her face.

Ruth looked at her granddaughter—at the face that was so familiar, so loved—and felt the weight of what she was passing on. These words had been carried by generations of women—her mother, her grandmother, her grandmother's mother. And now they would be carried by Lily.

"The words are not just sounds," Ruth said, her voice weak but urgent. "They are keys. Each one opens a door—to a memory, to a feeling, to a way of thinking that the system has tried to delete. When you carry these words, you carry a piece of the old world. A piece of what it meant to be human."

Lily nodded, her hand warm in Ruth's. "I will carry them. I will remember. I will pass them on."

Ruth felt something settle in her chest—not the weight of the words, but the weight of their release. They were safe now. They would survive.

"There is one more thing," Ruth said, her voice barely audible. "The technique. The way to hide the words in the deep places of your mind. You must teach others. You must pass it on."

"I will," Lily said, her voice thick with tears. "I promise."

Ruth closed her eyes, feeling the fog pressing in. But beneath the fog, the memories were still there—her mother's voice singing in Yoruba, Chukwuemeka's face in the classroom, the children gathered in her room, their faces bright with attention.

She had done what she was meant to do. She had kept her promise. She had passed on the words.

"Grandmother," Lily said, her voice breaking. "I'm scared. I don't want you to go."

Ruth opened her eyes, seeing Lily's face through a haze of tears. "I am not going, child. I am staying. In you. In the words you carry. In the stories you will tell."

She reached up, her hand trembling, and touched Lily's face.

"When you speak the words I taught you, I will be there. When you tell the stories I told you, I will be there. When you teach others, I will be there." She smiled, feeling the peace settle over her. "That is how we survive. That is how we continue. That is how we resist."

Lily nodded, tears streaming down her face. "I understand. I will carry you with me."

Ruth felt the fog pressing closer, stealing the edges of the room, the sound of Lily's voice, the feeling of her hand. But the words remained—clear and strong, hidden in the deep places of her mind where the system couldn't reach.

*"Nitori omo ni n se baba, nitori omo ni n se iya..."*

Because of the child, one becomes a father. Because of the child, one becomes a mother.

She had been a mother. She had been a grandmother. And now she would be a memory—a story carried in the mind of a child who had learned to listen.

"The story continues," Ruth whispered, her voice fading. "Pass it on."

"I will," Lily said, her hand tightening on Ruth's. "I promise. The story will continue."

Ruth smiled, feeling the peace settle over her. The words were safe. The stories would survive. In the minds of children who had learned to listen, the old language would live on.

She closed her eyes, letting the fog take her. But even as the memories faded, the words remained—*Ìmìnira, ayafe, omoluwabi, Ìbàjé, Ìtàn*—five seeds planted in the darkness, waiting for the light.

The story would continue. The words would survive. And Ruth Okonkwo would live on in every child who learned to carry them.
