# Chapter 4: The Story

Lily came back the next day, and the day after that. Each time, she brought questions—questions that started with "why" and "how" and "what does it mean." And each time, Ruth answered with stories. Stories from her childhood, from her mother, from her mother's mother. Stories that had been passed down for generations, surviving colonization, surviving war, surviving everything. And now, somehow, they would survive this too.

On the third day, another child appeared in the doorway—a boy with curious eyes and a hesitant smile. He stood at the threshold, watching, uncertain.

"Can I... can I listen too?" he asked, his voice barely a whisper.

Ruth looked at him, seeing the same hunger she had seen in Lily—the same openness, the same willingness to hear. "Come in," she said, her voice warm. "There is always room for one more."

The boy crossed the room and sat on the floor beside Lily's chair, his eyes bright with attention. "My name is Marcus," he said. "I heard about the stories. From Lily."

"Stories are meant to be shared," Ruth said, feeling the familiar warmth of purpose settle in her chest. "Let me tell you a story."

She closed her eyes for a moment, reaching for the memory. It surfaced slowly, vivid and whole.

"In the old days, in a land far from here, there lived a tortoise. The tortoise was clever—too clever, some said. He could talk his way out of any trouble, trick any animal in the forest."

Ruth opened her eyes, seeing the children's faces bright with attention. She let her voice take on the rhythm of the old storytellers—the pauses, the repetitions, the calls and responses that had been part of oral tradition for centuries.

"One day, the tortoise heard that the sky god had a pot of wisdom. This pot contained all the wisdom in the world, and the tortoise wanted it for himself. So he climbed and climbed, all the way to the sky, and found the pot sitting on a cloud."

"What did he do?" Marcus asked, his voice urgent.

"He tried to put the pot on his back, to carry it down to earth. But the pot was too heavy. He tried to roll it, but it was too large. He tried to push it, but it wouldn't move." Ruth paused, letting the suspense build. "Finally, he realized the truth: wisdom cannot be carried in a pot. Wisdom can only be shared."

The children were silent, waiting.

"So the tortoise opened the pot and let the wisdom flow out. It spread across the sky, across the earth, into every living thing. And from that day forward, wisdom was not something you could keep—it was something you had to give away."

Lily's eyes were bright. "What does it mean?"

"It means that stories, like wisdom, are not meant to be kept. They are meant to be shared. When I tell you this story, it becomes yours. And when you tell it to someone else, it becomes theirs. That is how stories survive—not by being locked away, but by being passed on."

Marcus was quiet for a moment. Then he said, "Will you teach me the words too? The old words?"

Ruth looked at him—at the hunger in his face, the openness in his eyes—and felt the purpose grow stronger. "Yes," she said. "I will teach you."

She began with the same words she had taught Lily—*baba, iya, omo, ayafe*. Marcus repeated them carefully, his mouth shaping sounds that Optima had never taught him.

And as she taught, Ruth felt something shift in the room—a sense of community, of connection, of purpose. She was not just teaching words. She was passing on a legacy.

By the end of the afternoon, two more children had appeared in the doorway, drawn by the sound of Ruth's voice. They sat on the floor, their eyes bright, their minds open.

And Ruth told them stories—stories of Nigeria, of her childhood, of the wisdom that had been passed down through generations. She taught them words—*omi* for water, *eran* for meat, *ewa* for beauty. And with each word, each story, she felt the legacy growing, spreading, taking root in minds that would carry it forward.

That evening, after the children had gone, Ruth sat alone in her room, the silence pressing in on her. But the silence felt different now—not empty, but full of possibility.

She had found her purpose. She had found her students. And she would teach them everything she knew.

The next day, more children came. And the day after that, more still. They filled her small room, sitting on the floor, leaning against the walls, their faces bright with attention.

Ruth looked at them—at their young faces, their open minds, their hunger for something the system couldn't give them—and felt something she hadn't felt in years. She was a teacher again. She had something to give. And these children were worth everything.

She began each session with a story—the tortoise and the wisdom pot, the elephant and the rain, the bird who learned to sing. And then she taught them words—words for family, for love, for the things that connected them.

*"Nitori omo ni n se baba, nitori omo ni n se iya..."*

The children repeated the words, their voices merging into a chorus that filled the small room. And Ruth felt the legacy growing, spreading, taking root.

She knew, somewhere in the back of her mind, that this was dangerous. The system did not approve of the old language. The system did not approve of gatherings that weren't optimized. But she also knew that these children—these bright, curious, hungry children—were worth the risk.

The words would survive. The stories would continue. And somewhere, in the minds of children who had learned to listen, the old language would live on.

That night, Ruth dreamed of her mother—not the confused, fragmented memories that usually surfaced, but a clear, vivid image. Her mother was young, standing in the kitchen, her hands shaping dough into small balls.

*"The stories are not mine," her mother said, her voice clear. "They were given to me by my mother, and her mother before her. And now I give them to you. Carry them forward. Pass them on. That is how we survive."*

Ruth woke with tears on her face, the words echoing in her mind. She would carry them forward. She would pass them on. And the stories would survive.
