# Chapter 3: The Child

The child appeared in the doorway like a question—small, dark-skinned, with eyes that seemed to see more than they should. Ruth looked at her, and something stirred in the fog of her memory. She knew this face. She knew these eyes. But from where? From when?

"Grandmother?" The child's voice was soft, uncertain. "May I come in?"

Grandmother. The word brought a flash of recognition—this was her granddaughter. Lily. The daughter of her daughter. The child who had visited before, though Ruth couldn't remember when.

"Lily," Ruth said, the name surfacing from somewhere deep. "Come in, child."

Lily crossed the room and sat in the chair beside the bed, her movements careful and deliberate. She was young—maybe twelve or thirteen—with a seriousness that seemed too old for her face.

"How are you today, Grandmother?" Lily asked, her voice formal but warm.

"I am..." Ruth searched for the right words. "I am here. I am remembering."

"Remembering what?"

Ruth paused, trying to articulate the fragments that floated in her mind. "My mother. Nigeria. The rain. Words that sound like music."

Lily's eyes sharpened. "Words that sound like music?"

"Yes. Old words. Words from before." Ruth looked at Lily, seeing something in the child's face that she hadn't seen in Adanna's—curiosity, hunger, a willingness to listen. "Do you want to hear them?"

Lily nodded, leaning forward. "Yes. Please."

Ruth closed her eyes, reaching for the words that lived somewhere deep in her memory. They surfaced slowly, like fish rising from dark water.

*"Nitori omo ni n se baba, nitori omo ni n se iya..."*

She spoke the words aloud, her voice raspy but clear. The Yoruba felt strange on her tongue after so long, but also familiar—like coming home to a place she had almost forgotten.

"What does it mean?" Lily asked, her voice barely a whisper.

"Because of the child, one becomes a father. Because of the child, one becomes a mother." Ruth opened her eyes, seeing Lily's face bright with attention. "It's from a song my mother sang to me. Her mother sang it to her. And now..."

She trailed off, the grief rising again. She had sung it to Adanna, once. But Adanna couldn't hear it now—couldn't understand the words, couldn't feel the weight of generations that they carried.

"Will you teach me?" Lily asked, her voice urgent. "Will you teach me the words?"

Ruth looked at her granddaughter—at the hunger in her eyes, the openness in her face—and felt something shift inside her. This child could hear. This child could understand. This child could carry the words forward.

"Yes," Ruth said, her voice steady. "I will teach you."

She began with simple words—words for family, for love, for the things that connected them. *Baba*—father. *Iya*—mother. *Omo*—child. *Ayafe*—beloved.

Lily repeated each word carefully, her mouth shaping sounds that Optima had never taught her. And with each word, Ruth felt something settle in her chest—a sense of purpose, of connection, of hope.

"Where do the words come from?" Lily asked, after they had practiced for a while.

"Nigeria," Ruth said, the name bringing a flood of memories. "A country far from here. A place where my mother was born, and her mother before her."

"Tell me about it."

Ruth closed her eyes, and the memories came—not in fragments now, but in waves, whole and vivid.

*I am seven years old, sitting in the kitchen with my mother. The rain is falling outside, drumming on the tin roof. My mother is making jollof rice, the smell of tomatoes and peppers filling the air. She is telling me a story—a story about a tortoise and a wisdom pot, a story her mother told her.*

*"Why does the tortoise want the wisdom pot?" I ask.*

*"Because he thinks wisdom will make him powerful," my mother says. "But wisdom is not something you can keep in a pot. Wisdom is something you share."*

Ruth opened her eyes, the memory fading but the warmth remaining. "My mother taught me that stories are not just entertainment. They are inheritance. They carry the wisdom of generations."

"What happened to Nigeria?" Lily asked, her voice soft.

Ruth felt the grief rise again. "It's still there, I think. But the language... the stories... they have been optimized away. Deleted from the world." She looked at Lily, her eyes fierce. "But they survive in memory. In the minds of those who remember. And now, in you."

Lily was quiet for a moment. Then she said, "I dream about words. Words that don't exist in Optima. Words that sound like music." She looked at Ruth, her eyes bright. "I think... I think the words you're teaching me are the words from my dreams."

Ruth felt something shift inside her—a recognition, a connection, a sense that this was meant to be. Lily was different from other children. Lily could hear the words that others couldn't. Lily could carry what Ruth had to give.

"Will you come back?" Ruth asked, the words thick in her throat. "Will you come back and learn more?"

Lily nodded, her hand finding Ruth's. "I'll come back. I want to learn. I want to remember."

And in that moment, Ruth felt something she hadn't felt in a long time—purpose. She had something to give. She had something to teach. And someone was willing to learn.

The afternoon passed in a blur of words and stories. Ruth taught Lily the names of things—*omi* for water, *eran* for meat, *ewa* for beauty. She told her the story of the tortoise and the wisdom pot, and the story of the elephant and the rain.

By the time the evening chime sounded, Lily had learned a dozen words, each one hidden in the deep places of her mind where the system couldn't reach.

"I have to go," Lily said, standing reluctantly. "But I'll come back tomorrow."

"Tomorrow," Ruth repeated, the word feeling like a promise.

Lily squeezed her hand one more time, then slipped out the door, disappearing into the white corridor beyond.

After she was gone, Ruth sat alone in her room, the silence pressing in on her. But the silence felt different now—not empty, but full of possibility. She had found someone who could hear. She had found someone who could carry the words.

And she would teach her everything she knew.

That night, Ruth dreamed of Nigeria—of the rain on the tin roof, of her mother's voice singing in Yoruba, of the stories that had been passed down for generations. The dreams were vivid and whole, not the fragmented memories that usually surfaced.

And when she woke, the words were still there, clear and strong, waiting to be passed on.

She would teach Lily. She would teach her everything. And the words would survive.
