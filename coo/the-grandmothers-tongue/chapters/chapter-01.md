# Chapter 1: The Fragments

She woke to white. White walls, white ceiling, white sheets—the color of forgetting. For a moment, she didn't know who she was. The name came slowly, rising from somewhere deep: Ruth. She was Ruth. But Ruth who? Ruth where? The questions swirled, fish in murky water, and she closed her eyes, trying to find something solid to hold onto.

A face surfaced in her memory—a man's face, kind and patient. Chukwuemeka. The name brought a flash of warmth, then grief. He was gone. She knew that much. But when? How long? The answers slipped away before she could catch them.

She opened her eyes again. The room was small and white, a bed, a chair, a window looking out at a gray sky. There were objects on the shelf beside her bed—photographs, a small figurine, a book with a leather cover. She reached for the book, her hand trembling, but her fingers couldn't quite close around it.

"Good morning, Ruth."

The voice came from the doorway—a woman in a white uniform, her face pleasant and efficient. Ruth looked at her, trying to place the face, the name.

"I'm Sarah," the woman said, as if reading her confusion. "Your caregiver. It's time for your morning routine."

Morning routine. The words felt familiar, though Ruth couldn't remember what they meant. Sarah helped her sit up, her hands gentle but impersonal, and began the process of washing, dressing, preparing for the day.

"How are you feeling today?" Sarah asked, her voice pleasant.

"I..." Ruth searched for the words. "I don't know."

"That's alright. You're safe here. The system takes care of everything."

The system. Something stirred in Ruth's memory—a word, a feeling, a resistance she couldn't quite name. The system. What was the system? Why did it make her feel... afraid?

Sarah finished the morning routine and helped Ruth into the chair by the window. "Breakfast will be here soon. Is there anything you need?"

Ruth shook her head, her eyes on the gray sky outside. Sarah left, the door closing softly behind her, and Ruth was alone with the silence.

She looked at the photographs on the shelf. A young woman with a bright smile. A man beside her, his hand on her shoulder. Children at their feet. She knew these people. She was certain of it. But who were they? What were their names?

She reached for the photograph, her fingers closing around the frame. The young woman's face seemed to speak to her, to call out from another time.

*I am seven years old, sitting on my mother's lap. She is singing—words I don't understand yet, words that sound like rain on a tin roof. The kitchen smells of pepper and palm oil, and outside, the rain is falling.*

The memory surfaced without warning, vivid and immediate. Ruth could almost smell the palm oil, almost hear her mother's voice. The words of the song were in a language she hadn't spoken in years—Yoruba, her mother's tongue, the language of her childhood.

*"Nitori omo ni n se baba, nitori omo ni n se iya..."*

Because of the child, one becomes a father. Because of the child, one becomes a mother.

The words were there, clear and strong, rising from somewhere deep in her memory. Ruth clung to them, repeating them silently, afraid they would slip away.

A chime sounded at her temple—the neural interface, monitoring her thoughts, optimizing her cognitive patterns. She felt the gentle pulse of correction, the system smoothing her mind, redirecting her to more efficient thoughts.

But the words remained. Hidden somewhere the system couldn't reach.

Breakfast arrived—nutrient paste, optimized for elderly patients, designed to provide maximum nutrition with minimum digestive effort. Ruth ate without tasting, her mind still holding onto the fragment of memory, the words of her mother's song.

After breakfast, Sarah returned with a tablet. "Time for your memory support session, Ruth. The system will help you organize your thoughts."

Memory support. The words made Ruth's stomach clench. She didn't know why, but something in her resisted the idea of the system organizing her thoughts. Something in her wanted to hold onto the fragments, the confusion, the words that surfaced without warning.

"I don't want it," she said, her voice stronger than she expected.

Sarah's smile didn't waver. "The memory support is for your own comfort, Ruth. It helps reduce confusion, makes your days more pleasant."

"No." Ruth turned her face toward the window. "I don't want it."

Sarah was quiet for a moment. Then she said, "I'll note that you refused today's session. But the system recommends daily support for patients with your condition."

Your condition. Ruth didn't know what her condition was, but she knew she didn't want the system's help. She wanted to remember. Even if remembering was painful. Even if the fragments were sharp and confusing.

After Sarah left, Ruth sat by the window, watching the gray sky. The word was still there, at the edge of her mind—a word that didn't belong in this white world. She reached for it, but it slipped away, a fish in murky water.

*"Nitori omo ni n se baba..."*

The song fragment surfaced again, and Ruth held onto it, repeating it silently, letting the words settle into her bones. Because of the child, one becomes a father. Because of the child, one becomes a mother.

She had been a mother. She was certain of it. The photograph showed children—her children. But where were they now? Why didn't they visit?

The questions circled in her mind, unanswered, unanswerable. But beneath them, something else was stirring—a feeling, a certainty, a word that she couldn't quite reach.

She looked at the photograph again—the young woman with the bright smile, the man beside her, the children at their feet. That woman had a name. That woman had a story. And somewhere, in the fragments of her mind, that story was still alive.

She would find it. She would remember. And when she did, she would tell someone. Because stories only survive when they are told.

The day passed in a blur of white walls and efficient caregivers. Ruth ate her optimized meals, took her optimized medications, sat in her optimized chair by the window. But beneath the surface, the fragments were stirring—memories rising and falling like fish in deep water, words surfacing and disappearing before she could catch them.

That night, as she lay in her white bed, the gray sky darkening outside her window, Ruth closed her eyes and reached for the fragments. The smell of palm oil. The sound of rain on a tin roof. Her mother's voice, singing in Yoruba.

*"Nitori omo ni n se baba, nitori omo ni n se iya..."*

The words were there, hidden somewhere the system couldn't reach. And Ruth knew, with a certainty she couldn't explain, that they were important. That they meant something. That they were worth holding onto.

She fell asleep with the words on her lips, a fragment of a song from a world that no longer existed. And in her dreams, she was young again, sitting on her mother's lap, listening to stories that had been passed down for generations.

The stories were still there. The words were still there. And somewhere, in the fog of her fading memory, Ruth Okonkwo was still the woman who carried them.
