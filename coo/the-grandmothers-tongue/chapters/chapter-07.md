# Chapter 7: The Teaching

The children came every afternoon now, filling her small room with their questions, their attention, their hunger for something the system couldn't give them. Ruth had never felt more alive. Even as her memory fragmented, even as the fog crept closer each day, she had purpose. She had something to give. And these children were worth everything.

"Today," Ruth said, her voice clear despite her age, "I will teach you how to hide the words. How to keep them safe from the system that wants to delete them."

The children leaned forward, their attention absolute.

"The system reads your thoughts through the interface," Ruth explained, her voice low. "But it cannot read what you do not think in the front of your mind. You must learn to hold the words in a different place—in the back, where dreams come from, where memories live. That is where the old words survive."

She guided them through the process—visualizing the word, feeling its weight, then letting it sink into the deeper parts of the mind. It was hard work, requiring concentration and practice. But slowly, one by one, the children began to understand.

"Close your eyes," Ruth said, her voice gentle. "Think of a memory. Not a recent one—a memory from when you were very small. Before the interface was fully integrated."

The children closed their eyes, their faces scrunched in concentration.

"Now hold onto that memory. Feel where it lives in your mind. Is it in the front, where the interface watches? Or is it deeper, in the place where dreams come from?"

Lily spoke first, her voice soft. "It's deep. Far down. Like it's at the bottom of a well."

"Good," Ruth said, feeling the warmth of success. "That is the place where the old words live. That is where you must hide them."

She led them through the exercise again and again—finding the deep place, feeling its shape, learning to recognize it. It was exhausting work, and by the end of the afternoon, the children were tired but triumphant.

"I found it," Marcus said, his eyes bright. "The deep place. I can feel it now."

"Good," Ruth said, smiling. "Now we will practice hiding words there."

She began with simple words—words for family, for love, for the things that connected them. *Baba, iya, omo, ayafe.* The children repeated each word, then visualized it sinking into the deep place, settling there like a stone at the bottom of a well.

"Ìmìnira," Ruth said, watching their faces. "Freedom. Hold it. Feel it. Then let it go."

The children closed their eyes, their faces scrunched in concentration. And one by one, Ruth saw the moment when the word settled—not in the front of their minds, where the interface could read it, but in the back, where the system couldn't reach.

"I did it," Lily said, her eyes flying open. "I can feel it there, but it's hidden."

"Good," Ruth said, feeling the warmth of success. "Now the next word. Ayafe. Beloved."

They practiced for hours, the afternoon slipping away as the children learned to hide the words in the deep places of their minds. By the time the evening chime sounded, each child had hidden at least one word—a word that the system couldn't reach, couldn't delete, couldn't optimize away.

Ruth looked at their faces—at the wonder, the fear, the hope—and felt something settle in her chest. The words were safe. The stories would continue. And even when she was gone, even when her mind finally failed completely, something of her would survive in these children.

"There is one more thing," Ruth said, her voice dropping to a whisper. "The words are not just for hiding. They are for sharing. When you are ready, you must teach others. You must pass on what you have learned."

The children looked at her, their faces serious.

"The words survive when they are shared," Ruth continued. "They die when they are kept. If you hide them and never speak them, they will fade. But if you pass them on—if you teach them to someone else—they will live on."

"Who will we teach?" Marcus asked, his voice uncertain.

"Other children," Ruth said. "Children who dream differently. Children who ask questions. Children who hunger for something the system cannot give." She looked at each of them in turn. "You will know them when you see them. And when you do, you will teach them what I have taught you."

Lily's hand found Ruth's, her small fingers warm and strong. "We will, Grandmother. We promise."

Ruth smiled, feeling the tears on her face. "I know you will. I have faith in you."

That night, Ruth dreamed of her mother—not the confused, fragmented memories that usually surfaced, but a clear, vivid image. Her mother was standing in the kitchen, her hands shaping dough, her voice singing in Yoruba.

*"The words are not yours," her mother said, her voice clear. "They were given to you by those who came before. And now you give them to those who will come after. That is how we survive."*

Ruth woke with the words echoing in her mind, the weight of generations pressing on her shoulders. She would carry them forward. She would pass them on. And the words would survive.

The next day, more children came. And the day after that, more still. The room was full now, the children spilling out into the corridor, their faces bright with attention.

And Ruth taught them everything she knew—words for love, for family, for the things that connected them. Stories of Nigeria, of her childhood, of the wisdom that had been passed down through generations. Techniques for hiding the words in the deep places of their minds, where the system couldn't reach.

The words were spreading. The stories were continuing. And somewhere, in the minds of children who had learned to listen, the old language was growing—seeds of resistance, planted in the darkness, waiting for the light.

Ruth knew, somewhere in the back of her mind, that this was dangerous. The system did not approve of the old language. The system did not approve of gatherings that weren't optimized. But she also knew that these children—these bright, curious, hungry children—were worth the risk.

She would keep teaching. She would keep passing on the words. And when she was gone, the children would carry them forward.

That was how stories survived. That was how language lived on. That was how they would resist the system that wanted to delete everything that made them human.

One word at a time. One child at a time. One story at a time.
