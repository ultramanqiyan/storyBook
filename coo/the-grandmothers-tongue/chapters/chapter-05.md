# Chapter 5: The Gathering

They came at first in twos and threes—children who had heard about the grandmother who told stories, who spoke words that sounded like music. Then they came in larger groups, filling the small room, sitting on the floor, their eyes bright with curiosity. Ruth looked at them—at their faces, so young, so open—and felt something she hadn't felt in years. Purpose. She had something to give. And they were hungry to receive it.

"Today," Ruth said, her voice clear despite her age, "I will teach you the most important word I know."

The children leaned forward, their attention absolute.

"Ìmìnira," Ruth said, the word rolling off her tongue like a wave. "Say it with me. Ì-mì-ni-ra."

The children repeated it, their voices merging into a chorus that filled the small room.

"What does it mean?" Marcus asked, his voice eager.

"Freedom," Ruth said, feeling the weight of the word settle into the room. "The ability to choose. To be the author of your own story. To ask 'why' and expect an answer."

The children were silent, the word hanging in the air between them.

"In the old days," Ruth continued, "this word was spoken freely. People knew what it meant. They knew what it felt like to choose, to question, to be free." She looked at their faces—at the confusion, the curiosity, the hunger. "But now, the word has been deleted. Optimized away. Because the system knows that if you cannot say the word for freedom, you cannot imagine it."

Lily's hand found Ruth's, her small fingers warm and strong. "Will you teach us more?"

Ruth nodded, feeling the purpose grow stronger. "Ayafe," she said. "Beloved. Say it with me."

"Ayafe," the children repeated.

"Omoluwabi," Ruth continued. "A person of good character. Someone who does what is right, even when no one is watching."

"Omoluwabi," the children echoed.

"Ìbàjé," Ruth said, her voice dropping to a whisper. "To question. To ask why something is the way it is. This is the most dangerous word of all, because once you ask why, you can never go back."

The children repeated it, their voices hushed, as if they understood the weight of what they were learning.

Ruth looked around the room—at the faces of the children who had gathered to hear her words. There were twelve of them now, maybe more. They came from different parts of the facility, different backgrounds, different lives. But they all shared the same hunger—the same need for something the system couldn't give.

"The words I teach you are not just sounds," Ruth said, her voice steady. "They are keys. Each word opens a door—to a memory, to a feeling, to a way of thinking that the system has tried to delete. When you carry these words, you carry a piece of the old world. A piece of what it meant to be human."

The children were silent, the weight of her words settling over them.

"But you must be careful," Ruth continued, her voice dropping. "The system does not want you to know these words. It will try to delete them, to optimize them away. You must learn to hide them—in the deep places of your mind, where the interface cannot reach."

"How?" Lily asked, her voice barely a whisper.

Ruth closed her eyes, reaching for the technique her mother had taught her, the technique she had used her whole life to preserve the words that mattered.

"The system reads your thoughts through the interface," she explained, her voice low. "But it cannot read what you do not think in the front of your mind. You must learn to hold the words in a different place—in the back, where dreams come from, where memories live. That is where the old words survive."

She guided them through the process—visualizing the word, feeling its weight, then letting it sink into the deeper parts of the mind. It was hard work, requiring concentration and practice. But slowly, one by one, the children began to understand.

"Ìmìnira," Ruth said, watching their faces. "Hold it. Feel it. Then let it go."

The children closed their eyes, their faces scrunched in concentration. And one by one, Ruth saw the moment when the word settled—not in the front of their minds, where the interface could read it, but in the back, where the system couldn't reach.

"I did it," Marcus said, his eyes flying open. "I can feel it there, but it's... hidden."

"Good," Ruth said, feeling the warmth of success. "Now the next word. Ayafe."

They practiced for hours, the afternoon slipping away as the children learned to hide the words in the deep places of their minds. By the time the evening chime sounded, each child had hidden at least one word—a word that the system couldn't reach, couldn't delete, couldn't optimize away.

Ruth looked at their faces—at the wonder, the fear, the hope—and felt something settle in her chest. The words were safe. The stories would continue. And even when she was gone, even when her mind finally failed completely, something of her would survive in these children.

"Will you come back tomorrow?" Lily asked, her hand still warm in Ruth's.

"Tomorrow," Ruth said, the word feeling like a promise. "And every day after that. As long as I have words to give, I will give them."

The children filed out of the room, their voices soft with excitement and fear. And Ruth sat alone in the silence, the weight of what she had done settling over her.

She knew it was dangerous. She knew the system would notice eventually. But she also knew that these children—these bright, curious, hungry children—were worth the risk.

That night, Ruth dreamed of her grandmother—a woman she had never met, but whose face she knew from old photographs. Her grandmother was standing in a village, surrounded by children, telling stories that had been passed down for generations.

*"The words are not yours," her grandmother said, her voice clear. "They were given to you by those who came before. And now you give them to those who will come after. That is how we survive."*

Ruth woke with the words echoing in her mind, the weight of generations pressing on her shoulders. She would carry them forward. She would pass them on. And the words would survive.

The next day, more children came. And the day after that, more still. The room was full now, the children spilling out into the corridor, their faces bright with attention.

And Ruth taught them everything she knew—words for love, for family, for the things that connected them. Stories of Nigeria, of her childhood, of the wisdom that had been passed down through generations.

The words were spreading. The stories were continuing. And somewhere, in the minds of children who had learned to listen, the old language was growing—seeds of resistance, planted in the darkness, waiting for the light.
