# Chapter 05: The Documents

Marcus spent the next week gathering evidence. He interviewed other participants, reviewed technical documents, consulted with experts in neuroscience and AI ethics. The picture that emerged was damning—Meridian had created a system for harvesting human consciousness, and they'd done it with minimal oversight and even less transparency.

But the most damning evidence came from an unexpected source: a whistleblower inside Meridian.

The email arrived late one night, from an anonymous address. It contained a link to a secure server and a simple message: *The truth about Neural Cluster. They're still doing it.*

Marcus accessed the server and found thousands of documents—internal communications, technical specifications, financial projections, and participant data. It was everything he needed to prove that Meridian was continuing the Neural Cluster project despite the public controversy.

He spent the entire night reading, his horror growing with each page. The documents showed that Meridian had learned from their mistakes. They'd improved the consent process, making it more explicit about what participants were agreeing to. But they'd also expanded the program, recruiting more participants and connecting them to an even larger network.

*They're not stopping,* Marcus realized. *They're scaling up.*

The financial projections were staggering. Meridian estimated that the Neural Cluster would generate over $50 billion in revenue over the next decade, from AI-generated innovations in medicine, energy, and technology. The participants would receive a small fraction of that—just enough to keep them compliant, not enough to compensate them for what they were giving up.

*Their minds,* Marcus thought. *They're selling people's minds.*

He found internal debates about the ethics of the project. Some Meridian scientists had raised concerns, arguing that the long-term effects on participants were unknown and potentially dangerous. But those concerns had been dismissed by Richard Harper, who argued that the benefits to humanity justified the costs to individuals.

*The greater good,* Marcus thought bitterly. *That's always the excuse.*

By dawn, Marcus had downloaded everything he could access. He had enough evidence to blow the story wide open—but he needed to be smart about how he released it. Meridian would fight back, and they had the resources to bury the story if he wasn't careful.

He called Sarah Chen. "I have the documents. We need to meet."

---

They met at a secure location—a private office that Marcus had used for sensitive stories before. Sarah brought Michael Torres, the neurologist who had been helping her monitor the participants. Marcus brought his editor, Janet, who had greenlit the original Meridian investigation.

"This is everything," Marcus said, spreading the documents across the table. "Internal communications, technical specs, financial projections. They're still running the Neural Cluster, and they're expanding it."

Sarah picked up one of the documents—a technical specification for the new BCI headsets. "These are more advanced than the original models. They can create deeper neural connections, with less calibration time."

"Which means they can network more people, faster," Michael said grimly. "And the effects will be more profound."

Janet, Marcus's editor, was scanning the financial projections. "They're projecting $50 billion in revenue. That's more than most countries' GDP."

"From selling human consciousness," Marcus said. "That's what this is. They're commodifying people's minds."

Janet looked up, her expression grim. "This is a big story, Marcus. Maybe the biggest we've ever run. But it's also dangerous. Meridian will come after us with everything they have."

"I know," Marcus said. "But we have to do it. People need to know what's happening."

Sarah nodded. "I've already lost my research funding because of this. Meridian is punishing me for speaking out. But I'm not going to stop."

"Neither am I," Marcus said. "I helped create this technology. I have a responsibility to expose what it's being used for."

Michael looked at Marcus sharply. "You helped create this technology?"

Marcus took a deep breath. He'd been dreading this moment, but he knew it had to come. "I worked at Meridian from 2025 to 2028. I was part of the neural interface team. I helped develop some of the early prototypes for the BCI headsets."

Sarah's eyes widened. "You didn't tell me that."

"I know. I should have. But I was ashamed." Marcus met her gaze steadily. "I left Meridian because I was scared of what we were creating. And I became a journalist to expose the kind of exploitation I'd helped make possible. If you want me to step back from this story, I will."

Sarah was quiet for a long moment. Then she said, "No. You're the best person to cover this. You understand the technology, and you have the motivation to see it through. Just... be transparent about your past. Let the readers decide if it affects your credibility."

Marcus nodded, relieved. "I will. I'll include it in the article."

Janet stood up. "Alright. We have the evidence, we have the witnesses, we have the experts. Let's get this story out. I want it published by the end of the week."

---

Marcus spent the next three days writing. He interviewed more participants, consulted with ethicists and legal experts, and verified every claim with multiple sources. The article was long—over 10,000 words—but it needed to be. The scope of the deception was vast, and the public needed to understand every aspect of it.

He included his own history with Meridian, explaining how he'd helped develop the early technology and why he'd left. It was a risk—some readers might see it as a conflict of interest—but Marcus believed in transparency. He'd rather be honest about his past than hide it and be accused of bias later.

The article was titled: "Meridian Systems Continues Neural Cluster Project Despite Public Outcry: Inside the Harvesting of Human Consciousness."

It was scheduled to publish on Monday morning. Marcus spent Sunday night proofreading, fact-checking, and preparing for the storm that would follow.

He knew what was coming. Meridian would deny everything, attack his credibility, threaten legal action. But he also knew that the truth was on his side. And this time, he wasn't just a journalist covering a story. He was a witness to his own redemption.

*This is what I owe them,* Marcus thought, thinking of Elena and the thousands of other participants whose minds had been harvested. *This is how I make amends.*

The article published at 6 AM. By noon, it had been shared over a million times. By evening, Meridian's stock price had dropped 40%.

The truth was out. And there was no putting it back.
