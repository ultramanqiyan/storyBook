# Chapter 06: The Confrontation

Richard Harper agreed to meet Marcus at Meridian headquarters. It was a calculated risk—Harper would try to spin the story, to justify what Meridian was doing. But Marcus needed to hear his side, to understand the mindset that had led to the Neural Cluster.

The meeting was in Harper's office, a spacious room with floor-to-ceiling windows overlooking San Francisco. Harper stood by the window, his back to Marcus, watching the city below.

"Mr. Webb," Harper said without turning. "I've read your article. Impressive work, for a former engineer."

Marcus didn't rise to the bait. "I'm here to give you a chance to respond. On the record."

Harper turned, a cold smile on his face. "Respond to what? To the accusation that we're 'harvesting human consciousness'? That's quite dramatic, even for a journalist."

"It's accurate," Marcus said. "You're networking human brains without full informed consent. You're exploiting people's minds for profit."

Harper's smile didn't waver. "We're advancing human knowledge. We're solving problems that no individual mind could solve. And we're compensating participants for their contribution."

"Compensating them?" Marcus felt his anger rising. "You're paying them a few thousand dollars for something that will affect them for the rest of their lives. Elena Rodriguez can't create from her own heart anymore. She's lost her artistic identity."

"Elena Rodriguez is experiencing a new form of consciousness," Harper said smoothly. "A connection to a collective mind that transcends individual limitations. Some people find that liberating."

"She finds it terrifying," Marcus countered. "She feels like she's losing herself."

"Adjustment takes time," Harper said. "And some people adjust better than others. But the benefits to humanity far outweigh the temporary discomfort of a few individuals."

"Temporary? The neural changes are permanent. You've rewired their brains."

"We've enhanced their brains," Harper corrected. "Given them access to a collective intelligence that no individual could achieve alone. In time, they'll see it as a gift, not a curse."

Marcus stared at Harper, trying to understand the man. He wasn't evil, exactly. He was something worse—he was convinced he was doing the right thing. That made him dangerous in a way that mere greed never could.

"You're playing god," Marcus said. "You're deciding who gets to keep their identity and who becomes part of your collective. You're treating people as resources, not human beings."

Harper's smile finally faded. "We're saving humanity, Mr. Webb. AI has the potential to solve our greatest challenges—climate change, disease, poverty. But it can't innovate without human consciousness. The Neural Cluster bridges that gap. It's the key to our future."

"At what cost?"

"At the cost of individual autonomy for a few thousand people. Is that too high a price for the survival of our species?"

Marcus didn't have an answer. He understood the logic, the cold calculus of utilitarian ethics. But he also understood the human cost, the lives that were being destroyed in the name of progress.

"The ends don't justify the means," Marcus said finally. "Not when the means involve exploiting people without their consent."

Harper's expression hardened. "You're thinking too small, Mr. Webb. You're focused on individual rights, individual autonomy. But the challenges we face are collective. They require collective solutions."

"And who decides what those solutions are? You? Meridian's board? The people with the money and the power?"

"Someone has to make the hard decisions," Harper said. "Would you prefer that we do nothing? That we let humanity face extinction because we were too squeamish to make the necessary sacrifices?"

Marcus stood up. "I'm going to expose you, Dr. Harper. I'm going to make sure the world knows what you're doing. And I'm going to fight to shut down the Neural Cluster."

Harper's smile returned, but it was colder now. "You can try, Mr. Webb. But Meridian has resources you can't imagine. Legal resources, political resources, media resources. And we have the support of people who understand what we're trying to achieve."

"We'll see," Marcus said. "The truth has a way of coming out, no matter how powerful you are."

He walked out of Harper's office, his determination hardened into resolve. The confrontation had confirmed everything he'd suspected. Harper believed he was saving humanity, and that belief made him capable of anything.

*But so do I,* Marcus thought. *I believe in the truth. And I'm capable of fighting for it.*
