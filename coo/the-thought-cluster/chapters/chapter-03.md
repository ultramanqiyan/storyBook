# Chapter 03: The Past

Marcus drove home from the coffee shop, his mind racing. Sarah Chen's evidence was damning—internal documents, neural scans, participant testimonies. If this story checked out, it would be the biggest tech scandal in history.

But as he pulled into his driveway, a familiar weight settled in his chest. Guilt. Because Marcus hadn't just covered Meridian before. He'd worked there.

He sat in his car, engine off, staring at his house. The memories came flooding back—his first job out of college, the excitement of working at a cutting-edge AI company, the sense of being part of something revolutionary.

*That was before I knew what they were really doing,* Marcus told himself. *Before I understood the cost.*

But was that true? Had he really been that naive? Or had he chosen not to see?

---

Marcus had joined Meridian Systems in 2025, fresh out of Stanford with a degree in computer science and a head full of idealism. The company was small then, just fifty people working out of a converted warehouse in SOMA. But they had big dreams—AI systems that could solve humanity's greatest challenges, from climate change to disease.

His job was to work on the neural interface team, developing algorithms that could interpret brain signals. It was cutting-edge work, the kind of stuff that science fiction writers had dreamed of for decades. Marcus threw himself into it, working late nights, weekends, barely noticing the passage of time.

The team was led by Dr. Richard Harper, a brilliant neuroscientist with a vision that bordered on obsession. Harper believed that AI's greatest limitation was its inability to truly innovate—to have original ideas, not just recombine existing ones. He talked about "the innovation gap" constantly, arguing that true creativity required consciousness, and consciousness was uniquely human.

"AI can generate," Harper would say in team meetings, pacing the room with barely contained energy. "But it cannot create. It can synthesize, but it cannot imagine. That's the gap we need to bridge."

Marcus had been fascinated by the challenge. How could they give AI the ability to innovate? It seemed impossible—consciousness wasn't something you could program. But Harper had ideas, wild ideas that seemed more like science fiction than science.

"What if we could network human minds?" Harper had proposed one evening, late in 2026. "Create a collective consciousness that could provide AI with the innovative capacity it lacks?"

Marcus had laughed at the time. It sounded like something from a dystopian novel. But Harper was serious, and over the next year, he began assembling a team to explore the concept. Marcus was part of that team.

They worked on the early prototypes—BCI headsets that could read neural signals, algorithms that could interpret those signals, systems that could connect multiple brains together. The work was technical, abstract, focused on the engineering challenges rather than the ethical implications.

*We were just solving problems,* Marcus thought now, sitting in his car. *We didn't ask what the solutions would be used for.*

By 2027, Marcus had started to feel uncomfortable. The prototypes were working—test subjects showed increased creative output when their brains were networked. But there were side effects. Some subjects reported hearing voices, feeling thoughts that weren't their own. A few had identity crises, struggling to distinguish their own ideas from the collective's.

Marcus had raised concerns with Harper. "Are we sure this is safe? The subjects seem... affected."

Harper had dismissed the concerns. "They're experiencing a new form of consciousness. Of course there's adjustment. But the potential benefits far outweigh the temporary discomfort."

*Temporary discomfort.* That's what Harper called it. But Marcus had seen the data, and it didn't look temporary. The neural changes were persistent, even after subjects were disconnected from the network.

Marcus had left Meridian in early 2028, taking a job at a different tech company. He told himself it was for better pay, more responsibility. But the truth was, he'd been scared. Scared of what they were creating, scared of what it might do to people.

And then, two years later, he'd become a journalist. He told himself it was a career change, a new direction. But deep down, he knew the truth. He was trying to atone. Trying to expose the kind of exploitation he'd helped create.

*And now here I am,* Marcus thought, *about to expose Meridian again. But this time, I'm part of the story.*

---

He sat in his car for another hour, wrestling with his conscience. Should he tell Sarah about his past? Should he recuse himself from the story? If he stayed involved, was he serving the truth or his own redemption?

In the end, he made a decision. He would tell Sarah everything. She deserved to know. And if she wanted him to step back, he would. But if she wanted him to continue, he would do so with full transparency.

Because that's what journalism was about—not hiding the truth, even when it was uncomfortable. Especially when it was uncomfortable.

Marcus got out of the car and walked into his house. He had work to do.
