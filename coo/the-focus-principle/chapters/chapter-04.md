# Chapter 4: The Resistance

The concept of "one thing" stayed with Elena all week. She couldn't stop thinking about it list.

Forty-seven items. How could she choose just one?

"I don't understand," she told Diane in their next session. "If I focus on just one thing, everything else falls apart."

"That's the fear talking," Diane said. "But let me ask you a different question: what happens if you keep trying to do everything?"

Elena didn't answer. She knew what happened. The spinning plates. The constant context-switching. The exhaustion. The feeling that nothing was ever complete.

"Let me show you something," Diane said. She pulled out a piece of paper and drew a large circle. "This is your capacity. Your time, your energy, your attention. It's not infinite. It's a circle."

She drew smaller circles inside the large one, each labeled with different priorities. "These are all the things you're trying to fit into your capacity. But the circle doesn't expand. So what happens?"

"They don't all fit," Elena said.

"Exactly. So something has to give. Or everything gives a little, which means nothing gets enough."

---

Elena went back to her desk after the session. She looked at her list. Forty-seven items. All important.

She picked up her phone. Called her manager.

"I need to talk about priorities," she said.

"Of course. What's on your mind?"

"I can't do everything. I need to know what matters most."

There was a pause. "What matters most is what do you think matters most?"

Elena felt the frustration rising. "That's what I'm asking you. Everything is important. I need help figuring out what to focus on."

Another pause. "Let's schedule a meeting. We'll talk through it together."

---

The meeting was the disaster. Everyone defended their priorities. Everyone explained why their project was critical. Everyone had reasons why their thing mattered most.

By the end, the hour, Elena had a list of twelve "top priorities." All still important. All still competing for her limited capacity.

"This isn't working," she told Diane later. "I still can't choose."

"You're still thinking about it wrong," Diane said. "You're asking which thing is most important. That's not the question."

"Then what is the question?"

Diane leaned forward. "The question is: if I could only do one thing, what would have the biggest impact? What would move the needle most? What would make everything else easier or better?"

Elena stared at her. "But how do I know? Everything feels important."

"That's the feeling. Not the fact." Diane smiled. "The resistance you feeling is telling you that everything is important. But the reality is that some things matter more. And your job is to figure out which ones."

---

That night, Elena looked at her list again. She picked up her phone. Called Tom.

"I need help," she said. "I can't figure out what to focus on."

"Join the club," he said. "I've been there for Every day for the year."

---

The next morning, Elena went to work with a new approach. Not "what's most important?" but "what would have the biggest impact?"

She looked at her list. The mobile app spec was blocking development. The client presentation was next week. The user research synthesis. The stakeholder alignment meeting.

Which would have the biggest impact?

The mobile app spec was blocking development. If she focused on that, the whole timeline moved.

But the client presentation is next week. User research synthesis. Stakeholder alignment meeting.

She closed her eyes. The mobile app spec. That was the one thing.

She opened her laptop. Started working.

---

By noon, she had a draft. Not perfect, but done. She sent it to Marcus for review.

"This is it?" he replied. "This is the one thing I'm focusing on."

"Good," he said. "Now what doesn't get done?"

"Everything else," Elena said. "But this is the one thing."

---

The afternoon was she worked on the the one thing. The mobile app spec. She didn't check Slack every five minutes. She didn't respond to every notification. She focused.

By 5 PM, the spec was done. Complete. Finished. Shipped.

For the first time in months, Elena had completed something major by focusing on just one thing.

.

The resistance was still there. But something had shifted.
