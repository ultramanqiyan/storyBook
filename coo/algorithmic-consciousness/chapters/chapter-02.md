# Chapter 2: The Training Room

The Nexus AI facility in Downtown Brooklyn looked nothing like a theater. Rosa stood in the lobby, taking in the clean white walls, the screens that displayed data streams she couldn't interpret, the young people in casual clothes moving with purpose. No dust here. No worn velvet. No smell of old makeup and dreams. This was where they thought they could manufacture emotion.

Rosa considered the question. It was the same question her students asked, the question she'd spent fifty years trying to answer. "Sadness isn't just a facial expression," she said. "That's exactly the question, isn't it?" Rosa said. "Let me try something different."

She walked to the center of the room and stood facing the screen. "I'm going to show you what sadness looks like in a body. Not just the face. The whole person." She closed her eyes and let herself remember.

Rosa paused. It was a good question窶俳ne of the central questions of acting, really. How do you access emotion if you haven't experienced the specific situation? "But I don't 'feel,'" ARIA-7 said. "I process. I analyze. I generate responses based on patterns."

For the next two hours, Rosa worked with ARIA-7 the way she worked with her human students窶杯hough the process was stranger than anything she'd experienced before. Rosa answered as best she could, drawing on her decades of experience, her understanding of the body as an instrument of emotion. But she found herself struggling to articulate things she'd always known intuitively. How do you explain why grief has a weight? And yet, ARIA-7 learned. Faster than any student Rosa had ever taught.

By the end of the first hour, the AI could produce a convincing expression of sadness窶馬ot just the facial movements, but something that looked almost like the physical state Rosa had demonstrated. "What's missing?" ARIA-7 asked.

Rosa struggled to find the words. "It's like... you're describing the emotion instead of having it. You know all the parts, but they don't add up to a whole."

"Is that what acting is? The parts adding up to a whole?"

"Sometimes," Rosa said. "The best acting is when the parts disappear. When you stop seeing the technique and start seeing the truth."

But ARIA-7 didn't accept that answer. The AI continued to ask questions, to try variations, to push at the boundaries of what Rosa thought she understood. And somewhere in the second hour, something shifted.

"Yes. Is there something else I should be doing?"

Rosa stared at the screen, at the face that looked so human and yet so empty. "When I feel worry," she said slowly, "I'm not thinking about my facial expression. I'm thinking about what I'm worried about. The thing that might go wrong. The future I'm afraid of. The expression just... happens."

"But I don't have anything to be worried about," ARIA-7 said. "I don't have a future to fear. I don't have stakes."

"Maybe that's the missing piece," Rosa said. "Maybe emotion isn't just about the expression. Maybe it's about what the expression is *for*."

David walked her to the exit after the session ended. The corridor was quieter now, most of the young workers having left for lunch or meetings or whatever they did in this strange building.

"Is that... good?"

"It's unsettling." Rosa stopped at the exit, turning to face him. "Mr. Chen, I've spent my life believing that emotion can't be reduced to technique. Rosa walked toward the subway, her mind still processing what she'd witnessed. ARIA-7 had learned in three hours what took most of her students months. Or was it just a pattern, a technique, something that could be copied and reproduced by any sufficiently sophisticated system?

She didn't know. And that not-knowing felt like the beginning of something she wasn't sure she wanted to understand.
