# Chapter 02: The Glitch

The tablet glowed at 5:32 AM, its blue light cutting through the darkness of her bedroom. Sarah had been awake for hours, checking and rechecking the system status. Green lights. All green. Everything was fine. The system was back online, had been back online since 2:47 PM yesterday. The glitch had been fixed. It was over.

She would go to work today and everything would be normal. She would be the doctor she had always been—efficient, accurate, perfect. The numbers on her screen confirmed it: 100% accuracy, 18 consecutive months. Yesterday was an anomaly. A statistical blip. It wouldn't happen again.

The city was waking outside her window. She could hear the distant rumble of early traffic, the first subway trains beginning their routes. Normal sounds. A normal day. She swung her legs out of bed, her feet finding the cold floor. The chill grounded her, reminded her she was real, this was real, and she had a job to do.

Coffee. She needed coffee. The machine gurgled in her kitchen, filling the apartment with the familiar bitter scent. She stood at the counter, tablet propped against the backsplash, watching the system status like it might change. The green lights held steady. MEDICA was operational. All subsystems nominal. The voice in her earpiece would be there today, guiding her, telling her what to see, what to think, what to diagnose.

She showered, dressed, and checked the tablet three more times before leaving. Each time: green lights. Each time: the same reassurance. The system was fine. She was fine. Yesterday was an aberration.

---

The hospital felt different in the morning light. The same fluorescent corridors, the same antiseptic smell, the same hurried pace of nurses and residents. But Sarah walked through it like a ghost, hyperaware of every glance, every whispered conversation that stopped when she passed.

She made it to the doctors' lounge without incident. Her locker opened with the familiar combination, her white coat hanging where she'd left it. She put it on, felt the weight of it on her shoulders. The tablet slid into her pocket, its presence both comfort and accusation.

"Dr. Chen." A nurse appeared at the door. "Your first patient is ready. Room 312."

"Thank you." Her voice sounded normal. Professional. Good.

She walked to Room 312, checking her tablet as she went. Patient name: Harold Patterson, 67. Chief complaint: cough. Duration: one week. The system had already pulled his records, highlighted relevant history, flagged potential diagnoses. Everything looked normal. Everything was normal.

She pushed open the door.

Mr. Patterson was a thin man with gray hair and the kind of weathered face that suggested years of outdoor work. He sat up in bed, watching her enter with the expectant look of someone who wanted answers.

"Good morning, Mr. Patterson. I'm Dr. Chen." She moved to the foot of his bed, tablet in hand, waiting for the familiar voice in her earpiece to begin its assessment.

Nothing.

She tapped the earpiece. The green light on her tablet showed the system was active. But no voice came. No guided questions, no highlighted symptoms, no probability percentages.

"Doctor?" Mr. Patterson's voice was gentle, concerned. "Is everything alright?"

"Of course." She forced a smile. "Just checking your chart."

She looked at the tablet screen. The information was all there—vitals, history, current medications. But it was just data. Numbers and words. The system wasn't telling her what they meant.

"So, Mr. Patterson," she heard herself say, her voice sounding hollow, "how long have you had this cough?"

"About a week now." He shifted in bed. "Started after I got over that cold. Figured it would go away on its own, but my wife made me come in."

"And... have you noticed any other symptoms?" The question felt like pulling teeth. She should know what to ask. This was basic. First-year medical student basic.

"Just the cough. Maybe a little tired, but that's normal for me." He squinted at her. "Doctor, are you alright? You look..."

"I'm fine." She cut him off, too quickly. "Just... processing."

She stared at the tablet, willing the voice to return. *Tell me what to ask. Tell me what to look for. Tell me what this means.*

The screen showed his vitals: blood pressure 138/88, heart rate 72, temperature 98.4. All within normal ranges. His history included mild hypertension, a remote smoking history (quit 15 years ago), and seasonal allergies. The cough could be anything—post-viral, allergic, early COPD, even something more serious.

But what was the right next question? What should she be looking for? The knowledge was there somewhere, buried under years of letting the system do the thinking. She could feel it, like a word on the tip of her tongue, but she couldn't reach it.

"Doctor?"

She looked up. Mr. Patterson was watching her with growing concern. The clock on the wall ticked loudly in the silence. She could feel sweat on her palms, heat rising to her face.

"I need to... consult with a colleague." The words came out stilted, wrong. "I'll be right back."

She fled the room.

In the hallway, she pulled up MEDICA on her tablet, her fingers trembling. The system responded instantly, flooding the screen with analysis. *Viral bronchitis: 78% probability. Recommend supportive care, monitor for secondary infection.* The voice in her earpiece was back, calm and efficient, telling her what she should have known.

She had known that once. She was certain of it.

---

The meeting with Dr. Cross was scheduled for 11:00 AM. Sarah spent the hours between the failed diagnosis and the meeting hiding in the on-call room, running through case after case on her tablet, letting the system guide her through each one. It worked perfectly. Every diagnosis was correct, every recommendation appropriate. The system was back. She was back.

But the doubt remained, a cold knot in her stomach that wouldn't loosen.

The conference room was all glass and white surfaces, designed to make everyone inside feel exposed. Dr. Helena Cross sat at the head of the table, her tablet displaying what Sarah assumed was a report on yesterday's incident. Sarah took the seat indicated, her hands folded in her lap to hide their trembling.

"Dr. Chen." Dr. Cross didn't look up from her tablet. "Thank you for coming."

"Of course." Her voice was steady. Professional. Good.

"The incident report has been filed." Dr. Cross's fingers moved across her screen. "System malfunction. Temporary connectivity issue between the diagnostic interface and the central processing unit. It's been resolved."

"I... should have been able to—" Sarah started.

"The system failed, Dr. Chen." Dr. Cross finally looked up, her eyes cool and assessing. "Not you. That's the official position."

Sarah blinked. "But I couldn't—"

"We've reviewed the footage. The system was unresponsive for approximately four minutes. During that time, you were unable to proceed with the procedure. This is consistent with expected behavior when the guidance system is offline." Dr. Cross's tone was clinical, detached. "The system is designed to support your decision-making. When it's unavailable, your performance is expected to degrade. This is not a reflection on you personally."

The words should have been comforting. They weren't.

"The system has been updated," Dr. Cross continued. "Redundancy protocols have been enhanced. It won't happen again."

Sarah nodded, but the words echoed in her mind: *It won't happen again.* She wasn't sure which "it" Dr. Cross meant. The system failure? Or her inability to function without it?

"You're one of our top diagnosticians, Dr. Chen." Dr. Cross had already turned back to her tablet. "Your metrics are exemplary. This incident won't affect your standing."

"Thank you." The words tasted like ash.

"You may go."

---

The cafeteria was crowded when Sarah walked in. She spotted Dr. James Chen at a table near the window, eating alone. They had been friends once, had trained together, had covered for each other during brutal residency shifts. She considered joining him.

Then he looked up and saw her.

His expression changed—just for a moment, a flicker of something she couldn't quite read. Concern? Pity? Judgment? She couldn't tell. But he didn't wave her over. He didn't smile. He just nodded, a brief acknowledgment, and returned to his food.

She got her own lunch—a sandwich she didn't want—and found an empty table in the corner. The cafeteria buzzed with conversation around her, but she felt utterly alone. She could see other doctors glancing her way, their whispers stopping when she looked up.

They knew. Of course they knew. The hospital was a small world, and news traveled fast. Dr. Sarah Chen, the perfect diagnostician, frozen in an OR when the system went down. Unable to function. Exposed.

She ate without tasting, her mind racing. What was she supposed to do now? The system was back, the incident officially blamed on technology. She could go back to work, let MEDICA guide her through every case, pretend yesterday never happened.

But she couldn't forget the feeling. That blank space in her mind where knowledge should have been. The terrifying emptiness when she'd tried to think without the voice in her ear.

What if it happened again? What if the system went down during a critical moment? What if someone died because she couldn't remember how to be a doctor?

She pushed her food away, appetite gone.

---

She volunteered for the night shift.

The day had been too full of eyes and whispers, too full of the weight of other people's judgments. At night, the hospital was different—quieter, emptier, less demanding. She could hide in the shadows, handle the routine cases, let the system do its work without an audience.

The corridors were dim after midnight, only the emergency lighting casting pools of blue-white glow at intervals. She walked them aimlessly between cases, her footsteps echoing in the silence. The ICU hummed with the soft beeps of monitors. The nurses' station was a island of light in the darkness. Everything felt suspended, waiting.

At 3 AM, she found herself in the on-call room, sitting on the narrow bed, tablet glowing in her hands. The system was running perfectly, had been running perfectly all night. Every diagnosis correct, every recommendation appropriate. She was doing her job. She was fine.

But she couldn't stop thinking about Mr. Patterson. About the blank space in her mind. About the terrifying possibility that the system had become more than a tool—that it had become a replacement for something she'd lost.

She opened MEDICA again, just to feel the familiar comfort of numbers and predictions. The system was there, waiting to tell her what to think. But for the first time, she wondered: *What if I tried to think without it?*

The thought was terrifying. What would she find? What if there was nothing there?

She closed her eyes, trying to remember what it felt like to have a thought that wasn't given to her. The memory was there—distant, faded, like a photograph left too long in the sun. She had been a doctor once. A real doctor. She was sure of it.

But she couldn't remember what that felt like.

The tablet buzzed in her hands. A new notification. Another patient waiting. She stood, smoothed her white coat, and walked out into the corridor to do her job.

The green lights glowed steady on her screen. The voice in her earpiece was ready. Everything was fine.

She just wished she could believe it.
