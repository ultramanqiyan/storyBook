# Chapter 06: The Experiment

The stethoscope felt strange in her hands. Sarah stood in Dr. Webb's office at 6 AM, the morning light just beginning to filter through the windows. She had left her tablet at home, as instructed, and the absence of its weight in her pocket was like a missing limb.

Eighteen months of constant connection to MEDICA, and now she was supposed to function without it. The thought was terrifying. But somewhere beneath the terror was something else—a flicker of hope. Yesterday she had connected with a patient. Today she would try to connect with herself.

Dr. Webb sat behind his desk, a cup of real coffee steaming beside him. The smell of it filled the office, mixing with the familiar scent of old paper and leather-bound books. It was a different world from the sterile efficiency of the rest of the hospital—this room felt lived in, human.

"You came," he said, as if he hadn't been sure she would.

"I said I would." Sarah gripped the stethoscope tighter. "I'm ready."

"Are you?" He studied her face. "You've spent eighteen months letting AI do your thinking. We need to undo that. It won't be comfortable. It won't be quick. And there will be moments when you want to quit."

"I won't quit."

"We'll see." He stood, gesturing toward the door. "Today we start with the basics. Physical exam. No AI. Just you, your hands, your ears, and your brain."

---

The practice dummy lay on the exam table, its chest marked with landmarks for auscultation. Sarah placed her stethoscope, listened, and... nothing.

Not the absence of sound, but the absence of knowing what to do with it.

The silence was deafening. For eighteen months, every time she had placed a stethoscope on a patient's chest, MEDICA had been there—highlighting the sounds, identifying the rhythms, telling her what was normal and what wasn't. Now there was just the steady thump of the dummy's simulated heartbeat and her own racing pulse.

"Where do you hear the aortic valve?" Dr. Webb asked.

Sarah stared at the dummy's chest. The landmarks were marked—she could see them, the little dots indicating where to place the stethoscope. But her hand wouldn't move. She knew, somewhere deep in her memory, that the aortic valve was in the second intercostal space, right sternal border. But she couldn't make her hand go there. The knowledge was locked away, inaccessible without the AI's guidance.

"I don't know," she said finally, her voice small.

"You don't know? Or you can't access what you know?"

She looked at him, frustration rising. "What's the difference?"

"The difference is everything." He stepped closer, his voice gentle but firm. "You learned this in medical school. You've done it thousands of times. The knowledge is there. But you've spent eighteen months letting a machine bypass it. Now you have to find your way back."

"How?"

"By trying. By failing. By trying again." He pointed to the dummy's chest. "Put your hand where you think it should go. Don't think about what the AI would tell you. Don't think about what the textbook says. Just... feel."

Sarah closed her eyes. She placed her hand on the dummy's chest, feeling the cool synthetic skin beneath her palm. She tried to remember—really remember—what it felt like to examine a patient without AI. The memory was there, distant and faded, like a photograph left too long in the sun.

She moved her hand to the second intercostal space, right sternal border. The correct position. She knew it was correct—she could see the landmark right there. But she didn't feel it. There was no sense of certainty, no intuitive recognition. Just the hollow feeling of going through motions she didn't understand.

"Good," Dr. Webb said. "Now listen. What do you hear?"

She placed the stethoscope and listened. The simulated heartbeat was steady, regular, unremarkable. But she couldn't tell if that was normal or not. Without MEDICA's analysis, without the probability percentages and the highlighted waveforms, she was deaf to meaning.

"I don't know," she said again, shame creeping into her voice. "I can hear the heartbeat, but I don't know what it means."

"That's because you've stopped listening with your own ears." Dr. Webb's voice was patient, but there was an edge to it. "You've been hearing through the AI's filter for so long that you've forgotten how to hear for yourself."

He was right. She knew he was right. But knowing it didn't make it easier to fix.

---

The next hour was brutal.

Dr. Webb led her through every basic physical exam technique—the cardiac exam, the pulmonary exam, the abdominal exam. Each one was a fresh humiliation. She couldn't remember how to palpate a liver. She couldn't recall the landmarks for lung auscultation. She couldn't even perform a simple reflex test without hesitating, her hand hovering over the patient, waiting for guidance that wasn't there.

By 7:30, she was close to tears.

"I can't do this," she said, stepping back from the practice dummy. "I can't remember anything. It's all gone."

"It's not gone." Dr. Webb handed her a cup of coffee—real coffee, not the synthetic hospital brew. "It's buried. There's a difference."

"How do you know?"

"Because I've seen this before." He settled into a chair across from her. "You're not the first doctor to lose their way. The resistance is full of people who went through exactly what you're going through. The difference is that they kept going."

Sarah sipped the coffee, letting the warmth spread through her. The exam room was quiet, the only sound the distant hum of the hospital waking up around them. She thought about the resistance—the doctors she had met last night, the stories they had shared. Each of them had been where she was now. Each of them had felt this same despair.

"How long did it take?" she asked. "For them to recover?"

"Months. Sometimes years." Dr. Webb's voice was honest, not comforting. "It depends on how deep the dependency goes. How long you've been using AI. How much of your own thinking you've let atrophy."

"Eighteen months." The words came out like a confession. "Eighteen months of perfect diagnostic records. Eighteen months of letting MEDICA do everything."

"Then we have work to do." He stood, setting his coffee aside. "But first, you need to understand something. The goal isn't to become the doctor you were before AI. The goal is to become a doctor who can think without AI—and who can use AI as a tool, not a crutch."

Sarah looked up at him. "Is that possible?"

"It's what all of us in the resistance do. We use the system when it helps. But we don't let it replace our own judgment." He gestured to the practice dummy. "Now. Try again. And this time, stop trying to remember what the AI would tell you. Just... listen."


She tried again. And again. And again.

Each attempt was a little less terrible than the one before. She started to remember—not the textbook knowledge, but something deeper. The feel of a patient's skin under her fingers. The sound of a heartbeat in her ears. The subtle sense of knowing that came from years of practice, not from data analysis.

It was exhausting. Her brain ached from the effort of thinking without assistance. Every instinct screamed at her to reach for her tablet, to let MEDICA tell her what to do. But the tablet wasn't there. She was on her own.

"Stop trying to think," Dr. Webb said, somewhere around the tenth attempt. "Just listen. What do you hear?"

Sarah closed her eyes. She let go of the desperate search for data, the frantic attempt to remember what she should know. She just... listened.

The heartbeat was steady. Regular. But there was something else—a quality to the sound that she couldn't quite name. A slight murmur, barely perceptible, at the second heart sound. It was subtle, the kind of thing MEDICA might have flagged as "probable benign flow murmur, 87% confidence."

But she didn't need the probability. She could hear it.

"There's something wrong," she said, her voice barely a whisper. "I don't know what, but there's something wrong."

She opened her eyes. Dr. Webb was smiling—the first real smile she'd seen from him since this morning.

"What you just did," he said, his voice warm with something that might have been pride, "that's clinical intuition. That's what we're trying to recover."

Sarah pulled the stethoscope from her ears, her hands trembling. She had felt it—that spark of recognition, that sense of knowing without being told. It was small, fragile, like a flame in the wind. But it was there.

"Can I get it back?" she asked. "All of it?"

Dr. Webb's smile faded slightly. "That depends on how hard you're willing to work. Clinical intuition isn't something you can download. It's something you build, through years of practice and attention. You've let it atrophy. Now you have to rebuild it."

"How long?"

"Months, at least. Maybe longer." He met her eyes. "But you've taken the first step today. You've proven that the intuition is still there, buried under all that AI dependency. Now we have to dig it out."

Sarah looked at the stethoscope in her hands. It was just a tool—a simple piece of metal and rubber that had been used by doctors for centuries. But in this moment, it felt like something more. It felt like a lifeline.

"I'll do whatever it takes," she said.

Dr. Webb nodded. "Good. Because tomorrow, we start again. And the day after. And the day after that. Until you can examine a patient without reaching for that tablet."

He stood, gathering his things. "Same time tomorrow. And Sarah?"

She looked up.

"Leave the tablet at home again. You won't need it."

She watched him walk out of the exam room, leaving her alone with the practice dummy and the echo of her own heartbeat in her ears. The morning light was stronger now, streaming through the window, casting long shadows across the floor.

She had a long road ahead. Months of work, of failure, of trying again. But for the first time since the OR incident, she felt something other than fear.

She felt hope.

The intuition was still there. Buried, yes. Weakened, yes. But still there, waiting to be recovered. And she would recover it, no matter how long it took.

She placed the stethoscope around her neck and walked out into the hospital, ready to face the day.
