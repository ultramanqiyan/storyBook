# Chapter 7: The Bigger Risk

The garden marketing plan was coming together. Sarah had drafted social media posts, sketched out a newsletter template, and even brainstormed event ideas—a harvest festival, a plant swap, a workshop series. She looked at the document on her screen and felt something she hadn't felt in a long time: pride. Not performative pride, not the kind that came from projecting confidence she didn't feel, but the quiet kind that came from actually doing something.

She saved the document and closed her laptop. Tomorrow was another work day. But tonight, she'd done something that mattered.

---

"Sarah, I want to talk to you about something."

Her manager's face filled the screen, her expression serious but not unkind. The Thursday afternoon video call had started normally—project updates, timeline questions—and then her manager's tone had shifted.

Sarah's stomach dropped. She knew that tone. She'd heard it before, in other contexts, other disappointments.

"We have a new project launching next month. High visibility. Cross-functional team. I think you'd be good for it."

Sarah blinked. "Me?"

"Yes, you." Her manager smiled slightly. "You've been here three years. You know the work. And frankly, I think you need something to sink your teeth into. Something that matters."

The words landed like a weight in Sarah's chest. A high-visibility project. Cross-functional. Something that mattered.

"I... that's a big opportunity."

"It is." Her manager leaned forward slightly. "And it comes with risk. You'd be presenting to leadership. You'd be visible. If it goes well, it opens doors. If it doesn't..." She spread her hands. "Well. It's a risk."

Sarah's mind was already spinning. Visible. Leadership. Risk. The words she'd been avoiding for months, the words that described everything she feared.

"Take the weekend to think about it," her manager said. "Let me know Monday."

Sarah nodded, but her mind was already spinning.

---

The spiral started immediately. By Thursday night, Sarah had opened twenty-three tabs about the project type, the client, the risks, the potential for failure. By Friday morning, she was ready to say no.

The voice was back, louder than it had been in weeks. *You can't do this. It's too big. You'll fail. Everyone will see. Stay safe. Stay small.*

She almost listened. She drafted the email three times: "Thank you for the opportunity, but I don't think I'm the right fit." Each version sounded reasonable. Professional. Safe.

But then she looked at the document on her desktop—the garden marketing plan, the thing she'd created, the thing that had made her feel capable. And she thought about Elena's words: "Resistance is normal. The voice gets louder right before it loses."

The voice was screaming. Did that mean she was close to something?

By Friday afternoon, she was paralyzed. She couldn't say yes. She couldn't say no. She was stuck in the familiar loop, the research spiral, the overthinking trap. She needed to hear a voice that wasn't her own.

She picked up her phone. David would be home by now.

"Hey, sis. What's up?"

David's voice was warm, familiar. Sarah felt the tears before she could stop them.

"Hey." She paused, trying to collect herself. "Can I talk to you about something?"

"Of course. What's going on?"

So she told him. About the garden, the marketing plan, the growing confidence. About the work opportunity, the visibility, the fear. About the spiral, the voice, the almost-decision to say no.

"I don't know if I can do this," she said finally. "It's too big. What if I fail?"

"What if you do?"

Sarah stopped. "What?"

"What if you fail? What happens then?"

The question was so simple, so obvious, that she hadn't even considered it. What if she failed?

"Everyone would know," she said slowly. "I'd look stupid. My manager would regret trusting me."

"And then what?"

Sarah thought about it. "I don't know. Life goes on, I guess. I'd still have my job. I'd just... not get another opportunity like this for a while."

"Right. Life goes on." David's voice was gentle but firm. "You've been playing it so safe, Sarah. Safe isn't working. You're miserable. You're stuck. And now something comes along that might change that, and you want to run away."

"I'm not running away. I'm being realistic."

"Are you? Or are you scared?"

The question hung in the air. Of course she was scared. She was terrified.

"Both," she admitted.

"That's honest." David paused. "You know what I tell my students? You don't have to be ready. You just have to be willing."

"What do you mean?"

"Everyone waits to feel ready before they do something. But ready is a myth. You do the thing, and then you become ready for it. You don't have to be ready. You just have to be willing to try."

Sarah closed her eyes. The words felt like a key turning in a lock.

"You don't have to be ready," she repeated. "You just have to be willing."

"Exactly. Look, I'm not saying it won't be scary. It will be. I'm not saying you won't fail. You might. But you've been so afraid of failing that you haven't been living. And that's its own kind of failure."

They talked for another twenty minutes. David shared his own story—taking the teaching job after years in corporate, the fear, the uncertainty, the eventual joy. He didn't tell her what to do. He just reminded her that she had a choice.

"Thanks, David," she said at the end. "I needed this."

"Anytime, sis. That's what brothers are for."

---

Sunday night. The email was drafted, the cursor blinking. All she had to do was hit send.

*Dear [Manager],*

*Thank you for the opportunity. I would like to accept the project.*

*Best,*
*Sarah*

Three sentences. Three sentences that would commit her to something big and scary and uncertain. Three sentences that would make her visible.

The voice was still there—"what are you doing? you're going to fail"—but it was quieter now. Or maybe she was just better at hearing past it.

She looked at the notes she'd written: Elena's wisdom, David's words. Two keys now. Two reminders that action comes before certainty, that willing is more important than ready.

She hit send before she could overthink it.

The email disappeared into the digital void, and for a moment she couldn't breathe. Then she could. She'd done it. She'd said yes to something big and scary and uncertain. Now she had to live with it.

The voice in her head was already starting up—"what did you just do?"—but she wrote down David's words next to Elena's: "You don't have to be ready. You just have to be willing."

Two keys now. Two reminders that action comes before certainty.

She closed her laptop and sat in the quiet of her apartment. Tomorrow was Monday. Tomorrow she'd find out what came next. But tonight, she'd done something brave. And that was enough.
