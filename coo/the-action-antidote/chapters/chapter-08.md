# Chapter 8: The Fall Forward

Monday morning. The project kickoff meeting filled her screen with faces she barely knew—team members from different departments, the client liaison, her manager. Sarah took a breath and clicked "join with video." Her face appeared in the grid, and for a moment she saw herself as others might: professional, present, trying.

The nervous energy that had kept her up Sunday night translated into something useful now—attentiveness, engagement, the appearance of competence. She took notes, asked questions, contributed where she could. By the time the meeting ended, she had her assignments and a strange feeling in her chest. Not anxiety, exactly. Something closer to... purpose.

She was doing something. Something visible. Something that mattered.

---

The work felt good. For three days, Sarah dove into the project, her skills clicking into place like puzzle pieces she'd forgotten she had. The research spiral that had plagued her for months transformed into something useful—she could analyze data, spot patterns, synthesize information. She'd just been directing those skills at the wrong target: herself.

Now she had a target that wasn't her own anxiety. A client presentation. A strategy document. A timeline that needed managing. The keyboard clicked steadily under her fingers, and words appeared on screen like evidence of her capability.

She was contributing. She was visible. She was doing something.

Wednesday afternoon, she sent her first major deliverable. The response was positive: "Great work, Sarah. This is exactly what we needed." She read the Slack message three times, letting the words sink in. Maybe she could do this. Maybe she wasn't as incompetent as she'd thought.

The voice in her head was quiet. Or maybe she was just too busy to hear it.

---

Thursday afternoon, her manager's face appeared on screen. The expression was kind but serious.

"Sarah, I need to talk to you about the project."

Her stomach dropped. She knew that tone. She'd heard it before, in other contexts, other disappointments.

"What is it?"

"The client decided to go in a different direction. Your work was good—it really was—but they've shifted their priorities. They want to focus on a different aspect of the campaign."

Sarah stared at the screen. "Different direction?"

"They're going to pause the work you were doing. It's not a reflection on you—these things happen all the time. Clients change their minds. Budgets shift. It's normal."

"But..." She stopped. What could she say? *But I tried so hard? But I was finally doing something? But this was supposed to work?*

"I understand," she said instead. She didn't, not really. But she understood enough.

The call ended. Sarah sat staring at her blank screen. She'd tried so hard. And it hadn't been enough.

---

The apartment felt smaller that night. Sarah sat on her couch, the TV off, the silence heavy. The voice was back, louder than it had been in weeks.

*See?* it said. *This is what happens when you try. You fail. You should have stayed safe.*

She almost listened. She almost opened her laptop and drafted the email to her manager—*I'm sorry I couldn't deliver. I understand if you don't want to give me another opportunity.* She almost retreated to the familiar comfort of self-criticism.

But then she thought about the garden. About Elena's words. About David's wisdom. About the three keys she'd collected: *Resistance is normal. You don't have to be ready. The voice gets louder right before it loses.*

The voice was screaming. Did that mean she was close to something?

Tomorrow was Saturday. Garden day. She'd go. She'd talk to Elena. Maybe there was something to learn here.

---

Saturday morning, Sarah went to the garden. She didn't want to talk about what happened, but Elena saw it in her face.

"Walk with me," Elena said.

They walked between the raised beds, past the tomato plants that were finally ripening, past the herb garden that Sarah had helped weed weeks ago. The smell of soil and green things filled the air, grounding her in a way her apartment never could.

"Tell me what happened," Elena said finally.

So Sarah told her. About the project, the work, the hope. About the client's decision, the disappointment, the voice that said she should have stayed safe.

"I tried," Sarah said, her voice cracking. "I really tried. And it didn't matter."

Elena stopped walking. She turned to face Sarah, her weathered face kind but firm.

"It mattered," Elena said. "You tried. That's the point."

"But I failed."

"You fell forward." Elena gestured at the garden around them. "That's the only way to move."

"What do you mean?"

Elena knelt beside a bed of kale, pulling a weed from the soil. "Every plant in this garden has failed at some point. Died back. Struggled. Been eaten by bugs. Been overwatered or underwatered. But they're still here. Still growing." She looked up at Sarah. "Failure isn't the opposite of success. It's part of it. You fell forward. That's movement."

Sarah knelt beside her, hands finding the familiar rhythm of weeding. The dirt was cool under her fingers, real and solid in a way that nothing in her work life seemed to be.

"I thought if I tried, it would work out," Sarah said.

"That's the myth," Elena said. "We think action guarantees success. It doesn't. Action just guarantees movement. Sometimes you move forward. Sometimes you fall. But falling forward is still movement."

They worked in silence for a while. The sun warmed Sarah's back, and the smell of soil filled her lungs. The disappointment was still there, but it felt smaller now. More manageable.

"The project didn't work out," Elena said eventually. "But you did something. You took a risk. You were visible. That's not nothing."

"It feels like nothing."

"It feels like failure. But failure is just information. It tells you what didn't work. It doesn't tell you what will work next time." Elena smiled. "You know what I did before I came to this garden? I was a corporate HR director. I failed at that too. Burned out completely. Took me two years to find my way here."

Sarah looked at Elena with new eyes. She'd assumed the older woman had always been this way—calm, wise, grounded. But she'd had her own failures. Her own falls forward.

"How did you keep going?" Sarah asked.

"One day at a time. One small action at a time. I showed up here. Just like you did. I put my hands in dirt. I talked to people. I learned that failure isn't the end." Elena stood, brushing off her knees. "You fell forward, Sarah. That's the only way to move. The question is: what's your next step?"

---

She went home and added Elena's words to her collection: "You fell forward. That's the only way to move."

Three keys now. Three reminders that action isn't about perfection—it's about movement.

The project hadn't worked out the way she'd hoped. But she had tried. And she was still trying. That was something. That was enough.

She looked at her garden calendar—tomorrow was Sunday, but next Saturday she'd be back. The plants didn't care if she'd failed at work. They just kept growing. And so would she.
