# Chapter 6: The Ripple Effect

Saturday arrived with less fanfare than the first time. Sarah woke up, made coffee, and got dressed without the hour of hesitation that had plagued her before. The voice was still there—"what if they don't want you back?"—but quieter now, more like background noise than a shout.

She looked at the card on her desk, the one with Elena's number, and the words she'd written beneath it: "Resistance is normal. The voice gets louder right before it loses." She'd read those words every day this week. They were starting to feel true.

The morning light through her window was bright, almost aggressive in its optimism. She grabbed her keys and headed out. The garden was waiting. So was she.

---

The twelve blocks felt shorter this time. Sarah noticed things she hadn't before—the bakery on the corner with its croissants in the window, the family walking their golden retriever, the way the light filtered through the oak trees lining the street. She was present. Not lost in her head, rehearsing escape routes, but actually here, in her body, on a Saturday morning.

She walked past the coffee shop where she'd met Marcus. Through the window, she could see him at his usual spot, laptop open. He looked up as she passed and raised a hand in greeting. She raised hers back. A small thing. A wave. But it meant something.

The garden gate appeared ahead. She pushed through without stopping. She was getting better at this.

---

"Sarah! You came back!"

Elena's voice carried across the garden, and Sarah felt something loosen in her chest. They remembered her. She mattered enough to be remembered.

"I said I would," she said, walking toward Elena.

"I know." Elena smiled, her weathered face creasing in all the right places. "But saying and doing are different things. You did it."

Sarah felt something shift inside her. "Yeah. I did."

Other volunteers greeted her—names she actually remembered this time. Tom, who ran the compost operation. Priya, who managed the herb garden. A young man whose name she'd forgotten but who nodded at her like they'd met before. They remembered her. She was becoming part of something.

She found her spot from last week—the tomato bed she'd weeded—and knelt in the dirt. This was becoming familiar. This was becoming hers.

---

The work flowed easier this time. Sarah weeded, planted, watered—her hands knowing what to do before her mind could overthink it. She talked with Elena about nothing and everything: the weather, the tomatoes, the strange satisfaction of pulling a weed out by its roots.

"You're getting good at this," Elena said, watching Sarah work.

"It's easier when you stop thinking about it."

"Isn't everything?"

They worked in companionable silence for a while. The sun warmed Sarah's back, and the smell of soil filled her lungs. She was sweaty, dirty, and tired. She was also happy. When was the last time she'd felt that?

---

"Hey, Sarah."

Marcus appeared beside her as she was cleaning up her tools. He had a thoughtful look on his face, the kind that preceded a question.

"Can I ask you something? Elena mentioned you're in marketing."

Sarah tensed slightly. Work. The word felt foreign here, in this space that had nothing to do with Slack or spreadsheets or career anxiety.

"Yes," she said carefully. "I'm a marketing strategist. Remote."

"That's what I thought." He leaned against the shed, arms crossed. "We've been talking about getting the word out about the garden. We have this amazing space, but most people in the neighborhood don't even know we exist. We need someone who understands... all that."

Sarah stared at him. "You want marketing help?"

"Desperately. We have a website from 2019 that no one updates. A Facebook page with twelve followers. We're invisible." He spread his hands. "Would you be interested in helping?"

"Helping how?"

"Whatever you think. Social media, maybe a newsletter, events. We have no idea what we're doing on that front. But you—" He gestured at her. "You know what you're doing. And you're here anyway."

Sarah's mind raced. Marketing. Here. For a community garden. Not for a corporation, not for a product she didn't care about, but for this place, these people, this dirt she'd been kneeling in for two weeks.

"You want me to... do marketing? Here?"

"Why not? You know what you're doing. And you're here anyway."

The words echoed in her head. *You know what you're doing.* Did she? She'd spent months feeling like she didn't know anything, like she was falling behind, like everyone else understood something she didn't. But here was Marcus, asking for her help. Valuing her skills.

"I'll think about it," she said.

But she was already thinking about it. She couldn't stop.

---

The walk home felt different again. Not just because she'd been twice now, but because something had shifted. An opportunity. A possibility.

She thought about Marcus's words: *You know what you're doing. And you're here anyway.* He'd said it like it was simple. Like her skills were valuable. Like she had something to offer.

The same streets passed by—the bakery, the coffee shop, the families walking dogs. But she saw them differently now. Not as a backdrop to her anxiety, but as a neighborhood. A community. A place where she might belong.

She got home and opened her laptop. Not to check Slack, not to spiral through research tabs about AI and career anxiety, but to think about what she could offer.

A marketing plan for a community garden. Social media. Events. The words felt strange in her mind—she'd spent so long feeling like she was barely keeping up with her actual job. But here was something different. Something small, something local, something where she could actually see the impact.

She opened a new document and started typing. Just ideas. Just possibilities.

*Website refresh*
*Instagram account*
*Newsletter signup at the gate*
*Community event—harvest festival?*

For the first time in months, she was thinking about what she could give, not what she lacked. The ripple from one small action—showing up at a garden—had spread further than she could have imagined.

And she was still moving.
