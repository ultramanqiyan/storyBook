# Chapter 1: The Weight of Stillness

The alarm had been off for three minutes, and Sarah had already checked her email, Slack, LinkedIn, and Twitter. She hadn't gotten out of bed. The gray light through the blinds told her it was another overcast Portland morning, but her phone told her everything else. Three emails from her manager, a Slack DM she'd been avoiding since yesterday, and a LinkedIn notification that someone from her past had viewed her profile. She closed the app, then opened it again. Nothing had changed. It wouldn't for hours, but she checked anyway.

This was the problem with working from home. There was no commute to separate night from day, no office door to walk through, no colleagues to nod at in the kitchen. Just her, her phone, and the slow accumulation of decisions she hadn't made.

She finally swung her legs out of bed, the cold floor a small shock against her warm feet. Twenty minutes gone. The day hadn't started, and she was already behind.

---

The home office was really just the spare bedroom with a desk crammed into the corner. Two monitors glowed blue in the morning light, and Sarah settled into her chair with the practiced motion of someone who had done this exact thing hundreds of times. Coffee steamed beside her keyboard—a small ritual that made her feel productive, even when she wasn't.

Forty-seven messages. Sarah stared at the red badges across eight Slack channels and felt her shoulders creep toward her ears. This was normal. This was fine. She clicked on the first channel, scanned the messages, and felt her chest tighten.

The marketing team was debating the Q2 campaign direction. Someone had tagged her for input three hours ago. Someone else had already responded, which meant her opinion was either redundant or contradicted, and she couldn't tell which was worse.

She opened the thread, read through the debate, and started typing a response. Then deleted it. Started again. Deleted again. What if she said the wrong thing? What if everyone already knew something she didn't? What if her input revealed that she'd been barely holding it together for months?

The Slack notification chimed, and she jumped.

**jennifer.walsh**: Hey! Quick q about the Q2 report - do you have the latest numbers?

Sarah stared at the message. She had seventeen versions of those numbers saved in various folders, each with slightly different parameters, each potentially the "latest" depending on what Jennifer needed them for. She could ask for clarification. She could send all seventeen versions. She could—

Another notification.

**mike.chen**: @sarah.mitchell - thoughts on the new creative direction? We're meeting in 30 if you want to join.

She hadn't been invited to the meeting. Was that intentional? Should she ask? Should she just show up? Should she respond to Jennifer first, or was Mike's message more urgent? What was the protocol for—

Her inbox dinged. An email from HR: *Optional Professional Development Session: "Strategic Thinking in Uncertain Times" - Registration closes today at noon.*

Sarah clicked the link. The session was in two weeks, led by someone named Dr. Elena Vasquez. She opened a new tab and searched the name. A LinkedIn profile appeared, then a university page, then a YouTube video of a keynote speech. She clicked play, watched thirty seconds, paused. Was this worth her time? Would it actually help, or was it just another thing to add to the list of things she'd meant to do?

She opened another tab and searched "strategic thinking training worth it reddit." The results were mixed. Some people swore by professional development. Others called it a waste of time. One thread devolved into an argument about whether soft skills could even be taught.

Forty minutes had passed. She'd watched thirty seconds of a video and read twelve Reddit comments. The coffee on her desk had gone cold.

She should respond to Jennifer. She should answer Mike. She should decide about the training session. She should—

Another Slack notification.

**marketing.general**: Has anyone heard from Sarah? Need her input on the Q2 numbers.

Her face grew hot. They were talking about her. In a public channel. Because she hadn't responded to a message that had arrived less than an hour ago.

She typed quickly: *Sorry, pulled into something! Sending now.*

Then she opened the folder with the Q2 numbers, selected the most recent file, attached it without checking, and hit send. The response came within seconds:

**jennifer.walsh**: Thanks! This is perfect.

Sarah exhaled. One thing done. But the training registration deadline was in two hours, and she had twelve tabs open and no idea whether she should go.

---

By 5:30, her eyes ached from screen time and her shoulders had permanently settled somewhere near her ears. She'd attended two video meetings she barely remembered, responded to messages she'd been avoiding for days, and still hadn't decided about the training session. The registration deadline had come and gone while she was reading reviews of Dr. Vasquez's book on Amazon.

Her phone buzzed. A text from her friend Maya.

**Maya**: Hey! Can we reschedule tonight? Work thing came up 😅

Sarah felt her shoulders drop—relief, not disappointment. She'd been dreading dinner all day, trying to decide what to wear, what to talk about, how to explain why she'd been so hard to reach lately. Now she didn't have to.

**Sarah**: No problem! Let me know when works for you.

She added an emoji—casual, breezy, everything was fine—and put the phone down. The apartment was quiet. Too quiet. She should eat something. She should go outside. She should do something other than sit in this chair and stare at screens.

Instead, she ordered Thai food and turned on the TV. Another night alone. Another night she didn't have to decide anything.

---

The ceiling was barely visible in the dark, but Sarah knew every crack and water stain by heart. She'd spent many nights staring at them. Tonight was no different.

Her phone sat on the nightstand, silent for once, but she could feel its weight even in the dark. She should have gone to dinner. She should have registered for the training. She should have responded to Mike's invitation instead of letting the meeting happen without her.

The day ran through her mind like a highlight reel of non-accomplishments. She'd been busy all day—checking, responding, scrolling, researching—but what had she actually done? Nothing that mattered. Nothing that moved anything forward. Just motion without progress, like a car spinning its wheels in mud.

Her phone buzzed. A notification from Slack, probably someone in a different time zone posting something that could wait until morning. She didn't check it.

Tomorrow she would decide about the training—except the deadline had passed, so that decision had been made for her. Tomorrow she would respond to the messages she'd avoided—except new messages would have piled up by then, and she'd have to prioritize, and prioritizing meant deciding, and deciding meant—

Tomorrow she would be different.

The thought felt hollow, but she held onto it anyway. Tomorrow she would wake up and not reach for her phone immediately. Tomorrow she would tackle the hardest thing first. Tomorrow she would be the version of herself who had things under control.

Outside, traffic hummed on the distant highway. Someone's dog barked twice, then stopped. The world was full of people doing things, going places, making decisions. And here she was, lying in the dark, promising herself that tomorrow would be different.

The word felt heavy, then light, then heavy again as sleep finally came.
