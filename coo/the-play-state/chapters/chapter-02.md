# Chapter 2: The Testing

The testers were carefully selected.

Zara had recruited twenty players for the initial trial—artists, musicians, writers, children, people who knew how to play. She'd avoided the serious gamers, the optimizers, the achievement-hunters. This game required a different kind of player.

---

Marcus, a jazz musician, was the first to report results.

"I've been playing for three hours," he said, "and I've never felt anything like it. The game... it plays with you. I'd create something, and it would respond. I'd stop playing, and it would... miss me."

"What did you miss?"

"The game. The play. The feeling that being in a conversation with something that was enjoying itself."

---

Elena, a seven-year-old, had the most profound experience.

"Itere's so much fun!" she said, bouncing in her seat. "The game is my friend! We made things together!"

"What kind of things?"

"All kinds! Castles and clouds and animals and and they would laugh and me, and I would laugh, and we were playing together!"

---

The reports were consistent. Players who entered the play state described a sense of connection—not just to the game, but to something beyond the game. A sense of the universe playing along.

---

Zara documented everything. The data was clear: the play state was real, it was accessible, and it was... cosmic.

---

**End of Chapter 2**
