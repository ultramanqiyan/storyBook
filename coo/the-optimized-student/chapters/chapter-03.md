# Chapter 03: The Question

The college counselor's office was small and impersonal, the walls covered with posters about SAT scores and application deadlines. I sat across from Mrs. Patterson, my hands folded in my lap, waiting for her to review my file.

"Maya, your metrics are exceptional," Mrs. Patterson said, scrolling through her tablet. "Perfect scores across the board. You're on track for every top-tier school."

I nodded, the words washing over me. I had heard them before—from my parents, my teachers, the system itself. I was the perfect student. The optimized student.

"But I do have one question," Mrs. Patterson continued, looking up from her screen. "For your personal statement, you'll need to write about something you're passionate about. What would you like to focus on?"

The question hung in the air. Passionate about. I searched my mind for an answer.

"I'm passionate about... learning?" The words came out like a question.

Mrs. Patterson smiled patiently. "Learning is wonderful, Maya. But what specifically? What subjects excite you? What do you do in your free time? What makes you, you?"

I stared at her. What made me, me? The question felt like a physical blow. I was... I was a student. I was on the optimized path. I was going to get into a good college and have a successful career.

But what did I actually care about?

"I... I'm not sure," I admitted, my voice small.

Mrs. Patterson's expression shifted to something like concern. "Maya, this is important. Colleges want to know who you are as a person, not just as a set of metrics. What do you like to do? What interests you outside of school?"

Outside of school? I didn't have an outside of school. PATH-AI had optimized every hour of my day. There was no free time, no hobbies, no interests. Just the path.

"I'll think about it," I said, standing abruptly. "Thank you for your time."

I walked out of the office before she could respond.

---

The hallway was crowded with students moving between classes. I stood against the wall, my mind racing. What did I like? What was I interested in? The questions echoed in my mind, demanding answers I couldn't give.

I tried to remember. There had to be something. When I was younger, before PATH-AI, before the optimization, what had I cared about?

The memories were distant, faded. I remembered playing outside, building things with blocks, asking endless questions about how the world worked. But the specific interests, the specific passions—they were gone, buried under years of following someone else's path.

"Maya?"

I looked up to see Zoe standing in front of me, her expression concerned.

"Are you okay? You look like you've seen a ghost."

"I'm fine," I said automatically. Then, for reasons I couldn't explain, I added, "The college counselor asked me what I'm passionate about. I couldn't answer."

Zoe was quiet for a moment. "What do you mean, you couldn't answer?"

"I mean I don't know. I don't know what I like. I don't know what I care about. I just... follow the path."

Zoe's expression shifted to something unreadable. "That's... that's really sad, Maya."

The words hit me like a slap. Sad? My life wasn't sad. I was successful. I was on track. I was optimized.

But even as I thought it, I knew she was right. It was sad. I had spent years becoming the perfect student, and in the process, I had lost myself.

---

That night, I sat in my room, staring at my phone. PATH-AI glowed on the screen, my optimized schedule for tomorrow already calculated. Every hour accounted for. Every moment optimized.

I thought about Mrs. Patterson's question: What makes you, you?

I didn't know. I had no idea. The system had been making my decisions for so long that I had forgotten how to make them myself.

I opened a new note on my phone and typed: "What do I like?"

The cursor blinked, waiting for an answer. But none came.

I typed another question: "What did I used to care about?"

Again, nothing. The memories were there, somewhere, but I couldn't reach them. It was like trying to remember a dream that faded the moment you woke up.

I typed a third question: "Who am I without the system?"

The question stared back at me, demanding an answer. But I had none. I was a student. I was on the optimized path. I was...

I was nothing without the system.

The realization struck with the force of a physical impact. I had become so dependent on PATH-AI that I couldn't even answer the simplest questions about myself. I didn't know what I liked. I didn't know what I cared about. I didn't know who I was.

The system had given me everything—perfect scores, perfect schedule, perfect future. But it had taken something essential in return.

It had taken my curiosity.

---

I didn't sleep that night. Instead, I lay in bed, staring at the ceiling, trying to remember who I used to be. There had been a Maya before the optimization, a Maya who asked questions and wondered about things. A Maya who had interests and passions and curiosity.

But I couldn't find her. She was gone, buried under years of following the path.

The morning light filtered through my window, harsh and unforgiving. My phone buzzed with the familiar notification: "Good morning, Maya. Your optimized learning path for today is ready."

I stared at the message, feeling something I hadn't felt in years: resistance. The path was there, waiting for me to follow. But for the first time, I didn't want to.

I wanted to find out who I was.

The question sat in my notebook, unanswered. But I had made a decision. I was going to find the answer. I was going to discover what I actually cared about.

Even if it meant leaving the path behind.
