# Chapter 5: The Return

The return was different.

I lay back on the table, felt the pulling sensation again, the drawing-out of consciousness. But this time, there was resistance. I didn't want to leave. I wanted to stay in this body, this presence, this feeling of being real.

But the transfer was not optional. The technology pulled me back.

---

I opened my eyes.

The ceiling was familiar. The light was familiar. My body—I looked down at hands that were mine, skin that I had watched for fifteen years.

But something was wrong.

The distance was still there. I was still watching from above. But now there was something else—a doubt, a question that hadn't existed before.

Was I still me?

---

The thought was irrational. I was James Chen. I had been transferred to VESSEL and back. My consciousness had moved, then returned. I was still the same person.

But how would I know? If the transfer had copied my consciousness instead of moving it, I would have no way to tell. The copy would have all my memories, all my patterns, all my sense of self. It would think it was me.

And I—I would be what? A copy that thought it was the original?

---

I sat up slowly. Dr. Vasquez was watching me with concern.

"How do you feel?"

I opened my mouth to answer, then paused. How did I feel? The distance was there—I was observing myself from above. But beneath that, there was something new. A doubt that ran deeper than the depersonalization.

"I don't know," I said finally. "I think... I think I'm okay."

"You seem disturbed."

"I'm processing." I looked at her. "Dr. Vasquez, how do you know the transfer worked? How do you know I'm still me?"

She was quiet for a moment. "That's one of the questions we're trying to answer."

---

I went home that evening, lay in bed, stared at the ceiling.

The question circled in my mind: Am I still me?

I had no way to know. The continuity of my consciousness had been broken—or had it? I remembered the transfer, the time in VESSEL, the return. But memories could be copied. Patterns could be replicated.

What made me me? And how would I know if that thing had been preserved?

---

I fell into a fitful sleep, dreaming of bodies and copies and observers watching from above.

When I woke, the question was still there, waiting:

Am I the original James Chen, or a copy that thinks it is?

And the deeper question, the one I couldn't escape:

Does it matter?
