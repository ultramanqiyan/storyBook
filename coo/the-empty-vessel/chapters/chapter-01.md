# Chapter 1: The Observer

I watch myself from a distance.

This is not a metaphor. This is my daily experience. I sit at my desk, typing code, and somewhere above and behind me, I observe the scene: a man in his late thirties, dark hair thinning slightly, fingers moving across the keyboard. The man is me. I know this. But I feel like a camera, recording without participating.

Depersonalization-derealization disorder. That's what the psychiatrist called it. A dissociative condition where you feel detached from your body, your thoughts, your self. You become an observer of your own existence.

I've lived with this for fifteen years.

The morning routine is automatic. Wake. Shower. Coffee. Commute. I observe myself performing each action, noting the efficiency of the movements, the absence of feeling. The man in the mirror looks tired. I notice this clinically, the way one might notice a warning light on a dashboard.

At work, I write code for a healthcare startup. The work is interesting enough to hold my attention, which is rare. When I'm deep in a problem, the distance closes slightly. I become the typing, the thinking, the solving. For brief moments, I am present.

But then the problem is solved, and the distance returns. I watch myself check email, attend meetings, make small talk with colleagues. The words come out of my mouth, but I don't feel them forming. They just appear, appropriate and hollow.

The psychiatrist says I should try grounding techniques. Feel your feet on the floor. Name five things you can see. Touch something and describe the texture.

I try. The floor is cold. The wall is white. The coffee cup is ceramic. But the words feel like data, not experience. I am still the observer, noting the sensations from a distance.

What would it feel like to actually be here? To not watch, but to be?

I don't know. I've forgotten, if I ever knew.

At night, I lie in bed and wonder: Is this what consciousness is for everyone? Do other people feel present in their bodies, connected to their thoughts? Or is everyone watching from a distance, pretending to be here?

I can't ask. The question is too strange, too intimate. So I lie in the dark, observing myself not sleeping, waiting for consciousness to fade.

One morning, I see an advertisement.

**RESEARCH PARTICIPANTS NEEDED**

**Consciousness Studies Group**

**Seeking individuals with dissociative conditions for groundbreaking research**

**Compensation provided**

I stare at the screen. The words seem to pulse with significance. I observe myself considering the possibility. I observe myself clicking the link. I observe myself filling out the application.

And for a moment, just a moment, I wonder if the observer is hoping for something the observed cannot name.

**END OF CHAPTER 1**
