# Chapter 03: The Preparation

The facility was smaller than James had imagined—more like a clinic than a laboratory. The empty vessel system, in the center of the main room, looked almost organic: a smooth, white pod, roughly human-shaped, with no features, no identity, just... space.

"This is where your consciousness will temporarily reside," Dr. Morrison said, gesturing to the pod.

James looked at the blank form and felt something he rarely felt: a shiver of genuine fear.

The vessel was approximately seven feet tall, its surface smooth and slightly iridescent, like mother-of-pearl. There was no face, no distinguishing marks—just the suggestion of a human shape, a container waiting to be filled. The top portion was open, revealing an interior that seemed to absorb light rather than reflect it.

"It looks... empty," James said, the word inadequate.

"That's the point," Dr. Morrison replied. "VESSEL—that's what we call the system—is designed to host consciousness without imposing any pre-existing identity. When your awareness transfers here, you'll experience existence without the context of your personal history. No memories, no habits, no sense of who you 'should' be. Just pure consciousness."

James circled the pod slowly, trying to imagine himself—or whatever he was—inside it. The idea was both terrifying and strangely appealing. To exist without the weight of his past, without the constant performance of being James Chen. To simply *be*.

"How does the transfer work?" he asked.

Dr. Morrison led him to a chair near the pod, its design similar to a dentist's chair but with additional sensors and monitors. "You'll sit here. We'll attach sensors to your temples and the base of your skull. The transfer process takes approximately thirty seconds. You'll feel a sensation of falling or floating, and then you'll be... elsewhere."

"And when I'm in the vessel?"

"You'll be aware. You'll be able to think, to perceive, to experience. But you won't have access to your memories or your sense of personal identity. You'll be consciousness without context." Dr. Morrison's voice was calm, clinical. "We'll be monitoring you the entire time. You'll be able to communicate with us through the vessel's audio systems."

James nodded slowly. The procedure still seemed impossible, like something from a science fiction novel. But here he was, standing in a laboratory, preparing to leave his body behind.

"What if something goes wrong?"

"We have protocols. If your vital signs destabilize, we'll abort the transfer and bring you back immediately. If you experience distress in the vessel, we can end the session early. Your safety is our primary concern."

James looked at the empty vessel again. The blank form seemed to stare back at him, waiting. *What are you?* he thought. *And what will I become when I'm inside you?*

The preparation took most of the morning. James was shown to a small room where he changed into a simple gray jumpsuit, then led through a series of baseline tests—cognitive assessments, memory checks, emotional response measurements. The researchers were efficient and professional, treating him with the careful attention reserved for human subjects in experimental procedures.

Dr. Morrison conducted the final briefing in a conference room overlooking the courtyard garden. The sunlight streaming through the windows felt impossibly normal, a reminder that the world outside continued regardless of what was about to happen inside.

"Do you have any final questions?" Dr. Morrison asked.

James had dozens. But most of them couldn't be answered. What would it feel like to exist without identity? Would he still be himself? Would he want to come back?

Instead, he asked: "Has anyone ever refused at the last minute?"

Dr. Morrison nodded. "A few. It's a significant decision, and it's not unusual for people to reconsider when faced with the reality of the procedure. There's no shame in changing your mind."

James thought about Sarah, waiting in the lobby. She had insisted on coming, despite his protests. "I need to be here," she had said. "I need to know you're okay."

"I'm not going to change my mind," he said. "I've been living in uncertainty for years. This is my chance to find out if there's anything on the other side."

Dr. Morrison studied him for a moment, then nodded. "Then let's begin."

The procedure room was colder than James had expected, the air conditioned to keep the equipment at optimal temperature. The chair sat in the center, surrounded by monitors and sensors, with the empty vessel positioned nearby like a silent sentinel.

James sat down, and a technician began attaching sensors to his temples, the base of his skull, his wrists. The adhesive was cool against his skin, slightly uncomfortable. Dr. Morrison stood at a console, watching readings that James couldn't interpret.

"Your vital signs are stable," she announced. "Heart rate slightly elevated, but within normal parameters for pre-procedure anxiety. How are you feeling?"

"Terrified," James admitted. "But ready."

"That's honest." Dr. Morrison's voice was gentle. "The transfer will begin in approximately five minutes. I want you to close your eyes and focus on your breathing. Try to relax as much as possible."

James closed his eyes. In the darkness behind his eyelids, he saw Sarah's face, the way she had looked at him in the lobby—fearful, loving, hopeful. He saw his own face in the mirror that morning, the stranger who wore his expressions.

*Am I still me?*

The question had haunted him for years. In a few minutes, he might finally have an answer. Or he might have more questions than ever.

"Two minutes," Dr. Morrison's voice came through the haze of his thoughts. "James, I need you to stay calm. The transfer can be disorienting. Whatever you experience, remember that we're monitoring you. You're safe."

James opened his eyes. The empty vessel stood a few feet away, its blank surface reflecting the harsh light of the procedure room. *What are you waiting for?* he thought. *What am I?*

"One minute."

James's heart was pounding now, his breath coming in short gasps. He felt the sensors against his skin, the chair beneath him, the cold air on his face. These were the last sensations he would experience as James Chen, in his own body, before the transfer.

*What if I don't come back?* he thought. *What if I come back and I'm not me?*

But these were the same questions he had been asking for years. The only difference was that now, he might actually get an answer.

"Transfer initiating in ten seconds. Nine. Eight."

James closed his eyes again. He thought about Sarah. He thought about Chairman Meow, asleep on the windowsill. He thought about the morning ritual, the years of verification, the distance that never closed.

"Three. Two. One."

The world dissolved.
