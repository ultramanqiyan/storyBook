# Chapter 2: The Distance

The worst episodes last hours.

I call them "the distance"—moments when the gap between observer and observed becomes so wide that I lose all sense of connection. I can see myself, hear myself, even control myself, but there is no "I" in the experience. Just a body moving through space, a voice making sounds, a face expressing emotions I cannot feel.

---

The first time it happened, I was twenty-three. I was at a party, talking to a woman I wanted to impress. Mid-sentence, I felt myself float upward. Suddenly I was watching the scene from above: the woman smiling, the man (me) gesturing, the room full of people I didn't know.

I tried to speak, to say something, but I couldn't find the connection. The man below kept talking, kept smiling, kept being charming. But I was gone.

The episode lasted twenty minutes. When I returned, the woman was gone, and I was standing alone by the drinks table. I had no memory of what I'd said or done. I still don't.

---

Over the years, the distance has become more frequent. Not always severe—sometimes just a slight disconnection, a thinning of the thread that ties me to myself. But sometimes it's complete. I lose hours. I find myself in places I don't remember going, having conversations I don't remember starting.

The psychiatrist calls it "dissociative amnesia." I call it living with a stranger who wears my face.

---

Last week, I had an episode at work. I was in a meeting, presenting a project update. Mid-sentence, I felt the familiar lift. I was suddenly above the table, watching myself speak.

The man below was confident, articulate, professional. He answered questions, made jokes, charmed the room. I watched with a kind of awe. Who was this person? How did he know what to say?

When the meeting ended, I returned. The room was empty. My notes were closed. I had no memory of what had happened.

Later, my boss stopped by my desk. "Great presentation, James. You really handled those questions."

I smiled and said thank you. But inside, I was screaming: *Who are you talking about? Who handled those questions? It wasn't me. I wasn't there.*

---

The distance is getting worse. The episodes are longer. The thread is thinner.

I applied to the research study because I don't know what else to do. I've tried therapy, medication, meditation. Nothing has closed the gap. Nothing has brought me back.

Maybe someone, somewhere, understands what this is. Maybe someone knows how to fix it.

Or maybe this is just what it means to be me: an observer, forever watching, never present.
