# Chapter 02: The Application

The application asked questions James had been asking himself for years: *Describe your sense of self. Rate your continuity of identity. Explain what "you" means to you.*

He sat at his computer, the cursor blinking, and typed: "I observe myself from a distance. I am never certain the observer and the observed are the same person. I perform 'being me' without feeling 'me.'"

The words looked strange on the screen—too honest, too raw. He had never said them out loud, not even to Sarah. But the application demanded truth, and James was tired of lies.

He continued through the form, answering questions about his history with depersonalization, his previous treatments, his hopes and fears. The final question made him pause: *What do you hope to gain from this experience?*

James thought about the morning ritual, the constant verification, the distance that never closed. He thought about the break room, the moment of total unreality, the terror of not knowing if he existed.

He typed: "I hope to understand what it means to be me. Or to accept that I might never know."

He submitted the application at 11:47 PM and lay awake for hours, wondering if he had made a mistake.

The response came three days later. James was at work when his phone buzzed with an email notification: *Thank you for your application. We would like to invite you for an interview with Dr. Morrison, lead researcher on the consciousness transfer study.*

The interview was scheduled for the following Monday. James spent the weekend in a state of nervous anticipation, alternating between hope and dread. Sarah noticed his distraction.

"You seem preoccupied," she said over dinner on Saturday night. "Is everything okay?"

James looked at her—at the woman who had loved him for six years, who had seen him through countless episodes, who still reached for his hand even when he couldn't feel it. He wanted to tell her about the study, about the hope that burned in his chest like a small, desperate flame.

But the words caught in his throat. What if she thought he was crazy? What if she didn't understand?

"I've been thinking about trying something new," he said finally. "A different kind of treatment."

Sarah's face softened with concern. "What kind of treatment?"

"There's a study at the university. They're doing something called consciousness transfer. It's experimental." He hesitated. "I applied."

Sarah set down her fork. "Consciousness transfer? What does that mean?"

"They would temporarily move my awareness into a system—an empty vessel—and then bring me back. The theory is that the contrast will help me understand my own identity."

Sarah was quiet for a long moment. When she spoke, her voice was careful. "That sounds... intense. Is it safe?"

"They say it is. But it's never been done before, not exactly like this." James met her eyes. "I need to try something, Sarah. I can't keep living like this—watching myself from a distance, never feeling real. I need to know if there's a way back to myself."

Sarah reached across the table and took his hand. Her touch was warm, present, real in a way that James's own existence never felt. "I'm scared for you," she said. "But I understand why you need to do this. I've watched you struggle for years. If this might help..."

"You'll support me?"

"I'll always support you." Her grip tightened. "Just promise me you'll come back."

The promise felt impossible to make. How could he promise to come back when he wasn't sure he was fully here? But he saw the fear in her eyes and nodded. "I'll come back."

The research facility was housed in a sleek building on the edge of the university campus. James arrived early on Monday morning, his stomach tight with anxiety. The lobby was modern and impersonal—all white walls and abstract art, designed to put visitors at ease while suggesting scientific rigor.

Dr. Morrison met him in the waiting area. She was younger than James had expected, perhaps in her early forties, with gray-streaked hair and a calm, assessing gaze. Her handshake was firm but brief.

"Mr. Chen. Thank you for coming. Shall we talk in my office?"

The office was small but comfortable, with a window overlooking a courtyard garden. Dr. Morrison settled behind her desk and pulled up James's application on her screen.

"I've read your responses," she began. "Your experience with depersonalization is consistent with what we're studying. But I need to make sure you understand what this procedure involves."

James nodded. "I've read the materials you sent. But I'd like to hear it from you."

Dr. Morrison leaned back in her chair. "The consciousness transfer procedure involves temporarily relocating your conscious awareness to what we call an 'empty vessel'—a system designed to host consciousness without having any pre-existing identity or memories of its own. You would spend approximately one hour in this state, experiencing existence without the context of your personal history. Then we would transfer you back to your body."

"And the purpose?"

"To create contrast. Most people take their sense of self for granted—they never question what it means to be themselves. For someone with depersonalization, that sense of self is disrupted. By experiencing existence in a completely neutral context, you might gain insight into what your own identity actually consists of."

James thought about this. "What if I prefer the empty vessel? What if I don't want to come back?"

Dr. Morrison's expression was serious. "That's a valid concern. Some participants in similar studies have reported a sense of peace in the neutral state—a relief from the burden of identity. However, the transfer is temporary. The vessel cannot sustain consciousness indefinitely. You would have to return."

"And what if I come back and I'm still... not me?"

"That's the question, isn't it?" Dr. Morrison's voice was gentle. "We can't guarantee results. This is experimental. You might gain clarity, or you might simply return to the same uncertainty. But you've tried other approaches—therapy, medication, mindfulness. Has any of it worked?"

James shook his head. "Nothing has worked."

"Then perhaps this is worth the risk." Dr. Morrison slid a packet of papers across the desk. "These are the consent forms. They outline the procedure, the risks, and your rights as a participant. Take your time reading them. You don't have to decide today."

James picked up the forms, his hands trembling slightly. The pages were dense with legal language, but the key points were clear: temporary consciousness transfer, one hour duration, unknown psychological effects, right to withdraw at any time before the procedure.

"What are the risks?" he asked.

"Disorientation, temporary worsening of depersonalization symptoms, existential distress. In rare cases, participants have reported difficulty reintegrating with their sense of self after the procedure." Dr. Morrison met his eyes. "We've never had a participant who couldn't return. But we've also never had a participant with your specific condition. There are unknowns."

James nodded slowly. Unknowns. His entire life was an unknown. What was one more?

"I have one more question," he said. "Why did you choose me? There must be many applicants."

Dr. Morrison smiled slightly. "Your application was honest in a way that most aren't. You didn't try to minimize your condition or present yourself as a perfect candidate. You admitted uncertainty. That kind of self-awareness is rare—and valuable in this type of study."

James wasn't sure if that was a compliment or a warning.

He took the consent forms home and read them three times. Sarah sat beside him on the couch, reading over his shoulder, asking questions he couldn't always answer.

"What if something goes wrong?"

"They have protocols for that."

"What if you can't come back?"

"They've never lost anyone."

"What if you're different when you return?"

James looked at her—at the fear in her eyes, the love, the hope. "I'm already different," he said quietly. "I've been different for years. I'm just trying to understand what that means."

That night, James sat at his desk with a pen in his hand. The consent form waited in front of him, the signature line blank. He thought about his morning ritual, the years of asking *Am I still me?*, the exhaustion of performing normalcy.

*What if I go there,* he thought, *and never come back?* Or worse—what if I come back and I'm still not me?

He thought about Sarah's hand in his, warm and present. He thought about the break room, the moment of total unreality. He thought about the stranger in the mirror, looking back at him with his own eyes.

He signed his name.
