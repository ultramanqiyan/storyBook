# Chapter 4: The Resistance

The resistance took many forms.

Work was the primary one. Sarah threw herself into her practice with an intensity that surprised even her. She took on new clients, extended her hours, filled every available moment with the work she was good at. The familiar rhythms of coaching—the questions, the frameworks, the breakthroughs—provided a structure that kept the threshold at bay. Every session was a small victory against the void that waited at the edge of her awareness. Every client was proof that she was still useful, still valuable, still the person she'd always been.

She stopped sleeping properly. The dreams had started three weeks ago—always the same pattern. She'd find herself standing at the edge of a cliff, looking down into fog. Or standing before a row of doors, each one leading to a version of herself she couldn't quite see. Or walking through her own life, watching herself make choices she didn't remember making, becoming someone she didn't recognize. She'd wake in the dark, heart racing, the sense of something approaching still lingering in her chest.

She didn't tell anyone about the dreams. They were just stress, she told herself. The natural result of working too hard. She'd take a vacation eventually. Slow down. Rest. But first, she had to get through this busy period. First, she had to help David navigate his threshold. First, she had to finish the book proposal her publisher was waiting for. First, she had to prepare for the conference presentation next month.

First, first, first. Always another reason to delay.

David noticed. In their fourth session, he commented on her energy.

"You seem different. More intense. Is everything okay?"

"Everything's fine. Just busy."

"Busy is a form of avoidance."

She almost smiled. "You've been studying the methodology."

"I have. And I'm starting to recognize when someone is running from their own threshold."

The observation landed harder than it should have. Sarah felt a flash of defensiveness, then let it go. David was right. She was running. She'd been running for weeks, maybe months, maybe years. The threshold had been approaching for a long time, and she'd been filling her life with work to avoid seeing it.

"You're right. I'm avoiding. But that's not what this session is about."

"Isn't it? You've told me repeatedly that the coach's state affects the client's work. If you're avoiding your own threshold, how does that affect our work?"

It was a fair question. One she would have asked if their positions were reversed. The methodology was clear on this point: the guide's presence was the most important tool in threshold work. If the guide was fragmented, avoiding, running, the client would sense it. The work would suffer.

"My threshold and your threshold are separate processes. I can guide you through yours while navigating my own. That's part of the skill."

"Is it? Or is that another form of avoidance?"

Sarah was quiet for a moment. David had grown more perceptive over their weeks of work together. He was beginning to see through the professional facade she'd built. The thought was uncomfortable—she was supposed to be the guide, not the guided. She was supposed to have answers, not questions.

"Let's focus on your process," she said finally. "You're approaching your own threshold. What are you noticing?"

The deflection worked. David shifted into client mode, describing his experiences—the growing clarity about what he wanted, the fear of making changes, the resistance from his board and investors. Sarah guided him through the familiar territory, asking the questions she always asked, providing the framework she always provided.

But something had shifted. The session felt different—less authentic, more performed. She was going through the motions of coaching while her own threshold pressed against her awareness. Every question she asked David echoed in her own mind. Every insight she offered felt like a message she needed to hear herself.

When the session ended, she felt relieved and guilty in equal measure. Relieved to have gotten through it without revealing her own struggle. Guilty for not being fully present for a client who was paying for her complete attention.

---

After David's session, she had back-to-back appointments. Three more clients, each navigating their own thresholds. She gave each one her full attention—or tried to. But underneath the professional focus, she could feel the resistance building like pressure behind a dam.

The first client was a woman navigating divorce, terrified of who she'd be without her marriage. Sarah guided her through the familiar exercises—the identity mapping, the values clarification, the threshold visualization. The woman left with tears in her eyes and hope in her voice. Sarah felt nothing.

The second client was a man facing retirement, afraid of the emptiness that waited. He'd built his entire identity around his career, and now that career was ending. Sarah helped him see the threshold as an opportunity rather than a threat. He left with a new sense of possibility. Sarah felt hollow.

The third client was a young professional questioning her career path, scared of disappointing her parents. She'd done everything right—top school, prestigious job, impressive trajectory—and found herself miserable. Sarah helped her distinguish between her own desires and her parents' expectations. The client left with clarity. Sarah felt lost.

By evening, she was exhausted. Not the good exhaustion that came from meaningful work, but the depleted exhaustion that came from avoidance. She'd spent the entire day helping others navigate transformations she was refusing to make herself. She'd offered wisdom she wasn't following. She'd provided guidance she wasn't taking.

She went home, ate dinner without tasting it, and tried to sleep. But the dreams came again—the cliff, the doors, the versions of herself she couldn't become. In tonight's dream, she stood before a mirror that showed her older, different, somehow more herself than she'd ever been. The woman in the mirror smiled, and Sarah felt a longing so intense it woke her.

She lay in the dark, staring at the ceiling, the longing still aching in her chest.

The resistance was costing her. She could feel it in her body—the tension in her shoulders, the knot in her stomach, the persistent fatigue that no amount of sleep could address. She was pouring energy into staying the same, and the cost was increasing. Every day, the effort required to maintain her current identity grew larger. Every day, the threshold pressed closer.

She thought about the clients she'd seen that day. All of them were moving toward their thresholds. All of them were afraid. And all of them were doing the work—showing up, asking the hard questions, taking the steps they needed to take. They were brave in ways she wasn't.

Sarah was doing none of those things. She was showing up for clients, but not for herself. She was asking hard questions, but only of others. She was taking steps, but only backward, away from the threshold that waited.

She got up, went to her home office, and opened her notebook. The page where she'd written "Threshold approaching. Subject: me" stared back at her. She'd written it three weeks ago, after the first dream. She hadn't looked at it since.

She turned to a fresh page and began to write.

*What am I afraid of?*

The question was simple. The answer was not.

She wrote for an hour, filling page after page with the fears she'd been avoiding. Fear of losing her identity. Fear of becoming someone who couldn't do this work. Fear of discovering that her methodology was incomplete—that she'd been guiding others through territory she didn't fully understand. Fear of change itself, even though she'd built her career on helping others change. Fear of the unknown. Fear of losing control. Fear of becoming irrelevant. Fear of being seen as a fraud. Fear that everything she'd built was a house of cards.

When she finished, she had pages of fears. But underneath all of them, one fear stood out.

*What if I cross the threshold and discover that who I become can't help people the way I do now?*

It was the fear beneath the fear. The one she'd never fully acknowledged. She'd built her entire identity around being a guide—a helper—someone who facilitated transformation in others. What if transformation meant losing that? What if who she became couldn't do this work anymore? What if the threshold led away from everything she valued?

She sat with the question in the predawn darkness. Outside, the city was beginning to wake. Inside, she was finally facing what she'd been running from.

The question wasn't really about losing her ability to help. It was about losing her identity as the helper. It was about discovering that she wasn't the expert, the guide, the one with answers. It was about becoming a student again. A seeker. Someone who didn't know.

And that, she realized, was exactly what her clients faced. Every one of them had to let go of being the expert in their own lives. Every one of them had to become a seeker. Every one of them had to accept not-knowing as the price of transformation.

She was asking them to do what she wasn't willing to do herself.

---

The next day, she tried something different.

Instead of filling her schedule with clients, she cleared it. She cancelled appointments, rescheduled sessions, created space in her calendar. The resistance screamed at her—this was irresponsible, unprofessional, a betrayal of her commitments. Her assistant looked at her with concern when she gave the instructions. Her phone buzzed with messages from confused clients. But she pushed through.

She spent the morning sitting in her office, not working, not avoiding, just being. The threshold pressed against her awareness, no longer distant, no longer theoretical. It was here, now, demanding attention. She could feel its presence like a weight in her chest, like a door waiting to open.

She tried to apply her own methodology. The twelve conditions for threshold access. The preparation practices. The entry techniques. She'd taught these to dozens of clients. She'd refined them over years of practice. She knew them inside and out.

None of them worked.

It was one thing to guide someone else through these steps. It was another to guide herself. The coach's voice in her head kept analyzing, evaluating, measuring. Every time she tried to enter the threshold, she found herself observing the attempt instead of making it. She was too aware of the process to surrender to it.

She was, she realized, trapped by her own expertise. She knew too much about the process to surrender to it. The methodology that helped others was preventing her own transformation. The map she'd drawn was keeping her from walking the territory.

She needed help. But from whom?

Most of her colleagues were coaches like her—professionals who guided others through thresholds they'd never fully crossed themselves. Her friends would be supportive but wouldn't understand the depth of what she was facing. Her therapist was skilled, but this wasn't a therapeutic issue. It was something else. Something she didn't have words for.

The answer came immediately: Wei.

Her brother. The meditation master. The one who'd been navigating thresholds for decades, long before she'd developed her methodology. They'd taken different paths—he into contemplation, she into coaching—but they'd arrived at similar understandings. He'd crossed thresholds she'd only studied. He'd walked territory she'd only mapped.

She picked up her phone and called him.

"Sarah." His voice was warm but unsurprised, as if he'd been expecting her call. "How are you?"

"I need help."

"I know. I've been waiting for you to ask."

The response should have surprised her, but it didn't. Wei had always seemed to know things before they happened. It was part of what had drawn him to meditation in the first place—a sensitivity to patterns, to undercurrents, to the movements of consciousness beneath the surface of ordinary life.

"Can we meet today?"

"Come to the center. I'll be here."

---

They met that afternoon at his meditation center—the same center where he'd guided countless practitioners through their own thresholds. The space was familiar to Sarah; she'd visited many times over the years. But today it felt different. Today she wasn't visiting as a sister or a fellow professional. Today she was visiting as a seeker.

The center was quiet in the mid-afternoon, between the morning and evening sessions. A few practitioners sat in the main hall, their stillness a presence in itself. The smell of incense lingered in the air. The light through the windows was soft and diffuse.

Wei led her to a small room off the main hall—a space for private interviews, for guidance, for the intimate work of transformation. He sat across from her, his presence calm and unhurried. He didn't ask questions. He simply waited.

The silence stretched between them, comfortable on his end, weighted on hers. Sarah felt the urge to fill it with words, with explanations, with justifications. She resisted.

"I've been avoiding my threshold," she said finally. "For years. And now it's here, and I can't enter it. My own methodology is working against me."

"How so?"

"I know too much. Every time I try to enter the threshold, I find myself analyzing the process. Evaluating whether I'm doing it right. Measuring my progress. The coach's voice won't shut up."

Wei nodded slowly. "The map is not the territory. You've spent years drawing maps. Now you need to walk the territory without the map."

"How? The map is all I have."

"The map is what you've created. The territory is what's always been there. You learned to navigate thresholds by studying others. But there's another way to learn—by being guided."

"By you?"

"If you're willing. But I need to understand something first." He met her eyes, his gaze steady and penetrating. "Why have you been avoiding this? What's the real fear?"

Sarah was quiet. The question cut through all her rationalizations, all her professional justifications, all her intellectual defenses. She could try to deflect, to offer a surface answer, but Wei would see through it. He always had.

"I'm afraid of losing who I am," she said finally. "I've built my entire identity around helping others transform. What if I transform into someone who can't do that anymore? What if I lose the very thing that makes me... me?"

Wei was quiet for a long moment. Then he smiled—a gentle, knowing smile that reminded her of their childhood, of the older brother who'd always seemed to understand things before she did.

"Sarah, the threshold doesn't take away who you are. It reveals who you've always been. The identity you're protecting—that's not who you are. That's who you've become. There's a difference."

"What's the difference?"

"The self that transforms is the self that was there before the transformation. The identity, the role, the expertise—those are layers on top of something deeper. The threshold strips away the layers. It doesn't destroy what's underneath."

"How do you know?"

"Because I've crossed many thresholds. And I'm still here. Still your brother. Still teaching. Still practicing. The layers have changed, but what's underneath hasn't."

Sarah felt something shift—a loosening of the resistance she'd been holding. The fear was still there, but it was less absolute. Less final. The threshold wasn't death. It was revelation.

"Will you help me?" she asked.

"I've been waiting to," Wei said. "The question was whether you were ready to ask."

**End of Chapter 4**
