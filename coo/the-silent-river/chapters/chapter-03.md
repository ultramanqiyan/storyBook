# Chapter 3: The Investigation

The permits were perfectly in order. Elena had spent three days going through them, looking for a crack, a loophole, anything that would give her a foothold. NeuralCore had followed every procedure, obtained every signature, dotted every i. The water extraction was legal—technically, narrowly, obscenely legal. She threw the latest file across her desk in frustration.

Maria, her paralegal, looked up from her own stack of documents. "Still nothing?"

"Nothing that would hold up in court." Elena rubbed her temples, feeling the beginning of a headache. "They've built a fortress of paperwork. Every permit cross-references three others, every approval is contingent on conditions that were met on paper but not in reality. It's like trying to dismantle a building by removing one brick at a time."

Maria set down her pen. "What about the quarterly reports? The ones you found last week?"

Elena pulled those files to the top of the pile. "That's our best lead. NeuralCore's own documents show they're extracting nearly twice their permitted volume. But it's circumstantial—we need someone on the inside who can testify to what's really happening."

"Any candidates?"

Elena thought about James Whitfield's smooth smile, his veiled threats disguised as job offers. "Not yet. But I'm working on it."

---

The NeuralCore facility sprawled across what had once been farmland, a massive complex of glass and steel that seemed to have grown from the earth itself. Elena parked her car in the visitor lot and studied the building through her windshield. It was impressive, in a cold, corporate way—the kind of architecture that said "future" without any warmth or humanity.

The cooling towers rose from the back of the complex, their white plumes rising into the clear sky. That was where the water went—pumped through the facility to keep the servers from overheating, then released back into the environment. Or at least, that was what the permits claimed.

Elena had requested a tour of the facility as part of her discovery motion. NeuralCore had fought it, but the judge had granted limited access—enough to see the public areas, not enough to examine the actual operations. It was a compromise that satisfied no one, but it was better than nothing.

James Whitfield met her at the security entrance.

"Ms. Vasquez." His smile was as polished as it had been in his office. "Welcome to the future."

---

The tour was a carefully choreographed performance. Whitfield led Elena through gleaming corridors, past rows of humming servers, into control rooms where technicians monitored screens filled with data. Everything was clean, efficient, impressive. The kind of facility that would make any politician proud to have in their district.

"This is our primary data processing center," Whitfield said, gesturing at a room filled with server racks. "We handle computational work for clients around the world—medical research, financial modeling, climate prediction. The work we do here saves lives, Ms. Vasquez. Literally saves lives."

Elena made notes, asked questions, played the role of the curious visitor. But her eyes were looking for what wasn't shown—the water intake systems, the cooling infrastructure, the waste treatment facilities. Those areas were "restricted for security reasons," Whitfield explained. Proprietary technology. Trade secrets.

"And the water?" Elena asked as they passed a series of pipes running along the ceiling. "Where does it come from? Where does it go?"

Whitfield's smile didn't falter. "We draw from the aquifer, under strict permit guidelines. The water is treated, used for cooling, treated again, and returned to the environment. It's a closed-loop system, essentially. Very efficient."

"But the aquifer is being depleted. Wells are going dry across the valley."

"The aquifer levels fluctuate naturally," Whitfield said. "We monitor them carefully. Our extraction is sustainable." He paused. "I understand the town's concerns, Ms. Vasquez. But correlation is not causation. The drought affecting this region is a climate phenomenon, not something NeuralCore created."


After the tour, Whitfield walked Elena to her car. The afternoon sun was hot, the air dry and still. In the distance, Elena could see the brown hills that had once been green, the skeletal remains of orchards that had died for lack of water.

"You're persistent," Whitfield said. "I'll give you that. Most lawyers would have given up by now."

"I'm not most lawyers."

"No." He studied her with those calculating eyes. "You're not. Which is why I'm going to give you some advice, off the record."

Elena waited.

"NeuralCore has invested billions in this facility. We have contracts with governments, corporations, research institutions around the world. The work we do here is important—not just profitable, but genuinely important to the future of humanity." His voice dropped. "We will not allow that work to be disrupted by a small-town dispute over water rights. Do you understand what I'm saying?"

Elena felt a chill despite the heat. "Are you threatening me?"

"I'm informing you." Whitfield's smile was cold now. "The legal system has many avenues for resolving disputes. Some are faster than others. Some are more... expensive. NeuralCore has resources you can't imagine, Ms. Vasquez. We can bury you in motions, appeals, delays. We can drag this out for years until your clients have nothing left to fight with. Or..."

"Or?"

"Or you can find a more productive use of your considerable talents." He stepped back. "Think about it. That offer we discussed—it's still on the table."


Elena drove away from the NeuralCore facility with her hands tight on the steering wheel. The tour had been useless—she'd learned nothing she didn't already know, seen nothing that would help her case. Whitfield had used the opportunity to intimidate her, to remind her of the power she was up against.

But something nagged at her. A detail she'd noticed without consciously registering. She pulled over to the side of the road and closed her eyes, trying to reconstruct the tour in her mind.

The server rooms. The control centers. The corridors with their gleaming floors and their pipes running overhead. The pipes...

Elena opened her eyes. The pipes. They'd been labeled with color-coded bands—blue for intake, green for treatment, red for... what? She hadn't been able to see the full labels, but she'd noticed that some of the red-banded pipes led away from the facility, not toward the treatment area.

Where were those pipes going?


The River's Bend Diner was a relic from another era—vinyl booths, checkered floors, a counter where you could watch the cook work the grill. It had been serving the community for sixty years, and the walls were covered with photographs of the town as it used to be: the river full and flowing, the fields green and productive, the main street bustling with activity.

Elena sat in a back booth, nursing a cup of coffee that had gone cold while she waited. She'd put out feelers among the community, asking about anyone who might have worked at NeuralCore, anyone who might be willing to talk. Most people had refused—too afraid of losing jobs, of retaliation, of being labeled troublemakers in a town that couldn't afford more division.

But one name had come up more than once: David Park. He'd worked in NeuralCore's facilities department for two years before quitting suddenly six months ago. No one knew exactly why he'd left, but the word was that he'd seen something he shouldn't have.

The door opened, and a man in his late thirties slipped in. He was thin, with nervous eyes that scanned the room before settling on Elena. He walked to her booth without hesitation, sliding into the seat across from her.

"You're the lawyer," he said. Not a question.

"Elena Vasquez." She didn't offer her hand. "And you're David Park."

He glanced around again. "I can't stay long. They watch everyone who leaves."

"Who watches?"

"NeuralCore. Security. They have people everywhere." Park's hands were shaking slightly. "I shouldn't be here. But I couldn't stay silent anymore."

Elena leaned forward. "Tell me what you saw."


Park spoke quickly, in low tones, his eyes constantly moving. "I worked in facilities management. Maintenance, repairs, that kind of thing. I wasn't supposed to see the big picture—just fix what was broken. But you can't work somewhere for two years without noticing things."

"What kind of things?"

"The water intake, for one. The permits say they're drawing from the main aquifer. But I saw the schematics. They've drilled deeper—much deeper. There's a secondary aquifer that no one knew about. They're draining it dry."

Elena felt her pulse quicken. "Do you have proof?"

Park hesitated. "I copied some files before I left. Schematics, maintenance logs, internal memos. But I don't know if it's enough. And I don't know if I can testify—I have a family, Ms. Vasquez. NeuralCore has lawyers, security people. They could make my life hell."

"They've already threatened me," Elena said. "I know what you're facing."

Park looked at her for a long moment. "Why are you doing this? You could walk away. Find easier cases, safer clients. Why risk everything for a town that can't even pay you?"

Elena thought about the dry riverbed, the silent valley, the hope in Sarah Miller's eyes. "Because someone has to," she said. "Because if people like me don't fight, then people like Whitfield win by default."

Park nodded slowly. "I'll send you what I have. Encrypted files, through a secure server. But I can't promise more than that. Not yet."

"It's a start." Elena slid a card across the table. "When you're ready to talk more, call this number. Day or night."

Park took the card and slipped away without another word, disappearing into the afternoon crowd.


The files arrived that night, encrypted and anonymized, routed through a series of servers that made them impossible to trace. Elena stayed up until dawn, poring over schematics and logs and internal communications.

What she found was worse than she'd imagined.

NeuralCore had indeed discovered a secondary aquifer—a deep, ancient reservoir of water that had been sealed off from the surface for thousands of years. They'd drilled into it secretly, without permits, without environmental review. And they were draining it at a rate that would exhaust it within five years.

But the deeper aquifer was connected to the surface water system through a complex network of underground channels. Draining it was causing the surface aquifer to collapse, sucking water away from wells and streams and rivers throughout the valley. It was like pulling the plug in a bathtub—drain the deep water, and everything above it would follow.

The memos showed that NeuralCore's engineers had warned about this. They'd raised concerns, suggested alternatives, recommended slower extraction rates. But the executives had overruled them. The profits were too good, the demand too high, the future too important to slow down for something as mundane as water.

Elena sat back in her chair, staring at the screen. She had what she needed—proof of wrongdoing, evidence of cover-ups, documentation of decisions that had sacrificed a community for corporate profit. But she also had something else: a target painted on her back.

Whitfield had warned her. NeuralCore had resources she couldn't imagine. And now she held documents that could cost them billions.

She saved the files to an encrypted drive and made three backup copies. Then she called Marcus Chen.

"I need to meet with the community," she said. "I have something to show them."
