# Chapter 8: The Resistance

Elena spent three days in her office, not answering calls, not responding to emails, not doing anything except staring at the dismissal ruling and wondering where she'd gone wrong. She'd followed every procedure. She'd found the evidence. She'd made the arguments. And none of it had mattered. The law had failed. She had failed.

On the fourth day, Maria came in with coffee and a determined expression.

"Get up," she said. "We're going to River's Bend."

"I can't face them."

"You have to face them." Maria set the coffee on Elena's desk. "They're still your clients. They still need you."

"They need a lawyer who can win."

"They need a lawyer who shows up." Maria pulled Elena to her feet. "Get dressed. We're leaving in thirty minutes."

---

The drive to River's Bend took longer than usual. Elena took the scenic route, winding through the valley roads, past the darkened fields and the skeletal orchards. She stopped once at a bridge that crossed what had been the main river, getting out of her car to stand at the railing and look down at the dry bed below.

The moon was nearly full, casting enough light for her to see the cracks in the earth, the debris of dead plants, the empty channels where water should have flowed. Marcus Chen stood at the front of the room, his weathered face set in hard lines.

"I know we lost," he said. "I know the court dismissed our case. But I'm not giving up. And I don't think you should either."

A murmur ran through the crowd. Elena saw heads nodding, saw the tight set of shoulders and the clasped hands.

"NeuralCore thinks this is over," Marcus continued. "They think they can just drain our valley and walk away. But they're wrong. This isn't over. This is just beginning."


Sarah Miller stood up from the front row. "I've lived in this valley my whole life. My parents lived here. My grandparents. My great-grandparents. Four generations of Millers have worked this land."

She turned to face the crowd. "I'll be damned if I'm the generation that gives up."

The room erupted. Not in despair, but in anger. In determination. In the kind of energy that Elena hadn't seen since the case began.

One by one, people stood up and spoke.

"My well went dry two months ago. I had to truck in water for my cattle. Cost me everything. But I'm still here."

"My kids moved away. Said there was no future here. But I'm not leaving. This is my home."

"They can take our water. They can take our farms. But they can't take our fight."

Elena felt something shift inside her. She'd been so focused on the legal battle that she'd forgotten the most important thing: these people weren't fighting because they thought they would win. They were fighting because it was the right thing to do.

NeuralCore's response came through James Whitfield, who held a press conference on Tuesday. Elena watched from her office, her jaw tight.

"The allegations in recent media reports are false and defamatory," Whitfield said smoothly. "NeuralCore has operated in full compliance with all applicable laws and regulations. The water shortage in River's Bend is the result of unprecedented drought conditions, not our operations."

"Then why did your engineers warn about the deep aquifer?" a reporter asked.

But Elena could see the cracks in his facade. The reporters weren't letting him off easy. They were asking hard questions, demanding real answers.


By the end of the week, the story had gone national. Major news outlets picked it up. Environmental organizations issued statements. Politicians were forced to respond.

Governor Chen held a press conference, her face tight with barely concealed anger.

"I'm deeply disappointed by the misrepresentation of my office's support for economic development," she said. "The documents released by Ms. Vasquez have been selectively edited to create a false impression."

Elena nodded. "He was right."

"He also said that sometimes you have to lose before you can win." Sarah turned to her. "We lost in court. But we're winning in the court of public opinion."

Elena looked at the lights of NeuralCore's facility in the distance, still glowing, still running, still draining the valley. They hadn't stopped the water extraction. They hadn't won the legal battle. But they had something they hadn't had before: attention. Visibility. A voice.

"What happens now?" Sarah asked.

Elena thought about the appeal, the media strategy, the growing movement. "Now we keep fighting. Until we win. Or until there's nothing left to fight for."
