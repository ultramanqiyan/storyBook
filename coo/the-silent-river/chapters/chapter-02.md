# Chapter 2: The First Client

The River's Bend Community Center had been built for celebrations—graduations, weddings, the annual harvest festival that had once drawn visitors from across the region. Tonight, it held something else entirely: two hundred people crammed into folding chairs and standing along the walls, all of them waiting to hear if there was any hope left for their town.

Elena stood in the back, her legal pad already filling with names and dates, trying to understand how a river could simply disappear. The air in the room was thick with the smell of coffee and sweat and something harder to define—the particular desperation of people watching their world slip away.

Marcus Chen stood at the front of the room, his weathered face illuminated by the harsh fluorescent lights. He'd been a town council member for fifteen years, Sarah had told her, and before that an engineer at the hydroelectric plant that had once operated on the river. Now the plant was closed, the jobs were gone, and Marcus was the closest thing River's Bend had to a leader.

"Thank you all for coming," Marcus said, his voice carrying the particular weight of a man who had given too many speeches to empty rooms. "I know we've been through a lot these past months. I know some of you are tired. Some of you are angry. Some of you are just trying to figure out how to survive."

A murmur ran through the crowd. Elena saw heads nodding, saw the tight set of shoulders and the clasped hands. These were people who had lived through droughts and floods and market crashes, but this was different. This was the slow death of everything they'd built.

"Three years ago," Marcus continued, "NeuralCore came to us with promises. Jobs. Investment. A future for our children. Some of us believed them. Some of us didn't. But we all had to live with the consequences."

He gestured to a large map mounted on an easel beside him. It showed the valley, with the river marked in blue and the NeuralCore facility marked in red. The blue line was broken now, interrupted by long stretches where the river had simply ceased to exist.

"This is what we've lost." Marcus tapped the map with a calloused finger. "The North Fork hasn't flowed in eight months. The South Fork is down to a trickle. And the main river—the river our grandparents built this town around—is drying up faster than anyone predicted."

---

Sarah Miller sat in the front row, her hands folded in her lap. Elena had offered to drive her to the meeting, but Sarah had insisted on coming early to help set up. Now she sat with her back straight, her eyes fixed on Marcus, as if her attention alone could keep him speaking.

"Last week," Marcus said, "the state environmental agency released its quarterly report. They say the water levels are within acceptable parameters. They say the aquifer is stable. They say there's no evidence that NeuralCore's operations are causing harm."

A man in the third row stood up. He was tall and broad-shouldered, with the sun-darkened skin of someone who worked outdoors. "That's a lie," he said, his voice rough. "My well went dry two months ago. Had to drill three hundred feet before we hit water. Cost me everything I had saved for my daughter's college."

Another voice joined in. "My cattle are dying. Can't afford to truck in enough water to keep them alive."

"The orchard my grandfather planted—eighty years it's been in my family. Now the trees are dying because there's no water for irrigation."

Marcus raised his hands, asking for silence. The room quieted, but the anger remained, a palpable thing that pressed against the walls.

"I know," Marcus said. "I know what you're going through. My own farm is suffering. But the state won't listen to individual complaints. They say we need data. They say we need proof. And every time we try to get it, NeuralCore blocks us."

---

Elena stepped forward, moving through the crowd until she stood beside Marcus. The room fell silent, all eyes turning to her. She felt the weight of their attention, the hope and suspicion and exhaustion mingled together.

"My name is Elena Vasquez," she said. "I'm an environmental lawyer. Sarah Miller came to me last week and asked for my help."

She let that sink in, watching the faces before her. Some showed hope. Others showed distrust. A few showed nothing at all—the blank expressions of people who had been disappointed too many times.

"I've spent the past three days reviewing documents," Elena continued. "Permits. Environmental reports. Corporate filings. What I've found is troubling. NeuralCore's water extraction is significantly higher than their permits allow. And there are connections between their executives and the state officials who approved those permits."

A woman in the second row raised her hand. "Can you prove it?"

"Not yet," Elena admitted. "The documents suggest wrongdoing, but I need more. I need testimony from people who've seen what's happening. I need access to records that NeuralCore doesn't want to share. I need to build a case that can survive in court."

"And if you can't?" The question came from a man standing against the back wall, his arms crossed over his chest. "What then? We've already lost so much. How much more do we have to lose before someone helps us?"

Elena met his gaze. "I won't lie to you. This is a difficult case. NeuralCore has resources we can't match—lawyers, money, political connections. The odds are against us. But I've seen what happens when communities give up. I've seen what happens when people stop fighting." She paused. "I'd rather lose fighting than win by surrender."

---

The meeting lasted another hour. Elena listened more than she spoke, letting the residents tell their stories. The farmer whose wells had gone dry. The teacher whose students were leaving because their parents couldn't find work. The business owner whose customers had disappeared along with the water. Each story added another piece to the picture, another thread in the tapestry of loss.

When the meeting finally ended, the crowd filed out slowly. Some stopped to shake Elena's hand, to thank her for coming, to warn her about what she was getting into. Others left without a word, their faces unreadable.

Marcus Chen remained at the front of the room, folding chairs and stacking them against the wall. Elena moved to help him.

"You've done this before," he said, watching her work.

"I grew up in a town like this," Elena said. "Small. Agricultural. Dying by inches." She set another chair against the wall. "My family lost their farm when I was twelve. The bank took it. My father never recovered."

Marcus nodded slowly. "That why you became a lawyer?"

"That's part of it." Elena paused, considering her words. "I wanted to understand how it happened. How people could lose everything they'd built. I thought if I understood the law, I could protect people from the same thing happening to them."

"And has it? Protected people?"

Elena thought about all the cases she'd taken, all the clients she'd helped and failed to help. "Sometimes," she said. "Not always. But more often than if I'd done nothing."

---

Sarah Miller joined them as they finished putting away the last of the chairs. Her face was drawn, the lines around her eyes deeper than they'd been that morning.

"Thank you," she said to Elena. "For coming. For listening."

"Thank you for asking me," Elena replied. "But I need to know something before we go any further."

Sarah waited.

"Why me?" Elena asked. "Why did you come to my office? There are bigger firms in the city. Lawyers with more resources, more experience with cases like this."

Sarah exchanged a glance with Marcus before answering. "Because you're here," she said. "Because you're from a place like this. Because when I looked at you, I didn't see someone who would file some papers and then disappear when things got hard." She paused. "I saw someone who would fight."

Elena felt the weight of those words settling on her shoulders. It had been a long time since anyone had looked at her and seen a fighter. Most days, she felt more like a survivor—someone who had learned to pick her battles carefully, to accept small victories and move on.

But looking at Sarah's face, at the hope she was trying so hard to hide, Elena knew that this wasn't a battle she could pick carefully. This was a battle that had picked her.

"I'll need your help," Elena said. "All of you. I'll need documents, testimony, access to records. I'll need people willing to speak publicly about what they've seen. And I'll need time—this isn't something that can be won overnight."

"How long?" Marcus asked.

"Months. Maybe years." Elena saw the disappointment flicker across their faces. "I know that's not what you want to hear. But if we're going to take on NeuralCore, we have to do it right. One mistake, one procedural error, and the case gets thrown out. Then we have nothing."

Sarah straightened her shoulders. "We've been fighting this for three years already. What's a few more?"

---

They walked out of the community center together, into a night that was cooler than Elena had expected. The sky above River's Bend was clear and dark, the stars brighter than she ever saw in the city. For a moment, she could imagine what this valley had been like before—the river running silver under the moon, the fields green and alive, the town thriving.

"The governor is coming next month," Marcus said as they reached their cars. "Campaign stop. She's running for re-election."

Elena paused with her hand on the car door. "Governor Chen?"

"Distant cousin," Marcus said with a wry smile. "The irony isn't lost on me. Her family stayed in the city, made their fortune in politics. Mine came out here and farmed the land." He shook his head. "She approved the permits that let NeuralCore take our water. Now she wants our votes."

"Will she get them?"

Marcus looked out at the dark valley. "People are angry. But they're also tired. And NeuralCore has been promising jobs, economic development, a future. Some people want to believe them." He turned back to Elena. "If we're going to fight this, we need to show people the truth. Not just the legal arguments—the real cost of what's happening."

Elena nodded. "I'll start building the case. You keep the community together. And when the governor comes..." She paused, thinking. "Maybe we should have a conversation with her."

---

The drive back to her office took longer than expected. Elena took the scenic route, winding through the valley roads, past the darkened fields and the skeletal orchards. She stopped once at a bridge that crossed what had been the main river, getting out of her car to stand at the railing and look down at the dry bed below.

The moon was nearly full, casting enough light for her to see the cracks in the earth, the debris of dead plants, the empty channels where water should have flowed. The silence was absolute—not even the sound of wind, just a stillness that felt unnatural, as if the land itself had stopped breathing.

*This is what we're fighting for*, Elena thought. *Not just water. Not just jobs. But this—the life of a place, the soul of a community.*

She had told Sarah and Marcus that this would be a long fight. She hadn't told them how afraid she was that they would lose. NeuralCore wasn't just a corporation; it was a symbol of everything that was reshaping the world—technology, efficiency, progress at any cost. Standing against that felt like standing against the tide.

But then, the law was supposed to be a tide too. A force that couldn't be stopped, only redirected. The question was whether Elena could find a way to make it flow in the right direction.

She got back in her car and drove toward her office, already composing the first motion in her head. There was work to do.

---

The next morning, Elena filed a formal request for documents from NeuralCore, the state environmental agency, and the governor's office. It was a fishing expedition—broad, aggressive, designed to cast a wide net and see what turned up. Most of the requests would be denied or delayed. But some would yield results, and those results would form the foundation of her case.

She also sent a letter to NeuralCore's legal department, putting them on notice that River's Bend was represented by counsel and that any further water extraction without proper environmental review would be challenged in court. It was a shot across the bow, a warning that the days of unopposed expansion were over.

The response came faster than she expected.

---

James Whitfield's office was on the top floor of a glass tower in downtown Nordland City. The reception area was sleek and modern, all polished surfaces and abstract art, with a view that stretched across the urban sprawl to the distant mountains. Elena felt out of place in her practical suit and sensible shoes, but she kept her face neutral and her posture confident.

"Ms. Vasquez." Whitfield rose from behind his desk as she entered, extending a hand that was smooth and well-manicured. He was younger than she'd expected—early forties, with the kind of polished good looks that came from good genes and better skincare. His suit probably cost more than her monthly rent. "Thank you for coming on such short notice."

"I was surprised to receive your invitation," Elena said, taking the chair he offered. "Usually, corporations prefer to communicate through legal channels."

Whitfield smiled—a practiced expression that didn't reach his eyes. "I prefer a more personal approach when possible. It's so much easier to resolve misunderstandings face to face."

"Is that what this is? A misunderstanding?"

Whitfield leaned back in his chair, steepling his fingers. "NeuralCore has been a good neighbor to River's Bend. We've brought jobs, investment, economic growth to a region that was struggling. We've followed every regulation, obtained every permit, complied with every environmental standard. Whatever problems the town is facing, they're not caused by us."

"The river is dry," Elena said. "The aquifer is depleted. Wells are failing. And your facility is extracting millions of gallons of water every day."

"Legally extracted," Whitfield corrected. "From a sustainable source, according to every scientific study we've commissioned. The drought conditions affecting the region are a result of climate change, not our operations. I'm sorry the town is suffering, but scapegoating NeuralCore won't bring the rain."

Elena studied him, looking for cracks in the facade. He was good—smooth, confident, well-prepared. But there was something in his eyes, a flicker of something harder to define. Not guilt, exactly. More like calculation.

"The documents I've requested will tell a different story," Elena said.

Whitfield's smile didn't waver. "The documents will show exactly what I've told you. NeuralCore has nothing to hide." He paused. "But I wanted to meet with you for a reason, Ms. Vasquez. I wanted to offer you an alternative."

---

Elena waited.

"NeuralCore is expanding," Whitfield continued. "We're building new facilities, developing new technologies, creating new opportunities. We need people who understand environmental law, who can help us navigate the regulatory landscape while ensuring we remain good stewards of the resources we use."

"Are you offering me a job?"

"I'm offering you a career." Whitfield leaned forward. "You're talented, Ms. Vasquez. I've reviewed your record. But you're wasting your potential on cases like this—small-town disputes that go nowhere, clients who can't pay, battles you can't win. At NeuralCore, you could make a real difference. You could help shape the future instead of fighting against it."

Elena felt a chill that had nothing to do with the air conditioning. The offer was smooth, professional, almost flattering. But beneath it was something else—a warning dressed as an opportunity. *Join us*, Whitfield was saying. *Or be crushed*.

"I appreciate the offer," Elena said, keeping her voice even. "But I've already committed to representing River's Bend."

Whitfield's smile finally faded. "I was afraid you'd say that." He stood, signaling the end of the meeting. "I hope you'll reconsider, Ms. Vasquez. The legal landscape can be... treacherous for those who choose the wrong battles."

Elena rose as well. "I'll keep that in mind."

She walked out of the office with her head high, but her hands were shaking by the time she reached the elevator. Whitfield had confirmed what she'd suspected: NeuralCore wasn't just going to defend itself. It was going to attack.
