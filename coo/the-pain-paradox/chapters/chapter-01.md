# Chapter 01: The Performance

The crisis began at 9:15 AM, which was inconvenient because Sarah had been planning to feel something by noon. The client on the video screen was panicking, her colleagues were exchanging worried glances, and Sarah sat perfectly still, watching it all like a nature documentary. The conference room smelled of fresh coffee and the faint chemical tang of the cleaning solution used on the glass table. "Let's walk through this step by step," she said, and her voice didn't waver because it never did. In the glass reflection of the conference room wall, she could see her own face—calm, composed, the picture of professional competence. What the reflection didn't show was the thirty-four years of practice that had gone into that expression, or the hollow space behind it where something should have been.

"They're saying we breached contract," Marcus said, his voice climbing half an octave. "The penalty clause alone—"

"Show me the clause they're referencing." Sarah pulled her tablet closer, her fingers moving across the screen without tremor. The city skyline stretched behind her through the floor-to-ceiling windows, all sharp angles and morning light. Somewhere out there, people were feeling things. Stress, probably. Anxiety. Fear. She could see the physiological markers in her colleagues—the tightness around Jennifer's eyes, the way David's leg bounced under the table, the sheen of sweat on Marcus's forehead. She had learned to read these signs the way other people learned to read music.

"Here." Marcus highlighted the paragraph, his hand shaking slightly.

Sarah read it. Analyzed it. Cross-referenced it with three other sections of the contract. Her mind worked efficiently, unencumbered by the noise of emotion that she assumed clouded other people's thinking. "The clause is conditional," she said. "It only activates if we fail to respond within forty-eight hours of notification. When did they first contact us?"

"Tuesday," David said.

"And today is Thursday." Sarah looked at the client on the screen, whose face had gone blotchy red. "Mr. Hendricks, I see the confusion. Your team flagged the issue on Tuesday, but formal notification under the contract requires written notice to our legal department. That didn't happen until yesterday at 4 PM. We have until tomorrow at 4 PM to respond, which we will do. In the meantime, let me walk you through our proposed resolution."

She guided them through it. Step by step. Her voice remained level, her hands steady, her face arranged in what she had learned was an expression of "professional concern"—slight furrow between the eyebrows, lips pressed together but not too tightly. She had practiced this in the mirror for years, cataloguing which expressions made people trust her, which ones made them feel heard.

By 10:30, the crisis was resolved, the client was calmer, and her colleagues were looking at her with that mixture of admiration and unease she'd learned to recognize. "How do you stay so calm?" Jennifer asked, and Sarah smiled the smile she'd practiced in the mirror.

"Experience," she said, and it was true in a way—she had thirty-four years of experience not feeling what she should.

The office kitchen smelled like burnt coffee and the artificial lemon of the cleaning spray someone had used on the counter. The fluorescent lights hummed overhead, casting a harsh white glow that made everything look slightly clinical. Sarah was reaching for a mug—the ceramic cool and smooth under her fingertips—when she heard it: the crack of porcelain, the splash of liquid, and then something else. A sound that came from Jennifer's throat, sharp and involuntary.

Sarah turned. Jennifer was clutching her hand, blood welling up between her fingers where a shard of the broken mug had caught her palm. The red was vivid against the white porcelain fragments scattered across the counter, against the beige of Jennifer's cardigan where droplets had sprayed.

"Shit," Jennifer said, and her face had transformed—eyes squeezed shut, jaw clenched, forehead creased. Sarah watched the way Jennifer's whole body had curled inward, protective, alive with something Sarah had never experienced.

"Are you okay?" Sarah asked, and the words felt like lines in a play she'd been performing for thirty-four years.

"It's deep." Jennifer's voice was tight, strained. "I need a bandage. Or stitches. I don't know."

Sarah moved to the first aid kit on the wall, her movements efficient. She gathered gauze, antiseptic wipes, adhesive tape. When she returned to Jennifer, she took the injured hand gently and began to clean the wound.

"You're shaking," Jennifer said, and Sarah looked up, confused.

"I'm not."

"Oh." Jennifer blinked, winced as Sarah applied pressure. "I thought—I don't know. You just seem so calm. Most people would be freaking out."

"Would they?" Sarah kept her eyes on the wound. The blood was slowing, the cut cleaner now that she'd wiped away the coffee. It was a neat slice, about an inch long, not deep enough for stitches. She applied the gauze and began wrapping the tape.

"God, that stings," Jennifer said, and Sarah watched her face again—the way the pain moved through it like weather. "How are you so calm? Doesn't this bother you?"

"It bothers me that you're hurt," Sarah said, which was true in the way that saying "I love you" was true. She knew it should bother her. She knew the appropriate response was concern. She delivered the appropriate response.

Jennifer flexed her bandaged hand, winced again. "You're kind of amazing, you know that? I would be useless right now."

Sarah disposed of the bloody wipes, the broken mug fragments. She washed her hands at the sink, the water warm against her skin. She could feel temperature—that was one of the sensations her condition had spared her. Hot and cold, pressure and texture. Just not pain.

"Let me know if it starts throbbing," she said. "That could mean infection."

Jennifer nodded, still cradling her hand against her chest. "I will. And Sarah? Thanks." She smiled, and it was a real smile, warm and grateful, the kind of smile Sarah had learned to recognize but had never naturally produced.

Sarah watched Jennifer leave, watched the way she was still protecting her injured hand, still moving carefully around the kitchen as if the whole world had suddenly become sharp and dangerous. *That,* Sarah thought. *That's what I'm missing. That's what being real feels like.*

The garage was dark except for the glow of her phone, the cold concrete floor radiating chill through the soles of her shoes. Michael's voice came through like a lifeline she couldn't quite reach, warm and present through the tinny speakers. "How was your day?" he asked, and Sarah opened her mouth to answer with the truth—I watched someone bleed today and felt nothing—but what she said was, "Fine. The usual."

"Crisis meeting?"

"Resolved it."

"Of course you did." His voice was warm, admiring. She could hear the smile in it, the pride. "You're amazing, you know that? I don't know how you handle all that pressure."

Sarah gripped the steering wheel. The leather was cool under her palms, textured. She could feel that. "It's just work."

"It's not just work. You're brilliant at it." A pause. "I miss you."

"I was home this morning."

"I know. I still miss you." Another pause, longer this time. "Are you okay? You sound... I don't know. Distant."

Sarah looked at her reflection in the car window, ghostly in the darkness. "I'm fine. Just tired."

"You've been tired a lot lately."

"Work's been busy."

"Okay." His voice was gentle, accepting. It made something twist in her chest—not pain, she didn't feel pain, but something. A pressure. A weight. "I love you," he said.

The words settled into the silence between them. Sarah had studied love—the physiology of it, the psychology, the way it manifested in facial expressions and tone of voice and behavior. She knew what love was supposed to look like. She knew Michael loved her. She could see it in everything he did.

"I love you too," she said, and she meant it the way she meant everything—with the part of her that knew what those words should feel like, even if she couldn't feel them herself.

"See you tonight?"

"See you tonight."

She hung up and sat in the dark, the phone's glow fading as the screen went black. The garage hummed with the distant sound of other cars, other people going home to other lives. *Do I love him?* she wondered, not for the first time. *Or do I just know what love looks like? Is there a difference?*

She didn't have an answer. She never had an answer.


Every night, Sarah conducted an inventory. Not of possessions, but of damage. She stood in front of the bathroom mirror—the light harsh and clinical, the bulbs she had chosen specifically for their clarity—and catalogued what her body had endured without her knowledge: the bruises, the cuts, the burns she only discovered after they'd already scabbed over.

The bathroom light was harsh, clinical. She had chosen the bulbs specifically for this purpose, for the clarity they provided. She unbuttoned her blouse and examined her arms in the mirror. A small bruise on her left forearm, yellowing at the edges. She pressed her finger into it, watching the skin blanch white, then release. Nothing. No sensation, no confirmation that this mark was connected to her.

She removed her slacks and continued the inventory. A larger bruise on her right thigh, purple and green. She must have hit it against something—the corner of her desk, probably, or the edge of a table. She didn't remember. She never remembered. That was the nature of her condition: her body accumulated evidence of living, and she remained unaware until she looked.

Tonight there were seven: a bruise on her left thigh (corner of her desk, probably Tuesday), a cut on her right palm (unknown origin), five smaller marks scattered across her arms and ribs. She traced each one with her finger, pressing hard enough that a normal person would have felt pressure, would have felt the boundary between self and world. She felt only the texture of her own skin, the slight give of flesh over bone.

She turned off the light and stood in the darkness. *I am a ghost haunting my own life,* she thought, and the thought felt true in a way that nothing else did.


The advertisement appeared between articles about office productivity and stress management, as if the algorithm knew what she needed before she did. Sarah had been scrolling without really reading, her mind half on the day's events and half on nothing at all, when the words caught her attention.

**Participants needed for groundbreaking study on haptic feedback systems and sensory integration.**

She stopped scrolling. Her finger hovered over the screen.

**Researchers at the Neural Integration Institute are seeking individuals with sensory processing disorders to participate in a first-of-its-kind study. Participants will receive a neural interface designed to translate physical stimuli into conscious experience. Compensation provided.**

The words blurred, then sharpened. *Translate physical stimuli into conscious experience.* She read them again. And again.

**"Experience sensation." "Neural interface." "First-of-its-kind opportunity."**

Her heart was beating faster—she could feel that, the rhythm in her chest, the slight flush of warmth across her skin. Her body was responding even though she couldn't feel the emotion that should accompany it. Or maybe this was what hope felt like. She had read about hope. She had seen it in other people's faces. Maybe this was it, finally, after thirty-four years.

She thought of Jennifer's face, twisted in pain. She thought of Michael's "I love you" and her practiced response. She thought of the seven wounds she'd catalogued tonight, each one a message from a body she didn't inhabit.

*What if,* she thought. *What if I could finally be real?*

She clicked.
