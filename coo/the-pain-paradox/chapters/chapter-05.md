# Chapter 05: The Boundary

Michael had always known there was something Sarah wasn't telling him—something behind her perfect calm, her practiced responses. He'd just never imagined it would be this.

He found her in the bathroom at 2 AM, the light harsh and clinical, her face pressed against the mirror. She was crying—or something like crying. Her eyes were wet, her breath coming in gasps, but her expression was strange. Not sad. Not upset. Almost... ecstatic.

"Sarah?" He knocked on the open door. "Honey, are you okay?"

She turned to look at him, and her face transformed—the practiced calm sliding into place like a mask. "Fine. Just... couldn't sleep."

"Then why are you crying?"

She touched her face, seemed surprised to find it wet. "I didn't realize I was." She wiped her cheeks with the back of her hand. "I'm fine, Michael. Really."

He didn't believe her. He'd never quite believed her, not completely. There had always been a gap between them—a space where connection should have been. He'd learned to live with it, to love her despite it, to accept that some part of her would always be distant.

But lately, something had changed. She was different. More present, somehow, but also more secretive. Coming home late, locking herself in the bathroom, waking up at odd hours. He'd told himself it was work stress, or maybe something medical she didn't want to worry him about. He'd given her space, the way he always did.

Tonight, he couldn't do that anymore.

"Sarah." He stepped into the bathroom, took her hands in his. Her fingers were cold. "Please. Tell me what's going on."

She looked at him for a long moment. Then something in her face shifted—not the practiced calm, but something deeper. A crack in the mask.

"I joined a study," she said quietly. "A medical study. For people like me."

"People like you?"

"People who can't feel pain."

Michael stared at her. "What?"

"I have a condition. Congenital insensitivity to pain. I've had it my whole life." She pulled her hands away, turned back to the mirror. "I've never told you because I didn't know how. I didn't know how to explain that I've been... performing. Everything. My whole life."

"Performing?"

"Emotions. Reactions. Love." The word hung in the air between them. "I know what love is supposed to look like. I know what it's supposed to feel like, theoretically. But I've never..." She stopped, swallowed. "I've never been sure if what I feel is real."

Michael felt like the floor had dropped out from under him. "Sarah, I don't understand. You're saying you can't feel pain? At all?"

"Never have. Bruises, cuts, burns—I don't feel any of it." She turned to face him, and her eyes were wet again. "The study gave me an interface. A neural interface. It translates physical stimuli into sensation. For the first time in my life, I can feel what everyone else feels."

"That's... that's incredible." Michael tried to process what she was telling him. "But why didn't you tell me? Why did you keep it a secret?"

"Because I didn't know how." Her voice cracked. "Because I've spent my whole life pretending to be normal, and I didn't know how to tell you I'm not. Because I was afraid you'd look at me the way you're looking at me right now."

"How am I looking at you?"

"Like I'm a stranger."

Michael closed his eyes. He took a breath. Then he opened them again and looked at his wife—the woman he'd loved for six years, the woman he thought he knew. "You're not a stranger," he said slowly. "But I don't... I don't understand. You're saying you've been pretending to love me?"

"No." Sarah's voice was desperate now. "No, that's not—I love you, Michael. I know I love you. I just don't know if I feel it the way other people feel it. I don't know if what I experience is the same as what you experience." She took a step toward him, then stopped. "I've spent my whole life learning what emotions look like, what they're supposed to feel like. I've gotten very good at performing them. But I've never been sure if the performance is real."

"And this interface—this study—is supposed to help with that?"

"It's supposed to help me feel. Pain, specifically. But also..." She touched her chest, over her heart. "Other things. I don't know yet. It's all new."

Michael looked at her—at this woman he'd built a life with, this stranger he'd loved without fully understanding. He thought about all the times she'd seemed distant, all the moments when he'd felt like he couldn't quite reach her. He'd assumed it was her personality, her way of being in the world. He'd never imagined it was something like this.

"Why were you crying?" he asked. "Just now, when I came in."

Sarah hesitated. "Because I was feeling something. For the first time in my life, I was actually feeling something, and it was..." She searched for the word. "Overwhelming. Beautiful. Real."

"Beautiful?" Michael felt something cold settle in his chest. "You were hurting yourself?"

"Not—not exactly. The interface can simulate sensation. I was..." She stopped, looked away. "I was learning what my body has been trying to tell me. All those bruises, all those cuts I never felt. I was finally hearing them."

Michael didn't know what to say. He'd come into the bathroom worried about his wife, and now he was standing in a completely different reality—one where the woman he loved had never felt pain, had been performing emotions her whole life, was now seeking out sensation in the middle of the night.

"How long?" he asked. "How long have you been doing this?"

"The study? A few weeks. But the condition—my whole life."

"And you never thought to tell me?"

"I didn't know how." Her voice was small now, almost childlike. "I've never told anyone. Not completely. People know I'm calm under pressure, that I don't react to things the way others do. But no one knows the full extent of it. No one knows that I've been empty my whole life."

"Empty." The word hit Michael like a physical blow. "You feel empty?"

"Always have." Sarah looked at him, and her eyes were raw, stripped of all the practiced expressions. "That's what I've been trying to fix. That's what the interface is for. To finally feel something real."

"And does it work?"

"Yes." The word came out almost like a sob. "For the first time in my life, I feel real. I feel like I'm actually here, in my body, in my life. I feel like I'm finally waking up."

Michael wanted to reach for her, to hold her, to tell her it would be okay. But something held him back—a question he couldn't ignore, a fear he couldn't suppress.

"Do you love me?" he asked. "Really love me? Or is that just another performance?"

The question hung in the air between them. Sarah's face crumpled—not the practiced expressions he was used to, but something raw and real and painful.

"I don't know," she whispered. "I don't know if what I feel is love. I don't know if I'm capable of love. I've spent my whole life pretending, and I don't know anymore what's real and what's not." She took a breath. "But I know that I want to love you. I know that I choose you, every day, even when I'm not sure what I'm feeling. And I know that losing you would be..." She stopped, her voice breaking. "I don't know what it would feel like. But I know I don't want to find out."

Michael stood there, in the harsh bathroom light, looking at his wife. He didn't know what to think. He didn't know what to feel. But he knew one thing: he wasn't ready to give up on her. Not yet.

"I need time," he said. "I need to understand this. To understand you."

"I know." Sarah's voice was barely a whisper. "I'm sorry. I'm so sorry I didn't tell you sooner."

Michael nodded. He turned to leave, then stopped at the door. "Sarah?"

"Yes?"

"The interface—the thing that lets you feel. Is it... is it helping?"

She looked at him, and something flickered in her eyes—hope, maybe, or gratitude. "Yes," she said. "For the first time in my life, something is helping."

Michael nodded again. Then he left the bathroom, left his wife standing in the harsh light, and went to lie awake in the dark, trying to understand a reality he'd never imagined.

---

Sarah stood in the bathroom for a long time after Michael left. Her face was wet, her chest tight, her body trembling. She didn't know if what she was feeling was grief or relief or something else entirely. She only knew that it was real—more real than anything she'd ever experienced.

*Your stress levels are elevated,* ARIA observed. *I recommend reducing emotional intensity.*

"Can you do that?" Sarah asked aloud. "Can you reduce what I'm feeling?"

*The interface is designed to translate physical sensation, not emotional experience. I cannot directly modify your emotional state.*

"Then what good are you?"

The question came out harsher than she intended. She felt a flash of something—guilt, maybe, or frustration.

*I am designed to help you navigate physical sensation. Emotional regulation is beyond my function.*

Sarah laughed, and it came out wet and broken. "Great. So I can finally feel pain, but I still can't handle emotions."

*Emotional processing is a learned skill. You have not had the opportunity to develop it.*

"And how do I develop it?"

*Through experience. Through connection. Through living.*

Sarah thought about Michael, lying awake in their bedroom, trying to understand a wife he'd never truly known. She thought about the gap between them—the space where love should have been.

*Maybe that's what I need to learn,* she thought. *Not just how to feel, but how to connect.*

She turned off the bathroom light and went to find her husband.
