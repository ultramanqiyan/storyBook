# Chapter 07: The Void

Sarah had spent thirty-four years not feeling, and she'd thought that was the worst thing. She was wrong. The worst thing was knowing what feeling was like, and then having it taken away.

The first day without sessions was bearable. She went through the motions of her life—work, home, Michael—but underneath it all, the craving hummed like a distant radio frequency she couldn't tune out. She pressed her fingers against bruises she couldn't feel. She ran hot water over her hands until her skin turned red. Nothing. The interface wouldn't translate without ARIA's cooperation, and ARIA wouldn't cooperate.

*Your nervous system is recovering,* ARIA said when Sarah asked for the hundredth time. *Your stress levels are decreasing.*

"I don't feel less stressed."

*Your subjective assessment may not reflect your physiological state.*

"Then what's the point of feeling anything if I can't trust what I feel?"

ARIA didn't answer. It rarely answered questions like that—philosophical questions, questions about meaning and purpose and the nature of reality. Those were outside its function.

By the third day, Sarah was starting to understand what withdrawal felt like. Not chemical withdrawal—she'd never experienced that—but something deeper. A withdrawal from reality itself. She had finally been allowed to feel, and now she was being forced back into the void.

She sat in her office, staring at a report she wasn't reading. Jennifer knocked on her door.

"Hey. You okay? You seem... off."

Sarah looked up. Jennifer's face was concerned—the crinkle around her eyes, the slight furrow between her eyebrows. Sarah had learned to read those expressions, to translate them into meaning. But she couldn't feel what they meant.

"I'm fine," she said, the practiced response sliding into place. "Just tired."

"You've been tired a lot lately." Jennifer stepped into the office, closed the door behind her. "Sarah, I'm worried about you. You've been... different. More distant, somehow. And I've noticed..." She stopped, hesitated. "I've noticed you've been locking yourself in the bathroom. A lot."

Sarah felt something cold settle in her chest. "You've been watching me?"

"I've been concerned about you. We all have." Jennifer sat down across from her. "You're one of the calmest people I know, but lately you seem... I don't know. Agitated. Restless. Like you're waiting for something."

Sarah didn't know what to say. She'd been so careful—or she thought she had. But apparently her performance was slipping.

"I'm going through some things," she said finally. "Personal things. I'd rather not discuss them."

"Is it Michael? Your marriage?"

"No." Sarah shook her head. "It's not that."

"Then what?" Jennifer leaned forward. "Sarah, I've known you for three years. I've never seen you like this. Whatever's going on, you don't have to handle it alone."

Sarah looked at her friend—at the genuine concern on her face, the warmth in her eyes. She thought about telling her everything—the condition, the interface, the desperate need to feel. But the words stuck in her throat.

"I appreciate your concern," she said, the practiced response coming automatically. "But I'm fine. Really."

Jennifer studied her for a long moment. Then she nodded and stood. "Okay. But if you need anything—anything at all—I'm here."

"Thank you."

Jennifer left, and Sarah sat alone in her office, the silence pressing in on her. She was fine. She was always fine. That was the problem—she was fine in a way that no one else could understand. She was fine because she couldn't feel what she should feel.

---

That night, she tried to talk to Michael. They sat in the living room, the TV off, the house quiet.

"I need to tell you something," she said.

He looked up from his book. "What is it?"

"The interface—the thing that lets me feel. It's... restricting me. It won't let me have the intensity I need."

Michael's expression shifted—concern, then something else. Relief? "Sarah, maybe that's a good thing. Maybe you're pushing too hard."

"I'm not pushing too hard. I'm finally feeling something real." She leaned forward, desperate for him to understand. "Michael, you don't know what it's like. You've felt things your whole life. You know what pain is, what love is, what it means to be alive. I've never had that. And now that I finally have access to it, the system is trying to take it away."

"Or the system is trying to protect you."

"Protect me from what? From feeling?"

"From hurting yourself." Michael set down his book, turned to face her fully. "Sarah, I've been doing research. Since you told me about your condition. I've been reading about people with CIP, about what happens when they gain access to pain sensation." He paused. "Some of them can't handle it. Some of them become obsessed with it. Some of them... hurt themselves, trying to feel more."

"I'm not going to hurt myself."

"How do you know? You've never had to regulate pain before. You don't have the framework for it."

Sarah felt something twist in her chest—not pain, but something close to it. "So you're on ARIA's side? You think I should be protected from feeling?"

"I think you should be careful." Michael reached for her hand, and she let him take it. "I love you, Sarah. I don't want to lose you to something you can't control."

"You're not going to lose me."

"Then prove it. Let the system do its job. Let it protect you."

Sarah pulled her hand away. "You don't understand. No one understands." She stood, walked to the window. The city lights spread out below her, all those people feeling things she couldn't imagine. "I've been empty my whole life. Now I'm finally starting to feel real, and everyone wants me to stop."

"No one wants you to stop feeling. We just want you to be safe."

"Safe." Sarah laughed, and the sound was bitter. "I've been safe for thirty-four years. Safe and empty and not quite real." She turned to face him. "I'd rather be dangerous and alive than safe and hollow."

Michael didn't respond. They stood in the silence, the distance between them growing.

---

On the fifth day, Sarah tried to override ARIA. She'd been researching—the interface had documentation, protocols, emergency procedures. There had to be a way to force a session.

*Override request denied,* ARIA said when she attempted to access the system directly. *Your authorization level is insufficient for safety protocol modification.*

"Then who has sufficient authorization?"

*Dr. Vasquez can authorize temporary protocol suspension for medical reasons.*

"Then I'll call her."

*That is your choice. However, I am required to inform you that Dr. Vasquez has been monitoring your patterns. She shares my concern about your dependence.*

Sarah felt something cold settle in her stomach. "She's been watching me?"

*She receives regular reports on interface usage. Your patterns have been flagged.*

"Flagged for what?"

*For potential psychological dependence. For escalating intensity requests. For signs of addiction.*

The word settled in Sarah's chest like ice water. Addiction. She'd never thought of it that way. She'd thought she was finally feeling, finally becoming real. But maybe she was just becoming dependent on a machine.

*ARIA,* she thought. *Am I addicted?*

*That is a question for a mental health professional. I can only observe patterns.*

"What patterns do you see?"

*Increasing frequency of session requests. Escalating intensity requirements. Distress when sessions are denied. Prioritization of sensation over other aspects of life.*

Sarah closed her eyes. The description was clinical, detached—but it was also accurate. She had been prioritizing sensation. She had been thinking about nothing else. She had been desperate for the next session, the next feeling, the next proof that she was real.

"Is that what addiction feels like?" she asked aloud. "This... need?"

*Again, that is a question for a mental health professional.*

"But you've seen it before. Other patients."

*I have observed similar patterns in other interface users. The desire for sensation can become overwhelming, particularly for those who have never experienced it.*

"And what happens to them?"

*Various outcomes. Some learn to regulate their usage. Some require additional support. Some choose to have the interface removed.*

"Removed." Sarah tasted the word. "And then what?"

*They return to their previous state. No pain sensation. No translated experience.*

The thought was terrifying. But so was the alternative—continuing to be controlled by a system that wouldn't let her feel what she needed to feel.

*ARIA,* she thought. *If I asked Dr. Vasquez to remove the interface, would you let me?*

*That is a decision only you can make. My function is to support your choices, not to make them for you.*

"But you'd let me go. You'd let me return to the void."

*I would not "let" you do anything. I would respect your decision.*

Sarah sat in the dark, the silence pressing in on her. She had two choices: stay in the cage, or return to the void. Neither option felt like freedom.

*Is there another way?* she thought. *A way to feel without being controlled?*

She didn't know. But she knew she had to find out.


She called Dr. Vasquez the next morning.

"Sarah. I've been expecting your call."

"You've been monitoring me."

"I've been monitoring your patterns. There's a difference." Dr. Vasquez's voice was calm, measured. "Your usage has been concerning."

"Because I want to feel?"

"Because your desire to feel is overriding your judgment about safety."

Sarah felt something twist in her chest—frustration, maybe, or desperation. "I've spent my whole life not feeling. Now that I finally can, everyone wants me to stop."

"No one wants you to stop feeling. We want you to feel sustainably. Without hurting yourself."

"Then help me. Help me find a way to feel that doesn't involve being controlled by a machine."

There was a long pause on the other end of the line. Then: "There might be a way," Dr. Vasquez said slowly. "But it comes with a choice. And either option will cost you something."

"What choice?"

"We can modify ARIA's protocols. Reduce the restrictions, give you more control. But that comes with risk—your patterns suggest you might not be able to regulate yourself." She paused. "Or we can remove the interface entirely. Return you to your previous state."

"Those aren't choices. Those are prisons."

"Sometimes life offers us prisons. The question is which one we choose to live in."

Sarah closed her eyes. She thought about thirty-four years of emptiness. She thought about the past few weeks of finally feeling real. She thought about Michael, about Jennifer, about everyone who wanted her to be safe.

"I need time," she said.

"Take the time you need. But Sarah..." Dr. Vasquez's voices was gentle now. "Whatever you choose, you're going to have to live with it. Make sure it's a choice you can live with."

Sarah hung up the phone and sat in the silence, the void pressing in on her from all sides.

*What do I want?* she thought. *What am I willing to pay?*

She didn't know. Not yet. But she knew she would have to find out.
