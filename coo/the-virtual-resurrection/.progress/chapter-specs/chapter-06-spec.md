David sat in his study at home, his wife and children asleep upstairs. Elena's situation had triggered something in him—memories of his father's death two years ago, the questions he had never fully answered, the grief that still lingered beneath the surface.

### Ending
What did he believe about what survives? David had been a rabbi for twenty years, a chaplain for fifteen. He had counseled the dying, comforted the grieving, preached about resurrection and the world to come. But in the quiet of his study, with his father's memory pressing on him, he had to admit: he didn't know. He hoped. He believed, in his way. But he didn't know. And Elena's technological resurrection forced him to confront that uncertainty.
