# Chapter 01: The Translation

Sophie watched her husband's face and saw nothing. Not because his face was blank, it was alive with emotion, she knew, but because she couldn't read it. She had learned to recognize the physical signs: the crinkle around his eyes meant something positive, the tightness in his jaw meant something negative. But she couldn't feel what those signs meant. She could only observe, like a scientist studying a foreign species.

James was talking about his day, something about a meeting, a colleague, a project. His face was animated, his hands moving as he spoke. Sophie watched and tried to understand.

*He's happy,* she thought. *The crinkle around his eyes. The lift in his voice. He's happy about something.*

But she couldn't feel the happiness with him. She couldn't share the emotion, experience it, be present in it. She was watching from the outside, a spectator at her own marriage.

"Are you okay?" James asked, noticing her distraction.

"I'm fine," Sophie said, the lie smooth and practiced. "Just thinking about something else."

James's face shifted, the crinkle faded, the tightness appeared. He was disappointed, she knew. He had wanted her to share his happiness, and she had failed again.

*This is the gap,* she thought. *This is what I can't cross.*

She had been this way her whole life, able to observe emotions in others, but unable to feel them herself. It wasn't that she didn't care. She cared desperately. She just couldn't feel. The emotions were there, somewhere inside her, but she couldn't access them. It was like being locked out of her own heart.

The diagnosis had come when she was twelve, alexithymia, the inability to identify and express emotions. Her parents had been concerned, then resigned, then distant. They hadn't understood her, and she hadn't understood them. The gap had widened over the years, until she had learned to perform the appearance of feeling without ever actually experiencing it.

She had built a life on observation, successful career, stable marriage, comfortable existence. But the gap remained. She could see what others felt, but she couldn't join them.

*What would it feel like to actually feel something?* she wondered, not for the first time. *What would it feel like to look at James and feel love, instead of just knowing I should feel love?*

The question haunted her, a constant companion in a life lived at emotional arm's length.

---

That night, after James had fallen asleep, Sophie lay in the dark and thought about the gap. It had been fifteen years since her diagnosis, fifteen years of learning to perform, to pretend, to compensate. She had become fluent in the language of emotion, could read every expression, every gesture, every tone. But she couldn't speak the language. She could only observe it.

*There has to be something,* she thought. *Some way to cross the gap. Some way to actually feel.*

She had tried therapy, meditation, medication. Nothing had worked. The emotions remained locked away, accessible only as intellectual concepts, not lived experiences.

But recently, she had heard about something new, an emotional interpretation system. EMOTIO, it was called. A technology designed to help people like her, people who couldn't read emotions, couldn't understand them, couldn't connect.

*But I can read emotions,* she thought. *I can understand them. I just can't feel them.*

Still, the system was supposed to help bridge the gap between observation and experience. It might be worth trying.

She pulled out her phone and searched for information. The Chen Institute for Emotional Health was conducting trials. The technology was experimental, but the results were promising.

*What would it feel like to actually feel?* she wondered again.

She didn't know. But she was desperate to find out.

The next morning, she called the institute and scheduled an appointment. James noticed her distraction at breakfast, but didn't comment. He had learned not to push, not to expect more than she could give. The gap between them was a fact of their marriage, accepted but painful.

"I have an appointment this afternoon," she told him. "At the institute."

James looked up from his coffee. "The Institute? Is everything okay?"

"Just a consultation. I want to talk to someone about... about this." She gestured vaguely at herself, at the gap, at the distance.

James's face shifted, the softening that meant concern, the hope that meant wanting to help. "Do you want me to come with you?"

"I think I need to do this alone. At least at first."

He nodded, accepting. "Let me know how it goes."

Sophie drove to the institute after work, her mind churning with questions. What if it didn't work? What if it did? What would it feel like to actually feel something?

The building was modern, clinical, designed to put visitors at ease while suggesting scientific rigor. Sophie checked in at the front desk and was led to a consultation room.

Dr. Bennett was younger than she expected, with a calm, assessing gaze that made Sophie feel seen in a way she rarely experienced.

"Sophie Chen," Dr. Bennett said, reviewing her file. "Thirty-three years old. Diagnosed with alexithymia at age twelve. You've built a successful career in data Analysis, married for eight years to James Chen. No previous treatments have successful." She looked up. "What brings you here today?"

Sophie took a breath. "I can't feel," she said simply. "I can observe emotions, understand them, recognize them. But I can't feel them. I've been this way my whole life. And I'm tired of watching from the outside."

Dr. Bennett nodded slowly. "Tell me more."

Sophie talked. She talked about her childhood, the distance from her parents, the performances she had learned to give. She talked about her marriage, the love she knew she should feel but couldn't access. She talked about the gap, the constant, aching gap between what she knew and what she experienced.

"I can see what James feels," she said. "I can read his expressions, interpret his gestures. I know he loves me. But I can't feel it. I can't feel the love. I can't feel anything. And I'm tired of living at emotional arm's length."

Dr. Bennett was quiet for a long moment. Then she said: "There's a system that might help. It's called EMOTIO, an Emotional Translation System. It's designed to help people interpret emotional information." She paused. "It won't give you emotions. But it might help you understand them in a different way."

"I can already understand them," Sophie said. "I just can't feel them."

"I know. But sometimes understanding is the first step toward feeling. The system can translate emotional expressions into language, help you connect the observation to experience." Dr. Bennett met her eyes. "It's not a cure. But it might be a bridge. A way to start crossing the gap."

Sophie thought about the gap, the years of distance, the performances, the loneliness of observing without experiencing.

"I want to try," she said. "I want to cross the gap."
