# Chapter 08: The Bridge

One year after beginning treatment, Sophie sat across from James at dinner. She looked at his face, the crinkle around his eyes, the softness of his mouth, and felt something. Not the overwhelming simulation that EMOTIO had once provided, but something quieter, more fragile, more real. *This is love,* she thought. *This is what it actually feels like.* It was small, but it was hers.

The journey had been long and uncertain. Sophie had moved from emotional dormancy to borrowed simulation to small, fragile authentic feeling. The gap hadn't closed completely, she still experienced emotions at a distance, still felt them more quietly than others seemed to. But the distance was smaller now. The gap was narrowing.

"I've been thinking about something," James said, his expression thoughtful.

"What?"

"About the simulation. About whether you still need it."

Sophie considered the question. She had been using the simulation less and less, only for significant emotional experiences now. Most of the time, she relied on her own small, fragile authentic responses.

"I don't know," she admitted. "Sometimes I feel like I need it. Like the small feelings aren't enough. Other times, I feel like the simulation is... getting in the way. Like it's louder than my own voice."

"What does Dr. Bennett say?"

"She says the decision is mine. The simulation can continue to provide emotional experience, but it might also be preventing my natural pathways from fully developing."

James nodded slowly. "What do you want?"

Sophie thought about the question. What did she want? She wanted to feel authentically. She wanted to experience emotions that were hers, not borrowed. But she was also afraid, afraid of losing the only emotional experience she had ever known.

"I want to try," she said. "Without the simulation. At least for a while. See what I can feel on my own."

James's expression shifted, the softening that meant support. Sophie didn't need EMOTIO to translate it. She could see it, sense it, feel it.

"I'll support whatever you decide," he said.

---

"I want to stop using the simulation," Sophie told Dr. Bennett the following week.

Dr. Bennett's expression was thoughtful. "Completely?"

"Yes. At least for a trial period. I want to see what I can feel on my own."

"That's a significant decision. Without the simulation, you'll be relying entirely on your natural emotional responses. They may be smaller, quieter, less intense than what you're used to."

"I know. But I want to know what's mine. I want to feel authentically, even if it's small."

Dr. Bennett nodded slowly. "I can prepare a transition protocol. Gradual reduction of simulation frequency until you're functioning entirely on your own. But Sophie, I want you to understand: you may feel less. The void may return. Are you prepared for that?"

Sophie thought about the void, the years of silence, the gap she had spent her life trying to cross. She thought about the small, fragile feelings she had begun to experience. She thought about the simulation, the overwhelming waves of borrowed emotion that had shown her what feeling could be.

"I'm prepared," she said. "I want to know what's real."

---

The transition was gradual. Over six weeks, Sophie reduced her use of the simulation from occasional to rare to never. Each reduction brought a new awareness, of how much she had relied on the borrowed feelings, of how small her authentic responses still were.

The first week without simulation was the hardest. Sophie moved through her days feeling... less. The small authentic emotions were there, warmth at James's smile, flutters at his touch, aches at sad stories. But they were quieter than the simulation had been, more fragile, more uncertain.

*Is this enough?* she wondered. *Is this small, fragile thing enough?*

She didn't know. But she was determined to find out.


"How do you feel?" James asked one evening, a month into the simulation-free period.

Sophie considered the question. How did she feel? She felt... something. Not the overwhelming simulation, but something quieter.

"I feel... content," she said. "I think. It's small, but it's there."

James's expression shifted, the softening that meant happiness. Sophie didn't need EMOTIO to translate it. She could see it, sense it, feel it.

"Is it enough?" he asked.

Sophie thought about the question. Was it enough? The small feelings were nothing like the overwhelming simulation. They were quiet, fragile, uncertain. But they were also real, hers, not borrowed.

"It's enough," she said. "It's small, but it's mine. And that makes it enough."

James reached out and took her hand. Sophie felt the warmth of his skin, the pressure of his fingers. And beneath the sensation, something else, a flutter that was becoming familiar, the small authentic response that was hers alone.

*This is what I wanted,* she thought. *Not the simulation. Not the borrowed feelings. This. This small, authentic, fragile thing that is mine.*


The months passed. Sophie continued to live without the simulation, continued to experience the small, fragile authentic emotions that were becoming more familiar. They were quiet, nothing like the overwhelming waves that EMOTIO had provided. But they were also real, and that made them precious.

She learned to appreciate the smallness, the quiet warmth of contentment, the fragile flutter of joy, the tiny pang of sadness. These were her emotions, in her voice, at her volume. Not borrowed, not simulated. Just hers.

"I'm proud of you," Dr. Bennett said during their final session. "You've made remarkable progress. Your emotional system is functioning authentically."

"Will it ever be... normal? Whatever that means?"

Dr. Bennett smiled. "What's normal? Everyone experiences emotions differently, some more intensely, some more quietly. Your authentic responses may always be smaller than average. But they're yours. And that's what matters."

Sophie nodded. She had spent her whole life unable to feel. Now she was feeling something, small, fragile, but real. And that was more than she had ever had before.


That night, Sophie sat with James, watching the sunset. The simulation was gone, but she didn't miss it. She could see the beauty of the colors, feel the warmth of the light. And beneath it all, something else, a small, fragile, authentic response that was hers alone.

"This is beautiful," she said.

James looked at her, his expression soft. "You feel it?"

Sophie nodded. "Small. But real. Not borrowed. Not simulated. Just... mine."

James smiled, the crinkle around his eyes that meant joy. Sophie didn't need EMOTIO to translate it. She could see it, sense it, feel it.

"I love you," she said.

"I love you too," he replied.

And Sophie felt something, a warmth that was becoming familiar. Not overwhelming, not intense. Just small, fragile, real. And enough.
