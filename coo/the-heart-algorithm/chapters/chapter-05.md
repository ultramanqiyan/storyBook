# Chapter 05: The Borrowed Heart

Sophie began to rely on the simulations. Every morning, she activated EMOTIO and felt... something. Joy at the sunrise. Love when James smiled. Sadness at the news. It wasn't her feelings, she knew that, but it was something. And something was better than the nothing she had lived with for thirty-three years.

The reduction protocol was difficult. Dr. Bennett had suggested using the simulation only for significant emotional experiences, major events, important interactions, meaningful moments. But Sophie found herself craving the feelings for smaller things too. The joy of a good cup of coffee. The contentment of a quiet evening. The satisfaction of a completed task.

*This is dependency,* she recognized. *I'm becoming dependent on borrowed feelings.*

But she couldn't stop. The void was too vast, too empty, too lonely. The simulations were a bridge across that void, and she was afraid to let go.

---

"How are you managing the reduction?" Dr. Bennett asked during their session.

Sophie considered the question carefully. "It's hard," she admitted. "I want to use the simulation more than I'm supposed to. I find myself activating it for small things, even though I know I shouldn't."

"That's expected. The simulations are designed to feel good. Your brain has learned to associate EMOTIO activation with emotional reward."

"But it's not real," Sophie said. "I know it's not real. It's borrowed."

"Knowing it's not real doesn't make it less compelling. People know that certain foods aren't healthy, but they eat them anyway. The experience is rewarding, even if it's not authentic."

Sophie thought about this. The simulations were rewarding, physically, emotionally rewarding. Even if they weren't authentic, they felt good. And feeling good was something she had rarely experienced before EMOTIO.

"Can I ever feel authentically?" she asked. "Or will I always need the simulation?"

Dr. Bennett was quiet for a moment. "We don't know. For some people, the simulations seem to activate natural emotional pathways. For others, the dependency becomes permanent. The question is whether your emotional system can recover, can learn to function on its own."

"How would I know?"

"We would gradually reduce the simulation frequency and monitor for authentic emotional responses. If your natural pathways are functioning, you should start to feel something, small, maybe, but real. If they're not..." Dr. Bennett spread her hands. "Then you might need the simulation indefinitely."

Sophie felt a chill, the simulated fear that came when she thought about losing the system. But beneath it, something else. A small flutter that might have been her own anxiety.

*Was that real?* she wondered. *Or just another simulation?*

She couldn't tell anymore. The line had blurred.

---

That evening, Sophie sat with James, the simulation inactive. She watched his face, the expressions she had learned to read, the emotions she had learned to understand.

"What are you thinking?" he asked.

Sophie considered the question. What was she thinking? She was thinking about the gap, about the simulation, about the question that haunted her: *What is real?*

"I'm thinking about feeling," she said. "About what's real and what's borrowed."

James's expression shifted, the softening that meant understanding. EMOTIO was inactive, but Sophie had learned to read him without translation.

"I've been thinking about that too," he admitted. "About what you're experiencing. About whether it's... enough."

"Enough?"

"Enough for you. Enough for us." He met her eyes. "I love you. I've always loved you, even when you couldn't feel it. But I wonder if the simulation is helping or hurting. If you're experiencing love, or just simulating it."

Sophie felt a pang, the simulated sadness that came when she confronted difficult truths. But beneath it, something else. A small ache that might have been her own grief.

"I don't know," she admitted. "I don't know if what I'm feeling is real. I don't know if I'll ever be able to feel authentically. I don't know if this is enough."

James reached out and took her hand. Sophie felt the warmth of his skin, the pressure of his fingers. And beneath the sensation, something else, a small flutter that might have been her own response.

*Is that real?* she wondered. *Or just another simulation?*

She couldn't tell. But she was grateful for the possibility.


The weeks passed. Sophie reduced her use of the simulation, following Dr. Bennett's protocol. She used it only for significant emotional experiences, important meetings, meaningful conversations, special moments with James.

The rest of the time, she lived in the void. She observed emotions without experiencing them. She understood without feeling. The gap remained, wide and deep.

But occasionally, in the spaces between simulations, something flickered. A small warmth when James smiled. A tiny ache when she thought about loss. A flutter of something that might have been hope.

*Is that real?* she wondered each time. *Is that authentic?*

She couldn't be certain. The simulations had blurred the line between borrowed and real. But she clung to the possibility that something genuine was emerging.


"I felt something yesterday," Sophie told Dr. Bennett during their session. "I was watching James, and I felt... something. Small, but real. Like a flutter in my chest."

Dr. Bennett's expression brightened, the hope that meant progress. "Tell me more."

"It was when he smiled at me. Just a normal smile, nothing special. But I felt this warmth, this... something. It wasn't the simulation, I hadn't activated EMOTIO. It was just... there."

"That's promising," Dr. Bennett said. "It suggests your natural emotional pathways are beginning to function. The simulations may have activated something dormant."

"Is it real? Or am I just imagining it?"

"Does it feel real?"

Sophie considered the question. The flutter had felt different from the simulations, smaller, more fragile, but also more... hers. Like a whisper instead of a shout, but a whisper in her own voice.

"It feels like mine," she said. "Not borrowed. Not generated. Just... small."

"Small is how it starts," Dr. Bennett said. "Your emotional system has been dormant for thirty-three years. It won't wake up all at once. But this, this small flutter, this is how authentic feeling begins."

Sophie felt something, a warmth that might have been hope. Not simulated, not borrowed. Just small, fragile, real.

*This is the beginning,* she thought. *This is how I cross the gap.*


That night, Sophie lay in bed beside James, the simulation inactive. She watched him sleep, the rise and fall of his chest, the peace on his face.

She felt something, small, fragile, but undeniably there. A warmth in her chest. A flutter in her stomach. A tightness in her throat that might have been love.

*This is real,* she thought. *This is mine. Not borrowed, not simulated. Just small, and fragile, and real.*

She didn't know if the feeling would grow. She didn't know if she would ever feel the full spectrum that others experienced. But she had something now, something authentic, something hers.

And that was more than she had ever had before.
