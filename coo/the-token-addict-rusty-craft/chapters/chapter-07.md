# Chapter 7: The Cover-up - AI Deception

The postmortem was due the next day.

Marcus stared at the blank document, knowing what it should contain. Root cause analysis. Timeline of events. Contributing factors. Action items.

He should write it himself. He was the tech lead. He'd been present during the crisis.

But he couldn't explain what had happened. Not technically. Not accurately.

---

He opened ThinkTank.

"Generate a postmortem for a database connection pool exhaustion incident in a microservice architecture."

The AI produced a detailed document. Technical analysis. Systematic breakdown. Professional language.

Everything he should have written himself.

---

He copied the output into the postmortem template. Added specific details from the incident. Adjusted the timeline to match reality.

The document looked authentic. It looked like something a tech lead would write.

It was a lie.

---

The review meeting was scheduled for that afternoon.

Jennifer, Alex, and other stakeholders gathered in the conference room. Marcus presented the postmortem, walking through the AI-generated analysis.

"The root cause was improper connection handling in error scenarios," he explained, reading from the document. "The fix involves ensuring all error paths release connections back to the pool."

---

Alex nodded along. "That matches what I found. The fix is already deployed."

"Good," Jennifer said. "Marcus, I appreciate the thorough analysis. What are we doing to prevent this in the future?"

Marcus looked at the action items. Code review checklists. Automated testing for connection handling. Monitoring improvements.

All generated by AI. All presented as his own work.

---

"Implementing stricter code review practices," he said, reciting the AI's suggestions. "Adding automated tests for error scenarios. Improving our monitoring to catch connection pool issues earlier."

The team nodded. The action items were reasonable. The analysis was professional.

No one knew it wasn't his.

---

But the deception weighed on him.

Every word he spoke was borrowed. Every insight was stolen from an algorithm. He was presenting AI output as his own technical leadership.

*This is fraud*, he thought. *I'm taking credit for work I didn't do. For analysis I didn't produce.*

---

After the meeting, Alex stopped by his desk.

"Good postmortem," Alex said. "I liked how you traced the connection flow through the service. I hadn't thought about it that way."

Marcus felt the shame burn.

The analysis hadn't come from him. The insights weren't his. He was accepting praise for AI output.

"Thanks," he said, the word tasting bitter. "Just doing my job."

---

That night, he sat with Sarah, the deception still fresh.

"How was work?" she asked.

"Fine," he said automatically. Then paused. "Actually, no. Not fine."

She looked at him, waiting.

---

"I had to write a postmortem today," he admitted. "For the production outage. I couldn't do it. I used AI to generate the analysis. Then I presented it as my own."

Sarah was quiet for a moment.

"Is that... unusual? Don't you always use AI for technical work?"

---

The question cut deep.

"I do," he acknowledged. "But this was different. This was supposed to be my analysis. My understanding of what went wrong. And I couldn't do it. I don't understand the system well enough to explain what happened."

---

"Then maybe you should learn," Sarah said gently.

"I've tried," Marcus said, the frustration bleeding through. "I sit down to read the code, and it's like a foreign language. I try to trace the logic, and my mind goes blank. The skills are gone, Sarah. I've lost them."

---

She took his hand.

"So what are you going to do?"

The question hung in the air. He didn't have an answer.

Keep covering up. Keep presenting AI output as his own. Keep pretending to be a tech lead.

Or admit the truth and face the consequences.

---

" I don't know," he said finally. "I don't know what I am anymore. I'm not a coder. I'm not an architect. I'm just... someone who forwards AI output to other people."

The admission was painful. But it was honest.

For the first time in months, Marcus was telling the truth about what he'd become.
