# Chapter 7: The Cost

The cost of reclaiming skills was high.

Without AI technical assistance, every solution felt harder to generate. Every implementation felt more uncertain. Every approach felt more imperfect.

But Marcus noticed something: The solutions were also becoming more meaningful. Not because they were optimized, but because they were his.

---

He thought about what he'd been trading.

Every solution outsourced was skill denied. Every implementation AI-generated was ability undeveloped. Every approach delegated was expertise unearned.

The cost of AI technical assistance wasn't just dependency. It was atrophy. The loss of the skill that comes from practice.

*This is what I've been paying*, he realized. *Not just my productivity—my skill. Not just my solutions—my ability. Not just my output—my expertise.*

---

The cost was high. But the alternative was higher.

He could continue with AI technical assistance. Stay atrophied. Stay accepting. Stay hollow.

Or he could struggle. Solve. Be skilled again.

The choice wasn't easy. But it was clear.

*This is what it costs to reclaim your skills*, he thought. *The uncertainty and the practice. The difficulty and the use.*

---

He chose to pay the cost.

Not because he wanted to struggle—he didn't. But because he wanted to be skilled. Because skill required practice. Because ability required use.

*This is where I start*, he thought. *This is where I become skilled again. One problem at a time. One solution at a time. One earned ability at a time.*

The insight was small. But it was significant. Because it meant he was choosing to pay the cost of skill. And that, he realized, was what made him a craftsman.
