# Chapter 3: The Delegation

The team grew under Marcus's leadership.

Eight engineers, each with their own specialties. Alex on backend. Priya on frontend. David on infrastructure. Others filling gaps as needed.

And Marcus? He delegated.

---

"Alex, I need you to implement the payment service," he said during standup. "Use the architecture we discussed. Let me know if you hit any blockers."

"Got it," Alex replied. "Should I start with the core service or the API layer?"

Marcus felt the familiar hesitation. He didn't know the best approach. He hadn't thought about it.

"Start with what feels right," he said, covering. "You have good instincts."

---

The delegation became his default mode.

Technical decisions? Let the team figure it out. Code reviews? Trust the AI's suggestions. Architecture questions? Ask ThinkTank.

He was a conductor, not a player. A director, not an actor.

*This is what leadership looks like*, he told himself. *Empowering others to do the work.*

---

But the relief came with a cost.

Every time he delegated a technical decision, he felt a small pang. Not guilt—something harder to name. A sense of distance from the craft he'd once loved.

*When was the last time I wrote code?* he wondered. *When was the last time I solved a problem with my own hands?*

---

He tried to remember.

Before CodeForge. Before ThinkTank. Before the promotion. He'd been a coder then. Someone who built things with his own skills.

Now he was... what? A manager of AI output? A curator of algorithmic decisions?

*That's still valuable*, he insisted. *The team needs direction. I provide that.*

---

Jennifer noticed the pattern.

"You're doing great work," she said during their one-on-one. "The team's velocity is up. Morale is high. You're exactly the leader we need."

"Thank you," Marcus said, accepting the praise.

"But I want to make sure you're staying technical," she continued. "A tech lead should still be hands-on. Can you tell me about the last piece of code you wrote?"

---

The question hung in the air.

Marcus's mind went blank. The last code he'd written... he couldn't remember. Every solution came from CodeForge. Every implementation was AI-generated.

"I've been focusing on architecture," he said, deflecting. "ThinkTank has been invaluable for system design."

---

Jennifer nodded, but her expression was thoughtful.

"Architecture is important. But don't lose touch with the code. A tech lead who can't code is just a manager."

The words stung. Because they were true.

---

That night, he tried to write code.

He opened his editor, stared at the blank file. A simple problem—a function to process a list of transactions. Something he could have written in his sleep, five years ago.

Now, the cursor blinked. His fingers hovered. Nothing came.

*Just start*, he told himself. *You know this. You've done this before.*

---

But he didn't know. Not anymore.

The syntax felt foreign. The patterns felt distant. The muscle memory was gone.

After twenty minutes, he closed the editor. Opened CodeForge. Typed a prompt.

The AI generated the function in seconds.

*This is fine*, he told himself, copying the code. *I'm still technical. I just use tools efficiently.*

But the lie was getting harder to believe.
