# Chapter 10: The Beginning

Three months later, Marcus sat at his desk, staring at a problem.

It was a real problem—not a prompt, not a generated scenario, but an actual architectural challenge that required thinking. His team was waiting for guidance. His metrics had dropped. Jennifer had expressed concern about his productivity.

He was thinking.

Not prompting. Not accepting. Not performing. Actually thinking. The mental model was forming slowly, the solution emerging from the fog of his own mind. It was messy, inefficient, far from the elegant responses AI would have generated.

But it was his.

*This is what it feels like*, he realized. *This is what I gave up.*

---

The solution wasn't perfect. It wasn't as elegant as what ThinkTank would have generated. It wasn't as fast as what CodeForge would have produced. But Marcus understood every decision, every trade-off, every line of code.

He presented it to his team, not as a performance of expertise, but as a genuine attempt to solve a problem. They asked questions. He answered from understanding, not from memorized responses. The discussion was real, the collaboration genuine.

*This is what technical leadership is supposed to be*, he thought. *Not AI-assisted performance, but actual thinking, actual guidance, actual mentorship.*

---

That evening, Marcus stayed late. Not to catch up on metrics, but to think. To rebuild. To reclaim the mind he'd given away.

The question from years ago still echoed: *Who is thinking now?*

For the first time, he had an answer.

*I am. Slowly, painfully, imperfectly—but I am.*

It was just a beginning. The dependency was deep, the erosion extensive. He might never fully recover what he'd lost. But he was trying. He was thinking. He was becoming an engineer again.

And that, for now, was enough.
