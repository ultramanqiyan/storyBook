# Chapter 9: The Team

The team meeting was scheduled for Monday.

Marcus had been dreading it all weekend. The usual agenda—project updates, technical discussions, planning. But now, everything felt different.

He wasn't a tech lead. He was a translator. And he didn't know how to lead a team from that position.

---

The meeting started normally.

"Alex, how's the payment service refactoring?"

"On track," Alex replied. "We're about 60% done. Should be complete by end of sprint."

"Good. Any blockers?"

"Just the usual—some edge cases in the transaction logic. I'm working through them."

---

Marcus nodded along. The words made sense. The progress was visible.

But he couldn't contribute. Couldn't offer technical guidance. Couldn't suggest approaches.

He was a spectator in his own team meeting.

---

"Marcus, I had a question about the architecture," Priya said. "For the frontend redesign, should we use a micro-frontend approach or keep it monolithic?"

The question was reasonable. A tech lead should have an opinion.

Marcus felt the familiar panic.

---

"What are the trade-offs you're seeing?" he asked, buying time.

"Well, micro-frontends would give us more flexibility," Priya explained. "But they add complexity. A monolithic approach is simpler but less scalable."

*Ask ThinkTank*, his mind urged. *Get the AI's opinion. Present it as your own.*

---

But something had shifted.

He was tired of pretending. Tired of forwarding AI output as his own thinking. Tired of being a translator who pretended to be a tech lead.

---

"I don't have a strong opinion," he said, the words feeling foreign. "This is a decision you should make based on your understanding of the requirements. You're closer to the frontend than I am."

The team looked at him, surprised.

---

"Are you sure?" Priya asked. "I was hoping for some guidance."

"I trust your judgment," Marcus said. "You're a senior engineer. You understand the trade-offs. Make the call that makes sense for the project."

---

 The room was quiet for a moment.

Then Alex spoke up. "That's... actually refreshing. Usually leads want to make every decision themselves."

"I'm trying something different," Marcus said carefully. "Empowering the team to make technical decisions. You're all capable engineers."

---

The meeting continued. But the dynamic had shifted.

Instead of deferring to Marcus for every technical question, the team started discussing among themselves. Trading ideas. Building on each other's suggestions.

Marcus watched, feeling both relief and loss.

---

After the meeting, Alex stopped by his desk.

"That was interesting today," Alex said. "You seemed... different."

"Different how?"

"Less... directive. More trusting. It felt like you were letting us do our jobs instead of trying to control everything."

---

Marcus considered the feedback.

He hadn't been controlling. He'd been deflecting. Hiding his lack of knowledge behind vague guidance and AI-sourced suggestions.

But the result was the same—the team had space to make decisions.

---

"I'm trying to be more hands-off," Marcus said. "Letting the team take ownership."

"I like it," Alex replied. "It makes me feel more trusted. More capable."

---

That evening, Marcus reflected on the meeting.

He hadn't provided technical leadership. He'd provided... space. Trust. Permission for the team to make decisions.

Was that leadership? Or was it just admitting his own limitations?

---

*Maybe it doesn't matter*, he thought. *Maybe what the team needs isn't a technical genius. Maybe they need someone who trusts them. Who gets out of their way. Who translates between them and the AI tools when needed.*

---

The role was becoming clearer.

Not a tech lead in the traditional sense. Not an engineer who could debug and code and architect.

But something else. A facilitator. A translator. Someone who helped the team work effectively, even if he couldn't do the work himself.

---

It wasn't what he'd wanted to be. It wasn't what he'd trained for.

But it was what he was now.

And maybe, just maybe, it was enough.
