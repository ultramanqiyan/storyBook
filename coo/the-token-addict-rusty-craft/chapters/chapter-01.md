# Chapter 1: The Promotion

Two years. It had been two years since Marcus first confronted the question that had haunted him: *Who is thinking now?* He'd never found an answer, but life had moved on. The promotion email arrived on a Tuesday morning, buried between AI-generated reports and meeting invites.

"Congratulations on your promotion to Tech Lead," it said, and Marcus read it three times before the words sank in. Tech Lead. After five years at Nexus Technologies, he'd finally made it. The title came with a salary bump, a larger team, and expectations he wasn't sure he could meet. But that was fine. He'd figure it out. He always did.

The question from two years ago still lingered somewhere in the back of his mind, but it had become quieter over time. He'd learned to live with it, to work around it, to succeed despite it. CodeForge had become such a natural part of his workflow that he barely noticed it anymore. The prompts were automatic, the responses accepted without question. He was productive, successful, and now—Tech Lead.

---

The team meeting was scheduled for 10 AM. Jennifer stood at the front of the conference room, her tablet displaying the org chart with Marcus's name highlighted in a new color.

"I'm pleased to announce that Marcus Webb has been promoted to Tech Lead," Jennifer said, her voice carrying that corporate enthusiasm that had become familiar over the years. "Marcus has been with Nexus for five years now, and his contributions have been exceptional. His productivity metrics are consistently top-tier, and his AI adoption has been a model for the entire engineering organization."

The team applauded. Sarah smiled from across the table. Mike nodded approvingly. Alex, the junior developer Marcus had helped years ago, looked at him with something like hero worship.

Marcus stood, accepted the congratulations, and felt the familiar weight of performance settling onto his shoulders. He was good at this now—the confident nod, the gracious acceptance, the appearance of competence that had become his trademark.

"Thank you, Jennifer. Thank you, everyone," he said, his voice steady. "I'm excited to take on this new role and help lead our team to even greater success."

The words felt hollow, but no one seemed to notice. They never did.

---

After the announcement, Jennifer called Marcus into her office. The door closed behind him, and she gestured for him to sit.

"Congratulations again, Marcus," she said, settling into her chair. "You've earned this. The company has big expectations for you."

"I'm ready," Marcus said, and the lie came easily.

"Good. Because Tech Lead isn't just about productivity anymore. It's about technical leadership." Jennifer leaned forward, her expression serious. "Your team will look to you for architectural guidance, technical mentorship, and problem-solving leadership. You'll be expected to make design decisions, review complex implementations, and guide junior engineers through technical challenges."

The words landed like weights, each one adding to the pressure building in Marcus's chest. Technical leadership. Architectural guidance. Problem-solving. These were the things he used to do, back before CodeForge had become his mind. These were the skills he'd outsourced, delegated, let atrophy.

"I understand," he said, keeping his voice steady. "I'm ready for the challenge."

Jennifer smiled, satisfied. "I knew you would be. That's why I recommended you for this role. You have the technical depth, the leadership skills, and the AI-first mindset that we need in our senior positions."

Technical depth. The phrase echoed in Marcus's mind. Did he still have technical depth? Or had it been replaced by AI depth—the ability to prompt effectively, to accept responses quickly, to perform competence without actually possessing it?

---

His first test came that afternoon.

A team member—new, someone Marcus didn't recognize—stopped by his desk with a question. "Marcus? I'm working on the authentication service, and I'm trying to decide between JWT and session-based auth. What do you think?"

The question was straightforward. Basic, even. Two years ago, Marcus would have answered without thinking—weighed the trade-offs, discussed the security implications, recommended an approach based on the specific requirements.

Now, he felt a familiar blankness. The knowledge was there, somewhere—the concepts, the general principles—but the details felt fuzzy, inaccessible. He should know this. He was Tech Lead. He was supposed to have technical depth.

"That's a good question," he said, buying time. "Why don't you research both approaches and put together a comparison? We can discuss it in tomorrow's standup."

The team member nodded, seemingly satisfied, and walked away. Marcus turned back to his screen, opened CodeForge, and typed: "Compare JWT vs session-based authentication for microservices architecture. Include security considerations and scalability implications."

The response appeared, comprehensive and detailed. Marcus read through it, memorized the key points, and prepared for tomorrow's discussion. He'd have the answer then. He'd appear knowledgeable, decisive, like a real Tech Lead.

But the question gnawed at him: *Shouldn't you have known this already?*

---

By the end of the day, Marcus had led his first team meeting, deflected three technical questions to "research," and smiled through a celebration lunch. The promotion was real. The title was his. The expectations were clear.

What wasn't clear was whether he could meet them.

Technical leadership, Jennifer had said. Show them how it's done. But showing required knowing, and somewhere in the past two years, knowing had become harder. The knowledge that used to be at his fingertips now required prompts to access. The thinking that used to be automatic now required AI assistance.

He opened CodeForge, the AI that had become his constant companion, and stared at the familiar interface. The cursor blinked, patient, waiting. It had been waiting for two years now, ready to provide whatever thinking he needed.

Tomorrow, he'd need to be the technical leader everyone expected. Tonight, he'd prepare. He typed another prompt, watched the response appear, and tried to ignore the question that still lingered:

*Can you still do this without AI?*

He didn't have an answer. He wasn't sure he wanted one.

*End of Chapter 1*
