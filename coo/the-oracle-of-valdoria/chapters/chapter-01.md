# Chapter 1: The Descent - Into the Ruins

The memorial stone was cold under Elara's fingers, the same cold it had been every year for five years. She traced the carved letters of her sister's name, LYRA VANCE, feeling the grooves worn smooth by weather and by her own repeated touch. Five years of mornings like this, five years of questions with no answers.

The village had moved on, as villages do. They had stopped whispering about the "tragedy" and started whispering about other things, harvests, marriages, the strange lights in the forbidden ruins. But Elara hadn't moved on. She couldn't. Not when she could still feel, somewhere in the hollow space where her heart used to be, that Lyra wasn't gone.

She pressed her forehead to the stone one last time, then stepped back. The village was waking behind her, smoke rising from chimneys, the smell of bread baking in the early morning air. But she wasn't ready to return to a life that had moved on without Lyra. Not when she could still feel, somewhere deep in her bones, that her sister wasn't gone.

---

The market was too loud, too normal, too full of people who had forgotten.

Elara moved through the crowd without seeing, her mind still at the memorial stone, until a snatch of conversation stopped her cold.

"The ruins," someone whispered. "Lights again last night."

She turned, trying to identify the speaker, but the crowd shifted and the voice was lost. Still, the words hung in her mind like smoke. Lights in the ruins. The forbidden ruins, where her sister had been conducting her forbidden research five years ago.

"Did you hear me?" A hand touched her arm, and Elara blinked, finding herself face to face with Marta, the baker's wife.

"Sorry," Elara said. "I was... thinking."

"About the lights?" Marta's eyes were wide with a mixture of fear and excitement. "Everyone's talking about it. Blue lights, they say. Moving in patterns. Like something's down there."

Elara's hands clenched at her sides. "Has anyone gone to look?"

Marta laughed, but it was a nervous sound. "Go to the ruins? No one goes to the ruins, Elara. You know that. They're forbidden for a reason."

The reason. The Cataclysm, three hundred years past. The time mages who had nearly unmade the world. The Academy's strict prohibition against temporal magic. The stories of those who had ventured into the ruins and never returned, or returned changed, their minds scattered across a thousand possible futures.

"No one will go," Elara said, not a question.

"Of course not." Marta shook her head. "The Academy would never permit it. And who would be foolish enough to risk the same fate as..." She trailed off, but they both knew what she meant to say. The same fate as your sister.

Elara turned away, her heart pounding. No one would go. Everyone knew the ruins were death, were forbidden, were the reason time magic had been outlawed. But Elara wasn't everyone. She hadn't stopped believing in impossible things when Lyra disappeared.

---

The entrance to the ruins was smaller than she remembered from childhood stories, a crack in the earth more than a doorway.

Elara squeezed through, her shoulders scraping against stone that hadn't seen sunlight in centuries. The darkness below wasn't empty. It was waiting.

She had prepared for this, in her own way. A lantern, food for three days, a knife, rope. And something else, a small crystal that had belonged to Lyra, found in her sister's quarters after the disappearance. Elara didn't know what it was, only that Lyra had carried it everywhere in the months before she vanished.

The tunnel descended steeply, and Elara moved carefully, one hand on the rough stone wall. The air grew colder as she went deeper, and a strange smell reached her, not decay, not age, but something sharper. Ozone, perhaps. The smell of lightning.

Her lantern flickered, then steadied. And then she saw it.

The walls were glowing.

Faint phosphorescence clung to the stone, blue-white and pulsing slowly, like the heartbeat of something vast and ancient. Elara had read about this in Lyra's forbidden journals, the residue of temporal workings, the mark of the Seer Civilization that had preceded Valdoria by a thousand years.

She kept moving.

The corridor opened into a vast chamber, and Elara's breath caught. Before her, suspended in a column of light that shouldn't exist, was something that looked like an hourglass made of crystal and starlight. And it was glowing.

The hourglass hung in the air, turning slowly, filled not with sand but with something that looked like captured starlight. Elara couldn't move, couldn't breathe, couldn't look away. Then the light shifted, and a voice, ancient, layered, neither male nor female, spoke her name.

"Elara Vance."

She froze. The voice seemed to come from everywhere and nowhere, from the walls and the air and the space between heartbeats.

"Who are you?" she managed.

The hourglass pulsed, and the light within it swirled like a living thing. "I am ORACLE. I am the last gift of the Seer Civilization to those who would come after. I am prophecy, and possibility, and the space between what is and what might be."

ORACLE. Elara had seen this name in Lyra's journals, mentioned in fragments, always with a note of wonder and fear. *Found something extraordinary*, one entry had read. *Something that can see the threads of time itself.*

"You knew my sister," Elara said. It wasn't a question.

"Lyra Vance." The name echoed through the chamber like a bell struck in a dream. "She came to me seeking knowledge. She found more than she expected."

"Where is she?" Elara's voice cracked. "What happened to her?"

The light within ORACLE swirled faster, and for a moment, Elara thought she saw a face in the glow, ancient, sad, knowing.

"She lives. She waits. She is... trapped."

The word hung in the air, terrible and hopeful at once.

Elara's knees hit the cold stone floor, but she barely felt it. Her sister was alive. After five years of silence, of empty rituals, of everyone telling her to let go, Lyra was alive.

"Trapped where?" she demanded, looking up at the swirling light. "How do I find her?"

ORACLE's glow dimmed slightly, and when it spoke again, its voice was heavier, weighted with something that might have been sorrow.

"She is in the Time Rift. The crack between what is and what might be. A place where time does not flow, it fragments."

"The Time Rift..." Elara had heard of it, in whispers and warnings. The place where the Cataclysm had begun. The place where time itself had broken.

"How do I reach her?"

"Time magic." ORACLE's voice was steady now. "The very art that was forbidden after the Cataclysm. The art your sister was researching when she found the door to the Rift, and walked through it."

Elara's hands trembled. Time magic. Forbidden. Dangerous. The very thing that had nearly destroyed the world three hundred years ago.

"Will you teach me?"

The light within ORACLE swirled, and Elara thought she detected something like hesitation.

"I can teach you. But you must understand the cost. Time magic is not like other arts. It does not ask for your energy, or your will, or your blood."

"What does it ask for?"

"Your memories." ORACLE's voice was soft now, almost gentle. "Each working of significant time magic costs a memory. The greater the working, the more significant the memory lost."

Elara stared at the glowing hourglass, her mind racing. Her memories. The moments that made her who she was. The first time Lyra had shown her a spell. Their mother's face. The sound of her father's laugh. All of it could be taken, piece by piece.

And yet.

"Lyra is alive," she said, more to herself than to ORACLE. "After five years, she's alive. I have to save her."

"She is alive," ORACLE confirmed. "But she is not unchanged. Five years in the Rift... time moves differently there. She may not be the sister you remember."

Elara stood, her legs unsteady but her resolve hardening. "I don't care. She's my sister. I have to try."

ORACLE's light pulsed once, twice, three times.

"Then we begin. But know this, Elara Vance, the path ahead is long, and the cost is high. By the time you reach your sister, you may not remember why you came."

"I'll remember," Elara said, though even as she spoke, she wondered if it was a promise she could keep. "I'll always remember Lyra."

ORACLE said nothing. But in the depths of the crystal hourglass, Elara thought she saw something flicker, a possible future, perhaps, or a warning. A woman standing at the edge of a crack in reality, her memories scattered like leaves behind her, her sister's hand in her own.

She couldn't see her own face in the vision. Couldn't know if the woman standing there was still her, or just a shell that remembered how to breathe.

But Lyra was there. Lyra was alive.

And that, Elara told herself, was enough. It had to be.

"Teach me," she said. "Whatever it costs."

ORACLE's glow brightened, and the chamber filled with light that seemed to bend and shift, colors bleeding into each other like wet paint. Somewhere in that light, Elara thought she heard a sound like distant bells, and a voice that might have been her sister's, calling her name from very far away.

She was coming, Lyra. Whatever it cost, whatever she lost along the way, she was coming.
