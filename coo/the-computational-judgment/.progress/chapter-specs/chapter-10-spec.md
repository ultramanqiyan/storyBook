### Opening
The algorithm went live three months later. It was not perfect—no system could be. But it was better than what had come before, and it had mechanisms for improvement built in. James watched the first results come in, a mixture of hope and anxiety.

### Ending
James stood before his ethics class, discussing the trolley problem once again. But now the questions felt different. Now he understood that judgment was not about finding the right answer. It was about asking the right questions, examining values, acknowledging uncertainty, and committing to fairness even when it was impossible to be certain. "There is no perfect algorithm," he told his students. "There is no divine judgment that will tell us what is right. There is only us—making decisions, examining our biases, trying to be fair. That's the work. That's the responsibility. And it never ends." The students looked back at him, and James felt something he hadn't felt in years: not certainty, but hope. Not faith, but purpose. And that, he realized, was enough.
