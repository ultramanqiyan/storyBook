# Chapter 9: The Revelation

The board accepted James's recommendations with modifications. The algorithm would be implemented, but with safeguards, with oversight, with an acknowledgment of its limitations. It was not a victory, but it was progress.

James sat in his office at the university, staring out the window at the London skyline. The news had come through that morning—an official statement from the board, outlining the changes that would be made to the Citizen Assessment Framework. Care work would be valued. Employment and income would be weighted less heavily. Ongoing bias audits would be conducted. The appeals process would be strengthened.

It wasn't everything he had asked for. The algorithm would still be deployed. The fundamental structure remained. But it was something. It was movement in the right direction.

He thought about Sarah Chen. He thought about the certainty he had carried, the judgment he had pronounced, the damage he had done. He thought about the algorithm, and the certainty it represented, and the damage it too would do.

But he also thought about what he had learned. About judgment. About values. About the relationship between the two.

His phone buzzed. A text from Adanna: *The children want to know if you'll be home for dinner. They have something to show you.*

He smiled, despite everything. Chidi and Ngozi, twelve and nine, still wanted to share their lives with him. Still believed he had something to offer.

He typed a reply: *I'll be there.*

Then he sat back in his chair and let his mind wander.

He had been thinking about judgment for weeks now—about the algorithm, about the church, about the nature of assessment itself. And something was becoming clear to him, something he had never understood before.

When he was a pastor, he had believed that judgment was about the judged. He had believed that God assessed people based on their actions, their hearts, their choices. He had believed that judgment was an objective evaluation of worth.

But now he saw it differently. Judgment was not about the judged. It was about the judges. It revealed the values, the assumptions, the biases of the person—or the system—doing the judging.

When he had judged Sarah Chen, he had not been seeing her clearly. He had been seeing his own values—his beliefs about marriage, about sin, about what God wanted. He had imposed those values on her, and called it divine judgment. But it wasn't divine. It was human. It was his.

The algorithm was the same. It claimed to be objective, to be neutral, to be fair. But it encoded the values of the people who had built it—their assumptions about what counted, their beliefs about productivity and contribution, their biases about who was worth investing in. It reflected those values back, dressed in mathematical form, and called it objective assessment.

But it wasn't objective. It was human. It was theirs.

And that was the revelation.

All judgment—divine, human, algorithmic—revealed more about the judges than the judged. The question was not whether judgment was fair. The question was whether the values underlying the judgment were fair. Whether they had been examined. Whether they could be defended. Whether they reflected the best of what humans could be, or the worst.

James stood up and walked to the window. The London skyline stretched out before him—buildings and streets and lives, all intersecting and diverging in patterns too complex for any algorithm to capture.

He had lost his faith in divine judgment. He no longer believed in a God who assessed worth and rendered verdicts. But he had not lost his belief in judgment itself. He still believed that some things were right and some things were wrong. He still believed that fairness mattered, that justice mattered, that how people were treated mattered.

What he had lost was certainty. The certainty that he knew what was right. The certainty that his values were the right values. The certainty that he could see clearly enough to judge.

And that loss was not a tragedy. It was a gift.

Because without certainty, he had to keep questioning. He had to keep examining his own assumptions. He had to keep asking whether his values were fair, whether his judgments were just, whether he was seeing people clearly or imposing his own biases.

The algorithm would do the same thing—or it would, if the safeguards worked. It would be examined. Its values would be questioned. Its biases would be exposed. And it could be changed, improved, made more fair.

That was the work. That was what he had committed to. Not building a perfect system, but building a system that could be examined and improved. Not achieving certainty, but maintaining the humility to keep questioning.

He thought about his former mentor, Reverend Michael Adebayo. Michael had told him, years ago, that doubt was not the opposite of faith. That certainty was. James had not understood then. But he understood now.

Faith was not about believing without question. It was about committing to something despite uncertainty. It was about doing the work even when you didn't know if you were right. It was about trusting that the effort mattered, even when the outcome was unclear.

He had lost his faith in God. But he had found a different kind of faith—not in a divine being, but in the work itself. In the commitment to fairness. In the willingness to keep trying, to keep questioning, to keep building systems that reflected the best of human values rather than the worst.

That was the faith he had left. And it was enough.

He gathered his things and left his office. The university corridors were quiet, students and faculty gone for the day. He walked toward the tube station, his mind clearer than it had been in weeks.

The algorithm would be deployed. It would make mistakes. It would reflect human biases. But it would also be examined, questioned, improved. The safeguards would work—or they wouldn't, and then they would have to be strengthened. The process would continue.

And James would be part of it. He would keep asking questions. He would keep pushing for fairness. He would keep doing the work, without certainty, without faith in a higher power, but with faith in the value of the effort itself.

The tube station was crowded with evening commuters. James joined the flow of bodies, moving toward the platform. The train arrived with a rush of air, and he stepped aboard, finding a seat by the window.

Through the glass, he watched the tunnel walls slide past. He thought about Sarah Chen, and the judgment he had pronounced on her life. He thought about the algorithm, and the judgment it would pronounce on millions of lives. He thought about the difference between them.

The difference was that the algorithm could be changed. It could be examined. It could be improved. His judgment of Sarah had been final—he had spoken, and she had died. But the algorithm would be ongoing. It would be subject to review, to revision, to correction.

That was not enough. It would never be enough. But it was something.

The train emerged from the tunnel into the London evening. Through the window, James could see the city lights beginning to glow—homes and offices and streets where people lived their lives, each one complex and valuable in ways that no algorithm could fully capture.

He thought about his children, waiting for him at home. He thought about Adanna, who still prayed for him even though he no longer believed. He thought about the life he had built, the work he had done, the faith he had lost and the faith he had found.

And he felt, for the first time in a long time, something like peace.

Not certainty. Not faith in a divine plan. But peace in the knowledge that he was doing what he could. That he was asking the right questions. That he was committed to fairness, even when he couldn't be sure what fairness looked like.

The train carried him home, through the dark tunnels and the lit stations, toward the people he loved and the work that remained.
