# Chapter 1: The Appointment

Dr. James Okonkwo stood before his ethics class at the University of London, discussing the trolley problem for the thousandth time. The afternoon light slanted through the tall windows, catching dust particles that drifted between him and his students. At forty-four, he had built a career on asking difficult questions without providing easy answers.

"So," he said, pacing slowly at the front of the lecture hall, "you have a switch. You can divert the trolley, killing one person to save five. Most of you say you would do it. Now consider: what if the one person is your mother? What if the five are strangers?"

A student in the third row raised her hand. "That's not fair, Professor. You're making it personal."

"Ethics is personal," James replied. "The moment we pretend it isn't, we've stopped doing ethics and started doing mathematics." He paused, feeling the familiar rhythm of a point about to land. "When I was a pastor, I would have told you that all lives are equal in God's eyes. But I'm not a pastor anymore. Now I ask: what makes a life count? What gives it weight?"

The lecture hall was quiet. James could smell the faint staleness of the room—old books, coffee from a student's cup, the particular mustiness of academic spaces. His phone buzzed in his pocket, but he ignored it. There were fifteen minutes left in the lecture, and he wanted to push them further.

"Next week," he continued, "we'll discuss whether a machine can make this decision. Whether an algorithm can weigh lives. Whether code can carry moral weight." He looked around the room, meeting eyes. "I want you to think about what it means to judge. Not just who dies, but who decides."

After class, James walked back to his office through corridors that smelled of floor polish and old paper. His phone buzzed again. And again. He pulled it out, expecting department notices or student queries.

The subject line read: *Official Appointment - AI Ethics Governance Board - Response Required.*

He stopped in the middle of the hallway. A student brushed past him, mumbling an apology. James barely noticed. He opened the email.

*Dear Dr. Okonkwo,*

*Following the recommendation of the Parliamentary Committee on Artificial Intelligence and after extensive review of your academic record and ethical philosophy expertise, you have been selected to serve on the newly established AI Ethics Governance Board.*

*The Board will oversee the development and implementation of algorithmic assessment systems for public resource allocation. Your experience in applied ethics and your unique perspective on moral reasoning will be invaluable to this work.*

*Please confirm your acceptance within 48 hours. The first meeting is scheduled for Monday, 14th March, at the Department for Science, Innovation and Technology.*

James read the email three times. Algorithmic assessment systems. Public resource allocation. The words were bureaucratic, neutral, the kind of language that made ordinary people's eyes glaze over. But James understood what they meant. The government was building a system to judge citizens. And they wanted him to help decide how it would work.

He sat at his desk, the leather chair creaking beneath him. Through his window, he could see the Thames, grey under the London sky, the city moving around him in its endless rhythm. His office was cluttered with books and papers—years of thinking about right and wrong, good and evil, the trolley problem and its infinite variations.

*When I was a pastor*, he thought, *I believed in divine judgment. I believed there was a standard beyond human opinion. Now they want me to help build a new kind of judgment.*

His chest tightened—that familiar pressure when a question refused to resolve. He thought of Sarah Chen. He had not expected to think of her today, but she came anyway, as she often did when questions of judgment arose. Sarah, twenty-three years old, who had come to his church seeking guidance. Sarah, who had been living with her boyfriend outside of marriage. Sarah, whom James had counseled with scripture and concern and the certainty of a man who believed he knew what God wanted.

Sarah, who had ended her life three weeks after their last conversation.

James set his phone down. The screen dimmed and went dark. In the reflection, he saw his own face—dark skin, graying temples, lines around his eyes that had not been there when he was a pastor. When he believed.

He thought of his wife, Adanna, who still attended church every Sunday with their two children. He thought of the distance that had grown between them, the conversations they no longer had, the way she prayed for him when she thought he wasn't listening. He thought of his former mentor, Reverend Michael Adebayo, who had told him, "James, doubt is not the opposite of faith. Certainty is."

The appointment email waited. James picked up his phone again and scrolled through the details. The Board would have twelve members. They would meet weekly. They would review the algorithm's design, approve or reject its criteria, and oversee its implementation. The system would assess citizens for housing priority, healthcare resources, educational opportunities—everything the government allocated based on need.

*And who defines need?* James thought. *That's the question. That's always the question.*

He remembered a sermon he had preached fifteen years ago, back when he believed. "God sees the heart," he had said. "Man looks at the outward appearance, but the Lord looks at the heart." It was from 1 Samuel, the story of David's anointing. James had preached it with conviction, certain that divine judgment was perfect because God could see what humans could not.

Now the government wanted to build something that could see. An algorithm that would look at data—employment records, health histories, educational achievements, criminal records, social connections—and render judgment. Not divine judgment. Not human judgment. Something new.

James stood and walked to his window. The Thames continued its slow movement toward the sea. A boat passed, small against the grey water. Somewhere in the city, millions of people were living their lives, unaware that a committee would soon decide how machines would judge their worth.

His phone buzzed again. A text from Adanna: *Dinner at 7? The children want to tell you about their day.*

James smiled, despite everything. His children—Chidi, twelve, and Ngozi, nine—still believed their father had answers. They did not know about his loss of faith, not really. They knew he no longer came to church, but they did not understand what that meant. They still asked him questions about right and wrong, expecting wisdom.

*What would I tell them about this?* he wondered. *That I'm helping build a machine that will judge people? That I'm going to decide what makes a life count?*

He typed a reply: *Yes. I have news to share.*

Then he sat back down at his desk and began to read the attached documents. The AI Ethics Governance Board had been established six months ago, following public concern about algorithmic bias in government systems. There had been scandals—predictive policing algorithms that targeted minority neighborhoods, healthcare algorithms that deprioritized disabled patients, hiring algorithms that discriminated against women. The government had responded by creating oversight.

But oversight was not the same as control. James could see that already. The Board would review, would advise, would recommend. But the algorithm would be built regardless. The only question was whether anyone would be watching when it made its decisions.

The documents described the system's purpose: to allocate public resources efficiently, fairly, and transparently. The words "efficiently" and "fairly" appeared often, sometimes in the same sentence, as if efficiency and fairness were natural allies. James knew they were not. Efficiency wanted speed, simplicity, clear metrics. Fairness wanted nuance, context, attention to individual circumstances. The two values pulled in opposite directions.

He read until the light outside his window began to fade. The Thames turned silver, then black. Streetlights flickered on along the embankment. His office grew dark, but he did not turn on the lamp. He sat in the dimness, thinking about judgment.

When he was a pastor, he had believed judgment belonged to God. He had believed that humans could not see clearly enough to judge each other, that only divine omniscience could render true justice. Now he no longer believed in divine omniscience. But he had not stopped believing in human blindness.

*We cannot see clearly*, he thought. *We never could. And now we're building machines to see for us. But machines see what we teach them to see. They learn our blindness.*

He thought of Sarah Chen again. He had judged her, back then. He had been certain he was right. He had quoted scripture, offered guidance, prayed for her soul. He had believed he was doing God's work.

And she had died.

The wound still ached, eight years later. Not the sharp pain of the first months, but a dull throb that surfaced at unexpected moments. When he saw a young woman on the tube who looked like her. When a student asked about suicide prevention. When questions of judgment arose, as they always did, in ethics and in life.

James finally turned on his desk lamp. The sudden light made him blink. He looked at the email again, at the request for confirmation. Forty-eight hours. He could take time to think. He could consult with colleagues, discuss it with Adanna, weigh the decision carefully.

But he already knew what he would do.

He would accept. Not because he was certain he could make a difference. Not because he believed in the system. But because someone had to watch. Someone had to ask the difficult questions. Someone had to stand in the room where judgment was being designed and say: *What are we doing? What are we really doing?*

He typed his response.

*Dear Committee,*

*I accept the appointment to the AI Ethics Governance Board. I look forward to contributing to this important work.*

*Dr. James Okonkwo*

He pressed send before he could change his mind. The email disappeared into the digital ether, and James sat back in his chair, feeling the weight settle onto his shoulders.

The thing was, he had spent fifteen years preaching about judgment. He had told his congregation that God would weigh their hearts, that divine justice was perfect, that no one could escape the eyes of the Almighty. He had believed it. He had built his life around it.

And then he had stopped believing. Not all at once, but slowly, like a building crumbling from within. Sarah's death had been the crack in the foundation, but the collapse had taken years. By the time he left the ministry, he was no longer certain there was a God to do the judging.

But judgment remained. The world still demanded it. Resources were limited. Decisions had to be made. The only question was who would make them, and how, and according to what values.

James gathered his things—his briefcase, his coat, the day's accumulation of papers. He turned off the lamp and walked out of his office into the darkened hallway. The building was quiet now, most of his colleagues gone home. His footsteps echoed on the linoleum floor.

Outside, the London air was cold and damp, carrying the smell of the river and the faint exhaust of passing cars. James walked toward the tube station, his breath visible in the evening air. He thought about dinner with his family, about the news he would share, about the questions his children might ask.

He thought about the algorithm that would soon begin judging citizens. He thought about the criteria it would use, the values it would encode, the decisions it would make. He thought about all the people who would never know that a committee had shaped the machine that shaped their lives.

*When I was a pastor*, he thought, *I believed judgment was God's domain. Now I'm helping build a new kind of judge. And I don't know if that's better or worse than the old kind.*

He did not have an answer. He was not sure he ever would. But he had accepted the appointment, and on Monday, he would walk into that government building and begin the work. He would ask questions. He would challenge assumptions. He would try, in whatever small way he could, to ensure that the machine did not simply replicate the blindness of its creators.

The tube station was crowded with evening commuters. James joined the flow of bodies, moving toward the platform, toward home, toward dinner and his children and his wife who still prayed for him. The train arrived with a rush of air and the screech of brakes. He stepped aboard, found a seat, and watched the dark tunnel walls slide past the window.

Somewhere in the city, the algorithm was already being built. Somewhere, engineers were writing code that would judge human worth. Somewhere, decisions were being made that would affect millions of lives.

And James Okonkwo, former pastor, current ethicist, man of uncertain faith, had just agreed to be part of it.

The train emerged from the tunnel into the London evening. Through the window, James could see the city lights beginning to glow—homes and offices and streets where people lived their lives, unaware that judgment was coming. He thought of the trolley problem, of the switch and the tracks and the impossible choice. He thought of Sarah Chen, of the certainty that had failed her, of the questions that still haunted him.

He thought of his children, and the world they would inherit, and the machines that would judge them.

And he knew, with the clarity that sometimes came in quiet moments, that this work would test everything he believed about ethics, about judgment, about what it meant to do good in a world without certainty.

The train carried him home.

**END OF CHAPTER 1**
