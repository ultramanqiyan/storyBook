# Chapter 10: The Future

The algorithm went live three months later. It was not perfect—no system could be. But it was better than what had come before, and it had mechanisms for improvement built in. James watched the first results come in, a mixture of hope and anxiety.

He sat in his office, staring at the screen. The data was still preliminary—the first wave of assessments, the first outcomes. Some citizens were receiving higher scores than they would have under the old system. Some were receiving lower. The overall pattern was mixed, inconclusive, a system still finding its footing.

But it was working. The safeguards were functioning. The bias audits were catching problems. The appeals process was giving citizens a voice.

It wasn't enough. It would never be enough. But it was a beginning.

James closed his laptop and stood up. The university semester was ending, and he had papers to grade, lectures to prepare, a life to return to. The board would continue its oversight, but his role was diminishing. The algorithm was in motion now, making decisions, affecting lives. His job was done—for now.

He walked out of his office and through the quiet corridors. The university was peaceful in the late afternoon, students gone for the holidays, faculty working in their offices. The smell of old books and floor polish filled the air, familiar and comforting.

He thought about the year that had passed. The appointment to the board. The discovery of the bias. The debates and the recommendations and the compromises. The work that remained.

He thought about Sarah Chen, who had started everything. He still carried her with him, a weight that never fully lifted. But the weight had changed. It was no longer just grief and guilt. It was also purpose. It was the reason he had fought. It was the memory that drove him forward.

He walked out of the building into the London evening. The air was cool, carrying the scent of autumn. The city moved around him—cars and pedestrians, shops and restaurants, the endless rhythm of urban life.

He thought about the future. The algorithm would continue to evolve. It would improve or fail. It would face challenges, controversies, crises. And James would continue to watch, to question, to push for fairness. That was the work. That was the responsibility.

He arrived home to the sound of laughter. Chidi and Ngozi were in the living room, watching a film together. Adanna was in the kitchen, cooking dinner. The house smelled of spices and warmth.

"Dad!" Ngozi ran to him, wrapping her arms around his legs. "You're home!"

"I'm home," James said, hugging her.

He walked into the kitchen, where Adanna was stirring a pot. She looked up and smiled.

"How was your day?" she asked.

"Different," he said. "The algorithm went live."

"I know. I saw the news." She paused. "Are you okay?"

He considered the question. "I don't know. Maybe. The system is working, but it's not perfect. It will make mistakes. It will hurt people. But it's better than what came before."

"That's all we can do," Adanna said. "Try to make things better."

She was right. That was all any of them could do.

James sat at the kitchen table, watching his wife cook and listening to his children's laughter from the other room. He thought about the future—the algorithm's future, his family's future, the world's future. It was uncertain. It was unclear. But it was also open. It could be shaped. It could be changed.

He had learned something this year. He had learned that judgment was not about certainty. It was about responsibility. It was about asking questions, examining values, acknowledging uncertainty, and committing to fairness even when it was impossible to be certain.

He had lost his faith in divine judgment. But he had found something else—a belief in the work itself. In the effort to be fair. In the commitment to keep trying.

That was enough. That had to be enough.

---

The next morning, James stood before his ethics class. The semester was ending, and this was the final lecture. He looked out at the rows of students—young faces, curious eyes, the future unfolding before them.

"Today," he said, "I want to talk about judgment."

He paced at the front of the lecture hall, his footsteps echoing on the hard floor. The morning light came through the tall windows, casting shadows across the room.

"When we talk about judgment, we usually think about determining what's right and wrong. We think about courts, about sentencing, about the final verdict. We think about certainty—knowing the truth, rendering justice."

He paused. "But I've learned something this year. I've learned that judgment isn't about certainty. It's about responsibility. It's about asking questions, examining our values, acknowledging uncertainty, and committing to fairness even when we can't be certain."

A student in the front row raised her hand. "Professor Okonkwo, can judgment ever be truly fair? Can any system—human or algorithmic—be completely objective?"

James shook his head. "No. No system can be completely objective. Every judgment reflects the values of the person—or the system—making it. That's not a failure. That's reality."

"Then what's the point?" another student asked. "If we can't be fair, why try?"

"Because the alternative is worse," James replied. "If we don't try to be fair, we let bias reign. We let prejudice determine outcomes. We let the powerful dominate the weak. Trying to be fair is not about achieving perfection. It's about limiting harm. It's about acknowledging our limitations and working to overcome them."

He looked around the lecture hall, meeting the eyes of his students.

"I've spent my career asking questions about ethics. What is right? What is fair? How should we live? I've lost my faith in easy answers. I've lost my certainty about what is true. But I've found something else: a commitment to the work itself. To keep asking questions. To keep examining values. To keep pushing for fairness, even when it's difficult."

He paused. "The algorithm that I've been working on—it's not perfect. It will make mistakes. It will reflect biases. But it's also being watched. It's being questioned. It's being improved. That's the key. Not a perfect system, but a system that's open to examination. Not certainty, but humility."

A student in the back raised her hand. "What does this mean for us? For our generation?"

James smiled. "It means you have a responsibility. You're inheriting a world where algorithms make decisions that affect your lives. You're inheriting systems that claim objectivity but encode values. Your job is to question those systems. To examine their values. To push for fairness. Not to find perfect solutions, but to keep working toward better ones."

He looked out at the students, these young people who would shape the future.

"There is no perfect algorithm," he said. "There is no divine judgment that will tell us what is right. There is only us—making decisions, examining our biases, trying to be fair. That's the work. That's the responsibility. And it never ends."

The lecture hall was quiet. James could feel the weight of his words, the seriousness of what he was asking of these students.

He thought about Sarah Chen. He thought about the judgment he had pronounced on her, and the judgment the algorithm would pronounce on millions. He thought about the future—uncertain, unclear, but open to shaping.

"The question isn't whether we can achieve perfect judgment," he said. "We can't. The question is whether we can keep trying to do better. Whether we can maintain the humility to question ourselves. Whether we can commit to fairness even when it's hard."

He paused, then continued. "That's what I've learned this year. That's what I want you to take away. Not answers, but questions. Not certainty, but responsibility."

The students looked back at him, and James felt something he hadn't felt in years: not certainty, but hope. Not faith, but purpose.

And that, he realized, was enough.
