# Chapter 8: The Choice

James stood before the board, his notes in hand. He had spent weeks preparing this moment, wrestling with what to say. He had no certainty. He had no faith. But he had a responsibility.

The board room was silent. Twelve faces looked up at him—Dr. Fiona Campbell, Dr. Priya Sharma, Marcus Webb, Helen Okoro, and the others. They had read his report. They knew what he had found. Now they waited to hear what he would recommend.

James looked down at his notes, then back at the faces before him.

"Before I give my recommendation," he said, "I want to tell you a story."

A murmur passed through the room. This was not how board meetings typically proceeded. But James had learned, over his years as a pastor and then as an ethicist, that stories were often the best way to communicate truth.

"Eight years ago," he continued, "I was a pastor at a church in South London. I believed in God. I believed in divine judgment. I believed that I knew what was right and what was wrong, and that it was my duty to help others see that too."

He paused, gathering himself. He could feel the old wound opening, but he pressed on.

"A young woman came to me for counseling. Her name was Sarah. She was struggling—depression, relationship problems, conflict with her family over her choices. She came to me because she wanted guidance. She wanted to know what was right."

James took a breath.

"I told her. I opened my Bible and I showed her what God required. I told her she was living in sin. I told her she needed to change. I told her, with certainty, that her choices were wrong."

The room was very quiet. James could hear his own heartbeat.

"A month later, Sarah Chen was dead. She took her own life. And I realized, in that moment, that my certainty had killed her. Not directly—I didn't push her. But I had imposed my judgment on her, dressed it up as God's will, and it had broken her."

He looked around the room, meeting each pair of eyes in turn.

"I lost my faith after that. I couldn't believe anymore in a God who would judge, or in my own ability to judge others. I became an ethicist—I study ethics, I teach ethics, I try to understand what is right and wrong. But I don't have certainty anymore. I don't know, with absolute confidence, what is fair or just or good."

He paused. "And yet, I'm here. On this board. Being asked to help design a system that will judge millions of people."

James shuffled his notes. He had prepared recommendations, but he realized now that they weren't what mattered. What mattered was this: the admission of uncertainty, the acknowledgment of fallibility, the recognition that judgment was dangerous.

"I've analyzed the algorithm," he said. "I've found significant bias—bias in the data, in the criteria, in the definition of 'social contribution.' The algorithm will discriminate against the disabled, the elderly, the marginalized. It will reproduce existing inequalities at scale. It's not neutral, and it's not fair."

He looked at Dr. Sharma. "Dr. Sharma has argued that the algorithm is mathematically objective—that it processes data without bias. She's wrong. The algorithm reflects the values of the people who built it. And those values are not neutral."

Dr. Sharma opened her mouth to respond, but James continued.

"I recommend we proceed with the algorithm," he said, "but with significant modifications. We must diversify the criteria to value care work, creativity, and community contribution—not just economic productivity. We must build in ongoing oversight to detect and correct bias. We must acknowledge that no algorithm can be truly neutral—that all judgment reflects values, and we must choose our values consciously."

He paused, feeling the weight of what he was about to say.

"I used to believe in divine judgment. I lost that faith. But I still believe in judgment—in the necessity of discerning what is right, what is fair, what is just. That judgment cannot be automated. It must be human, and it must be humble."

The room was silent. James could feel the tension, the uncertainty, the doubt.

"Here's my recommendation," he said. "First, we revise the criteria to include care work, unpaid labor, and community contribution. Second, we reduce the weight of employment and income, so that people who are unable to work are not penalized. Third, we implement ongoing bias audits, with results made public. Fourth, we create a robust appeals process that is truly accessible—not just in theory, but in practice. Fifth, we acknowledge the limits of the algorithm and ensure that human judgment remains central to decisions about individual cases."

He looked around the room one more time.

"These changes won't eliminate bias. They won't make the algorithm perfectly fair. But they'll move us in the right direction. They'll show that we're taking the concerns seriously. And they'll give the people who are affected by this system a chance to be heard."

He sat down. The room remained silent for a long moment.

Dr. Campbell spoke first. "Thank you, James. Your report and your recommendations are valuable. We'll need to discuss these proposals and vote on them."

Marcus Webb leaned forward. "Your recommendations are ambitious. They would require significant changes to the system—delays, additional costs, political complications."

"Those are not reasons to reject them," Helen Okoro said. "If the system is biased, we have a responsibility to fix it."

"The system is being deployed regardless," David Chen said. "The question is whether we implement it with modifications or not at all."

"That's not a choice," James said. "If the system is fundamentally flawed, we should not implement it at all. Not until the flaws are fixed."

Dr. Sharma spoke for the first time. "I understand your concerns, James. But the algorithm is based on the best available data and the most sophisticated modeling techniques. It's not perfect, but neither is any human system. The question is whether the benefits outweigh the risks."

"The benefits are efficiency," James said. "The risks are discrimination at scale. These are not equivalent."

The debate continued for another hour. James listened, occasionally adding points, but mostly letting the discussion unfold. The board was divided. Some wanted to push forward with the algorithm as planned. Others wanted fundamental changes. Still others wanted to delay implementation indefinitely.

In the end, they voted. The result was a compromise—some of James's recommendations would be implemented; others would be studied further. The algorithm would proceed, but with modifications.

It wasn't everything James had wanted. But it was something.

After the meeting, Dr. Campbell approached him.

"That was brave," she said. "Standing up like that."

"I wasn't brave," James replied. "I was just honest. The system has problems, and we need to address them."

"Some of us see that now," she said. "Because of you."

She walked away, leaving James alone in the hallway. He gathered his things slowly, feeling the exhaustion of the day settling into his bones. He had taken a stand. He had spoken truth to power, as the old phrase went. Whether it would make a difference, he didn't know.

He walked out of the building into the London evening. The air was cool, carrying the scent of rain. He stood on the steps, looking out at the city.

He thought about Sarah Chen. He thought about his certainty, and how it had failed. He thought about the algorithm, and the certainty it represented, and how it too would fail.

But he also thought about what he had said to the board. Judgment could not be automated. It had to be human, and it had to be humble. It had to acknowledge uncertainty, recognize fallibility, remain open to correction.

That was what he had learned, after all these years. That was what faith—no, not faith, something else, something smaller but still real—that was what kept him going.

He walked toward the tube station, his briefcase in his hand. The city moved around him, indifferent to his concerns. But somewhere out there, the algorithm would be changed. Somewhere, people who had been invisible would be seen.

It wasn't enough. It would never be enough. But it was a start.
