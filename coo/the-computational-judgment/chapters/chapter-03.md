# Chapter 3: The Criteria

The debate began immediately.

"We should optimize for social contribution," one board member argued. Marcus Webb, the lawyer from the Ministry of Justice, leaned forward in his chair. "Productivity, community involvement, civic engagement. These are measurable outcomes. They indicate citizens who contribute to society and should receive priority for resources."

"That discriminates against the disabled, the elderly, the marginalized," Helen Okoro countered. Her voice was sharp, her posture rigid. "We need to optimize for need, not contribution. The people who need help the most are often the ones who can contribute the least. That's precisely why they need help."

James listened, his mind racing. These were the questions he had studied for twenty years. But now they weren't theoretical. Now they would be encoded into an algorithm that would judge millions.

The board room was warmer today, or perhaps it just felt that way. The overhead lights cast a sterile glow across the polished table. James could smell coffee from the carafe in the corner, bitter and strong. His notebook lay open before him, pages already filled with questions.

"Let's be clear about what we're doing," Dr. Priya Sharma said from her position at the head of the table. She had not yet connected her laptop to the screen; today was for discussion, not demonstration. "The algorithm can optimize for any outcome we choose. The question is: what outcome do we want?"

"Efficiency," said a man named David Chen, representing the Treasury. His voice was calm, measured, the voice of someone accustomed to discussing budgets and projections. "We have limited resources. We should allocate them where they will do the most good. That means prioritizing citizens who are likely to achieve positive outcomes—stable employment, good health, productive lives."

"Define 'productive,'" Helen said. "Define 'positive outcomes.' A person with a severe disability may never be employed in the traditional sense. Does that mean they're less deserving of healthcare? Less deserving of housing?"

David shifted in his seat. "I'm not saying that. I'm saying we have to make difficult choices. We can't help everyone equally. The algorithm helps us make those choices systematically."

"Systematically," James said. The word hung in the air. "That's the problem, isn't it? A system that makes choices systematically also discriminates systematically. The question is whether that discrimination is fair."

All eyes turned to him. James felt the familiar pressure of a room waiting for him to speak. He had felt it in pulpits, in lecture halls, in seminars. The expectation that he would have something wise to say.

"When I was a pastor," he continued, "I used to preach about judgment. I believed there was a standard beyond human opinion—a divine standard that saw the heart, that understood circumstances, that judged fairly because it knew everything." He paused. "I don't believe that anymore. But I still believe the question matters. What standard are we using? Who set it? And who does it serve?"

Dr. Fiona Campbell, the chair, nodded slowly. "These are exactly the questions we need to address. Let's break this down. What values should guide our criteria?"

The discussion that followed was both exhausting and illuminating. Each board member brought their own perspective, their own priorities, their own understanding of what was fair.

Marcus Webb argued for contribution-based criteria. "Citizens who contribute to society—through work, through taxes, through community service—have earned priority. That's not discrimination. That's reciprocity."

Helen Okoro pushed back. "Reciprocity assumes everyone has the same capacity to contribute. They don't. A person born with a severe disability hasn't chosen to contribute less. Why should they be punished for circumstances beyond their control?"

"Because resources are limited," David Chen said. "If we allocate resources to someone who will never be able to contribute, we're taking those resources away from someone who might. That's the trade-off. We can't escape it."

James thought about Sarah Chen. She had been young, struggling, living with her boyfriend outside of marriage. She had been contributing, in her own way—working part-time, volunteering at a community garden. But she had been struggling too, with depression, with the weight of expectations, with the judgment of her church.

If the algorithm had assessed her, what score would she have received? Would it have seen her contributions? Or would it have seen only her struggles—her part-time employment, her mental health challenges, her unconventional living situation?

"The problem with contribution-based criteria," James said, "is that they assume we can measure contribution objectively. We can't. What about a mother who stays home to care for her children? What about an artist who creates work that enriches society but doesn't generate income? What about a person who volunteers their time but doesn't have a paying job? The algorithm will count some contributions and ignore others. Whose contributions count?"

Dr. Sharma spoke up. "That's a technical question, actually. We can include any factors the board decides are relevant. Unpaid care work, volunteer hours, community engagement—we can weight these however you choose."

"But we have to choose," Helen said. "That's the point. We're deciding what counts. We're deciding whose contributions matter. And those decisions reflect our values, whether we admit it or not."

The debate circled around several key tensions:

Efficiency versus equity. Should resources go to those who would benefit most, or to those who needed them most? The two were not always the same.

Individual responsibility versus structural circumstances. Should the algorithm consider why someone was unemployed, or only that they were? Should it account for discrimination, for illness, for family circumstances? Or should it treat all citizens as equally responsible for their own situations?

Short-term outcomes versus long-term potential. Should the algorithm prioritize immediate needs, or invest in citizens who might contribute more in the future?

Measurability versus meaning. Some factors were easy to measure—employment, income, education. Others were harder—character, resilience, potential. Should the algorithm focus on what was measurable, even if it missed what mattered?

James found himself arguing for nuance. "The algorithm will make decisions about real people," he said. "People with complicated lives, with circumstances that don't fit neatly into categories. If we reduce them to data points, we lose something essential. We lose the ability to see them as individuals."

"We can't assess every citizen individually," David Chen replied. "That's the point of the algorithm. It scales. It can process millions of assessments in the time it would take a human to process one."

"But at what cost?" James asked. "Efficiency isn't the only value. Sometimes the right decision takes time. Sometimes it requires understanding context that data can't capture."

"Then what do you propose?" Marcus Webb asked. "We can't go back to the old system—inconsistent, slow, biased in its own ways. We need something better."

James didn't have an answer. He wasn't sure there was one.

The meeting stretched into the afternoon. They debated specific criteria: Should criminal records count? For how long? Should mental health history be a factor? Should family composition matter? Should the algorithm consider the circumstances of someone's birth—their parents' income, their neighborhood, their access to education?

Each question led to more questions. Each answer revealed new complications.

Dr. Campbell finally called a break. "We're not going to resolve this today," she said. "Let's take some time to think. Write down your recommendations. We'll reconvene next week and try to reach some decisions."

James walked out of the building into the grey London afternoon. The air was cool, carrying the smell of rain that hadn't yet fallen. He stood on the steps, watching the city move around him—people walking, cars passing, the endless rhythm of urban life.

He thought about the criteria they were debating. He thought about the values they were encoding. He thought about the millions of people who would be judged by the system they were designing.

*When I was a pastor*, he thought, *I believed in a God who judged fairly because God knew everything. Now I'm helping build a system that judges based on what we can measure. And what we can measure is not the same as what matters.*

He walked toward the tube station, his briefcase heavy in his hand. The weight of the responsibility pressed on him. He had spent his career asking difficult questions. Now he was being asked to provide answers. And he wasn't sure he had any.

His phone buzzed. A text from Adanna: *How was the meeting?*

He typed a reply: *Difficult. Lots of questions. No answers.*

A pause, then: *The children want to know if you'll be home for dinner.*

*Yes*, he typed. *I'll be there.*

He put his phone away and continued walking. The tube station was ahead, its entrance spilling light onto the grey street. He would go home, eat dinner with his family, pretend that everything was normal. And tomorrow, he would return to the university, teach his classes, ask his students questions about ethics.

But underneath it all, he would be thinking about the algorithm. About the criteria. About the values they were encoding. About the millions of lives that would be shaped by decisions made in a sterile board room by people who would never know their names.

The meeting had ended without consensus. The criteria remained undecided. But James had glimpsed the fundamental problem: any criteria the board chose would reflect values, and values were not neutral. They would be shaped by who was in the room, by what they believed, by what they thought mattered. The algorithm would be fair only if the values it encoded were fair.

And who got to decide that?

The question followed him onto the tube, through the dark tunnels, toward home and the people he loved and the doubts that never left him.
