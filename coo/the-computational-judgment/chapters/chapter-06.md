# Chapter 6: The Public

The news broke before the board was ready. Someone had leaked information about the judgment algorithm, and the media was in a frenzy. Headlines proclaimed: "AI to Judge Citizens' Worth" "Algorithm Will Decide Who Gets Resources" "The End of Human Discretion?" James watched the coverage, knowing the reality was more complex than the headlines suggested.

He sat in his office at the university, his computer screen filled with news articles. The story had exploded across every major outlet—the BBC, The Guardian, The Times, even international publications. The government had tried to keep the project quiet until the board had finished its work, but someone had talked. Now the public knew, and the reaction was swift and polarized.

His phone buzzed. A text from Adanna: *Are you seeing this?*

He typed a reply: *Yes. It's going to be a long day.*

Another buzz. This time from Reverend Michael Adebayo: *James, call me when you can. I have questions.*

James set his phone down and rubbed his eyes. He had known this would happen eventually. The algorithm was too significant, too consequential, to remain hidden. But he had hoped for more time—time to analyze the bias, time to propose changes, time to shape the system before it went public.

Now that time was gone.

He clicked on a video from a morning news program. A presenter was interviewing a government spokesperson, who was trying to frame the algorithm as a tool for fairness.

"This system will eliminate human bias," the spokesperson said. "Currently, decisions about resource allocation are made by caseworkers who may have unconscious prejudices. The algorithm will apply consistent standards to everyone, ensuring that decisions are based on objective criteria."

"But critics say the algorithm itself encodes bias," the presenter countered. "That it will disadvantage the disabled, the elderly, the marginalized."

"The algorithm is being designed with input from ethics experts," the spokesperson replied. "We're committed to ensuring fairness."

James thought about the test cases he had seen. The elderly woman with a score of 34. The disabled woman with a score of 22. The artist with a score of 41. The algorithm was not fair. It was consistent, yes—but consistently biased.

He clicked to another article. This one was from a technology publication, titled "The Promise and Peril of Algorithmic Governance." The author laid out both sides of the debate: the efficiency and consistency of algorithmic decision-making, versus the risk of encoding bias at scale.

James read the comments section, against his better judgment. The opinions were divided, often angry.

*Finally, a system that can't be bribed or influenced. This is progress.*

*Another way for the powerful to control the rest of us. They'll game the algorithm just like they game everything else.*

*If it's based on data, it's fair. Data doesn't lie.*

*Data reflects the biases of the people who collect it. Wake up.*

His phone rang. It was Dr. Fiona Campbell, the board chair.

"James," she said, her voice tight. "Have you seen the news?"

"Yes. The leak."

"We're being called to an emergency meeting this afternoon. The Minister wants a statement. I need you there."

"I'll be there."

He hung up and stared at the screen. The algorithm was being debated in public now, stripped of nuance, reduced to headlines and sound bites. The complexity of the questions—the weightings, the criteria, the values encoded in the system—would be lost in the noise.

He thought about Sarah Chen. He thought about the certainty he had once carried, the belief that he knew what was right. He thought about the damage that certainty had caused.

The algorithm was a different kind of certainty—the certainty of numbers, of data, of mathematical objectivity. But it was still certainty. It still claimed to see clearly, to judge fairly. And it was still blind to the things it couldn't measure.

James gathered his things and left his office. The university corridors were quiet, students and faculty absorbed in their own concerns. He walked toward the tube station, his mind churning.

The emergency meeting was held in a conference room at the Department for Science, Innovation and Technology. The board members sat around a table, their faces tense. Government officials stood along the walls, watching.

"The Minister wants a statement by end of day," Dr. Campbell said. "We need to reassure the public that the system is being designed responsibly."

"The system has problems," James said. "We've seen the test cases. The bias is real."

"We can address that," Marcus Webb said. "We can adjust the weightings, add safeguards. But we need to show the public that we're taking their concerns seriously."

"We need to show them the truth," Helen Okoro countered. "The algorithm is not neutral. It encodes values. We should be transparent about that."

"Transparency will fuel the critics," a government official said. "We need to control the narrative."

James felt his chest tighten. Control the narrative. The phrase echoed in his mind. It was the same language he had heard in the church, when leaders wanted to manage perception rather than address problems.

"I won't lie to the public," he said. "The algorithm has problems. We've seen the data. If we pretend otherwise, we're no better than the systems we're supposed to be overseeing."

The room fell silent. Dr. Campbell looked at him, her expression unreadable.

"No one is asking you to lie, James," she said. "But we need to be strategic. We can acknowledge concerns while emphasizing the safeguards we're implementing."

"What safeguards?" James asked. "We haven't implemented anything yet. We've barely begun to address the bias."

"Then let's propose safeguards," Dr. Campbell said. "Let's show the public that we're taking action."

The discussion continued for hours. The board debated what to say, how to frame the issues, what commitments to make. James pushed for transparency, for honesty about the problems they had discovered. Others pushed for reassurance, for emphasis on the potential benefits of the system.

In the end, they produced a statement that tried to balance both. The algorithm would be reviewed for bias. Safeguards would be implemented. The board was committed to fairness. The language was careful, bureaucratic, designed to calm rather than inform.

James signed off on it, but he felt uneasy. The statement was not false, but it was not fully true either. It acknowledged problems without revealing their depth. It promised solutions without specifying what they would be.

After the meeting, he walked out of the building into the late afternoon. The London sky was grey, the air cool. His phone buzzed with interview requests—journalists who wanted to hear from the ethics professor on the AI governance board.

He should decline. He should let the official statement speak for itself. But he knew that would be a mistake. The public deserved to hear more than bureaucratic language. They deserved to understand what was at stake.

He accepted the first interview. A reporter from The Guardian met him at a café near the university.

"Dr. Okonkwo," the reporter said, setting up her recorder. "Can you explain what this algorithm does?"

James took a breath. "The algorithm assesses citizens for resource allocation—housing, healthcare, education. It produces a score based on factors like employment, income, education, health, and criminal record. Citizens with higher scores receive priority for resources."

"And is the system fair?"

"That's the question we're wrestling with. The algorithm is consistent—it applies the same standards to everyone. But consistency is not the same as fairness. The factors the algorithm considers are shaped by structural inequalities. Employment, income, education—these are not neutral. They reflect historical disadvantages that some groups have faced."

"So the algorithm could discriminate?"

"It could reproduce existing inequalities at scale. That's what we're trying to prevent. The board is reviewing the algorithm for bias, and we're considering changes to the weightings and criteria."

The interview continued for thirty minutes. James answered questions about the technology, the ethics, the safeguards. He tried to be honest without being alarmist. He tried to explain the complexity without losing the listener.

When it was over, the reporter thanked him. "One more question, off the record. Do you think this system should exist?"

James hesitated. "That's not for me to decide. My job is to ensure that if it exists, it's as fair as possible."

The reporter nodded and packed up her equipment. James sat alone at the table, his coffee cold, his mind heavy.

He gave interview after interview that week. Each one was an attempt to explain the nuances—the potential benefits of algorithmic governance, the risks of encoded bias, the work the board was doing to address the problems. He spoke to newspapers, radio programs, even a television news segment.

The public reaction was mixed. Some praised the government for using technology to improve efficiency. Others condemned the algorithm as a tool of oppression. The debate played out in headlines, in social media, in parliamentary questions.

James watched it all, feeling the weight of his role. He was not just an observer. He was part of the system. He had a responsibility to shape it, to make it better, to prevent the harm he could see coming.

He thought about Sarah Chen. He thought about the certainty that had killed her. He thought about the algorithm, and the certainty it represented—the certainty of numbers, of data, of mathematical judgment.

*The question was not whether to use AI for judgment*, he thought. *The question was how to ensure that the judgment was fair.*

And that question had no easy answers.

He walked home through the London evening, the city lights beginning to glow around him. Somewhere out there, the algorithm was being debated, dissected, defended and attacked. Somewhere out there, millions of people were learning that a machine would judge their worth.

James couldn't save everyone. He couldn't fix everything. But he could keep asking questions. He could keep pushing for transparency. He could keep fighting for the people the algorithm would overlook.

It wasn't certainty. It wasn't faith. But it was something.
