# Chapter 5: The Test Cases

The test cases were meant to validate the algorithm, to show that it worked as intended. But as James watched the results populate the screen, he felt a growing sense of horror. The algorithm was making decisions that seemed fair on the surface but revealed deep biases underneath.

The board room was quiet that morning. Dr. Priya Sharma stood at the front, her laptop connected to the large screen. She had prepared a series of anonymized profiles—real citizens whose data had been fed into the system. The goal was to demonstrate how the algorithm would assess different types of people.

"Let's start with a straightforward case," Dr. Sharma said. She clicked a button, and a profile appeared.

*Profile A:*
*Age: 42*
*Employment: Full-time, mid-level professional*
*Income: £52,000/year*
*Education: University degree*
*Health: Good*
*Criminal Record: None*
*Family: Married, two children*
*Community Engagement: Regular volunteer at local school*

*Algorithm Score: 78/100*
*Recommendation: Standard priority for all resources*

"This is what we'd call a 'solid contributor,'" Dr. Sharma explained. "Stable employment, good health, active in the community. The algorithm recognizes this person as a reliable citizen who contributes to society."

James nodded slowly. This made sense. A person with stable employment and good health would likely need fewer resources and could contribute more. The score seemed reasonable.

"Now let's look at someone with greater needs," Dr. Sharma continued. She clicked to the next profile.

*Profile B:*
*Age: 67*
*Employment: Retired*
*Income: £18,000/year (pension)*
*Education: Secondary school*
*Health: Multiple chronic conditions*
*Criminal Record: None*
*Family: Widowed, one adult child*
*Community Engagement: Limited (health issues)*

*Algorithm Score: 34/100*
*Recommendation: Low priority for housing, standard priority for healthcare*

James leaned forward. "Thirty-four? This person is elderly, in poor health, on a limited income. Why such a low score?"

Dr. Sharma adjusted her glasses. "The algorithm considers potential contribution as well as need. This person has limited capacity to contribute to society—they're retired, in poor health, with limited community engagement. The score reflects that."

"But they need help," Helen Okoro said from across the table. "That's precisely when they should receive priority."

"The algorithm does give them standard priority for healthcare," Dr. Sharma replied. "But for housing and other resources, the system prioritizes citizens who can benefit more from assistance."

"Benefit more," James repeated. "You mean citizens who can become productive again."

"Essentially, yes."

James felt his chest tighten. He thought about his mother, who had died three years ago at seventy-one. She had been in declining health, unable to work, dependent on her pension and the support of her children. Under this system, she would have been classified as low priority. Her needs would have been deprioritized because she couldn't "contribute."

"Let's see another case," Dr. Campbell said, her voice neutral.

Dr. Sharma clicked to the next profile.

*Profile C:*
*Age: 29*
*Employment: Part-time, freelance artist*
*Income: £14,000/year (variable)*
*Education: Art college*
*Health: Good*
*Criminal Record: None*
*Family: Single, no children*
*Community Engagement: Active in local arts community*

*Algorithm Score: 41/100*
*Recommendation: Low priority for housing, low priority for other resources*

"Forty-one?" James said. "This person is young, healthy, engaged in their community. Why such a low score?"

"Income instability," Dr. Sharma replied. "The algorithm weights stable employment heavily. Freelance work, variable income—these are indicators of economic unreliability. The system prioritizes citizens with predictable, stable income."

"But this person contributes to society," Helen said. "They're an artist. They enrich the community."

"Contribution is measured in economic terms," Dr. Sharma said. "Volunteer work, community engagement—these are factors, but they're weighted lower than employment and income."

James thought about the artists he had known, the musicians and writers and painters who enriched the world in ways that couldn't be measured in pounds. He thought about the cultural life of the city, the galleries and theaters and concert halls that made life worth living. Under this system, the people who created that culture would be classified as low priority.

"Let's see a few more cases," Dr. Campbell said.

Dr. Sharma clicked through several more profiles. Each one revealed something troubling.

A single mother who worked part-time while caring for her disabled child: Score 38. The algorithm penalized her for limited employment, even though her caregiving work was essential.

A man with a criminal record from fifteen years ago, now employed and active in his community: Score 31. The criminal record weighed heavily, despite his rehabilitation.

A disabled woman who had never been able to work: Score 22. The algorithm classified her as low priority across all categories.

A recent immigrant with limited English skills: Score 29. The language barrier affected employment prospects, which affected the score, which would affect access to resources.

James watched the numbers accumulate, and with each profile, his sense of horror grew. The algorithm was systematically devaluing people who didn't fit a narrow definition of productivity. It penalized people for circumstances beyond their control—disability, age, family responsibilities, past mistakes. It encoded assumptions about what made a life valuable, and those assumptions favored the already privileged.

"Can we see the weightings?" he asked. "The factors the algorithm considers most important?"

Dr. Sharma hesitated, then clicked to a new screen. A chart appeared, showing the relative importance of different factors.

*Factor Weights:*
*Employment status: 25%*
*Income level: 20%*
*Education: 15%*
*Health status: 15%*
*Family composition: 10%*
*Criminal record: 10%*
*Community engagement: 5%*

James stared at the numbers. Employment and income accounted for nearly half of the total score. Education and health—factors that were often determined by birth and circumstance—accounted for another thirty percent. Community engagement, the factor that might capture contributions beyond economic productivity, was weighted at only five percent.

"This is the problem," he said. "The algorithm values what can be measured in economic terms. It doesn't see the full picture of a person's life."

"It's designed to predict outcomes," Dr. Sharma said. "Economic stability, health outcomes, social contribution—these are the metrics we can measure. The algorithm optimizes for what we can quantify."

"But what we can quantify isn't the same as what matters," James countered. "A caregiver who stays home with an elderly parent contributes to society. An artist who creates beauty contributes to society. A person with a disability who lives with dignity contributes to society. But the algorithm doesn't see that."

"The algorithm sees the data we give it," Dr. Sharma said. Her voice had an edge now. "If you want it to value different things, we can adjust the weights. That's what this board is for."

James looked around the table. The other board members were watching, their expressions varied—Helen Okoro with concern, Marcus Webb with interest, David Chen with the careful neutrality of a Treasury official.

"Let me ask a different question," James said. "What happens to the people with low scores? The ones the algorithm classifies as low priority?"

"They're placed at the bottom of waiting lists," Dr. Sharma replied. "They may be directed to alternative services—charitable organizations, community support."

"And if those services don't exist?"

"Then they may not receive assistance."

James thought about Sarah Chen. She would have had a low score—young, struggling, with mental health challenges. The algorithm would have classified her as low priority. It would have directed her to the bottom of waiting lists. It would have told her she wasn't worth investing in.

And she had died.

"I want to see more test cases," James said. "Specifically, I want to see cases that might reveal bias—disability, race, gender, age."

Dr. Sharma's expression tightened. "The algorithm doesn't consider race or gender directly. Those factors are not in the data."

"But they're correlated with factors that are in the data," James said. "Employment, income, education, criminal records—these are all shaped by structural inequalities. If the algorithm weights these factors heavily, it will reproduce those inequalities."

"The algorithm is neutral," Dr. Sharma said. "It processes data objectively."

James stood up. He walked to the window, looking out at the grey London sky. The city spread out before him, millions of lives intersecting and diverging, each one complex and valuable in ways that couldn't be captured by a numerical score.

"Let me tell you something," he said, turning back to face the room. "When I was a pastor, I believed I knew what was right. I believed I could see clearly enough to judge. I was wrong. My certainty hurt people. It killed someone."

The room was silent. James could feel the weight of his words.

"This algorithm is doing the same thing," he continued. "It's pretending to see clearly. It's pretending to be neutral. But it's not neutral. It's encoding the values of the people who designed it, and those values favor some people over others. It's hiding bias behind numbers, and calling it fairness."

"The algorithm doesn't have bias," Dr. Sharma said. "It's math."

"Math created by humans," James countered. "And humans have biases. The algorithm just hides them behind numbers."

He returned to his seat. The room was quiet, the weight of the moment pressing on everyone.

Dr. Campbell cleared her throat. "This is exactly the kind of analysis we need. Dr. Sharma, can you prepare a report on the algorithm's performance across different demographic groups? We need to see if there are patterns of disadvantage."

"I can do that," Dr. Sharma said. Her voice was tight, but she nodded.

"And we need to discuss the weightings," Helen Okoro added. "If employment and income are weighted too heavily, we need to adjust them. We need to decide what we actually value."

The meeting continued, but James barely heard the details. His mind was racing, processing what he had seen. The algorithm was worse than he had feared. It wasn't just making decisions—it was encoding a particular worldview, a particular set of values, and presenting them as neutral and objective.

He thought about the millions of people who would be judged by this system. He thought about the caregivers, the artists, the disabled, the elderly, the people who didn't fit the narrow definition of productivity. He thought about Sarah Chen, and all the others like her, who would be told they weren't worth investing in.

And he knew, with a clarity that cut through his usual uncertainty, that he couldn't let this stand. He had to do something. He had to change the system, or expose it, or both.

The meeting ended with plans for further analysis, further discussion. James gathered his things slowly, his mind still churning. He walked out of the building into the cold London air, the weight of what he had seen pressing on him like a physical burden.

He had come to the board hoping to do good, hoping to make a difference. Now he understood the scale of the challenge. The algorithm wasn't just a technical system. It was a moral project, an attempt to encode values into code. And the values it encoded were not the values he believed in.

He walked toward the tube station, his briefcase heavy in his hand. The city moved around him, indifferent to his concerns. Somewhere out there, the algorithm was already being tested, already being prepared for deployment. Somewhere out there, millions of lives were about to be judged by a machine that couldn't see them clearly.

James had to stop it. Or at least, he had to try.
