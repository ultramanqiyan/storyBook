# Chapter 2: The System

The board room was sleek, modern, intimidating. James sat among the other members—lawyers, ethicists, AI specialists, a representative from civil society. The table was polished wood, dark and gleaming, reflecting the overhead lights. Through the window, James could see the London skyline, grey buildings against a greyer sky. The government building smelled of nothing—filtered, neutral, appropriate for spaces where important decisions were made.

Dr. Priya Sharma stood at the front, ready to explain the system they would oversee. She was thirty-eight, compact and energetic, with the precise movements of someone accustomed to technical presentations. Her laptop was connected to the large screen behind her, displaying a simple title: *The Citizen Assessment Framework*.

"Thank you all for being here," she began. "I know this is new for most of you. I'll try to make this as clear as possible."

James glanced around the table. To his left sat Dr. Fiona Campbell, the board chair, fifty-eight, with silver hair and the careful expression of someone who had navigated government committees for decades. To his right, a man named Marcus Webb, a lawyer from the Ministry of Justice, who had introduced himself with a brief, professional nod. Across the table, a woman named Helen Okoro, representing civil society organizations, was taking notes on a tablet. The others—two more ethicists, a data scientist, a representative from the Treasury—sat with varying degrees of attention.

"Let's start with the problem we're trying to solve," Dr. Sharma continued. She clicked a button, and a new slide appeared. "Public resources are limited. Housing, healthcare, education, social support—we can't provide everything to everyone. Decisions have to be made about who receives priority."

She clicked again. A chart appeared, showing a complex web of connections.

"Currently, these decisions are made by humans. Caseworkers, administrators, committees. The process is slow, inconsistent, and vulnerable to bias. Different regions use different criteria. Different caseworkers make different decisions about similar cases. The system is unfair because it's unpredictable."

James shifted in his chair. The leather creaked softly. He thought about the caseworkers he had known, the ones who decided which families received support and which did not. He thought about the randomness of it all—the luck of which caseworker you got, which office, which day of the week.

"The Citizen Assessment Framework," Dr. Sharma said, "is designed to bring consistency, transparency, and efficiency to this process. It uses machine learning to assess citizens' needs and contributions, producing a score that guides resource allocation."

"A score," James said. The word came out before he could stop it. The room turned toward him.

Dr. Sharma nodded. "Yes. A numerical score, between zero and one hundred. Higher scores indicate greater need and greater potential contribution to society. Lower scores indicate the opposite."

"And who decides what counts as contribution?" James asked.

"That's where this board comes in." Dr. Sharma smiled, but it didn't quite reach her eyes. "The algorithm learns from historical data. But we can adjust the weights. We can decide which factors matter more. The board's role is to ensure the values embedded in the system align with societal values."

She clicked to the next slide. A list appeared:

*Factors Considered:*
- *Employment history*
- *Income level*
- *Education*
- *Health status*
- *Family composition*
- *Criminal record*
- *Community engagement*
- *Social connections*

"The algorithm analyzes these factors and produces a score," Dr. Sharma explained. "Citizens with higher scores receive priority for resources. Housing assistance, healthcare interventions, educational opportunities—the system guides these decisions."

Helen Okoro leaned forward. "How many citizens will be assessed?"

"All of them," Dr. Sharma replied. "Every citizen who applies for public resources. Eventually, every citizen, period. The goal is comprehensive coverage."

James felt a chill run through him. Every citizen. Millions of people, reduced to a score. He thought of his children, Chidi and Ngozi. He thought of their scores, whatever they might be. He thought of Sarah Chen, who would have had a score, if she were still alive.

"Let me show you how it works," Dr. Sharma said. She clicked to a new slide, showing a sample profile.

*Sample Assessment:*
*Name: [Anonymous]*
*Age: 34*
*Employment: Part-time*
*Income: £18,000/year*
*Education: GCSE*
*Health: Chronic condition (managed)*
*Criminal Record: None*
*Community Engagement: Moderate*
*Social Connections: Strong*

*Score: 67/100*
*Recommendation: Priority for housing assistance, standard priority for healthcare*

"This is a real assessment, anonymized," Dr. Sharma said. "The algorithm has determined that this citizen has moderate need and moderate potential contribution. They qualify for priority housing assistance because their current housing situation is inadequate. Their healthcare priority is standard because their condition is managed."

Marcus Webb, the lawyer, spoke up. "What happens if someone disagrees with their score?"

"There's an appeals process," Dr. Fiona Campbell interjected. "Citizens can request a review. A human caseworker will reassess the decision."

"But the algorithm's score is the starting point," James said. It wasn't a question.

Dr. Campbell nodded. "Yes. The algorithm provides a baseline. Humans can override it, but the system is designed to minimize the need for human intervention. That's where the efficiency comes from."

James turned back to Dr. Sharma. "You said the algorithm learns from historical data. What data?"

"Government records, primarily. Employment records from HMRC, health records from the NHS, criminal records from the police, educational records from schools and universities. Plus voluntary data from citizens who choose to share additional information."

"And the historical decisions made by human caseworkers?"

Dr. Sharma hesitated. "Yes. The algorithm learns from past decisions to predict appropriate outcomes."

"So if past decisions were biased," James said slowly, "the algorithm will learn that bias."

"The algorithm doesn't have bias," Dr. Sharma said quickly. "It processes data objectively. Any patterns in the output reflect patterns in the input data, not bias in the algorithm itself."

James felt his chest tighten. He had heard this argument before, from technologists who believed that code was neutral. But code was written by humans. Humans chose what data to include, what factors to weight, what outcomes to optimize. The neutrality was an illusion.

"Let me ask a different question," James said. "What happens to someone with a low score? Say, someone who scores below thirty?"

Dr. Sharma clicked to another slide. "Citizens with scores below thirty are classified as 'low priority.' They may still receive resources, but they're placed at the bottom of waiting lists. In some cases, they may be directed to alternative services—charitable organizations, community support, rather than government programs."

"And if there aren't alternative services?"

"Then they may not receive assistance." Dr. Sharma's voice was matter-of-fact. "Resources are limited. The system ensures they go to those who need them most and can benefit from them most."

James thought about that. *Can benefit from them most.* The phrase was careful, clinical. It meant: those who are most likely to become productive, contributing members of society. It meant: those who are worth investing in.

"Who decides what counts as benefit?" he asked.

"The algorithm determines potential outcomes based on historical patterns," Dr. Sharma replied. "Citizens with certain characteristics are more likely to achieve positive outcomes—stable employment, good health, community engagement. The algorithm identifies those characteristics and weights them accordingly."

"So if someone is unemployed, has a chronic illness, and limited education," James said, "they're less likely to receive help because they're less likely to 'benefit' from it?"

Dr. Sharma shifted slightly. "The algorithm considers multiple factors. It's not that simple."

"But in essence," James pressed, "the system prioritizes people who are already doing well. People who are struggling—who need help the most—may receive less assistance because the algorithm doesn't think they're a good investment."

The room was quiet. James could feel the weight of the question hanging in the air. Dr. Campbell, the chair, cleared her throat.

"This is exactly the kind of discussion we need to have," she said. "These are the questions the board was created to address. Dr. Sharma, perhaps you could explain the adjustment mechanisms?"

Dr. Sharma nodded, recovering her composure. "Yes. The board can adjust the weights assigned to different factors. If you believe the algorithm is undervaluing certain characteristics—need, for example, rather than potential—you can instruct us to recalibrate."

"And how do we do that?" Helen Okoro asked. "How do we know what weights to set?"

"That's where your expertise comes in," Dr. Campbell said. "You're ethicists, lawyers, representatives of civil society. You understand the values we want to encode. The technical team will implement your decisions."

James looked around the table. He saw the other ethicists nodding, the lawyer making notes, the civil society representative frowning thoughtfully. They were being asked to decide what values should guide the judgment of millions of people. They were being asked to encode morality into a machine.

He thought of his years in the church, preaching about divine judgment. He had believed then that God's judgment was perfect because God could see the heart. Now he was being asked to design a system that could not see the heart, that could only see data points, and to pretend that it was fair.

The meeting continued for another hour. Dr. Sharma explained the technical architecture—the machine learning models, the data pipelines, the security protocols. She showed them the interface that caseworkers would use, the dashboards that would display aggregate statistics, the audit trails that would track every decision.

James listened, asked questions, took notes. His pen moved across the paper, the familiar scratch grounding him in the moment. He wrote down terms like "feature importance" and "outcome prediction" and "fairness metrics." He drew diagrams connecting the pieces of the system.

But underneath the technical language, he kept returning to the same question: *What are we really doing?*

When the meeting finally ended, James gathered his things slowly. The other board members filed out, chatting quietly about schedules and next steps. Dr. Campbell touched his arm as she passed.

"Good questions today," she said. "We need people who will push back."

James nodded, not trusting himself to speak. He walked out of the building into the London afternoon. The air was cold, sharp in his lungs. He stood on the steps, looking out at the city.

Somewhere out there, millions of people were living their lives. They were working, or looking for work. They were raising children, caring for parents, struggling with illness, fighting to stay afloat. They didn't know that a committee was meeting to decide how machines would judge them. They didn't know that their worth was being reduced to a score.

James thought of Sarah Chen again. She would have had a low score, he suspected. Young, struggling, living outside conventional norms. The algorithm might have classified her as low priority. It might have directed her to the bottom of waiting lists. It might have decided she wasn't worth investing in.

And she had died.

He walked toward the tube station, his briefcase heavy in his hand. The weight of the responsibility pressed on him like a physical burden. He had agreed to serve on this board. He had agreed to help design this system. He had told himself it was better to be in the room, to ask questions, to push back.

But now he wondered if that was enough. Now he wondered if any amount of adjustment could make a system like this fair. Now he wondered if the very act of reducing human worth to a score was itself a kind of violence.

The tube station was crowded. James joined the flow of commuters, moving toward the platform. The train arrived, and he stepped aboard, finding a seat by the window. The doors closed, and the train began to move.

Through the window, he watched the tunnel walls slide past. He thought about the algorithm, about the factors it considered, about the values it encoded. He thought about the board's role, about the power they had been given, about the responsibility they bore.

*When I was a pastor*, he thought, *I believed judgment belonged to God. Now I'm helping build a machine that will judge millions of people. And I don't know if I'm doing the right thing.*

He didn't have an answer. He wasn't sure there was one. But he knew he couldn't walk away. Not yet. Not until he understood what they were really building.

The train carried him home, through the dark tunnels beneath the city, toward his family and his doubts and the work that lay ahead.
