# Chapter 7: The Bias

James spent nights analyzing the algorithm, running his own tests, looking for patterns the official reports had missed. What he found was worse than he had imagined. The bias wasn't just in the criteria. It was in the data itself, in the assumptions underlying the model, in the very definition of "social contribution."

He sat in his study at home, the house quiet around him. Adanna and the children were asleep. His laptop glowed in the darkness, displaying data he had requested from Dr. Sharma—demographic breakdowns of the algorithm's assessments, historical patterns in the training data, the mathematical foundations of the model.

He had started with a simple question: why did the algorithm produce the scores it did? The official explanation was that the algorithm learned from historical data, identifying patterns that predicted positive outcomes. But James wanted to understand what patterns it was identifying, and why.

He had begun by examining the training data. The algorithm had been trained on ten years of government records—decisions made by human caseworkers about who received resources and who did not. The assumption was that these decisions represented a reasonable baseline, that the algorithm could learn from past practice and improve upon it.

But James saw the flaw immediately. The historical decisions were not neutral. They were made by humans, with all the biases and blind spots that implied. Caseworkers had made judgments based on limited information, cultural assumptions, and institutional pressures. Those judgments had been shaped by the same structural inequalities that shaped everything else—race, class, gender, disability.

And the algorithm had learned those patterns. It had encoded them in mathematical form, and now it would reproduce them at scale.

James created his own test cases, feeding them into a simulation of the algorithm. He designed profiles that were identical in every way except for one variable—race, gender, disability status, age. He wanted to see if the algorithm would produce different scores for people who were otherwise the same.

The results were damning.

A profile with a Nigerian name received a score three points lower than an identical profile with a British name. A profile indicating a disability received a score twelve points lower than an identical profile without. A profile indicating female gender received a score two points lower than an identical male profile. A profile indicating age over sixty received a score eight points lower than an identical younger profile.

The algorithm didn't explicitly consider race or gender. But it considered factors that were correlated with race and gender—employment history, income, education, criminal records. And because structural inequalities meant that certain groups were disadvantaged on these metrics, the algorithm reproduced those disadvantages.

James felt a cold anger building in his chest. This was not an accident. This was not a bug that could be fixed with a simple adjustment. This was the very nature of the system. The algorithm was a mirror, reflecting the inequalities of the society that had created it.

He dug deeper. He examined the definition of "social contribution" that the algorithm used. The term sounded neutral, objective. But when he traced it through the model, he found that it was defined almost entirely in economic terms. Employment. Income. Tax contributions. Economic productivity.

Care work—looking after children, elderly parents, disabled family members—was barely counted. Volunteer work was weighted at five percent. Community engagement was measured through formal organizations, not informal networks. The algorithm valued what could be easily measured, and what could be easily measured was what the market valued.

He thought about his mother, who had spent years caring for his father before he died. She had not been employed during that time. She had not earned income. But she had contributed—immeasurably—to the well-being of her family. Under this algorithm, she would have been classified as low priority. Her contribution would have been invisible.

He thought about Sarah Chen. She had been struggling, yes. But she had also been contributing—working part-time, volunteering at a community garden, supporting her friends. The algorithm would have seen her struggles. It would not have seen her contributions.

James stood up and walked to the window. The London night spread out before him, streetlights glowing in the darkness. Somewhere out there, millions of people were living lives that the algorithm could not see clearly. They were caring for family members, creating art, building communities, struggling with circumstances beyond their control. And the algorithm would judge them based on a narrow definition of value that favored the already privileged.

He returned to his laptop and continued his analysis. He found more problems.

The algorithm's prediction of "positive outcomes" was based on historical data about what happened to people who received resources. But that data was itself biased. People who had received resources in the past were more likely to have positive outcomes because they had received resources—not because they were inherently more deserving. The algorithm was learning to replicate past decisions, not to identify true need or potential.

The appeals process was designed to catch errors, but James saw that it would be difficult for citizens to navigate. They would need to understand how the algorithm worked, gather evidence to challenge its assessment, and present their case to a caseworker who might simply defer to the algorithm's judgment. The system was designed to minimize human intervention, which meant that errors would be difficult to correct.

And the transparency was limited. Citizens would receive a score, but they would not know exactly how it was calculated. They would not know which factors had counted against them, or what they could do to improve their score. The algorithm would be a black box, making decisions that affected their lives without explanation.

James closed his laptop. His eyes ached from hours of staring at the screen. His body was tired, but his mind was racing.

He had known, from the beginning, that the algorithm had problems. But he had not understood the depth of those problems until now. The bias was not superficial. It was structural. It was embedded in every layer of the system—in the data, in the criteria, in the assumptions about what counted and what didn't.

And the worst part was that the algorithm would present itself as neutral. It would claim to be objective, consistent, fair. It would hide its biases behind the veneer of mathematical authority. And people would believe it, because they wanted to believe that machines could be fair in ways that humans could not.

James thought about his years in the church. He had preached about divine judgment, about a God who saw the heart and judged fairly. He had believed that divine judgment was perfect because God was perfect. But human judgment—even human judgment encoded in algorithms—was not perfect. It was shaped by human limitations, human biases, human failures.

The algorithm was not God. It was not even close. It was a human creation, with all the flaws that implied. And it would judge millions of people based on those flaws.

He knew what he had to do. He had to expose the bias. He had to make the board understand the depth of the problem. He had to push for fundamental changes—or, if that failed, for the system to be abandoned.

But he also knew the risks. He was one voice on a board of twelve. The government wanted this system to work. The technology companies had invested millions. The narrative of progress, of efficiency, of technological solutionism was powerful. Going against it would be difficult, maybe even dangerous to his position.

He thought about Sarah Chen again. He had stayed silent when he should have spoken. He had been certain when he should have questioned. He had hurt someone because he believed he was right.

He would not make that mistake again.

James opened his laptop and began to write. He wrote a report documenting his findings—the demographic disparities, the structural biases, the flawed assumptions. He wrote clearly, methodically, with the precision of an academic and the urgency of someone who had seen the truth.

He would present this report at the next board meeting. He would force the board to confront what they were building. He would demand changes, or he would resign and go public with what he knew.

The night stretched on, dark and quiet. James wrote until dawn, his words accumulating like evidence in a case he was building against the system. When he finally stopped, the sky outside his window was beginning to lighten, the first grey light of morning seeping into the room.

The algorithm was a mirror. It reflected the values of the society that had created it—values that prioritized productivity over care, efficiency over compassion, individual achievement over community. It encoded these values in mathematical form and then presented them as neutral, objective, fair.

But they were none of those things. They were human values, with all the biases and blind spots that implied. And James had to decide what to do with that knowledge.

He had decided. He would act.
