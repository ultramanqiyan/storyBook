# Chapter 4: The Past

James couldn't sleep. The board's questions echoed in his mind, triggering memories he usually kept buried. He had been a pastor for fifteen years. He had preached about judgment, about sin, about divine justice. He had believed he knew what was right. And then Sarah had died.

He lay in bed, staring at the ceiling, listening to Adanna's steady breathing beside him. The room was dark, the only light coming from the streetlamp outside, casting pale shadows through the curtains. The house was quiet—Chidi and Ngozi asleep in their rooms, the ordinary peace of a family at rest.

But James was not at peace. He hadn't been at peace for eight years.

He slipped out of bed carefully, trying not to wake Adanna. The floor was cold under his bare feet as he walked to the kitchen. He filled a glass with water and stood at the window, looking out at the dark street.

His mind went back, as it always did when he couldn't sleep. Back to the church in South London where he had served as pastor for a decade. Back to the sermons he had preached, the counseling he had provided, the certainty he had carried like a shield.

*God sees the heart*, he had told his congregation. *Man looks at the outward appearance, but the Lord looks at the heart.* He had believed it. He had believed that divine judgment was perfect because God could see what humans could not. He had believed that his role was to point people toward that judgment, to help them understand what God required.

And then Sarah Chen had walked into his office.

James closed his eyes, the memory sharp and clear. It had been a Tuesday afternoon, eight years ago. The church office was small, cluttered with books and papers. Sarah had been twenty-three, a young woman with dark hair and tired eyes. She had grown up in the church, attended Sunday school, sang in the choir. But she had drifted away in her late teens, and now she had returned, seeking guidance.

"Pastor James," she had said, sitting across from his desk. "I need to talk to someone."

"Of course," he had replied. "What's on your heart?"

She had told him about her life—about moving out of her parents' home, about living with her boyfriend, about the tension between her faith and her choices. She had told him about feeling judged by her family, about struggling with depression, about wanting to do the right thing but not knowing what that was.

James had listened. And then he had done what he had always done. He had opened his Bible. He had shown her the passages about marriage, about sexual purity, about God's design for relationships. He had spoken with gentleness, with love, with the certainty of a man who believed he knew what God wanted.

"Sarah," he had said, "I know this is hard. But God's ways are best. Living together outside of marriage—it's not what God intends. It's putting a barrier between you and Him."

She had nodded, her eyes downcast. "I know. I've always known. But it's complicated. He's good to me. He loves me. And my parents—they won't even meet him because we're not married."

"Then maybe that's where you need to start," James had said. "If you want to honor God, you need to make changes. Move out. Seek counseling. Give your relationship a foundation that honors God."

He had prayed with her. He had promised to support her. He had believed he was doing the right thing.

Three weeks later, Sarah Chen was dead.

James opened his eyes, the kitchen still dark around him. The glass of water sat untouched on the counter. His chest ached with the familiar weight of guilt.

He had learned the details later. Sarah had moved out of her boyfriend's apartment, as James had counseled. She had tried to live according to what the church taught. But the isolation had been too much. The judgment from her family had continued. The depression had deepened. And one night, alone in a small bedsit, she had ended her life.

The funeral had been held at the church. James had stood at the pulpit, looking out at the grieving family, the weeping friends, the boyfriend who had loved her and whom the church had rejected. He had tried to speak words of comfort. But the words had felt hollow in his mouth.

After the funeral, Sarah's mother had approached him. Her face was swollen with grief, her eyes red.

"Did you tell her to leave him?" she had asked. "Did you tell her she was living in sin?"

James had nodded. "I counseled her according to Scripture."

"According to Scripture," she had repeated. "My daughter is dead because of your Scripture."

James had no answer. He had watched her walk away, and something inside him had cracked.

In the months that followed, he had tried to continue. He had preached sermons, led Bible studies, counseled other congregation members. But the certainty was gone. Every time he opened his Bible, he saw Sarah's face. Every time he spoke about God's will, he heard her mother's voice: *My daughter is dead because of your Scripture.*

He had begun to question everything. Had he been right to counsel Sarah as he did? Had he truly understood what God wanted? Or had he simply imposed his own understanding, his own cultural assumptions, his own limited perspective?

And if he couldn't trust his own judgment about something so fundamental, how could he trust any of his beliefs?

The crisis had been slow, agonizing. James had talked to his mentor, Reverend Michael Adebayo, who had listened with patience and wisdom.

"Doubt is not the opposite of faith, James," Michael had said. "Certainty is. Faith requires doubt. It requires the willingness to question, to seek, to grow."

But James's doubt had not led to deeper faith. It had led to the absence of faith. He had realized, gradually, that he no longer believed. The God he had preached about, the God who judged fairly because He knew everything—James could no longer believe in that God. Not after Sarah. Not after seeing what certainty could do.

He had resigned from the church eighteen months after Sarah's death. The congregation had been shocked, confused. Adanna had been devastated. She had begged him to reconsider, to pray, to seek counsel. But James had already done all of that. He had emerged on the other side without faith.

They had stayed married. They had to, for the children, for the life they had built. But the distance between them had grown. Adanna still attended church every Sunday, still prayed, still believed. James stayed home, grading papers, reading philosophy, trying to find a way to do good without certainty.

He had found it in ethics. The same questions that had haunted him in the church—what is right, what is fair, how should we live—could be asked in the secular world. He had earned a second degree, found a position at the University of London, built a new career. He had tried to leave the past behind.

But the past never stayed buried.

James picked up the glass of water and drank it slowly. The cold liquid settled in his stomach, grounding him in the present. He thought about the board, about the algorithm, about the criteria they were debating.

He thought about Sarah Chen, and the judgment he had pronounced on her life. He had been certain then, certain that he knew what God wanted, certain that he was doing the right thing. And he had been wrong. Not wrong about the Bible, perhaps—wrong about something deeper. Wrong about his own ability to judge. Wrong about his own certainty.

Now he was being asked to help design a system that would judge millions of people. An algorithm that would assess worth, allocate resources, determine who received help and who did not. And the same question that had haunted him for eight years haunted him now: *What makes us think we can judge?*

He thought about the criteria the board was debating. Contribution versus need. Efficiency versus equity. Measurable outcomes versus human dignity. These were the same questions he had wrestled with in the church, dressed in different language. Who counts? What makes a life valuable? Who gets to decide?

The difference was scale. In the church, his judgment had affected one person—Sarah. The algorithm would affect millions. The weight of that responsibility pressed on him, heavier than anything he had carried before.

He heard footsteps behind him. Adanna appeared in the kitchen doorway, her robe wrapped around her, her face soft with sleep.

"James?" she said. "Are you alright?"

He turned to face her. "I couldn't sleep."

"Thinking about the board?"

"Thinking about everything."

She walked to him and placed a hand on his arm. Her touch was gentle, familiar. They had been married for twenty years. They had built a life together, raised children together, navigated his loss of faith together. But there was always a distance between them now, a space where shared belief had once been.

"Do you want to talk about it?" she asked.

He shook his head. "Not tonight. Go back to bed. I'll be there soon."

She hesitated, then nodded. She left the kitchen, and James was alone again with his thoughts.

He thought about Sarah Chen, about the sermon he had preached the Sunday before she died. He had spoken about God's judgment, about the importance of living according to God's will. He had believed every word.

*Sarah had come to him for counseling about her relationship. He had told her, gently but firmly, that living with her partner outside of marriage was against God's will. He had urged her to change her life. A month later, she was dead by suicide.*

*James had never recovered. His certainty had died with her. Now he taught ethics without certainty, trying to do good without knowing if he was right. And now he would help design an algorithm that would judge millions.*

The irony was not lost on him.

He finished his water and walked back to the bedroom. Adanna had left a space for him, her back turned, her breathing steady. He climbed into bed and lay in the dark, staring at the ceiling.

Tomorrow, he would return to the university. He would teach his classes, ask his questions, pretend that he had answers. And next week, he would return to the board room, to the debates about criteria and values, to the work of designing judgment.

He didn't know if he was doing the right thing. He wasn't sure he ever would. But he couldn't walk away. Not after Sarah. Not after learning what certainty could cost.

The night stretched on, dark and silent, and James lay awake, carrying the weight of the past and the burden of the future.
