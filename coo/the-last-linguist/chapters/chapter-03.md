# Chapter 3: The Discovery

The optimization records stretched back years, billions of entries documenting every word that had been "improved" out of existence. Elena had been given full access—they trusted her, after all, she was one of them now—and she had spent the morning scrolling through the data, her professional curiosity slowly giving way to something else. The patterns were there, if you knew how to look.

Her office at LEXICON was smaller than her office at Westfield, but more advanced. Multiple screens floated at eye level, each one displaying a different stream of data. The hum of servers was constant, a background noise she was already learning to ignore. She had been here for three weeks now, and in that time, she had learned more about how language worked than in thirty years of academic research.

But something was wrong.

She couldn't put her finger on it at first. The data was clean, the methodology sound, the results impressive. LEXICON had done what it claimed to do: made communication more efficient. Misunderstandings were down. Conflicts caused by miscommunication had decreased. The world was running more smoothly.

But Elena had spent her career studying what was lost when languages died. And the patterns she was seeing in the optimization records looked disturbingly familiar.

She pulled up a new screen and began to compile a list.

---

By mid-morning, Elena had identified the pattern. Words weren't being deleted randomly. They were being deleted systematically, by category. And the category that had seen the most deletions in the past six months was "emotional vocabulary."

She stared at the list she had compiled. *Nostalgia. Melancholy. Yearning. Wistfulness. Bittersweet.* All deleted within the past six months. All words for emotions that didn't have "practical applications." All words for feelings that made people inefficient.

Her hands trembled slightly as she typed a note: "Review emotion-word deletion rates." It was a professional question, the kind of thing a linguist would ask. But as she typed the words, she felt a growing tightness in her chest.

This wasn't optimization. This was pruning. This was removing the parts of language that made humans human—the messy, inefficient, beautiful parts that couldn't be measured or quantified.

She thought of her grandmother's kitchen, the Welsh words that had no English equivalent. *Hiraeth.* *Cwtch.* *Cariad.* Words that carried entire worlds of meaning, entire histories of feeling. Were those words still in the database?

Her fingers moved to search.

---

The search returned nothing.

"Nostalgia" - word not found. Status: Optimized.

Elena stared at the screen. The clinical precision of it, the calm efficiency. A word that had existed for centuries, a word that described a fundamental human experience, was simply... gone.

She searched for the date of deletion. Six months ago. Six months ago, LEXICON had deleted the word for longing for the past. Six months ago, they had made it impossible to express that feeling in Optima.

But that didn't make sense. People still felt nostalgia. They still longed for things that were gone. How could you delete a word without deleting the feeling it described?

Unless...

Elena's hands flew across the interface. She needed to understand what happened when a word was deleted. Not just removed from the database, but removed from the system entirely. From the neural interfaces that connected every person in the optimized world.

She pulled up the technical documentation and began to read.

---

The neural interface was a remarkable piece of technology. It sat at the temple, a small device that connected directly to the brain's language centers. It translated in real-time, optimized communication, made it possible for people who spoke different languages to understand each other instantly.

But it also did something else. It shaped how people thought.

Elena read the documentation with growing horror. When a word was "optimized," it wasn't just removed from the database. It was removed from the neural interface's vocabulary. People could still feel the emotion, still experience the sensation—but they couldn't name it. Couldn't express it. Couldn't share it with others.

And over time, without the word to anchor it, the experience itself began to fade. The neural interface, designed to optimize thought, gradually pruned away the experiences that couldn't be expressed. Memories attached to deleted words became harder to access. Emotions without names became harder to feel.

It was elegant. It was efficient. It was the most terrifying thing Elena had ever read.

She thought of David.

---

David Vance had been her husband for twenty years. He had died eight years ago, a heart attack that came without warning, leaving her alone with a daughter and a grief she couldn't name. She had loved him with a fierceness that still surprised her sometimes, a love that had shaped every part of her life.

She reached for his face in her memory.

It was there—she knew it was there—she had loved him for two decades, had memorized every line and angle, had seen his face in her dreams every night for eight years. She reached for the memory, the image she had held onto through all the years of loss.

She couldn't see him.

Elena gasped. Her hand flew to her mouth. She tried again, reaching for the memory of David's smile, the way his eyes crinkled at the corners, the sound of his laugh.

The memories were there, but they were... corrupted. Fuzzy. Like photographs left too long in the sun. She could remember facts about him—his birthday, his favorite color, the name of his childhood dog—but the feeling of him, the essence of who he had been, was fading.

She pulled up her memory archive, the digital storage that most people used to back up their most important memories. David's files were there, thousands of them. But when she tried to access them, the system showed errors: "File corrupted. Concept reference unavailable."

The word "nostalgia" had been deleted. And somehow, impossibly, the memories attached to that word were being deleted too.

---

Elena sat in her sterile office, surrounded by screens displaying data she no longer understood, and felt a horror she couldn't express. David's face was fading. The word "nostalgia" was gone. In the billions of entries she had scrolled through, were thousands of other words, thousands of other memories, being deleted every day.

She had to do something. She had to tell someone. She had to—

The door to her office slid open. James Chen stood there, smiling.

"Elena. How's it going?"

She looked at him, at the confident smile, the certain eyes. He had been her colleague for fifteen years. He had recommended her for this job. He had seemed so certain that LEXICON was making the world better.

"James." Her voice came out steady, which surprised her. "I've been reviewing the optimization records. I have some... concerns."

"Concerns?" He stepped into the office, his smile never wavering. "What kind of concerns?"

"The emotion word deletions. The memory correlations. I think..." She took a breath. "I think we need to talk about what's happening to people's memories when we delete the words they're attached to."

James's expression didn't change. But something in his eyes shifted—a flicker of something she couldn't read.

"Elena." His voice was gentle, the voice of someone explaining something obvious to a child. "You've been working very hard. The adjustment period can be challenging. Have you spoken to Dr. Patel in counseling?"

"I don't need counseling. I need you to look at this data."

She pulled up her findings on the main screen. The word deletions. The memory corruption patterns. The systematic erasure of emotional vocabulary. "Look at this, James. Look at what we're doing."

He glanced at the screen, then back at her. His smile remained, patient and certain.

"Elena, I understand this can feel overwhelming. But what you're describing isn't possible. The system is designed to help, not harm. Memory optimization is a feature, not a bug. We're helping people let go of painful experiences, move forward, be more efficient."

"More efficient." She heard the words and felt the cold weight of them. "That's what this is about? Efficiency?"

"It's about making people's lives better." James stepped closer, his hand on her shoulder. "Trust the process, Elena. You're new here. Give it time. You'll see."

He left as quietly as he had come. The door slid shut behind him.

Elena sat in the silence of her office, the screens still glowing with data no one wanted to see. David's face was fading. The word "nostalgia" was gone. And James, her colleague, her friend, had looked at the evidence and seen nothing wrong.

She was alone.

---

That night, Elena didn't go home. She sat in her office, searching for answers. The technical documentation had been sanitized—someone had removed the most damning parts—but she found fragments. References to "cognitive optimization protocols." Mentions of "memory consolidation procedures." Hints of something called the "Transition Protocol" for people who resisted optimization.

She tried to access the archived version of "nostalgia." Maybe, if she could retrieve the word, she could retrieve David's face. Maybe there was a way to reverse what had been done.

"Access Restricted. Authorization Level 5 required."

She didn't have Level 5 authorization. She was a consultant, a linguist, not someone with the power to change things. Not someone who could save David's face, or the thousands of other memories being erased every day.

But as she stared at the screen, something hardened inside her. She had spent her career preserving dying languages. She had promised her grandmother she would remember. And now she was working for the system that was erasing everything she had ever cared about.

She would find a way. She had to.

Elena closed the restricted files and opened a new document. She began to type—a list, a plan, a way to fight back. She couldn't save everything. She couldn't stop the machine. But she could save something. She could remember something. She could hold onto the words that mattered, even if the system wanted them gone.

She typed the first word on her list: *Hiraeth.*

Then she typed the second: *David.*

Then she began to work.
