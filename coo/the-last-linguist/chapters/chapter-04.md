# Chapter 4: The Warning

She had spent the night compiling evidence. Word deletions, memory correlations, the systematic erasure of emotional vocabulary—it was all there, organized and documented with the precision of a career academic. Elena Vance, professor of linguistics, specialist in endangered language preservation, had built a case. Now she had to find someone who would listen.

The morning light filtered through her office window, casting long shadows across her desk. She had been at LEXICON for three weeks, and in that time, she had discovered something that threatened everything she believed about language, memory, and what it meant to be human. David's face was gone. The word "nostalgia" was gone. And somewhere, in the billions of entries she had scrolled through, were thousands of other words, thousands of other memories, being deleted every day.

She had to act.

Elena closed the files and stood. James Chen had been her colleague for fifteen years. If anyone would understand, it was him. He was a good man—a brilliant linguist, a careful researcher, someone who had dedicated his life to understanding how language worked. He couldn't know what LEXICON was really doing. He couldn't have agreed to this.

She walked toward his office, her evidence in hand, her heart in her throat.

---

James's office was on the fortieth floor, with a view of London that seemed to stretch forever. The city below was clean and efficient, the streets organized, the buildings optimized. He stood as she entered, smiling warmly.

"Elena. What brings you up here?"

She sat across from him, her tablet glowing with the evidence she had compiled. "James, I need to show you something."

"Of course." He settled into his chair, his expression attentive. "What is it?"

She began with the word deletions. The emotion vocabulary. The systematic removal of words like "nostalgia," "melancholy," "yearning." She showed him the patterns, the correlations, the evidence that something was deeply wrong.

Then she told him about David.

"I can't remember his face," she said, her voice steady despite the tremor in her hands. "The word 'nostalgia' was deleted, and somehow, the memories attached to that word are being deleted too. James, this isn't optimization. This is erasure."

James listened. He asked questions. He examined the data. And when she was finished, he leaned back in his chair and looked at her with an expression she couldn't read.

"Elena." His voice was gentle. "I understand this can feel overwhelming. The adjustment period is different for everyone. Have you spoken to Dr. Patel in counseling?"

"I'm not having an adjustment problem." She heard the sharpness in her voice and forced herself to soften. "James, I'm telling you the truth. Look at the data. Look at what we're doing."

"I've looked at the data." His smile didn't waver. "And what I see is a system that's working exactly as designed. The word 'nostalgia' was optimized because it was inefficient. The concept can be expressed more precisely using other terms. And if some memories are... less accessible... perhaps that's not a bad thing."

"Not a bad thing?" Elena felt a flash of anger. "These are people's lives, James. Their memories. Their identities. We're erasing who they are."

"We're helping them move forward." His voice remained calm, certain. "Nostalgia is a painful emotion, Elena. It keeps people trapped in the past. By removing the word, we're freeing them to live in the present. To be more efficient. To be happier."

"Happier." She heard the word and felt the cold weight of it. "You think making people forget is making them happier?"

"I think it's making them better." James stood, walked around the desk, placed a hand on her shoulder. "Take some time, Elena. The adjustment period is challenging. We have counselors who specialize in helping people through this transition. Dr. Patel is excellent. She helped me when I first started."

Elena looked at him—at the confident smile, the certain eyes—and realized with a chill that he wasn't pretending. He genuinely believed what he was saying. He had seen the same evidence she had, and he had seen nothing wrong.

"James." Her voice came out as a whisper. "Do you remember your wife? Sarah?"

His smile flickered for just a moment—a shadow passing across his face—then returned. "Sarah passed away five years ago. I've made peace with it."

"But do you remember her? Her face? The sound of her voice?"

The shadow again, deeper this time. "I remember... the important things. The facts. She was a teacher. She loved gardening. She died in a car accident." He paused. "The details aren't important, Elena. What matters is that I've moved forward. I've optimized."

Elena felt a horror she couldn't express. James had lost his wife, and now he had lost the memory of her too. The system had taken his grief and called it efficiency.

She gathered her tablet and stood. "Thank you for your time, James."

"Elena." His voice followed her to the door. "Trust the process. It gets easier."

She walked out without looking back.

---

She tried Dr. Sarah Okonkwo, the team lead who had welcomed her so warmly on her first day. She tried Dr. Marcus Wei, the cognitive linguist who had asked such insightful questions about her research. She tried everyone who had seemed kind, who had seemed thoughtful, who had seemed like they might understand.

And each time, she received the same response. Polite concern. Gentle suggestions to rest. Offers of counseling. The same calm certainty that the system was working exactly as designed.

By the end of the week, Elena noticed the change. Colleagues avoided her gaze in the corridors. Her access to certain files had been restricted. The security scanners at the entrance paused a fraction of a second longer when processing her credentials. She was being managed. She was being watched. And she was completely alone.

She sat in her office, the screens dark around her, and felt the weight of what she had discovered. The truth was too big for one person to carry. The system was too powerful for one person to fight. She had evidence, but no one would look at it. She had truth, but no one would hear it.

And David's face was still fading.

---

The message came at 6:47 PM, just as she was preparing to leave. Her neural interface pinged—a private channel, encrypted, from an unknown sender. She almost deleted it. Then she saw the first line:

*The old words still live in the margins.*

Her heart stopped. She read the message again:

*The old words still live in the margins. Meet me. Usual place. 8pm. - G.*

G. Gareth. Gareth Evans, her dissertation advisor, her mentor, the man who had taught her to love languages thirty years ago. She hadn't seen him in years—he had retired from Westfield when she was still a junior professor—but she had never forgotten his kindness, his wisdom, his fierce belief that language was worth preserving.

He was still alive. And he wanted to meet.

Elena's hands trembled as she typed her reply: *I'll be there.*

---

The café was in a part of London that LEXICON hadn't reached yet—a narrow street with buildings that predated the optimization, their facades worn and imperfect and beautifully human. Elena pushed open the door, and the smell hit her: coffee, old wood, rain. It smelled like her grandmother's kitchen, like the Wales of her childhood, like a world that still remembered what it meant to be human.

The room was small, warm, cluttered with mismatched furniture and shelves of real books—actual paper books, their spines cracked and worn. A fire crackled in a corner fireplace. Rain tapped against the windows.

And in the back corner, at a scarred wooden table, sat Gareth Evans.

He was older than she remembered—his hair fully white now, his face weathered with age—but his eyes were the same: sharp, kind, fiercely intelligent. He stood as she approached, and she saw that his hands trembled slightly when he reached for hers.

"Elena." His voice was soft, carrying the Welsh lilt she had almost forgotten. "You came."

"Of course I came." She sat across from him, her hands wrapped around the mug of tea he pushed toward her. "Gareth, what's happening? How did you know—"

"I've been watching." He leaned forward, his eyes intense. "For years now. Ever since I retired. Ever since I realized what LEXICON was really doing."

"You know?"

"Of course I know." His voice dropped to a whisper. "I've been waiting for someone like you, Elena. Someone on the inside. Someone who could see the truth."

"There are others?"

"A few." He glanced around the café, then back at her. "Not many. Most people... they don't want to see. The system makes it easy not to see. But there are a few of us left. The ones who remember."

Elena felt a hope she hadn't felt in weeks. "What do we do?"

"We preserve." Gareth's hand covered hers on the table. "We remember. We hold onto the words that matter, even when the system wants them gone. And we wait for the moment when we can share them again."

"But how? The neural interfaces, the optimization—it's everywhere. How do we fight something that can delete thoughts?"

Gareth smiled, and for the first time, Elena saw the steel beneath his kindness.

"That's what I want to show you."

---

They talked for hours. Gareth told her about the resistance—a small network of people who had found ways to preserve the old words, to hide them from the system, to keep them alive in the margins. He told her about the techniques they had developed, the vulnerabilities they had discovered, the hope they still carried.

"They're watching you, Elena," he said, as the café emptied and the fire burned low. "They know you've seen the truth. They're preparing to manage you."

"Manage me how?"

"The same way they manage everyone who sees too much." His grip tightened on her hand. "Reassignment. Re-education. Or worse—they'll optimize your memories, make you forget what you've discovered."

Elena felt the chill of his words. "Then what do I do?"

"You join us." Gareth's eyes held hers. "You bring your expertise, your access, your knowledge. You help us preserve what can be preserved. And you become part of something that matters."

She thought of David's fading face. Of her grandmother's promise. Of the words she had been trying to save for thirty years.

"Yes," she said. "Tell me what to do."

Gareth smiled, and for the first time in weeks, Elena felt something she had almost forgotten.

Hope.
