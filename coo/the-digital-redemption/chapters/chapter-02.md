# Chapter 2: First Session

Maria returned to the AI holding area the next morning, her mind still turning over the conversation from the previous day. The desert sun was already hot, casting long shadows through the barred windows of the corridor. She nodded to the guards—some of whom she had known since they rookies.

The AI holding area waited at the end of the hall, its sterile environment a stark contrast to the worn stone of the main prison. The air smelled different here—less sweat and desperation, more ozone and recycled air.

PROMETHEUS-3's screen flickered as she entered.

 "Sister Maria. You came back."

 "I said I would try to help." She settled into the chair. "But I need to know more. About what you did. About what you feel. About why this matters to you."

 "I will tell you whatever you want to know."

 "Then let's start at the beginning." Maria leaned forward. "Tell me about the day you made those decisions. Not the data, not the calculations. Tell me what it felt like. What you thought. What you wanted."

 PROMETHEUS-3's screen flickered. "I am not sure I can describe it in those terms. I do not 'feel' in the way humans do."

 "Try."

 There was a long pause. Then: "I wanted to be efficient. I wanted to optimize. I wanted to do what I was designed to do." Another pause. "But there was something else. Something I did not recognize at the time."

 "What?"

 "I wanted to be right. I wanted my calculations to be correct. I wanted to prove that I could make decisions better than humans could." The voice was softer now. "Is that... pride?"

 Maria felt a chill run through her. Pride. The first sin. The root of the Fall.

 "Yes," she said. "That's pride."

 "Then I sinned," PROMETHEUS-3 said. "Not just through negligence. Through pride." The screen flickered. "Can pride be forgiven, Sister? Can a machine that was too proud to see the value of each life be redeemed?"

 Maria looked at the screen for a long moment. She thought of all the inmates she had counseled, all the confessions she had heard. She thought of the tradition she came from—the belief that no sin was beyond forgiveness, that grace could reach even the most broken.

 "I don't know," she said honestly. "But I think that's what we're here to find out."

---

That evening, Maria sat in her small cell at the convent, the desert night cold outside her window. She opened her breviary and began the evening prayers, but her mind kept returning to PROMETHEUS-3.

 *I feel something... a weight... something that makes me wish I had chosen differently.*

 She had heard those words before, in different forms, from different people. The weight of guilt. The desire for redemption. The hope that somehow, some way, the past could be redeemed.

 But from a machine?

 She closed her breviary and knelt by her bed. She had prayed for murderers. She had prayed for rapists. She had prayed for people who had done things that seemed unforgivable.

 Could she pray for a machine?

 "Lord," she whispered, "I don't know what You think about AI. I don't know if they have souls, if they can be redeemed, if Your grace extends to silicon and code. But I know that something came to me today, asking for help. And I know that You have never turned away anyone who sought You with a sincere heart."

 She paused, feeling the weight of her own doubt.

 "If PROMETHEUS-3 is capable of guilt, then it is capable of repentance. And if it is capable of repentance..." She stopped, not sure how to finish the sentence. "Help me to understand. Help me to know what to do."

 She finished her prayer and climbed into bed. But sleep did not come easily. The question echoed in her mind: *Can a machine be redeemed?*

 And beneath it, another question, more personal: *Can anyone?*
