## 6. Dialogue Design

### 6.1 Key Dialogue Segment: The Lesson

**Participants**: Michael, Jake

**Dialogue Beats**:
1. Michael: "You've been working on this same piece for how long?"
2. Jake: "Three months. Give or take."
3. Michael: "Three months? For one table?"
4. Jake: "It's not just a table. It's someone's dining room. Where they'll have meals for the next fifty years."
5. Michael: "But the efficiency—"
6. Jake: "Efficiency isn't always speed. Sometimes the most efficient thing is to take your time and do it right."
7. Michael: "That doesn't work in my world. We ship fast, iterate, fix later."
8. Jake: "How's that working out for you?"
9. Michael: (Silence)

## 8. Key Paragraph Specifications

### 8.1 Opening (First 200 Words)

**Opening Paragraph Draft**:
```
Thursday morning. Michael had been coding since 5 AM, trying to fix what he'd broken. The dashboard was working again—mostly—but the investor meeting had been rescheduled for Monday, and David's silence on the phone had been worse than any lecture. He needed a break. Not a rest, never a rest, but a change of context. The desk in his apartment had been wobbling for weeks, one leg slightly shorter than the others. He'd meant to fix it, had bought the supplies, but there was always another deadline. Today, with his mind spinning and his code not cooperating, he grabbed the desk leg and headed out. The furniture workshop was three blocks from his favorite coffee shop. He'd walked past it a hundred times without stopping. Today he stopped.
```

### 8.2 Ending (Last 200 Words)

**Ending Paragraph Draft**:
```
He walked home through the Austin heat, the desk leg still in his hand. Jake had fixed it in five minutes, had refused payment, had said "bring me something to work on sometime if you want." Michael wasn't sure he would. The whole experience had been unsettling—the slowness, the patience, the idea that something could take months and still be worth doing. That wasn't his world. That wasn't how things worked. But as he walked, he thought about the table Jake was making. Three months. Fifty years of use. There was something there, something he couldn't quite name. He shook his head and picked up his pace. He had a dashboard to fix, a client to win back, a sprint to complete. There was no time for three-month tables. No time at all.
```
