# Chapter 6: The Error

The morning passed in a blur of signatures.

Mike's pen moved across documents with mechanical precision, his name appearing at the bottom of approvals and denials he barely registered. Forty-three claims by noon—his dashboard told him this, the number glowing in the corner of his screen like a score in a game he'd forgotten he was playing.

*The Martinez property*, he thought, clicking open the next file. *No, that's tomorrow.* He stared at the screen, trying to orient himself in the queue. The claims all looked the same after a while—data points and probability scores, client profiles and property specifications, each one a decision that bore his signature but not his thought.

His coffee had gone cold again. He couldn't remember pouring it.

Around eleven, something shifted.

It wasn't a dramatic change—just a subtle tightening in his stomach, a sensation so unfamiliar that it took him a moment to recognize it. He paused, his hand hovering over the mouse, and tried to name what he was feeling.

*This feels wrong.*

The thought came from somewhere deep, somewhere he hadn't accessed in months. He looked at the claim on his screen—a property evaluation for a client named David Chen, a young family looking for their first home in the Austin suburbs. HomeMatch had already rendered its verdict: approve, 94% confidence. The property was a perfect match, the algorithm said. Four bedrooms, good schools, updated systems, reasonable price.

Everything looked right.

But his stomach was tight.

He scrolled through the data, searching for what had triggered the sensation. The photos showed a clean, well-maintained colonial. The inspection report was clear. The neighborhood statistics were favorable. Every metric HomeMatch tracked was green, every indicator positive.

*Then why does this feel wrong?*

He tried to remember the last time he'd felt this way—this physical sense that something was off, that the data was missing something important. It had been months. Maybe longer. The sensation was like a language he'd once spoken fluently but had slowly forgotten, words and grammar fading until only the vague shape of meaning remained.

*Approve*, the screen urged. *94% confidence. Client match: excellent.*

His hand moved toward the approve button, automatic, trained by months of trusting the algorithm's judgment over his own. But his stomach stayed tight, and his finger paused above the mouse.

*What am I missing?*

He opened the full property file, scrolling past the summary into the raw data. Square footage. Lot size. Year built. Previous sales. Tax history. Everything was there, everything was green, everything was right.

Except.

His eyes caught on something in the inspection report—a notation about the foundation, brief and technical. *Minor settling observed. No structural concern.*

*Minor settling.*

The words triggered something, a memory just out of reach. He'd seen this before—this neighborhood, this type of construction, this particular issue. There was something about the soil composition in this part of Austin, something about the way the clay expanded and contracted with the seasons, something about—

But the memory wouldn't come.

He reached for it, straining, like trying to recall a word that sat on the tip of his tongue. He'd known this once. He was certain of it. Twenty-five years of walking through houses had taught him things that no algorithm could capture—the subtle signs of foundation stress, the particular way certain neighborhoods settled, the warning signs that inspections often missed.

But the knowledge was gone. Outsourced. Migrated to the cloud and replaced by a probability score that said *94% confidence*.

*Approve*, the screen urged again. *Client match: excellent.*

He clicked approve.

---

The tightness in his stomach didn't go away.

It followed him through lunch—a sandwich he didn't taste, eaten at his desk while he processed more claims. It followed him through the afternoon, a low-grade anxiety that hummed beneath his skin like an electrical current. It followed him home, sitting beside him at the dinner table while Susan talked about her day, her students, the upcoming parent-teacher conferences.

"Mike? Are you listening?"

He blinked, focused on her face. "Sorry. Long day."

"You've had a lot of those lately." Her voice was careful, measured. "Is everything okay?"

*No*, he wanted to say. *Something's wrong. I approved a property today that I shouldn't have, and I can't explain why, because I've forgotten how to explain things.*

"Fine," he said instead. "Just tired."

---

That night, he couldn't sleep.

He lay in bed, staring at the ceiling, his mind circling back to the David Chen file. The property. The foundation notation. The *minor settling* that had triggered something in his body if not his mind.

*What did I know?* he asked himself. *What did I used to know that I've forgotten?*

He got up quietly, careful not to wake Susan, and went to his home office. The computer glowed to life, and he pulled up the property file again, studying every detail. The photos. The inspection report. The neighborhood statistics.

Everything looked right.

But his stomach was still tight.

He opened a new browser tab and started searching—soil composition in that Austin neighborhood, foundation issues in homes built in that era, the particular type of settling that occurred in that part of the city. The search results were technical, dense, overwhelming.

*This is what HomeMatch does*, he realized. *This is why I stopped doing it myself. Because the algorithm can process all of this in seconds, and I can't.*

But the algorithm had missed something. He was certain of it. The tightness in his stomach was proof—evidence of a knowledge that lived in his body if not his mind, a sensory expertise that no amount of data processing could replicate.

*What did I used to notice?* he asked himself. *What did I used to feel?*

He closed his eyes and tried to remember. The way a floor tilted just slightly under his feet. The sound a foundation made when it settled. The cracks in the walls that appeared in particular patterns. The doors that stuck in certain seasons. The windows that wouldn't close quite right.

*These were the signs*, he thought. *These were the things I used to know.*

But he couldn't remember how he'd known them. The knowledge had been embodied, tacit, the kind of expertise that lived in muscle memory and physical sensation rather than conscious thought. And now it was gone, replaced by a probability score that said *94% confidence* while his stomach screamed *wrong, wrong, wrong*.

---

The next morning, he called David Chen.

"Mr. Harrison?" The young man's voice was surprised. "I wasn't expecting to hear from you. HomeMatch already sent the property recommendations."

"I know." Mike's hand was tight on the phone. "I wanted to... follow up. Personally."

There was a pause on the line. "Is something wrong?"

*Yes*, Mike thought. *Something is wrong. I just can't tell you what it is.*

"No, nothing's wrong," he said instead. "I just wanted to make sure you had all the information you needed. About the property on Maple Ridge."

"It looks great," David said. "The match score was the highest we've seen. We're planning to make an offer this week."

*Don't*, Mike wanted to say. *There's something wrong with the foundation. I can feel it. I just can't prove it.*

But he couldn't say that. He had no evidence. The inspection report was clear. The algorithm's confidence was high. All he had was a tightness in his stomach and a memory he couldn't access.

"The neighborhood is good," he said, his voice hollow. "Good schools. Stable property values."

"That's what the data said." David's voice was warm now, trusting. "We really appreciate your help, Mr. Harrison. HomeMatch has made this whole process so much easier."

*Easier*, Mike thought. *Yes. Easier. And wrong.*

He hung up the phone and sat in the silence of his office, the tightness in his stomach now a knot that wouldn't unravel. He'd done everything right—followed the algorithm, trusted the data, approved the match. By every metric that mattered, he'd done his job.

But he'd also done something wrong. Something he couldn't name or explain or prove. Something that lived in the space between data and knowledge, between probability and understanding.

*This is what I've become*, he thought. *A man who can feel that something is wrong but can't explain why. A man who has outsourced his expertise so completely that he can no longer defend his own instincts.*

He stared at the screen, at the David Chen file, at the *94% confidence* that glowed like a verdict.

*What if I'm wrong?* he asked himself. *What if the algorithm is right and I'm just... obsolete?*

The question sat in his chest, unanswered.

---

Two weeks later, David Chen called back.

"Mr. Harrison." His voice was tight, controlled. "We had the foundation inspected. Independently."

Mike's stomach dropped. "And?"

"There's significant structural damage. The kind that doesn't show up in standard inspections. The kind that..." He paused, and Mike could hear him breathing. "The kind that someone with experience should have noticed."

*Someone with experience*, Mike thought. *Someone like me. Someone who used to know things.*

"I'm sorry," he said, and the words felt inadequate, hollow. "I should have—"

"The inspection report was clear," David said. "HomeMatch said it was a 94% match. We trusted the system." His voice hardened. "We trusted you."

The line went dead.

Mike sat in the silence, the phone still pressed to his ear, the tightness in his stomach now a chasm that threatened to swallow him whole. He'd been right. His body had known what his mind had forgotten. The embodied expertise that had made him Mike Harrison had tried to warn him, and he'd ignored it.

Because the algorithm had said *94% confidence*.

Because he'd stopped trusting himself.

Because he'd become a rubber stamp for decisions he could no longer explain or defend.

---

He didn't go home that night.

Instead, he drove to the Maple Ridge property, parking on the street and walking up the driveway in the fading light. The house looked the same as it had in the photos—clean, well-maintained, promising. But now he could see what he'd missed before.

The crack in the foundation, subtle but visible if you knew where to look. The way the front door stuck slightly, evidence of shifting. The uneven gap between the roof and the walls, invisible from a distance but obvious up close.

*I would have seen this*, he thought. *I would have felt this. Twenty-five years ago, ten years ago, even five years ago, I would have walked up this driveway and known something was wrong before I reached the front door.*

But he hadn't. Because he'd stopped walking driveways. Because he'd stopped trusting his feet. Because he'd outsourced his expertise to an algorithm that could process data but couldn't feel a house.

The sunset painted the sky in shades of orange and red, and Mike stood on the porch of a house he should have known was wrong, feeling the weight of a mistake he couldn't explain.

*This is what cognitive outsourcing costs*, he thought. *Not money. Not time. But the ability to know what you know.*

He pulled out his phone, opened HomeMatch, and stared at the interface that had replaced his judgment. The *94% confidence* was still there, glowing blue and certain, unaware of its own error.

*You were wrong*, he thought at the algorithm. *And I let you be wrong because I stopped trusting myself.*

He closed the app and stood in the gathering dark, a man who had once known houses in his bones now unable to recognize the warning signs his own body had tried to tell him.

*What have I become?*

The question echoed in the empty space where his expertise used to live.
