# Chapter 3: The Time Saved

Three months into his partnership with HomeMatch AI, Mike Harrison received the kind of recognition that most realtors only dreamed of. The Austin Business Journal named him one of the city's "Top 10 Agents to Watch," and his sales numbers had increased by forty percent compared to the same quarter last year.

"You're a machine," his colleague Janet told him at the office, shaking her head. "I don't know how you're doing it."

"It's the system," Mike said, the response that had become automatic. "HomeMatch helps me work more efficiently."

But even as he said it, he felt a twinge of somethingŌĆödiscomfort, perhaps, or the faint recognition that he was taking credit for work that wasn't entirely his own. The system was doing more than helping. It was anticipating, analyzing, remembering. It was performing the cognitive labor that had once been his alone.

The time savings were undeniable. Tasks that used to take hours now took minutes. Property research that once required visits to the county clerk's office, phone calls to previous owners, and drives through unfamiliar neighborhoods could be accomplished with a few swipes on his tablet. The system compiled comparative market analyses in seconds, drafted listing descriptions in his voice, and scheduled showings with a precision that eliminated the gaps and overlaps that had plagued his calendar for years.

Mike found himself with something he hadn't had in decades: free time.

At first, he didn't know what to do with it. He would finish his work by mid-afternoon and find himself standing in his office, surrounded by the paper files he no longer needed, unsure of how to fill the hours until dinner. He started going to the gym, something he'd always meant to do but never had time for. He took long walks through his own neighborhood, noticing details he'd overlooked for years. He even tried cooking dinner a few times, though Susan gently suggested he leave the culinary arts to those with actual talent.

"This is what retirement will feel like," he told Susan one evening, as they sat on their back porch watching the sunset. "Except I'm not retired. I'm working harder than ever."

"You're working differently," Susan corrected. "The system is handling the tedious parts. You're free to focus on what matters."

What matters. Mike turned the phrase over in his mind. What did matter, in his work? The client relationships, certainly. The satisfaction of matching people to homes. The sense that he was using his expertise to guide people through one of the most significant decisions of their lives.

But the expertise itselfŌĆöthe slow, sensory accumulation of knowledge that had defined his careerŌĆöwhere did that fit in the new regime?

He didn't ask the question aloud. Susan was a physician, trained to value efficiency and accuracy above all else. To her, the system was a tool like any otherŌĆöa stethoscope, an MRI machine, a diagnostic algorithm. It enhanced human capability rather than replacing it.

Mike wasn't so sure. But he kept his doubts to himself.

---

The changes came gradually, then all at once.

It started with the small things. Mike noticed that he no longer remembered the addresses of properties he'd shown the previous week. When a client called to ask about a house they'd viewed together, he had to pull up the app to refresh his memory. The information was there, instantly accessible, but it no longer lived in his mind.

Then came the larger gaps. He was showing a property in Tarrytown when the client asked about the neighborhood's flood history. Mike opened his mouth to answerŌĆöand realized he didn't know. The knowledge was somewhere in his memory, he was certain of it. He'd worked Tarrytown for decades. But the specific details, the years and streets and severity levels, refused to surface.

He pulled out his phone and checked the app. The information appeared instantly: the 2015 Memorial Day floods had affected properties along Shoal Creek, including several in Tarrytown. The house they were standing in had taken on three inches of water in the garage.

"There was some flooding in 2015," he told the client, reading from the screen. "This property was affected, but only in the garage. The living spaces stayed dry."

The client nodded, satisfied. But Mike felt a cold knot in his stomach. He should have known that. He did know that, somewhere in the recesses of his memory. But the path to the information had grown over, obscured by the easier route of the app.

That evening, he mentioned it to Susan.

"I couldn't remember the flood history," he said. "I've sold houses in that neighborhood for twenty years. But when the client asked, I drew a blank."

"You've been relying on the system for three months," Susan replied. "Your brain is adapting. It's normal."

"Normal?" Mike stood up from the couch, pacing. "Susan, I've spent my career building this knowledge. And now I'm losing it because an app is doing the work for me?"

"You're not losing it. You're storing it differently." Susan's voice was calm, clinical. "Think about it like this: before smartphones, people memorized phone numbers. Now we don't. Does that mean we've lost the ability to remember? Or does it mean we've freed up cognitive resources for other things?"

Mike stopped pacing. "I'm not sure this is the same thing."

"Isn't it?" Susan patted the couch beside her. "Sit down. You're overthinking this."

He sat, but the knot in his stomach didn't loosen. That night, he dreamed about houses again. In the dream, he was walking through a property, trying to evaluate it, but every time he looked at a room, the information appeared on a screen in front of his eyes. He couldn't see the room itselfŌĆöonly the data about it. Square footage, condition score, estimated renovation cost. The house had become invisible behind the numbers.

He woke at 4 AM and didn't go back to sleep.

---

The following week, Mike's daughter Sarah came to visit. She was finishing her master's in social work and had been offered a position at a community health center in Austin. She wanted to buy a house.

"Dad, I need your expertise," she said over breakfast. "I've been looking at listings online, but I don't know what I'm looking at. Can you help me?"

"Of course." Mike pulled out his tablet, ready to demonstrate the HomeMatch system. "I have access to everything now. We can pull up listings, compare neighborhoodsŌĆ?

"I don't want the app, Dad." Sarah smiled, but there was something serious in her eyes. "I want you. I want your gut. The way you used to walk through a house and just know if it was right."

Mike set the tablet down. "The app is good, Sarah. It knows things I could neverŌĆ?

"It knows data. You know houses." She reached across the table and took his hand. "When I was growing up, you used to take me on property tours. You'd close your eyes and tell me what was wrong with a house just by smelling the air. Remember that?"

He did remember. The Saturday mornings when he'd bring her along, the way she'd giggle when he'd announce "water damage in the basement" before they'd even gone downstairs. The way she'd called it his superpower.

"I remember," he said quietly.

"Then do that for me. Not the app. You."

That afternoon, Mike drove Sarah through Austin neighborhoods without the tablet. He'd left it at home, deliberately, though the absence felt like a missing limb. They looked at three houses, and at each one, Mike forced himself to slow down, to breathe, to notice.

The first house was a Craftsman in Hyde Park. Mike walked through the rooms with his eyes half-closed, trying to recapture the sensory awareness that had once been second nature. The floors were original, he could tell by the give under his feet. The windows had been replaced, probably in the 1990s, based on the vinyl frames. There was a faint smell of mold in the back bedroom, suggesting a roof leak that had been patched but not properly addressed.

"Water damage," he told Sarah. "Probably the flashing around the chimney. They fixed the interior but not the source."

She beamed at him. "That's my dad."

The second house was a ranch-style in Allandale. Mike stood in the living room and tried to feel the space, to read its history the way he once had. But the knowledge came slowly, reluctantly, like pulling a book from a shelf that had grown too high to reach.

"The floors are new," he said finally. "But the subfloor has issues. You can feel it when you walk. Probably termite damage that was covered up."

"How can you tell?"

"The bounce. New hardwood shouldn't bounce." He paused, uncertain. "At least, I don't think it should."

The third house was a bungalow in Cherrywood. Mike walked through it twice, trying to summon the intuitive sense that had always guided him. But something was missing. The house was just a houseŌĆöwalls and windows and doors. It no longer spoke to him the way it once had.

"I don't know," he admitted finally. "I can't tell if this is a good house or not."

Sarah looked at him with concern. "Dad, are you okay?"

"I'm fine. Just tired." He pulled out his phone, instinctively reaching for the app, then stopped himself. "Let me think about it. I'll call you tomorrow."

That night, Mike lay in bed staring at the ceiling. Susan was already asleep, her breathing steady beside him. He thought about the houses he'd seen with Sarah, about the way his knowledge had felt distant, inaccessible, like a language he'd once spoken fluently but was now struggling to remember.

He got up quietly and went to his study. The tablet was on his desk, the screen dark. He picked it up and turned it on. The HomeMatch app loaded instantly, displaying the three properties he'd visited that day.

The data was comprehensive. The Hyde Park Craftsman had a documented roof repair in 2018, but the permit showed only interior workŌĆöconfirming his suspicion about the flashing. The Allandale ranch had a termite treatment on record from 2015, but no evidence of structural repair. The Cherrywood bungalow had a clean history, good bones, and a price point that made it an excellent value.

Mike read through the information, feeling both relieved and diminished. The app knew things he hadn't been able to access. It remembered what he'd forgotten. It saw what he could no longer see.

He called Sarah the next morning. "The Cherrywood house," he said. "It's the one. Make an offer."

"How do you know?"

"The app told me." The words came out before he could stop them. "I meanŌĆöI evaluated it. The app helped me evaluate it."

There was a pause on the line. "Dad, I asked for your gut. Not the app."

"I know. I know you did." Mike closed his eyes. "I'm sorry, Sarah. I'm still figuring this out."

"Figure it out fast," she said gently. "Because I'm buying a house, and I want my father to help me. Not an algorithm."

After they hung up, Mike sat at his desk for a long time. The tablet was in front of him, the screen showing the Cherrywood property. All the information he needed, instantly available. All the knowledge he'd spent his career building, translated into data and delivered at the touch of a button.

It was efficient. It was accurate. It was everything the modern world demanded.

But somewhere along the way, Mike Harrison had become a different kind of realtor. And he wasn't sure he liked who he was becoming.

---

That afternoon, Mike drove to the Cherrywood bungalow alone. He didn't bring the tablet. He wanted to see the house with his own eyes, to feel it the way he used to, to prove to himself that his knowledge wasn't entirely gone.

He parked on the street and sat for a moment, looking at the property. It was a sweet house, built in the 1940s, with a front porch and mature trees. The kind of place that had sheltered generations of families, that had absorbed their lives into its walls.

He got out and walked up the path. The front steps were original concrete, worn smooth by decades of feet. The porch floor had been painted recently, but the boards underneath were sound. He could tell by the way they held his weight.

Inside, he moved slowly through the rooms. Living room with built-in bookshelves. Kitchen updated in the 1980s but still functional. Two bedrooms with good light. A bathroom that needed work but had good bones.

He stood in the center of the house and closed his eyes. He breathed in the air, felt the floor beneath his feet, listened to the particular silence of the space.

And there it wasŌĆöa faint sense, almost too quiet to perceive. The house was solid. The house was good. The house would shelter his daughter well.

It wasn't the vivid, detailed knowledge he'd once possessed. It was something smaller, more fragile, like a whisper from a room he'd forgotten existed. But it was his. It came from inside him, not from a screen.

Mike opened his eyes and took out his phone. He called Sarah.

"I looked at the house again," he said. "Without the app. And you know what? It feels right. The floors are solid, the light is good, the neighborhood is quiet. It's not the most updated place, but it has character. It has soul."

"That sounds more like you," Sarah said.

"It is me." Mike looked around the empty house, feeling something he hadn't felt in months. A sense of his own presence, his own perception, his own mind. "I'm still here, Sarah. I'm still your father. And I'm going to help you buy this house."

But even as he said it, he knew it wasn't that simple. The knowledge he'd accessed today was a fraction of what the app could provide. The certainty he felt was real, but it was incomplete. He could help his daughter, yes. But he couldn't be the realtor he'd once been, not without the system that had become his crutch.

The question was whether that mattered. Whether the efficiency and accuracy of the app were worth the gradual erosion of his own cognitive abilities. Whether the time saved was worth the knowledge lost.

Mike didn't have an answer. But as he locked up the Cherrywood house and walked back to his car, he felt the first stirring of a decision forming somewhere in the back of his mind.

The path he was on had a destination. He just wasn't sure he wanted to arrive there.
