He was thirty-five years old, standing in a house that would later become one of his most memorable sales. The property was old, built in the 1950s, with all the character and complications that came with age. The floors creaked. The windows rattled. The foundation had settled in ways that would have scared off most agents.

But Mike had walked through the house and felt its story.

They'd trusted him. Not because the data said so, but because he'd walked through the house with them, pointing out details they would have missed, sharing knowledge that came from twenty years of experience. He'd been their guide, their translator, the person who could read a house the way a conductor reads a score.

*That's who I used to be*, he thought. *That's what I've lost.*

---

The memory faded, and Mike opened his eyes to the fluorescent lights of his office. The house was gone. The clients were gone. The version of himself who could read a house with his body was gone.

All that remained was the algorithm, glowing blue and certain on his screen.

*Welcome back, Mike*, it said. *You have 15 new matches to review.*

He stared at the interface, feeling the familiar pull of the data, the seductive ease of letting the algorithm decide. It would be so simple to click through the matches, to sign his name to decisions he didn't have to understand, to let the empty space stay empty.

*But that's how I got here*, he thought. *That's how I became this person I don't recognize.*

He closed the laptop.

The gesture was small, almost insignificant. But it felt like the first real choice he'd made in months. The screen went dark, and for a moment, he sat in the silence of his own uncertainty, a man who had once known houses in his bones now unable to remember a single room he'd walked through.

*What do I do now?*

The question sat in the empty space where his expertise used to live, unanswered but no longer ignored.

He worked through the files slowly, each one a reminder of who he'd been.

Each decision had been his. Not the algorithm's. Not the data's. His.

*When did I stop trusting myself?*

He couldn't pinpoint the exact moment. It had been gradual, a slow surrender of agency to efficiency, of expertise to accuracy, of self to system. The algorithm had promised to make him better, and he'd believed it. He'd let it take over, bit by bit, until there was nothing left but the signature.

*This is what outsourcing costs*, he thought. *Not money. Not time. But identity. The self that used to know things.*

He closed the file and sat in the quiet of the file room, surrounded by decades of paper memories that his mind had forgotten how to hold.

*Can I get it back?* he wondered. *Can I become the person who used to notice things?*

It was a small observation, the kind of thing the algorithm would have told him instantly. But it felt different when it came from his own senses. It felt real.

*Maybe I can learn again*, he thought. *Maybe I can become the person I used to be.*

He walked through the house for an hour, trying to notice everything.

He closed the app and stood in the empty living room, feeling the afternoon light fall through the windows, feeling the floorboards creak under his weight, feeling the house settle around him like an old friend.

*This is what I've been missing*, he thought. *Not the data. Not the efficiency. This. The feeling of being present in a space. The knowledge that comes from feet and hands and nose.*
