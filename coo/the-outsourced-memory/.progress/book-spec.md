# Book Specification: The Outsourced Memory

## Basic Information
- **Title**: The Outsourced Memory
- **Meta Description**: A philosophical exploration of cognitive outsourcing. When a real estate agent outsources his memory to AI, he must question: what makes professional intuition human? A contemporary fiction examining memory, identity, and the price of convenience.
- **Keywords**: cognitive outsourcing, memory and identity, AI decision making, professional intuition vs AI, human agency crisis, embodied knowledge, real estate AI tools
- **Genre**: Contemporary Fiction / Philosophical Fiction / Literary Fiction
- **Target Audience**: 30-60 years, professionals, readers interested in technology and philosophy, fans of Kazuo Ishiguro
- **Theme**: Cognitive Outsourcing, Memory and Identity, Professional Intuition vs Pattern Recognition, Human Agency

## Narrative Style
- **Perspective**: Third-person limited
- **POV Character(s)**: Mike Harrison (real estate agent)
- **Language Style**: Contemporary, accessible prose with philosophical undercurrents; warm but increasingly unsettling
- **Sentence Characteristics**: Medium-length sentences with occasional fragments for emotional impact
- **Vocabulary Level**: Moderate - professional real estate terminology mixed with everyday language
- **Unique Elements**: Sensory-rich descriptions of houses; contrast between AI data outputs and messy human memories; Texas colloquialisms that fade as dependency grows

## Emotional Tone

## Character Voices

### Mike Harrison (Protagonist)
- **Occupation**: Real Estate Agent (25 years experience)
- **Age**: 52
- **Speech Characteristics**: Warm, conversational, uses Texas expressions; becomes more formal and hesitant as AI dependency grows
- **Word Choice**: "Y'all", "I tell you what", neighborhood names, street names, family details about clients; shifts to data points and statistics
- **Sentence Patterns**: Medium-length, storytelling style; becomes shorter, more tentative
- **Personality Traits**: Proud of local knowledge, community-oriented, slightly stubborn, genuinely cares about clients
- **Internal Monologue Style**: Narrative, anecdotal - thinks in stories and scenes; shifts to anxious questioning
- **Unique Expression**: "I've walked every street in this neighborhood", "That house has stories", "You can feel it in your feet"
- **Speech Quirks**: Slight Texas drawl (fades over book); pauses when searching for memories that won't come

### Elena Rodriguez (Supporting Character)
- **Occupation**: Younger real estate agent, tech-savvy
- **Age**: 28
- **Speech Characteristics**: Fast, efficient, data-driven, uses tech terminology
- **Word Choice**: "Analytics", "market trends", "optimization", AI feature names
- **Sentence Patterns**: Short, declarative, confident
- **Personality Traits**: Ambitious, genuinely believes AI is better, slightly condescending to "old school" methods
- **Unique Expression**: "The data says...", "HomeMatch recommends...", "Why would you do it the hard way?"

### Susan Harrison (Supporting Character)
- **Occupation**: Elementary school teacher, Mike's wife
- **Age**: 50
- **Speech Characteristics**: Gentle but perceptive, asks questions rather than states
- **Word Choice**: Emotional vocabulary, references to their history together
- **Sentence Patterns**: Medium-length, often ends with questions
- **Personality Traits**: Observant, patient, increasingly worried about Mike
- **Unique Expression**: "You used to remember...", "What happened to the Mike who..."

### HomeMatch AI (Antagonist/Systemic)
- **Nature**: AI system, not a character but a presence
- **Interface**: Phone app, tablet, office dashboard
- **Output Style**: Clean, confident, data-rich
- **Tone**: Helpful, authoritative, never uncertain
- **Motivation**: Optimization (not malicious, but systemically problematic)

## Theme Depth

- **Real-world Metaphor**: Environmental conservation through technology

## Prohibited Elements

### Tier 1 - Absolute Prohibitions (Zero Tolerance)
| Pattern | Example | Replacement Strategy |
|---------|---------|---------------------|
| "And somewhere..." endings | "And somewhere, a clock ticked." | Concrete sensory detail tied to character |
| "Neither of them knew..." | "Neither knew what would come." | Character's intuition: "Mike felt something shift..." |
| "will never be the same" | "Things would never be the same." | Show specific changes: "He opened HomeMatch before his coffee" |
| "just the beginning" | "It was only the beginning." | Concrete continuation: "Two more clients called" |
| Rule of Three structure | "It was X, it was Y, it was Z" | Varied structure with specific details |
| Abstract emotional labels | "He felt sad" | Physical manifestation: "He stood in the empty kitchen" |
| "Little did they know" | "Little did she know..." | Remove entirely, maintain POV discipline |
| "It all started when" | "It all started when..." | Start in medias res with action |

### Tier 2 - High Frequency Patterns (Strict Limit)
| Pattern | Maximum Allowed | Detection Method |
|---------|----------------|------------------|
| "However," / "Nevertheless," | 1 per 2000 words | Search for transitional adverbs |
| "Moreover," / "Furthermore," | 0 per chapter | Remove entirely |
| "Suddenly," | 1 per chapter | Use physical jolt instead |
| "Very," / "Really," / "Quite" | 2 per 1000 words | Search for intensifiers |
| "-ing" sentence openings | 2 per chapter | Vary sentence structure |
| "As if..." comparisons | 2 per chapter | Use concrete similes |
| "Seemed to..." / "Appeared to..." | 1 per chapter | Be definitive |
| Adverb-heavy dialogue tags | 0 preferred | Use action beats instead |

### Dialogue Tag Requirements
- **Minimum 5-6 different dialogue tags per chapter**
- **Maximum 2 "said" per conversation**
- **Use action beats instead of tags where possible**
- **Examples**: whispered, muttered, snapped, breathed, demanded, questioned, admitted, conceded, challenged

### Tension Vocabulary (2-3 words per 1000 words)
- **Physical**: clenched, trembling, rigid, frozen, coiled
- **Atmospheric**: suffocating, charged, brittle, heavy, electric
- **Psychological**: paranoia, disorientation, vertigo, dissociation, intrusion

## Chapter Outline

### Chapter 1: The House That Knew - Embodied Expertise
Mike shows house using sensory knowledge; broker introduces HomeMatch AI.
### Chapter 2: The First Match - When AI Gets It Right
HomeMatch makes perfect client match; Mike begins using it regularly.
### Chapter 3: The Time Saved - The Convenience Trap
Mike saves time using AI; stops walking neighborhoods on weekends.
### Chapter 4: The Forgotten Street - When Memory Fails
Client asks about street Mike can't recall; he checks HomeMatch instead.
### Chapter 5: The Distance - Total Dependence
Mike defers to AI on all decisions; Susan notices he's different.
### Chapter 6: The Error - When AI Gets It Wrong
HomeMatch misvalues a house; Mike doesn't notice because he's lost judgment.
### Chapter 7: The Empty Memory - Trying to Remember
Mike walks neighborhood; realizes he can't "read" houses anymore.
### Chapter 8: The Outsourced Self - When AI Becomes You
Client can't tell difference between Mike's email and AI-generated one.
### Chapter 9: The Choice - Who Chose This?
Mike talks to Susan; realizes problem isn't AI but his own choices.
### Chapter 10: The Remembering - Choosing to Choose
Mike walks house without checking HomeMatch first; open ending.
