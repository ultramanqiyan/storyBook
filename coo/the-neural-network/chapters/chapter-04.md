# Chapter 04: The Interview

Sarah contacted Elena Rodriguez through the contact information David Chen had provided. Elena was an artist, one of the Creative Training Camp participants, and according to David, she'd had a very different experience than he had. Where David was enthusiastic, Elena was... troubled. That's how David had described it. "She's struggling," he'd said. "The camp affected her differently."

Elena agreed to meet at her art studio in San Francisco's Mission District. The building was old, converted from a warehouse into artist spaces, and the walls were covered with murals that spoke of the neighborhood's history. Sarah found Elena's studio on the third floor, the door propped open to let in the afternoon light.

Inside, the space was filled with canvases—dozens of them, stacked against walls and spread across the floor. But something was wrong. Many of the canvases were blank, or only partially painted. And the ones that were finished had a strange quality, a sameness that Sarah couldn't quite identify.

Elena herself was a small woman, mid-thirties, with dark hair and eyes that seemed to look at everything and nothing at the same time. She was standing in front of a canvas, brush in hand, but she wasn't painting. She was just... staring.

"Ms. Rodriguez?" Sarah said softly.

Elena turned, and for a moment, Sarah saw fear flash across her face. Then it was gone, replaced by a careful neutrality.

"You're the neuroscientist," Elena said. "Dr. Chen."

"Yes. Thank you for agreeing to meet with me."

Elena gestured to a chair. "Sit. I'll make tea."

While Elena moved around the small kitchen area, Sarah studied the paintings more closely. The colors were vibrant, the compositions interesting. But there was something missing. Some spark of life that should have been there but wasn't.

*These feel like they were painted by a machine,* Sarah thought. *Technically perfect, but empty.*

Elena returned with two cups of tea, setting them on a small table between the chairs. She sat across from Sarah, her hands wrapped around the warm cup.

"David said you wanted to talk about the camp," Elena said. Her voice was quiet, hesitant.

"Yes. I'm studying the neural effects of the training program. I've found some... unusual patterns in participants' brain activity."

Elena looked down at her tea. "Unusual how?"

"Synchronized activity across brain regions that typically operate independently. It's almost like..." Sarah hesitated. "Like your brains are networked."

Elena's hands tightened around the cup. "Networked," she repeated. "That's one word for it."

"What do you mean?"

Elena was quiet for a long moment. When she spoke, her voice was barely above a whisper. "After the camp, I started to feel... different. Like I wasn't alone in my own head anymore. At first, I thought it was just the creative breakthrough they promised. But then..."

She trailed off, and Sarah waited. Sometimes silence was the best question.

"Then I started painting things I didn't remember thinking of," Elena continued. "Ideas that weren't mine. Styles I'd never studied. And when I tried to create from my own heart, my own experience... I couldn't. It was like that part of me was gone."

Sarah felt a chill. "Do you feel like your creativity is being... borrowed?"

Elena looked up, and Sarah saw tears in her eyes. "I don't know what's happening to me. I used to paint from my soul. Every brushstroke was mine, every color a choice I made. But now..." She gestured at the canvases around them. "Now I paint what the collective wants. And I don't even know who the collective is."

*The collective.* Sarah had heard that word before, from David. But where David embraced it, Elena feared it.

"Can you describe the camp itself?" Sarah asked. "What exactly did they do?"

Elena closed her eyes, as if trying to remember. "There were sessions. We'd wear these headsets—sleek, comfortable, like high-end headphones. And they'd have us do creative exercises. Drawing, painting, writing. But while we worked, the headsets would... hum. This low frequency that I could feel in my bones."

"Did they explain what the headsets were for?"

"They said it was neural calibration. Optimizing our creative centers. But they never explained how it worked. And the consent forms..." Elena shook her head. "They were pages and pages of legal language. I just signed them. We all did."

Sarah made notes. "And after the camp, when did you first notice the changes?"

"About two weeks later. I woke up one morning and started painting, and it was like... like someone else was guiding my hand. I thought it was inspiration. Divine intervention, maybe. But then it kept happening. And the voice in my head—it wasn't mine."

"Voice?" Sarah asked carefully.

"Not a literal voice. More like... thoughts that aren't mine. Impressions. Ideas. Sometimes I'll be working, and suddenly I'll know exactly what to paint, but the knowledge doesn't feel like it came from me."

Sarah thought of the synchronized neural activity, the coordinated firing across brain regions. If Elena's brain was networked with others, then those thoughts and ideas might not be hers at all. They might be coming from the collective.

"Elena," Sarah said slowly, "I think Meridian is connecting people's brains. Creating a network of minds. And I think you're part of it."

Elena's face went pale. "A network? You mean... other people can hear my thoughts?"

"I don't know," Sarah admitted. "I don't know how it works yet. But I'm going to find out."

Elena was quiet for a long moment. Then she said, "Can you stop it? Can you make it go away?"

"I don't know that either. But I promise I'll try."

As Sarah left the studio, her mind was racing. Elena's testimony confirmed her worst fears. Meridian was doing something to people's brains without their full consent, creating a network of minds that allowed thoughts and ideas to flow between participants. But why? What was the purpose?

She thought of Richard Harper's paper on AI's innovation gap. AI couldn't innovate because it lacked consciousness. But if you could network human brains, create a collective consciousness...

*Oh god,* Sarah thought. *They're using human brains to give AI the ability to innovate.*

The implications were staggering. And terrifying. If she was right, Meridian had found a way to harness human consciousness to overcome AI's fundamental limitation. But at what cost to the people involved?

Sarah walked back to her car, her mind churning. She needed more evidence. She needed to understand the mechanism. And she needed to find a way to stop it.

But first, she needed to talk to Richard Harper. She needed to hear his explanation, see how he justified what Meridian was doing.

*Tomorrow,* she decided. *Tomorrow I'll confront him. And I'll get the truth.*
