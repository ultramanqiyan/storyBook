# Chapter 05: The Breach

Sarah sat in her lab at midnight, the glow of her screen the only light in the room. She'd spent the past three days trying to get a meeting with Richard Harper, but Meridian's PR department had stonewalled her at every turn. "Dr. Harper's schedule is fully booked," they'd said. "We'll put you on the waiting list." But Sarah knew that waiting lists were just polite ways of saying no.

So she'd made a decision. A decision that crossed every ethical line she'd ever been taught. She was going to access Meridian's internal data without permission.

It wasn't as hard as it should have been. Sarah had friends in cybersecurity, people who owed her favors. And Meridian, despite its size, had vulnerabilities. Every system did. It was just a matter of finding them.

The breach took her four hours. By 2 AM, she was inside Meridian's research database, looking at files that were never meant to be seen by outsiders. And what she found made her blood run cold.

Project Neural Cluster.

The files were extensive—thousands of documents, videos, neural scans, and test results. Sarah started with the project overview, her hands trembling as she scrolled through the pages.

*Project Neural Cluster: Harnessing Human Consciousness for AI Innovation*

The document laid out the problem clearly: AI systems could generate content, but they couldn't truly innovate. They lacked consciousness, intentionality, the spark that made human creativity unique. Meridian had tried every conventional approach to solve this problem, but none had worked.

Then, three years ago, they'd had a breakthrough. A way to network human brains, creating a collective consciousness that could generate truly innovative ideas. The collective mind could imagine things that no individual could conceive, combining perspectives and experiences in ways that produced genuinely novel creations.

*The solution,* the document read, *is to create a neural network of human minds, connected through advanced BCI technology. This "Neural Cluster" can provide AI systems with the innovative capacity they lack, while compensating participants for their contribution.*

Sarah kept reading, her horror growing with each page. The compensation was minimal—a few thousand dollars for attending the Creative Training Camp. But the real cost was hidden in the fine print. Participants were agreeing to have their brains networked indefinitely. They were becoming part of a collective mind, their thoughts and creativity harvested for Meridian's AI systems.

*They're not just using people's brains,* Sarah realized. *They're turning people into components of a machine.*

She found the technical specifications. The headsets used at the Creative Training Camp were far more sophisticated than they appeared. They didn't just monitor neural activity—they created connections between brains, establishing neural pathways that allowed thoughts and ideas to flow between participants. The connections were subtle at first, barely noticeable. But over time, they grew stronger, until participants couldn't tell where their own thoughts ended and the collective's began.

*That's why Elena feels like her creativity isn't hers anymore,* Sarah thought. *Because it isn't. She's painting what the collective wants, not what she wants.*

She found the participant list. Over two thousand names, all creative professionals who had attended the Creative Training Camp. Each one was now part of the Neural Cluster, their brains contributing to Meridian's AI innovation engine.

*Two thousand people,* Sarah thought. *Two thousand minds, harvested without their true consent.*

She kept digging. There were internal communications, discussions about the ethics of the project. Some Meridian scientists had raised concerns, arguing that participants weren't fully informed about what they were agreeing to. But those concerns had been overruled by Richard Harper, who argued that the greater good justified the means.

*Humanity needs innovation,* Harper had written in one memo. *AI systems can solve problems that individual humans cannot. But without the ability to innovate, AI is limited. The Neural Cluster bridges that gap. Yes, there are ethical considerations. But the potential benefits to humanity outweigh the costs to a few individuals.*

Sarah felt sick. The utilitarian argument, the greater good—she'd heard it before, used to justify every kind of exploitation. But this was different. This was the harvesting of human consciousness itself.

She found the financial projections. Meridian estimated that the Neural Cluster would generate billions in revenue from AI-generated innovations. The participants would receive their small compensation, but the real profits would go to Meridian's shareholders.

*They're commodifying human consciousness,* Sarah realized. *Turning people's minds into profit centers.*

By dawn, Sarah had downloaded everything she could access. She had evidence—terrible, damning evidence of what Meridian was doing. But what could she do with it? If she went public, Meridian would deny everything. They'd claim the documents were fabricated, that she'd hacked their systems illegally. And they'd be right about the hacking part.

She needed allies. She needed someone who could help her expose this in a way that couldn't be denied.

She thought of Marcus Webb, the investigative journalist who'd broken the Meridian story two years ago. He'd exposed their use of user data without consent, and he'd faced similar legal threats. But he'd persisted, and eventually, the truth had come out.

*Marcus Webb,* Sarah thought. *He'll know what to do.*

She packed up her things and left the lab, the early morning light just beginning to filter through the windows. She'd been awake for over twenty-four hours, but she couldn't rest. Not now. Not when she knew the truth.

She drove to the coffee shop where she'd agreed to meet Marcus, her mind churning with what she'd learned. The Neural Cluster was worse than she'd imagined. It wasn't just a research project—it was the systematic exploitation of human consciousness. And it had to be stopped.

As she parked her car, her phone buzzed. A message from an unknown number.

*Dr. Chen,* it read. *I know what you've found. We need to talk. -R.H.*

Sarah stared at the screen. Richard Harper. He knew she'd accessed the files. Of course he knew—Meridian's security systems would have detected the breach.

*So be it,* she thought. *Let him try to stop me.*

She walked into the coffee shop, her determination hardening into resolve. The truth was going to come out. No matter what it cost her.
