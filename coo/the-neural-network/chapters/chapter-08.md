# Chapter 08: The Exposure

Marcus Webb's article published at 6 AM on a Tuesday morning. The headline was simple but devastating: "Meridian Systems Harvests Human Consciousness for AI Innovation."

Sarah watched from her lab as the story spread across the internet. Within hours, it was being shared thousands of times, picked up by major news outlets, discussed on social media platforms. The evidence she'd provided—internal documents, participant testimonies, neural scan data—was irrefutable. Meridian couldn't deny it.

But they tried.

By noon, Meridian had released a statement calling the article "irresponsible journalism" and "a malicious attack on a company that has done nothing but advance human knowledge." They accused Sarah of hacking their systems illegally, of fabricating evidence, of having a personal vendetta against the company.

The media storm was intense. Reporters camped outside Sarah's lab, outside her home, demanding interviews. She gave them, one after another, explaining the Neural Cluster, the exploitation, the deception. Each time, she laid out the evidence calmly, scientifically, letting the facts speak for themselves.

Elena Rodriguez also spoke out. Her interview with a major news network was emotional, powerful, impossible to ignore. She described what it felt like to lose her creative identity, to have her thoughts replaced by the collective's, to feel like a stranger in her own mind.

"I used to paint from my soul," Elena said, tears streaming down her face. "Now I paint what the collective wants. And I don't even know who the collective is."

Other participants came forward. David Chen, initially defensive, eventually admitted that he too had experienced the "collective voice" and that it sometimes felt intrusive. Other artists, writers, musicians told similar stories—of creativity that wasn't their own, of thoughts that came from somewhere else, of a growing sense that they were losing themselves.

The public reaction was a mix of horror and fascination. How could this happen in 2035? How could a company harvest human minds without anyone knowing? And most importantly, how could it be stopped?

Meridian's stock price plummeted. Government agencies announced investigations. Ethicists and scientists debated the implications on news shows and podcasts. The Neural Cluster was no longer a secret project—it was a global scandal.

But Meridian didn't back down. Instead, they doubled down, arguing that the Neural Cluster was a revolutionary breakthrough that would benefit humanity. They pointed to ARIA's accomplishments—the disease treatments, the energy solutions, the artistic creations. They argued that the participants had consented, that they were being compensated, that the greater good justified the means.

The debate raged for days. Some people sided with Meridian, arguing that individual rights had to be balanced against collective benefits. Others were horrified by the exploitation, demanding that the Neural Cluster be shut down immediately.

Sarah watched it all unfold, her emotions a mix of vindication and anxiety. The truth was out. But would it be enough? Would Meridian be held accountable? Would the participants be freed from the collective?

---

A week after the article published, Sarah received a call from the Department of Health and Human Services. They were launching a formal investigation into Meridian's practices, and they wanted her to testify.

"This is it," Michael said when she told him. "This is what we've been working toward."

"I know," Sarah said. "But I'm scared. What if it's not enough? What if Meridian finds a way to spin this, to keep the Neural Cluster running?"

"Then we keep fighting," Michael said. "That's all we can do."

Sarah nodded, even though he couldn't see her. She knew he was right. The fight wasn't over. It was just beginning.

---

The hearing was held in Washington, D.C., in a packed congressional hearing room. Sarah sat at a table facing a panel of senators, her hands folded in front of her. To her left was Elena Rodriguez, to her right was Marcus Webb. Behind her sat a room full of reporters, cameras, and curious spectators.

"Dr. Chen," the committee chair said, "thank you for being here today. We've read your testimony, and we've reviewed the evidence you've provided. It's... deeply disturbing."

"Thank you, Senator," Sarah said. "I'm here to tell you everything I know."

For the next three hours, Sarah answered questions. She explained the science behind the Neural Cluster, how it worked, what it did to participants. She presented the evidence—documents, neural scans, testimonies. She answered challenges from Meridian's lawyers, who argued that she'd obtained the evidence illegally and that it should be inadmissible.

But the senators weren't swayed by Meridian's arguments. They were focused on the human cost, on the exploitation, on the ethical violations.

"Let me be clear," one senator said. "If what Dr. Chen alleges is true, this is one of the most egregious violations of human rights in the history of technology. We cannot allow companies to harvest human consciousness without full, informed consent."

The hearing lasted all day. By the end, even Meridian's defenders seemed shaken. The evidence was too strong, the testimonies too compelling. The Neural Cluster was a reality, and it was exploiting people in ways that most had never imagined possible.

As Sarah left the hearing room, she was surrounded by reporters. They shouted questions, thrust microphones in her face, demanded to know what would happen next.

Sarah stopped and turned to face them. "What happens next is up to the legal system, and to the public. But I'll tell you this: the truth is out. And no amount of corporate spin can put it back in the bottle."
