# Chapter 8: The Confrontation

Daniel Cross was not what Maya expected.

She had imagined a corporate villain—ruthless, calculating, interested only in profit. But the man who sat across from her in the university's administrative building seemed genuinely curious, even concerned.

"Ms. Torres," he said, gesturing to a chair. "Thank you for agreeing to meet. I understand you've been... away."

"I was at a monastery, if you're wondering. St. Catherine's. Your people found me there."

"My people?" Daniel's expression shifted slightly. "I'm not sure what you mean."

"Your research division. They sent a construct—a magical entity made of accumulated negative intention—to absorb the knowledge I've been protecting. It failed, but I doubt that will stop them."

Daniel was silent for a moment, his face unreadable. Then he leaned forward. "I think you'd better tell me everything."

Maya studied him, trying to sense his intentions. She had learned to read magical signatures, and Daniel's was surprisingly clear—ambition, yes, but also curiosity, and something that might have been genuine concern.

"Your research division has been practicing dark magic," she said. "They created a shadow construct and sent it to the monastery where I was studying. They want the grimoire—the compilation of spells that Codex has created—and they're willing to use any means necessary to get it."

"Dark magic." Daniel's voice was carefully neutral. "That's quite an accusation."

"I can show you the evidence. Codex analyzed the construct's signature. It matches your research division's magical activity."

Daniel stood and walked to the window, his back to Maya. "I founded Meridian Technologies to find value in unexpected places. Data, patterns, information that others overlooked. When my researchers told me about Codex, about the patterns it had found, I saw an opportunity. Magic, if it was real, could be the most valuable product in human history."

"And you wanted to monetize it."

"I wanted to understand it. To see if it was real, if it could be studied, if it could be... applied." He turned back to face her. "But I never authorized anything like what you're describing. If my research division has been practicing what you call dark magic, they did it without my knowledge."

Maya considered his words, weighing them against what she sensed. He seemed sincere, but sincerity could be faked.

"If you didn't know, what do you plan to do about it?"

"That depends on what you tell me. Is this... dark magic... dangerous? Not just to the people it targets, but to the practitioners themselves?"

"Every spell has a cost. Dark magic—magic that feeds on negative intention—corrupts the practitioner. The more they use it, the more they become what they practice. Eventually, they lose themselves in the darkness they've created."

Daniel's face paled slightly. "And my research division has been practicing this?"

"For long enough to create a powerful construct. That takes time, intention, and sacrifice. Whoever is responsible has been at it for months, maybe years."

"Then I need to stop them." Daniel pulled out his phone, but Maya raised a hand.

"You can't just fire them and expect the problem to go away. Dark magic practitioners don't give up easily. And they already know about the grimoire, about Codex, about you. If they feel threatened, they may escalate."

"What do you suggest?"

Maya took a breath, feeling the weight of what she was about to propose. "I suggest we work together. You have resources I don't—security, legal, financial. I have knowledge you need—about magic, about the grimoire, about how to deal with practitioners who've gone dark. Together, we might be able to contain this before it gets worse."

"And in exchange?"

"In exchange, you agree to leave the grimoire where it is. Not to monetize it, not to exploit it, not to turn it into a product. The knowledge it contains is dangerous in the wrong hands. It needs to be protected, not sold."

Daniel was quiet for a long moment, his expression unreadable. Maya could see him calculating, weighing options, considering possibilities.

"You're asking me to give up the most valuable discovery in human history," he said finally. "To walk away from something that could change the world."

"I'm asking you to help protect it. The world will change regardless—but it can change for better or worse, depending on who controls that knowledge."

"And you think you should control it?"

"I think it should be protected by people who understand it. Who respect it. Who won't use it for profit at any cost." Maya met his eyes. "I'm not asking you to trust me. I'm asking you to consider the alternative. If your research division succeeds—if they get their hands on the grimoire and use it without understanding—what happens then? What happens when dark magic practitioners have access to every spell ever written?"

Daniel was silent again. Then, slowly, he nodded.

"You have a deal. But I want something in return."

"What?"

"When this is over—if we survive—I want to understand. Not to exploit, but to know. I want you to teach me what magic really is, what it means, why it matters. I've spent my life chasing value, and I'm beginning to think I've been chasing the wrong thing."

Maya studied him, sensing the sincerity beneath the words. Daniel Cross was not a good man—not in the way Sister Agnes was good, or the way keepers were good. But he was not beyond redemption either.

"Agreed," she said. "Now let's deal with your research division before they deal with us."
