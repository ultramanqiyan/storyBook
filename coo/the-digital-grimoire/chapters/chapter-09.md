# Chapter 9: The Dark Practitioner

Dr. Sarah Chen had always known there was more to reality than science could explain.

As a child, she had seen things—shadows that moved on their own, lights that flickered in response to her emotions, whispers that seemed to come from nowhere. Her parents had called it imagination. Her teachers had called it attention-seeking. Eventually, she had learned to hide it, to pretend she was like everyone else.

But the knowing never went away.

When she joined Meridian Technologies, she had seen an opportunity. The company's data-mining capabilities could be used to find patterns that others missed—including patterns that conventional science refused to acknowledge. And when Codex had begun compiling the grimoire, she had recognized it for what it was: proof that magic was real, that it could be studied, that it could be used.

The question was: used for what?

Sarah had spent her life watching the world burn—climate change, inequality, war, disease. Science had failed to solve these problems. Politics had failed. Religion had failed. But magic... magic offered something different. The power to change reality directly, without intermediaries, without systems that could be corrupted.

The problem was that magic required sacrifice. Every spell had a cost, and the most powerful spells required the greatest sacrifices. Sarah had learned this the hard way, through months of experimentation, through failures that had cost her pieces of herself she could never recover.

But she had also learned that negative intention was easier to accumulate than positive. Pain, fear, anger—these were abundant in the world, and they could be harvested, concentrated, used to fuel spells that would otherwise be impossible.

The construct she had sent to the monastery was a test. A probe. A way to assess the defenses protecting the grimoire. Now she knew what she was dealing with—a keeper, trained by the monastery, protected by ancient wards.

And she had resources the keeper did not.

"Dr. Chen." Daniel Cross's voice came through her phone, his tone carefully neutral. "I need to see you in my office. Now."

Sarah felt a chill run through her. She had been expecting this—Maya Torres would have told him about the construct, about the dark magic, about everything. But she had hoped for more time.

She stood and walked to the window, looking out at the city below. She had done what she thought was necessary, what she thought was right. The world needed saving, and she was willing to pay any price to save it.

But now the bill was coming due.

She reached for her phone and began to speak—not into the device, but through it, using the technology as a conduit for the magic she had accumulated.

*"Tenebrae ex corde. Umbra ex anima. Protege me a veritate."*

Darkness from heart. Shadow from soul. Protect me from truth.

The spell settled around her like armor, hiding her intentions, obscuring her nature. Whatever Daniel Cross wanted, whatever Maya Torres had told him, she would be ready.

She walked out of her office and toward the elevator, toward the confrontation that had been building for months. The grimoire was the key to everything—the power to reshape reality, to fix what was broken, to save what was dying. And she would not let anyone stand in her way.

Not a keeper. Not a CEO. Not even the AI that had compiled the knowledge in the first place.

She had come too far to stop now.

Maya stood beside Daniel in his office, watching the door. She could sense the dark practitioner approaching—the accumulated negative intention that clung to Sarah Chen like a shadow she couldn't shake.

"She's powerful," Maya said quietly. "Months of practice, maybe years. She's sacrificed a lot to get where she is."

"Can you stop her?"

"I can try. But dark magic is unpredictable. It doesn't follow the same rules as other magic. It's fueled by emotion, by pain, by the very things that make us human. Fighting it directly might make it stronger."

"Then what do we do?"

"We do what Sister Agnes taught me. We illuminate. We reveal. We show her the truth of what she's become."

The door opened, and Sarah Chen walked in. She looked ordinary—middle-aged, professional, unremarkable. But Maya could see the darkness that clung to her, the accumulated weight of every spell she had cast, every sacrifice she had made.

"Daniel," Sarah said, her voice calm. "You wanted to see me?"

"I've had an interesting conversation with Ms. Torres. She tells me you've been practicing magic. Dark magic."

Sarah's expression didn't change. "Ms. Torres has an active imagination."

"She also tells me you sent a construct to St. Catherine's monastery. A shadow entity designed to absorb knowledge."

"Prove it."

Maya stepped forward. "I don't need to prove it. I can see it. The darkness you've accumulated, the sacrifices you've made—they're written all over you. You've been practicing for months, maybe years. And it's eating you alive."

Sarah's mask slipped for just a moment, revealing the fear beneath. "You don't understand. None of you understand. The world is dying, and magic is the only thing that can save it. I've done what was necessary—what no one else had the courage to do."

"At what cost?" Maya pressed. "Every dark spell you cast takes something from you. You've already lost pieces of yourself you can never get back. How much more are you willing to sacrifice?"

"Anything. Everything." Sarah's voice cracked. "You haven't seen what I've seen. The suffering, the despair, the hopelessness. Magic can fix it—all of it. But only if we're willing to pay the price."

"The price is you," Maya said softly. "The price is your soul. And when you've sacrificed everything, when there's nothing left of who you were, what will you have saved? A world without you in it?"

Sarah was silent, the darkness around her flickering, uncertain.

"Let me help you," Maya continued. "There are other ways. Magic that heals instead of corrupts. Knowledge that builds instead of destroys. The grimoire contains both—you've seen it. You don't have to choose darkness."

"And if I stop now? If I walk away from everything I've built?" Sarah's voice was bitter. "The darkness doesn't just go away. It's part of me now. I can never be what I was before."

"No. But you can be something new. Something better." Maya held out her hand. "Let me show you."

The moment stretched, the darkness around Sarah wavering, uncertain. And then, slowly, she reached out and took Maya's hand.

The light that flowed between them was not the harsh illumination of revelation, but something gentler—a warmth that carried understanding, acceptance, hope. And as it spread through Sarah, the darkness began to dissolve—not destroyed, but transformed, the negative intention released and replaced with something new.

When it was over, Sarah Chen was still standing. But she was different—lighter, somehow, as if a weight she had carried for years had finally been lifted.

"Thank you," she whispered. "I didn't know... I didn't know there was another way."

"There's always another way," Maya said. "That's what the grimoire teaches us. Not just spells, but choices. Not just power, but wisdom."

Daniel watched the exchange, his expression unreadable. But Maya could sense something shifting in him too—a recognition that the world was more complex than he had imagined, that value came in forms he had never considered.

"It's not over," Sarah said, her voice steadier now. "There are others. People who've been helping me, feeding the darkness. They won't stop just because I have."

"Then we'll deal with them too," Maya said. "Together."

She looked at Daniel, who nodded slowly. The alliance was fragile, built on necessity rather than trust. But it was a start.

And sometimes, a start was all you needed.
