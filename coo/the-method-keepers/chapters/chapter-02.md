# Chapter 2: The Appeal

The morning after Sofia's classification, Elena drove to the Meridian System's headquarters downtown. The building was a massive structure of glass and steel, a symbol of the new world order she had helped create.

She parked in the underground garage, took the elevator to the 47th floor, and walked through the security checkpoints with practiced ease. Her neural interface already authorized access to the Architect's Wing.

The wing was quiet at this early hour. Elena made her way to her office, past framed photographs of her early years as a researcher, through her later years as a senior architect, and finally to her current position as Director of Methodology Stratification.

She sat at her desk and Pulled up Sofia's file. Stared at the classification letter again.

**COGNITIVE CASTE: SHUDRA. METHODOLOGY ACCESS: SIMPLIFIED KNOWLEDGE ONLY.**

The words burned into her mind. Shudra. Simplified knowledge only. Her daughter, classified as deficient, denied the ability to learn how to learn.

Elena closed her eyes. Tried to control her breathing. Then opened them and began to work.

The appeal process was a formality. Elena knew the system, knew the arguments that could be made, knew the data that would be presented. She had helped design the appeal process herself, years ago, as a way to provide a safety valve for families who believed their children had been misclassified.

But she also knew that appeals were almost never successful. The system was designed to be precise, and precision meant that errors were rare. When they did occur, it was usually because of exceptional circumstances, not algorithmic flaws.

Sofia's case didn't fit exceptional circumstances. Her scores were consistent with Shudra classification. Her methodology assessment results were below threshold. The system had done exactly what it was programmed to do.

But Elena filed the appeal anyway.

Three weeks later, Elena sat across from her daughter in a small conference room on the 23rd floor. The appeal had been denied, as expected.

"The system's decision is final," the committee chair said, her voice sympathetic but firm. "The classification stands."

Elena nodded, thanked them, and left. She had expected this result, but that didn't make it any easier to accept.

That evening, she sat in her office and began to work. Not on the appeal—there was nothing more to be done there—but on something else entirely.

She didn't try to change the classification. She knew that was impossible. The system was too entrenched, too powerful, too precise. But she could try to give Sofia what the system had taken away.

She could try to build a workaround.

Elena had designed the methodology stratification module. She knew its architecture, its algorithms, its decision trees. She knew how to access methodology training protocols without triggering the system's alarms.

It would be dangerous. It would be illegal. It would be a betrayal of everything she had worked for.

But it would give Sofia the chance to learn how to learn.

Elena opened her neural interface and began to code.

The workaround took three months to build. Elena worked on it in secret, during late nights and early mornings, stolen moments between her regular duties. She tested it in simulation, refined it, tested again. She made sure it would be undetectable, that it would grant methodology access without triggering any alarms, that it would give her daughter the chance to question.

When it was finally ready, she sat in her office, the workaround loaded on a secure drive, and stared at it for a long time.

This could change everything, she thought. Not just for Sofia, but for every child who had been denied methodology access because they asked too many questions.

She could release it to the network. She could give methodology access to thousands, maybe millions of people. She could challenge the entire cognitive stratification system.

But what would happen then?

Elena ran a simulation. She modeled what would happen if methodology access was suddenly granted to millions of people who had never been taught to question, to verify, to think critically.

The results were catastrophic.

Social unrest. Cognitive overwhelm. Institutions collapsing under the weight of too many questions. People who had been taught to accept authority suddenly challenging everything. The social fabric tearing apart at the seams.

Elena stared at the simulation results, her hands trembling.

She had wanted to give her daughter a chance. But the cost might be everything.

That night, Elena sat in her office, the workaround ready, the simulation results glowing on her screen. She had a choice to make.

She could use the workaround for Sofia. Just Sofia. Give her daughter the chance to learn how to learn, and accept the risk that she might be caught, that she might be betraying everything she had worked for.

Or she could release it to the network. Give everyone the chance. And accept the risk that society might collapse under the weight of too many questions.

Or she could do nothing. Accept Sofia's classification. Accept that her daughter would never learn to question. Accept that the system she had helped build was working exactly as designed, and that maybe that was the problem.

Elena sat at her desk, the three options stretching before her, and no way to know which was right.

She thought about Sofia, about the girl who asked too many questions, about the child who had been classified as deficient for doing exactly what Elena had taught her to do.

She thought about Robert, about the decades of work, about the system that had given everyone a place, about the social stability that came at the cost of suppressed potential.

She thought about herself, about the twenty years of work, about the methodology module that had classified her daughter as deficient, about the choice she now faced.

Elena stood up, walked to the window, and looked out at the city skyline. The lights of buildings were just beginning to flicker on as the sun set, the sky turning orange and pink.

Out in the city, Sofia was coming home from school, in a world where she would never learn to question.

Elena made her choice.

She sat back down at her desk, pulled up the workaround, and began to refine it. Not to release it to everyone. But to make it better. Safer. More targeted. More controlled.

She would find a way to give Sofia methodology access without triggering social collapse. She would find way to challenge the system without destroying it.

It would take time. It would be dangerous. But it was the only choice she could live with.

Elena began to work.
