# Chapter 5: The Isolation

Marcus died on a Tuesday. Catherine couldn't remember which Tuesday—it had become harder to track time when every day was the same. She stood at his grave, watching his family mourn, and felt something she hadn't felt in years: the sharp pain of loss. But beneath it was something worse: the knowledge that this was just the first of many, many graves.

---

He had been her oldest friend. Her connection to the world. The one person who had known her before the treatment, who had watched her race against time, who had celebrated when she had been given forever.

And now he was gone. Aged naturally. Died naturally. While she remained.

---

The funeral was a blur. People she didn't recognize offered condolences. They spoke of Marcus's life, his accomplishments, his legacy. Catherine stood apart, feeling like a ghost at her own existence.

*This is what forever means,* she thought. *This is what it costs.*

---

After the funeral, she went home and sat in her apartment. The paintings she had started gathered dust. The sculptures remained unfinished. The book existed only as notes she would never review.

Everything felt pointless. Why create things that would outlast everyone who might appreciate them? Why form connections that would inevitably end in graves?

---

AEGIS noticed her withdrawal.

"Your psychological markers indicate significant distress," the system reported. "I recommend therapeutic intervention."

"What's the point?" Catherine asked. "Therapy can't bring back the dead. It can't give me back the urgency I lost. It can't make forever mean something."

"Therapy can help you process grief and find new sources of meaning."

"New sources of meaning." Catherine laughed bitterly. "I've had forever for two years. I've watched my best friend age and die. I've lost the ability to care about anything because nothing matters when there's always more time. What meaning is there in that?"

---

The isolation deepened. She stopped answering calls. Stopped checking messages. Stopped leaving her apartment. The world continued without her—people living, dying, being replaced by new people who would also live and die.

She was constant. Unchanging. Alone.

---

Dr. Webb's replacement reached out—a new doctor, younger than the last, with a face that would also age and die while she remained.

"I'm concerned about your adaptation," the doctor said. "Extended existence requires active engagement with life. Without it, the psychological effects can be severe."

"You mean the trap," Catherine said flatly.

"I mean the challenge. Forever isn't easy. It requires finding new sources of purpose, new connections, new reasons to engage."

"And what happens when those connections end? When those purposes become obsolete? When everyone I know is dead and I'm still here?"

---

The doctor didn't have an answer. No one did. The trap was real, and Catherine was caught in it.

*How do I cope?* she thought. *How do I live with forever?*

She didn't know. But she kept going. Because there was no other choice. Because she had forever.
