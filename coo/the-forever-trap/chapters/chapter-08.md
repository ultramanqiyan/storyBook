# Chapter 8: The Choice

Catherine spent her last night of eternity watching the sunset. She had seen thousands of sunsets—had stopped counting, stopped noticing. But tonight, she watched with the urgency she had lost a century ago. Tomorrow, she would begin dying. And for the first time in a hundred years, she felt alive.

The termination procedure was scheduled for the morning. She had spent weeks preparing—saying goodbye to the few connections she had maintained, arranging her affairs, making peace with her choice.

"Are you sure?" AEGIS asked—a question that seemed almost human.

Catherine smiled. "I've never been more sure of anything. Forever was a trap. I didn't understand that death is what makes life mean something."

She looked at the sunset, at the world she would soon leave. "I'm ready to live again. Even if living means dying."

The night passed slowly. She didn't sleep—couldn't sleep. Instead, she sat by the window, watching the stars wheel overhead, feeling the preciousness of each moment for the first time in a century.

*This is what I lost,* she thought. *This is what forever took from me. The urgency. The meaning. The gift of finitude.*

Morning came. The doctors arrived. The procedure was explained one final time.

"Without the treatment, your body will age rapidly," the doctor said. "You may have a year. Perhaps less. The accelerated aging will be... significant."

"I understand," Catherine said. "I'm ready."

She lay on the table, feeling the nanomachines being deactivated. The sensation was strange—not painful, but... different. Like a weight being lifted. Like a clock starting again.

When it was over, she was still ancient. But she was no longer frozen. She was... dying.

And she had never felt more alive.

The first breath after the procedure was different. The air felt sharper, more real. The light was brighter. Everything had edges again, meaning again, urgency again.

*This is what it feels like to live,* she thought. *This is what it feels like to be mortal.*

She went home that evening and started a painting. Not because she had forever to finish it, but because she didn't. She had a year—maybe less. And every moment mattered.
