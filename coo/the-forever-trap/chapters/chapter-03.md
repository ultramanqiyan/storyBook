# Chapter 3: The First Year

The first year was strange. Catherine had spent her entire life racing against time—every day a step closer to death, every moment precious because it was limited. Now, time stretched before her like an endless road. There was no urgency. No rush. No reason to do anything today that couldn't be done tomorrow. Or next year. Or next century.

She started projects. Finished some, abandoned others. The painting she had been planning took shape slowly, without the pressure that had once driven her. The sculpture gathered dust in the corner. The book remained unwritten.

Without the deadline of death, everything felt... optional.

Marcus came by regularly. She watched him age—a line here, a gray hair there, a slight slowing of his step. She stayed the same. Ancient, but unchanged.

"You seem... different," he said during one visit.

"I have time now," she said. "All the time in the world."

"Is that good?"

She considered the question. "I don't know yet. It's... strange. I'm still learning how to live without dying."

The seasons changed. Spring became summer became fall became winter. Catherine watched the world cycle through its patterns while she remained constant. The leaves fell, the snow came, the flowers bloomed, the heat returned. And she was the same.

AEGIS monitored her, noting her adaptation.

"Your psychological markers show adjustment," the system reported. "But also... restlessness."

"Restlessness," Catherine repeated. "That's one word for it."

The restlessness grew. Without the urgency of mortality, Catherine found herself drifting. Days blurred into weeks. Weeks blurred into months. She started losing track of time—not because she was forgetful, but because time no longer mattered.

What was the difference between Tuesday and Wednesday when you had forever?

She tried to find purpose. Volunteered at a hospital. Took classes. Traveled. But everything felt temporary, fleeting, meaningless. The people she met would age and die. The places she visited would change. The things she learned would become obsolete.

What was the point of investing in a world that would leave her behind?

The first sign of the trap came on her birthday. She turned thirty—or would have, if her body hadn't been frozen at twenty-nine. Marcus brought a cake, candles, the whole ritual.

"Make a wish," he said.

Catherine looked at the candles, at the cake, at her friend who had aged another year while she stayed the same. What did she have to wish for? She had time. She had health. She had... forever.

"I don't know what to wish for," she admitted.

"Wish for happiness," Marcus suggested.

She blew out the candles, but the wish felt hollow. Happiness required something to strive for. And she had everything. And nothing.

That night, she asked AEGIS a question.

"Is this normal? This... emptiness?"

"The psychological effects of extended existence are not fully understood," AEGIS responded. "But reports from other long-term subjects suggest that adjustment can be challenging. The absence of mortality can create a sense of... purposelessness."

"Purposelessness." Catherine tasted the word. "That's it exactly. Without death, life has no... shape. No urgency. No meaning."

She lay in bed, staring at the ceiling. The clock ticked. Time passed. And she felt, for the first time since the treatment, a flicker of something like fear.

*What if forever isn't a gift?* she thought. *What if it's a trap?*
