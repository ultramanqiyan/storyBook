# Chapter 03: The Offer

The first year was strange. Catherine had spent her entire life racing against time, every day a step closer to death, every moment precious because it was limited. Now, time stretched before her like an endless road. There was no urgency. No rush. No reason to do anything today that couldn't be done tomorrow. Or next year. Or next century.

The seasons changed, and Catherine watched them with a detachment she couldn't shake. Spring arrived, bringing flowers she had seen twenty-nine times before. Summer followed, with its heat and long days. Autumn painted the trees in colors she knew by heart. Winter came, covering everything in snow that would melt and return again next year.

And through it all, Catherine stayed the same.

She started projects, a garden, a book, a series of paintings. She finished none of them. Why bother? There was always tomorrow. There was always next week. There was always next decade. The urgency that had driven her entire life was gone, and with it went the motivation.

"You seem... different," Marcus said during one of their weekly visits. He had aged a year, subtle changes that Catherine noticed with painful clarity. The lines around his eyes had deepened. His hair had begun to gray at the temples.

"Different how?" she asked.

"Less... present. Like you're somewhere else, even when you're here."

Catherine considered this. Was she less present? She had thought she was simply adjusting to her new reality. But maybe Marcus was right. Maybe she was drifting, untethered from the urgency that had once anchored her.

"I have time now," she said. "All the time in the world. It's hard to know what to do with it."

"Most people don't have enough time. You have too much. That's... unusual."

"It's more than unusual. It's disorienting." Catherine looked at him, at his aging face, his mortal body. "You're changing. Growing. I'm not. I'm just... here. The same as yesterday, the same as tomorrow, the same as forever."

"Is that bad?"

"I don't know. It's different. And I don't know how to feel about it."

The conversation stayed with her for days. Was it bad, this suspension in time? She had wanted to live. She had chosen forever over death. But she hadn't understood what forever would feel like, the endless stretch of days that blended together, the absence of urgency, the slow erosion of meaning.

She tried to find purpose. She volunteered at a hospital, helping patients with terminal illnesses. She thought that being around people who were dying might help her appreciate living. But it only made the gap more apparent. The patients had months, weeks, days. She had eternity. They were racing against time. She had already won that race, and found the prize hollow.

She started teaching, sharing her experience with others who had progeria or similar conditions. But the children she taught grew up, grew old, died. She remained, frozen at twenty-nine, watching generation after generation pass through her life.

"Does it ever get easier?" a young patient asked her during one of her hospital visits. The girl was twelve, her body already showing signs of accelerated aging. "Knowing you'll outlive everyone?"

Catherine looked at the girl, at the face that mirrored her own childhood experience. "I don't know yet," she admitted. "I've only been at this for a year."

"A year is a long time when you're dying."

"It's nothing when you're not."

The girl nodded slowly, understanding something that Catherine was only beginning to grasp. Time was relative. A year could feel like a lifetime or like a moment, depending on how much time you had left.

That night, Catherine wrote in her journal. *One year of forever. I've watched the seasons change, watched people grow and age and change. I've stayed the same. The urgency is gone. The motivation is fading. What is the point of doing anything when there's always more time to do it later?*

She closed the journal and looked out the window at the city lights. Somewhere out there, people were living their finite lives, making choices, creating meaning. She was here, suspended in time, waiting for something she couldn't name.

*What do I do with forever?* she wondered. *What does anyone do with forever?*

She didn't have an answer. But she had time, endless, stretching time, to find one.

The restlessness grew as the year progressed. Catherine found herself unable to focus on anything for more than a few days. She would start a project with enthusiasm, only to lose interest when she realized there was no deadline, no consequence for not finishing. She would begin a relationship, only to pull away when she remembered that it would end, with death, if nothing else.

"You're avoiding connection," Marcus observed during one of their visits. He was forty now, his face showing the marks of a decade of living. Catherine was still twenty-nine, still frozen in time.

"I'm not avoiding connection. I'm just... being careful."

"Careful of what?"

"Of getting attached to things that will end." Catherine looked at him, at the friend who had been with her since childhood, who was now aging while she remained the same. "You'll be gone in a few decades. Everyone I know will be gone. Why should I invest in relationships that are guaranteed to end?"

"Because that's what relationships are. They all end, eventually. Even for normal people."

"But for normal people, they end together. They grow old together. They die together. For me, they end alone. Every time. Forever."

Marcus was quiet for a long moment. "That's the cost, isn't it? The price of forever."

"Yes. And I didn't understand it until now. I thought I was choosing life. I didn't realize I was choosing loneliness."
