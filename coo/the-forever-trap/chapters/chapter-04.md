# Chapter 4: The Drift

Catherine started seventeen projects in the second year. She finished none of them. Why bother? There was always tomorrow, next week, next decade. The urgency that had driven her entire life was gone, and with it went the motivation. She was drifting, and she couldn't find a reason to stop.

The painting gathered dust. The sculpture remained unfinished. The book existed only as notes she never reviewed. Every project felt like it could wait, and so it did. Forever.

She stopped calling Marcus. It wasn't intentional—she just... forgot. Or didn't see the point. He was aging, changing, living a life she couldn't fully participate in. Their conversations had become awkward, full of gaps she couldn't bridge.

"You haven't called in three months," he said when he finally reached her.

Catherine tried to remember the last three months. Had it been three months? It felt like days. Or years. Time had become a blur, a meaningless stream of identical moments.

"I'm sorry," she said, but the words felt hollow. *Why invest in relationships that end?* she thought. *Why love people who will leave?*

The drift continued. She stopped leaving her apartment. Stopped creating. Stopped caring. Days passed without her noticing. Weeks. Months. The world continued without her.

AEGIS monitored, concerned.

"Your activity levels have dropped significantly," the system reported. "Your social interactions have ceased. Your creative output has stopped."

"I'm fine," Catherine said. "Just... adjusting."

"Adjustment should not involve complete withdrawal."

"Maybe this is what adjustment looks like for someone who has forever."

She spent her days in the same chair, looking out the same window, watching the same world change while she stayed the same. People walked by—couples, families, children. They aged. They grew. They lived.

She just... existed.

The emptiness grew. It wasn't depression, exactly. It was something deeper—a fundamental disconnection from life itself. Without the pressure of mortality, she had lost the motivation to live.

*What's the point?* she thought, over and over. *What's the point of anything if there's always more time?*

She tried to remember the woman she had been before the treatment. The one who had raced against the clock, who had crammed a lifetime into every day, who had lived with desperate intensity. That woman had been afraid of death. But she had also been alive.

*I traded death for emptiness,* Catherine realized. *I traded the pressure of time for the prison of forever.*

The thought haunted her. She had wanted more time. She had gotten it. But she hadn't understood what time without end would cost.

*Forever isn't a gift,* she thought. *It's a trap. And I walked right into it.*
