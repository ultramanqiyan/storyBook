# Chapter 04: The Decision

Catherine started seventeen projects in the second year. She finished none of them. Why bother? There was always tomorrow, next week, next decade. The urgency that had driven her entire life was gone, and with it went the motivation. She was drifting, and she couldn't find a reason to stop.

The garden she had planted in the spring was overgrown by summer, the vegetables rotting in the ground because she never harvested them. The book she had started writing sat unfinished, the characters frozen mid-sentence, waiting for an attention she couldn't sustain. The paintings gathered dust in her studio, half-finished landscapes that blurred together in her memory.

*What's the point?* she wondered. *What's the point of creating anything when there's always more time to create more?*

The question haunted her, gnawing at the edges of her consciousness. She had spent her entire life creating meaning from the pressure of time, every moment precious because it was limited, every achievement significant because it was completed before the clock ran out. Now there was no clock. There was no pressure. There was just... time. Endless, meaningless time.

She stopped calling Marcus. It wasn't a conscious decision, just a gradual fading of effort, a slow withdrawal from the relationships that reminded her of what she had lost. He called her, left messages, sent emails. She read them, listened to them, and felt nothing but a vague sense of obligation that she couldn't translate into action.

*Why invest in relationships that end?* she thought. *Why love people who will leave?*

The logic was cold, but she couldn't find a flaw in it. Every relationship she had ever formed had ended, with death, with distance, with the natural drift of lives moving in different directions. In her old life, she had accepted this as the price of connection. But in her new life, the price seemed too high. Why pay it again and again, forever?

AEGIS noticed the change. "Your social interactions have decreased by 73% over the past six months," the system reported. "This may indicate a need for intervention."

"I don't need intervention," Catherine said. "I need... I don't know what I need."

"Your psychological profile suggests a correlation between mortality salience and motivation. The absence of mortality awareness may be contributing to decreased engagement with life activities."

"You mean I'm not motivated because I'm not dying?"

"Precisely. Humans typically derive meaning from the finitude of existence. Without finitude, meaning structures may collapse."

Catherine laughed, a hollow, bitter sound. "So you're telling me that the treatment worked too well? That I'm not dying, so I'm not living?"

"That is one interpretation. Another interpretation is that you require new meaning structures appropriate to your extended existence."

"And where do I find those?"

"That is a question I cannot answer. Meaning is not within my programming."

Catherine sat in silence for a long time, the weight of the system's words settling over her. She had chosen forever to escape death. But she hadn't understood that death was what gave life its shape, its urgency, its meaning. Without death, life was just... existence. Empty, endless existence.

She tried to find new meaning. She volunteered at a hospice, helping people face the end she had escaped. But being around the dying only highlighted the gap between them, they had urgency, purpose, clarity. She had none of those things. She was just... there. Present but not participating, watching but not living.

"You seem sad," a patient observed one day. The woman was ninety-three, her body failing, her time running out. But her eyes were clear, her presence focused in a way Catherine's never was.

"I'm not sad," Catherine said. "I'm just... lost."

"Lost how?"

"I have all the time in the world, and I don't know what to do with it."

The woman smiled, a knowing, gentle expression. "That's not a problem most people have."

"It's not a problem I thought I'd have. I thought more time would mean more life. But it doesn't. It just means more time."

"Time is just a container," the woman said. "It's what you put in it that matters."

"But what do you put in it when there's no end? No deadline? No finish line?"

"You make your own finish lines. You set your own deadlines. You decide what matters, and you pursue it." The woman's voice was soft but certain. "That's what we all do, even those of us with limited time. We don't find meaning. We create it."

Catherine thought about this for days. Create meaning. Set her own deadlines. Decide what matters. It sounded simple, but it felt impossible. How do you create meaning from nothing? How do you set deadlines when there's always more time?

She tried. She set goals, write a book, learn a language, travel to every continent. But the goals felt arbitrary, meaningless. Why write a book when there was no urgency to finish it? Why learn a language when there was no one to speak it with? Why travel when there was always more time to travel later?

The drift continued. Days blurred into weeks, weeks into months. Catherine moved through her life like a ghost, present but not participating, watching but not living. She had escaped death, but she hadn't found life. She was suspended between the two, unable to move in either direction.

*This is the trap,* she realized one night, lying in bed, staring at the ceiling. *Not death. Not life. This in-between state where nothing matters because there's always more time.*

She had chosen forever. But she hadn't understood that forever wasn't a gift. It was a prison. And she had locked herself inside.
