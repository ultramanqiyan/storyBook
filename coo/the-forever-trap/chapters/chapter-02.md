# Chapter 2: The Freeze

The treatment felt like time stopping—which, in a way, it was. Catherine lay still as the nanomachines flooded her system, repairing, maintaining, freezing her cells in their current state. When it was over, she was still twenty-nine. She would always be twenty-nine. The clock had stopped.

The recovery room was quiet. Catherine lay on the bed, feeling... different. Not better—her body still ached, still felt ancient. But something had shifted. The pressure she had lived with for years was gone. The race was over.

"How do you feel?" Dr. Webb asked.

Catherine considered the question. Her body felt the same—old, tired, worn. But something had shifted in her perception. The urgency was gone. The race against time was over.

"I feel... different," she said. "Like I'm waiting for something, but I don't know what."

AEGIS introduced itself the next day. The Artificial Existence Guidance and Integration System was designed to monitor her, maintain the nanomachines, and help her adjust to her new existence.

"I am here to assist you," AEGIS said, its voice calm and clinical. "I will monitor your cellular activity, maintain the life extension treatment, and provide support as needed."

"Support for what?" Catherine asked.

"For the challenges of extended existence."

The first week was strange. Catherine woke each morning expecting to find new spots, new lines, new signs of aging. But there were none. Her body was frozen in time—ancient, but stable. The clock had stopped.

She started projects she had never had time for. A painting she had been planning for years. A sculpture she had sketched but never shaped. A book she had wanted to write.

There was no rush now. No urgency. She could take her time.

Marcus visited on the seventh day. He looked the same—thirty, healthy, young. She looked the same too—ancient, aged, but unchanged.

"How does it feel?" he asked. "To have forever?"

"I don't have forever," she said. "I have... now. Just now, stretched out."

"That sounds philosophical."

"It feels philosophical." She looked at her hands—still spotted, still trembling, but stable. "I'm not aging. But I'm not getting younger either. I'm just... here."

The adjustment was harder than she expected. For years, every day had been precious because it might be one of her last. Now, every day was the same. There was always tomorrow. Always next week. Always next year.

The urgency that had driven her was gone. And she wasn't sure what to replace it with.

AEGIS noticed the change.

"You seem... adrift," the system observed. "Your activity levels have decreased. Your project completion rate has dropped."

"I'm adjusting," Catherine said. "It takes time to learn how to live without running out of it."

"Time is no longer a constraint. You have an indefinite future."

"Indefinite." Catherine tasted the word. "That's the problem. I don't know what to do with indefinite."

That night, she lay in bed, staring at the ceiling. The clock on her nightstill still ticked, but it felt different now. It wasn't counting down. It was just... counting.

*What do I do with forever?* she thought. *What's the point of time without end?*

She didn't have an answer. But she had time to find one.
