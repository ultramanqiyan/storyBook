# Chapter 1: The Race

Catherine woke up and checked her hands. Yesterday, they had been spotted with age. Today, the spots had spread. At twenty-nine, her body was dying of old age—progeria, the doctors called it. She called it a clock that wouldn't stop ticking, no matter how much she wanted it to slow down.

---

The mirror told her the truth she already knew. Her face was lined, her hair thin and gray, her hands spotted and trembling. Inside, she was twenty-nine—full of thoughts, dreams, desires. Outside, she was ancient. Her body was aging at seven times the normal rate. By the calendar, she had decades left. By her cells, she had months.

She dressed slowly, her joints protesting. Everything hurt. Everything was slow. But she was still here, still breathing, still racing against the clock that wouldn't stop.

---

Breakfast was the same ritual it had been for years. Tea, toast, medication. The pills kept her comfortable, but they couldn't stop the aging. Nothing could stop the aging. Progeria was rare, cruel, and unstoppable.

Her phone buzzed. A message from Marcus, her oldest friend.

*Appointment today?*

She typed back: *Yes. 2 PM.*

---

Dr. Webb's office was in a medical building downtown. Catherine had been coming here for five years, ever since her diagnosis. The walls were white, the chairs uncomfortable, the magazines outdated. Everything about the place said "waiting room," which was appropriate, because that was what she was doing. Waiting.

"Catherine." Dr. Webb entered, his face carrying the careful neutrality she had learned to read. "I have something to discuss with you."

---

The treatment was experimental. Revolutionary. Potentially life-changing.

"It's called CONTINUUM," Dr. Webb explained. "Consciousness and Organic Neural Transfer for Unified Existence. It's designed to halt cellular aging and maintain your body in its current state indefinitely."

"Indefinitely?" Catherine felt her heart race. "You mean... I wouldn't age?"

"You wouldn't age. You wouldn't die of old age. Your body would be... frozen in time."

---

The explanation took hours. The treatment involved nanomachines that would repair and maintain her cells, preventing further aging. She would still be vulnerable to accidents, disease, injury. But the clock would stop. The race would end.

"There are considerations," Dr. Webb said carefully. "Your body would remain at its current biological age. You wouldn't grow younger. You would stay... as you are."

Forever twenty-nine on the inside. Forever ancient on the outside. Forever the same.

---

Catherine went home and sat in her apartment, surrounded by her art. Paintings she had made, sculptures she had shaped, photographs she had taken. All the things she had created while racing against the clock.

If she took the treatment, she would have time. Time to create more. Time to do everything she had ever wanted. Time—endless, stretching before her like an open road.

But something in Dr. Webb's voice had given her pause. A hesitation. A warning.

---

Marcus came over that evening. He had been her friend since childhood, had watched her age while he stayed young. He was thirty now, and looked it. She was twenty-nine, and looked two hundred.

"What did the doctor say?" he asked.

She told him about CONTINUUM. About the nanomachines, the halted aging, the forever.

"You're considering it?" His voice was careful.

"I'm considering everything." She looked at her hands, at the spots that had spread overnight. "I'm running out of time, Marcus. This could give me more."

---

That night, Catherine lay in bed, unable to sleep. The clock on her nightstand ticked steadily. Time passing. Time running out.

She thought about forever. About what it would mean to have endless time. About what it would mean to watch everyone she loved age and die while she stayed the same.

*Forever,* she thought. *Forever twenty-nine. Forever dying. Or forever living?*

---

The next morning, she called Dr. Webb.

"I'll do it," she said. "I'll take forever."
