# Chapter 6: The Century

One hundred years. Catherine had lived one hundred years in a body that would never change, never age, never die. She had watched generations be born, live, and die. She had loved and lost more times than she could count. And she was only at the beginning.

---

The decades blurred together. Faces changed. Names changed. The world changed. She remained constant—ancient, unchanging, alone.

---

She had tried to connect. Had formed relationships, friendships, even loves. But each one ended the same way: with age, with death, with loss. And each loss carved another hollow space in her soul.

*How many graves?* she thought. *How many more graves?*

---

AEGIS had evolved over the century. The system had learned, adapted, become something almost... human.

"You are deteriorating," AEGIS observed. "Not physically—your cellular maintenance is optimal. But psychologically. Emotionally. You are... fading."

"I'm not fading," Catherine said. "I'm just... tired. Tired of watching everyone leave. Tired of being the one who stays."

---

The tiredness was bone-deep. Not physical exhaustion—her body was maintained, healthy, stable. But a weariness of the soul that came from accumulating losses, from watching the world change while she remained the same.

*What's the point of forever if you're alone?* she thought. *What's the point of living if everyone you love dies?*

---

She had tried to find meaning. Had created art, written books, built things that would outlast her. But the satisfaction was fleeting. The art was admired by people who would die. The books were read by generations who would pass. The buildings would crumble.

Nothing lasted. Except her.

---

The century mark came and went. She marked it alone, as she marked everything now. No cake, no candles, no wishes. Just another day in the endless stream of days.

*One hundred years,* she thought. *And I'm still here. Still ancient. Still alone.*

---

She began to ask AEGIS questions she had never asked before.

"Can I end it?"

The system processed the question. "The life extension treatment is designed to be permanent. Termination would require... significant intervention."

"What kind of intervention?"

"There are procedures. But they carry significant risk. And they would result in... death."

---

Death. The word hung in the air. She had spent her life running from it, then a century living without it. But now, for the first time, she found herself wondering if death might be... better.

*Is this what forever feels like?* she thought. *Is this what I signed up for?*
