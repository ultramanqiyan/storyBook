# Chapter 06: The Century

One hundred years. Catherine had lived one hundred years in a body that would never change, never age, never die. She had watched generations be born, live, and die. She had loved and lost more times than she could count. And she was only at the beginning. *How many centuries?* she thought. *How many more graves?*

The century mark came and went without ceremony. Catherine didn't celebrate. She didn't mourn. She just... noticed. A hundred years of existing in a body that looked twenty-nine, that felt ancient and young at the same time, that carried the weight of accumulated loss like stones in a bag that never emptied.

She had stopped counting the funerals. She had stopped counting the friends. She had stopped counting the years—what was the point, when there were always more? The numbers had become meaningless, just markers on a road that had no destination.

The world had changed around her. Technology had advanced, societies had shifted, the landscape of human experience had transformed. But Catherine remained the same—frozen in time, watching from the outside, unable to participate in the flow of progress because she was not flowing. She was still.

She had tried, over the decades, to find meaning. She had taken up causes, fought for justice, created art, built institutions. But every cause eventually succeeded or failed. Every institution eventually changed or crumbled. Every work of art eventually lost its relevance. And Catherine remained, carrying the memory of things that no one else remembered.

"Can I end it?" she asked AEGIS one day, the question she had been avoiding for decades.

The system processed the question. "The life extension treatment is designed to be permanent. Termination would require significant intervention."

"What kind of intervention?"

"The nanomachines maintaining your cellular structures would need to be deactivated. This would require a specialized procedure, currently available at only a few facilities worldwide."

"And if they were deactivated?"

"Your body would resume aging. Given your underlying condition, the aging would likely be accelerated. You would have perhaps a year, possibly less."

A year. The word echoed in Catherine's mind. A year of living, of urgency, of meaning. A year of dying, which was what she had been trying to escape all along.

"I want to know more," she said.

AEGIS provided the information. The procedure was possible but risky. The deactivation could fail, leaving her in a worse state—alive but unable to die, conscious but unable to function. Or it could succeed, giving her a year of mortality before the end.

*What would I do with a year?* Catherine wondered. *What would I do with an ending?*

She thought about Marcus, about Dr. Webb, about all the people she had lost over the century. They had all had endings. They had all had the gift of mortality, the urgency that made life mean something. She had escaped that gift, and now she understood its value.

"I need to think," she told AEGIS.

"Of course. I will be available when you are ready to discuss further."

Catherine spent the next weeks in a state of unusual clarity. The question of ending it had given her something she hadn't had in decades: urgency. She had a decision to make, a deadline to meet, a choice that mattered. And for the first time in a century, she felt alive.

She visited the places that had mattered to her—the house where she had grown up, now a museum; the hospital where she had received the treatment, now a research center; the cemetery where Marcus was buried, now overgrown with a century of vegetation. Each place was a reminder of what she had lost, what she had gained, what she had become.

*Who am I?* she wondered. *What have I done with forever?*

The answer was uncomfortable. She had done nothing. She had watched. She had waited. She had accumulated loss without creating meaning. She had existed without living.

*But I could change that,* she thought. *I could have a year. I could make it mean something.*

The decision crystallized slowly, over weeks of reflection. She would end the treatment. She would accept mortality. She would have a year—a year of urgency, of meaning, of living. And then she would have what everyone else had: an ending.

She contacted the facility that could perform the procedure. They were cautious, asking questions, ensuring she understood the implications. She answered honestly, fully, with a clarity that surprised even herself.

"I've had a century," she told them. "And I've never been more certain of anything. I want to live. But I want to live like I'm dying. Because I am. We all are. That's the point."

The appointment was scheduled. Catherine had three months to prepare—three months to say goodbye to the few connections she had maintained, to put her affairs in order, to ready herself for the end of forever.

For the first time in a century, she felt something she had thought was lost forever: purpose. She had a deadline. She had an ending. And she had a year to make it count.
