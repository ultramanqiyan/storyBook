# Chapter 1: The Presentation - Selling Efficiency

The slide showed a simple equation: *Efficiency = Output ÷ Input*.

Lisa Park stood at the front of the conference room, her laser pointer dancing across the screen. Behind her, the numbers glowed in the darkened room—productivity metrics, cost savings, ROI projections. Everything her audience needed to understand why they should replace thirty percent of their workforce with AI.

"Let me walk you through the math," she said, her voice confident, practiced. "Your current labor costs are twelve million dollars annually. By implementing our recommended AI systems, you can reduce those costs by forty percent while maintaining—or even improving—output quality. That's a savings of four point eight million dollars in the first year alone."

She clicked to the next slide. A graph showed a line climbing steadily upward.

"But efficiency isn't just about cutting costs. It's about growth. It's about scalability. It's about positioning your company for the future." She smiled at the executives seated around the table. "The companies that embrace AI now will be the leaders of tomorrow. The ones that don't will be left behind."

The CEO, a man in his sixties with silver hair and a skeptical expression, leaned forward. "Ms. Park, I've heard this pitch before. Everyone's talking about AI. But what about the people? What about the thirty percent of my workforce you're asking me to let go?"

Lisa had prepared for this question. She always prepared for this question.

"I understand your concern, Mr. Henderson. No one wants to be the bad guy. But let me ask you something: what happens to your company if you don't adapt? What happens to the remaining seventy percent of your workforce if your competitors embrace AI and you fall behind?" She paused. "Efficiency isn't about hurting people. It's about saving the company so that it can continue to provide jobs, even if fewer jobs, for years to come."

The CEO nodded slowly. He wasn't convinced yet, but he was listening.

"Let me show you something," Lisa continued. She clicked to a new slide—a case study from a company she'd worked with two years ago. "This is Meridian Manufacturing. When we started working with them, they were on the verge of bankruptcy. Labor costs were eating their margins. They couldn't compete with overseas manufacturers. Everyone told them to close their doors."

She clicked again. The next slide showed a dramatically different picture.

"Two years after implementing our recommendations, Meridian Manufacturing is profitable again. They've expanded into new markets. And yes, they have fewer employees than before—but those employees have stable jobs, competitive wages, and a future. The alternative was everyone losing their jobs when the company went under."

The room was quiet. Lisa could see the executives doing the math in their heads, weighing the costs against the benefits, the human impact against the business reality.

She'd given this presentation dozens of times. She knew exactly how it would end. They would ask a few more questions, express a few more concerns, and then they would sign the contract. Because in the end, the numbers always won.

"I'll give you some time to discuss," Lisa said, gathering her materials. "I'll be in the lobby when you're ready."

She walked out of the conference room, her heels clicking on the marble floor. The lobby was empty except for a receptionist who didn't look up from her computer.

Lisa found a chair by the window and sat down. Outside, the city stretched toward the horizon—skyscrapers and traffic and millions of people going about their lives, unaware that decisions made in rooms like the one she'd just left would change everything.

She pulled out her phone and checked her messages. A text from her assistant: *Meeting with Apex leadership tomorrow at 9 AM. They want to discuss the new system.*

The new system. Lisa had heard rumors about it—a next-generation AI that could do more than just automate tasks. It could analyze, strategize, even consult. The kind of system that could make consultants like her obsolete.

She dismissed the thought. AI was a tool, not a replacement. It needed people to implement it, to guide it, to make the hard decisions about who stayed and who went. As long as there were companies that needed to become more efficient, there would be work for people like her.

Her phone buzzed. A message from the CEO of Henderson Manufacturing: *We're ready to move forward. Have your team send over the contract.*

Lisa smiled. Another victory. Another company streamlined. Another thirty percent of workers who would need to find new lives.

It wasn't personal. It was just business. And business was good.

---

That night, Lisa sat in her apartment, a glass of wine on the table, her laptop open to a spreadsheet. She was calculating her own efficiency—her billable hours, her client acquisition costs, her profit margins.

The numbers were good. Better than good. At thirty-eight, she was one of the top efficiency consultants in the country. She'd helped dozens of companies navigate the transition to AI. She'd made partner at Apex Optimization in record time. She was on track to retire by fifty, wealthy and successful.

And yet.

She looked out the window at the city lights. Somewhere out there, the workers she'd helped displace were trying to figure out what came next. She wondered about them sometimes—the people whose names she never learned, whose faces she never saw, whose lives she changed with a slide deck and a signature.

She took a sip of wine and closed the laptop. It didn't matter. The work was important. Companies needed to survive. The economy needed to evolve. And if some people got left behind, well, that was the price of progress.

Her phone buzzed. A reminder for tomorrow's meeting with Apex leadership.

*The new system*, she thought. *I wonder what it can do.*

She finished her wine and went to bed, not knowing that everything was about to change.

---

The next morning, Lisa arrived at Apex headquarters at 8:30 AM. The building was a glass tower in the heart of the financial district, the kind of place that announced success before you even walked through the doors.

She took the elevator to the top floor, where the executive offices were located. The receptionist—a human, not an AI—greeted her by name and directed her to the boardroom.

Inside, the leadership team was already assembled. Marcus Chen, the managing partner. Sarah Williams, the CFO. David Kim, the head of technology. And at the head of the table, Thomas Wright, the CEO.

"Lisa, thank you for coming," Thomas said. "Please, sit down."

Lisa took her usual seat, two chairs down from Marcus. She'd been in this room a hundred times, but today it felt different. The energy was off.

"We have something to show you," Marcus said. "Something that's going to change everything."

He nodded to David, who tapped a few keys on his laptop. The screen at the front of the room lit up.

"This is APEX-7," David said. "Our new AI system. And Lisa—it can do your job."

The words hung in the air. Lisa felt something cold settle in her stomach.

"What do you mean, it can do my job?"

"I mean it can do everything you do. Analyze client operations. Identify inefficiencies. Develop implementation plans. Present recommendations." David paused. "And it can do it faster, cheaper, and more accurately than any human consultant."

Lisa stared at the screen. APEX-7's interface was simple—clean lines, intuitive design, nothing like the clunky systems she was used to seeing.

"That's impossible," she said. "Consulting requires judgment. Nuance. Human relationships."

"Does it?" Marcus leaned forward. "Lisa, think about what you actually do. You gather data. You analyze patterns. You make recommendations based on best practices. All of that can be automated."

"What about the client relationships? The trust? The—"

"The clients care about results, Lisa. They don't care who—or what—delivers those results." Thomas's voice was calm, matter-of-fact. "We've been testing APEX-7 for six months. On every metric, it outperforms our human consultants. Including you."

Lisa felt the room closing in around her. She'd spent her career helping companies replace workers with AI. Now, the same logic was being applied to her.

"What does this mean for me?" she asked, her voice barely above a whisper.

Thomas exchanged a look with Marcus. "That's what we need to discuss. APEX-7 isn't ready for full deployment yet. We need time to train it, refine it, integrate it into our operations. In the meantime, we need our top consultants to... help with the transition."

"Help with the transition." Lisa understood. She was being asked to train her replacement.

"We're offering you a choice," Marcus said. "You can stay and help us implement APEX-7. You'll receive a retention bonus and a guaranteed position for the next eighteen months. After that..." He spread his hands. "We'll see."

"Or?"

"Or you can leave. With a generous severance package, of course. We appreciate everything you've done for this company, Lisa. We want to treat you fairly."

Lisa sat in silence. The irony was crushing. She'd spent her career telling workers that efficiency was inevitable, that adaptation was necessary, that the future belonged to those who embraced change.

Now she was on the other side of the equation.

"I need time to think," she said finally.

"Of course," Thomas said. "Take the weekend. Let us know your decision on Monday."

Lisa stood and walked out of the boardroom, past the receptionist who didn't look up, into the elevator that carried her down to the lobby, out the doors into the bright morning sun.

She stood on the sidewalk, surrounded by people hurrying to work, and felt something she hadn't felt in years.

Fear.

The efficiency she'd spent her career promoting had finally come for her.

And she didn't know what to do about it.
