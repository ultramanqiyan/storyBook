# Chapter 7: The Possibilities

The first healing happened by accident.

Amara had been in the third state for nearly an hour, exploring the territory she was beginning to map. The physics experiments with Marcus were yielding interesting results, small but measurable anomalies that suggested the third state really did operate by different rules. The temporal perception tests showed she could access information from near-future and near-past with greater accuracy than chance would allow. The spatial attention tests revealed that objects she focused on in the third state showed subtle but detectable changes in quantum-level measurements. But this afternoon, she wasn't conducting experiments. She was simply being. Resting in the vastness, letting the state hold her.

Her phone buzzed on the desk beside her, a sound that shouldn't have reached her in the third state, but did. The boundary between states had grown more permeable with practice. She could maintain the third state while still registering input from the waking world, like hearing distant sounds while deeply absorbed in a book.

She emerged enough to check the message. Her sister in Lagos: *Mom fell. Hospital says broken hip. Surgery tomorrow. Please call.*

The fear hit her like a wave, cold, sharp, immediate. Her mother was eighty-three, frail, living alone despite Amara's protests. A broken hip at that age could be a death sentence. The statistics were brutal: one in three elderly patients died within a year of hip fracture. Her mother had survived so much, poverty, displacement, the loss of her husband, the scattering of her children across continents. To die from a fall, alone in her apartment, seemed unbearably random. Unfair.

She started to rise, to call, to do something. But something held her back. Not resistance, something deeper. An impulse that arose from the third state itself, a knowing that preceded thought.

She settled back onto the cushion. Closed her eyes. Let the boundary dissolve again.

The third state opened around her, but different this time. Charged with the fear, the love, the urgency she felt for her mother. The emotions didn't block the entry, they seemed to fuel it, to deepen it. The state received her feeling without judgment, without resistance, incorporating it into the vastness rather than pushing it away.

She thought of her mother. Not analytically, not as a problem to solve. Just the presence of her mother in her awareness. The woman who had raised her, shaped her, believed in her when no one else did. The woman who had worked two jobs to send Amara to university, who had never complained, who had smiled through every hardship with a grace that Amara still didn't fully understand.

And in the third state, something happened.

She perceived her mother, not visually, not through any normal sense. But directly. As if the distance between New Avalon and Lagos had collapsed, as if the hospital room was present in her awareness alongside her own. The perception was immediate, intimate, more real than any memory or imagination.

Her mother lay in a bed, sedated, waiting for surgery. The hip was broken, the bone cracked, the joint displaced. Amara could perceive it, though "perceive" was inadequate. She was aware of it the way she was aware of her own body in the third state, not as an object, but as an aspect of herself. The fracture was present in her awareness, not as foreign information but as intimate knowledge.

And she felt an impulse. Not a decision, not a technique. Just an impulse, like the impulse to breathe. Natural. Unbidden. Arising from somewhere deeper than thought.

She let the impulse move.

There was no effort, no visualization, no intention. Just an allowing, a flowing, a movement of attention that seemed to carry something with it. Information, perhaps. Or something else, something the third state made possible. A connection that bypassed the usual barriers of space and time.

She stayed with her mother for what felt like hours. In the third state, she held her mother's presence, felt the fracture, allowed the impulse to move without directing it. There was no sense of doing anything. Just being with. Just presence.

When she finally emerged, the clock showed only forty minutes had passed.

She called her sister immediately, her hands trembling slightly.

"Amara, you won't believe it." Her sister's voice carried wonder and confusion in equal measure. "The doctors just did new X-rays. The fracture, it's not as bad as they thought. They're saying she might not even need surgery. Just rest and physical therapy."

Amara sat in silence, the phone pressed to her ear, her heart racing.

"Are you there? Amara?"

"I'm here. That's... that's wonderful."

"Miraculous, more like. The doctor said he's never seen a fracture heal so fast. He's calling it a spontaneous remission. He wants to study her, write a paper." Her sister laughed, the sound edged with hysteria. "Our mother, a medical marvel."

Amara ended the call and sat in the quiet lab, her mind spinning. Had she done that? Had the third state somehow affected her mother's injury? The question felt absurd, impossible. And yet the timing was undeniable. She had been with her mother in the third state, had felt an impulse, had allowed it to move. And now the fracture was healing.

She wanted to dismiss it as coincidence. The rational part of her brain demanded skepticism. Fractures didn't heal in hours. Spontaneous remissions didn't happen on demand. The human body didn't respond to distant intention. These were facts, established by centuries of medical science.

But she'd felt something. The impulse, the movement, the allowing. Something had happened in the third state, something she didn't understand. And her mother was healing.

She called Chen.

"You've discovered something," he said, without preamble. His voice carried a quality of knowing that still unsettled her.

"How did you know?"

"Your voice. It carries a particular quality when you've encountered the unexpected. The quality of someone whose worldview has just cracked open."

She told him about her mother, the healing, the inexplicable improvement. She described the perception, the impulse, the allowing. He listened without interrupting, his silence patient and receptive.

"This is one of the possibilities of the third state," he said when she finished. "Not the most important, but one of the most dramatic. The ability to affect physical reality from the non-dual perspective. The old traditions call it by many names, siddhi, miracle, grace. But it is simply a natural consequence of what the third state reveals."

"But how? What's the mechanism? How can my attention affect a bone fracture thousands of miles away?"

"The mechanism is the same as the physics you've been exploring. In the third state, the distinction between here and there, self and other, dissolves. When that distinction dissolves, influence can flow without the usual barriers. Your mother was not separate from you in that state. Her body was not separate from your awareness. The healing impulse moved the way attention moves, directly, without mediation, without the obstacles that distance and separation usually impose."

"But I didn't decide to heal her. I didn't even know it was possible. I just... felt an impulse and let it move."

"That's why it worked. If you had tried, you would have reinforced the very separation that makes healing impossible. The attempt to heal assumes a healer and a healed, a subject and an object. The third state doesn't operate through effort or intention in the usual sense. It operates through allowing. Through being. Through the dissolution of the doer."

Amara thought about this. The implications were staggering. If the third state could heal, if it could affect physical reality, what else could it do? What other possibilities lay hidden in the non-dual perspective?

"There are stories," Chen continued. "Old stories, from various traditions. Healers who could cure diseases, yogis who could influence events, mystics who seemed to bend probability. Most of these stories are exaggerations, or misunderstandings, or outright fabrications. But some of them may be accounts of what you experienced today. The third state, applied to physical reality. The non-dual perspective, expressed through action."

"How do I know when to use it? How do I know if I should?"

"That is the question of responsibility. The third state gives access to possibilities that the waking state cannot reach. But with access comes choice. And with choice comes consequence. The abilities are real, but they are not toys. They require wisdom to use, and wisdom to refrain from using."

Amara spent the next week in a state of turmoil. The healing had worked, her mother was recovering rapidly, baffling her doctors, becoming a minor medical celebrity. But the experience had opened a door she wasn't sure she wanted to walk through.

She experimented cautiously. In the third state, she tested her ability to perceive distant events, to sense the conditions of people she knew. The results were inconsistent but real, enough to confirm that the third state provided access to information beyond normal perception. She could sometimes feel when a friend was in distress, could occasionally sense the outcome of events before they occurred. Not reliably, not on demand, but often enough to know it wasn't imagination.

She also tested her ability to affect reality. Small things at first, a wilting plant that perked up after she held it in third-state awareness, a headache that vanished when she allowed an impulse to move through it, a fever that broke during a session with her nephew. The effects were subtle, but measurable. And they accumulated.

"You're learning the possibilities," Chen observed. "But you're also learning the dangers."

"Dangers? I thought you said the third state was beneficial. That it led to wisdom, compassion, freedom."

"The third state is neither good nor bad. It is a mode of consciousness, a way of perceiving and being. What makes it beneficial or harmful is how it is used. You have the ability to heal, to perceive, to influence. But you also have the ability to harm, to invade, to manipulate. The third state does not prevent misuse. It only makes it possible."

"How do I ensure I use it correctly?"

"You cannot ensure. You can only cultivate the qualities that lead to right action, compassion, wisdom, humility. And you can accept that you will sometimes make mistakes. The third state is not a path to perfection. It is a path to responsibility."

Amara thought about her mother. The healing had felt right, natural, appropriate, almost inevitable. But she hadn't chosen it deliberately. It had happened through her, not by her. The distinction was crucial.

"Is that the key?" she asked. "To let it happen through me rather than trying to make it happen?"

"That is part of it. But there's more. The third state reveals that all things are connected, that separation is an illusion, that the boundaries we perceive are constructs rather than facts. When you truly understand this, not intellectually, but experientially, the question of 'should I help?' becomes meaningless. There is no 'I' separate from the one who needs help. There is only the helping, arising naturally from the connection."

"But I still make decisions. I still choose when to enter the third state, what to focus on, whether to allow impulses to move."

"Yes. And those choices matter. The third state doesn't eliminate choice. It transforms it. You don't choose from the perspective of a separate self trying to affect an external world. You choose from the perspective of the whole, seeking balance, harmony, healing. The choice arises from connection, not from separation."

Amara sat with this for a long moment. The responsibility felt immense. She had access to something powerful, something that could affect lives, something that operated by rules she didn't fully understand. And she was still learning.

"Who else knows about this?" she asked. "The healing, the influence, who else can do what I'm doing?"

"There are others. Not many who have developed the ability to navigate deliberately. But some. And throughout history, there have been more. The traditions that speak of healing, of miracles, of supernatural abilities, these are not all fantasy. Some are accounts of third-state phenomena, preserved through oral tradition and sacred text."

"Should this be taught? Should more people learn to access the third state?"

Chen was quiet for a long moment. When he spoke, his voice carried weight.

"That is the question that has occupied contemplative traditions for millennia. The third state is available to all, in principle. Every human being has the capacity for non-dual awareness. But not all are ready for it. The abilities it confers can be misused. The insights it reveals can be destabilizing. Some traditions have kept the methods secret, sharing them only with those prepared to receive them. Others have taught openly, trusting that the process itself would filter out those not ready."

"And what do you think?"

"I think the time for secrecy is ending. The world is changing. The Emergence has made consciousness a subject of scientific study. People like you are discovering the third state through research, not tradition. The question is not whether this knowledge will spread, but how. Will it be shared responsibly, with appropriate guidance? Or will it be discovered haphazardly, with unpredictable consequences?"

Amara felt the weight of his words. She had stumbled into this through science, through data, through the signature that shouldn't exist. Others would follow. The third state would be discovered again and again, by researchers, by practitioners, by anyone curious enough to look.

The question was what would happen when they found it.
