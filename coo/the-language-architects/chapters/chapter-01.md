# Chapter 1: The Vocabulary

The word "hypothesis" disappeared at 9:47 AM.

Amira Hassan watched it happen on her screen—one moment the word existed in the Shudra lexicon, the next it was gone. Not just removed from the dictionary, but erased from every text, every conversation, every mind that had been using simplified language.

She sat back in her chair, staring at the notification. "Vocabulary Update Complete: 847 words restricted in the past quarter."

Eight hundred and forty-seven words. Words like "falsification," "paradigm," "epistemology," "skepticism." Words that enabled questioning, challenging, doubting. Words that allowed people to think for themselves.

Amira had designed the vocabulary restriction system herself, fifteen years ago. She had believed she was helping—simplifying language for those who couldn't handle its complexity, reducing cognitive burden, preventing the anxiety that came with too many choices.

But watching "hypothesis" disappear, she felt something cold settle in her stomach.

---

The Language Architecture Center occupied the top three floors of a building in downtown Philadelphia. From her office window, Amira could see the Delaware River, the bridges connecting Pennsylvania to New Jersey, the constant flow of traffic that kept the city alive.

She had worked here for twenty years, designing the vocabulary systems that kept society running smoothly. The Brahmin caste received full vocabulary access—50,000 words, including all the technical terms needed for complex thought. The Kshatriya received 25,000 words, enough for professional work but not for deep questioning. The Vaishya received 10,000 words, suitable for commerce and basic communication. The Shudra received 5,000 words, carefully selected to enable daily life without enabling dangerous questions.

And the Dalit received 1,000 words—the bare minimum for survival.

Amira had always believed this was necessary. That not everyone could handle the complexity of full language. That simplifying vocabulary reduced anxiety, prevented confusion, maintained social stability.

But lately, she had begun to wonder.

---

The notification pinged again. A message from Dr. Khalid Hassan, her mentor from graduate school, the man who had taught her everything she knew about linguistics.

The message was brief: "We need to talk. In person. Today."

Amira stared at the screen. Khalid rarely reached out directly—they communicated through academic channels, formal emails about research and publications. A personal message meant something was wrong.

She checked the time. 10:23 AM. She had a meeting with the Brahmin council at 2:00 PM to discuss the next quarter's vocabulary restrictions. She had a report due by end of day. She had three junior architects waiting for her feedback on their proposals.

But Khalid's message had an urgency that made all of that seem unimportant.

---

Khalid Hassan lived in a small house in West Philadelphia, the kind of neighborhood that had been middle-class before the caste system had transformed the city's geography. His street was lined with old trees and older houses, the kind of place where people had lived for generations.

Amira parked her car and walked up the front path. The house looked the same as always—white siding, green shutters, a small garden in the front yard where Khalid grew tomatoes every summer.

But something felt different. The curtains were drawn. The garden was overgrown. The house had the look of a place that was being maintained but not lived in.

She knocked on the door.

Footsteps inside. Then the door opened, and Khalid stood there, looking older than she had ever seen him.

"Amira," he said. "Thank you for coming."

---

They sat in his kitchen, the same kitchen where Amira had had countless conversations about linguistics, about language and thought, about the relationship between words and ideas. The room smelled of coffee and old books, the familiar scents of academic life.

But something was wrong. Khalid's hands trembled as he poured the coffee. His eyes had a distant look, as if he was struggling to focus.

"I need to tell you something," he said. "But I need you to listen carefully. And I need you to not interrupt."

Amira nodded.

"Three weeks ago, I was classified for vocabulary downgrade. The system determined that my language usage was 'cognitively destabilizing.' That I was using too many complex words, asking too many questions, causing too much... uncertainty."

Amira felt her breath catch. "Khalid, no—"

"Please. Let me finish." He took a breath. "I'm losing the words, Amira. Not just the ability to use them—the ability to think them. When I try to formulate a complex thought, the words just... aren't there anymore. It's like reaching for something that's been moved, and not being able to find it."

Amira stared at him. "But you're one of the most brilliant linguists in the country. Your work on language and cognition—"

"Is disappearing." Khalid's voice was steady, but his eyes were wet. "I can feel it happening. Every day, I lose a few more words. A few more concepts. A few more ways of thinking about the world."

He looked at her, and Amira saw something she had never seen in her mentor's eyes: fear.

"I'm not telling you this for sympathy. I'm telling you because you designed the system. You understand how it works. And you need to know what it's doing to people like me."

---

Amira left Khalid's house an hour later, her mind racing.

She drove back to the Language Architecture Center, but she couldn't focus on her work. Every time she looked at her screen, she saw Khalid's face. Every time she thought about vocabulary restrictions, she thought about her mentor losing the ability to think.

She pulled up the vocabulary restriction report she was supposed to present to the Brahmin council. The list of words to be restricted in the next quarter: "epistemology," "metacognition," "dialectic," "hermeneutics."

Words that enabled complex thought. Words that allowed people to question, to analyze, to understand.

Words that were being systematically removed from the vocabulary of millions.

---

The meeting with the Brahmin council was at 2:00 PM. Amira sat in the conference room, her report in front of her, the council members watching her expectantly.

"Dr. Hassan," one of them said. "We're waiting for your presentation on the next quarter's vocabulary restrictions."

Amira looked at the report. At the list of words that would disappear. At the millions of people who would lose the ability to think certain thoughts.

She thought about Khalid, about the fear in his eyes, about the words he was losing.

She thought about the system she had designed, about the intentions she had had, about the consequences she had never anticipated.

"I need to ask a question first," she said. "Before I present the report."

The council member nodded. "Go ahead."

"What happens to people who lose access to complex vocabulary? Not just the ability to use the words, but the ability to think the thoughts?"

The council member looked at her with a puzzled expression. "They adapt. They function within their cognitive capacity. They live happy, stable lives without the burden of questions they can't answer."

"And if those questions are important? If those thoughts are necessary for understanding the world?"

"Then they're not necessary for them. The system determines what each person can handle. It's not cruel—it's protective. It prevents cognitive overwhelm, reduces anxiety, maintains social stability."

Amira nodded slowly. But she couldn't stop thinking about Khalid, about the words he was losing, about the thoughts that were becoming impossible.

---

After the meeting, Amira returned to her office and pulled up the vocabulary restriction system's source code.

She had written it herself, fifteen years ago. Every algorithm. Every decision about which words to restrict, which concepts to simplify, which thoughts to prevent.

She had believed she was helping. She had believed she was protecting people from the burden of complexity.

But now she wasn't so sure.

The word "hypothesis" had disappeared at 9:47 AM. And somewhere in the city, someone had tried to formulate a question, and found that they couldn't find the words to ask it.

Amira closed the code and opened a new file. She began to type, not a report, not a proposal, but a question:

"What if the system isn't protecting people? What if it's controlling them?"

She stared at the question for a long time. Then she saved the file, encrypted it, and began to plan.
