# Chapter 1: The Vocabulary

The vocabulary restriction report glowed on Amira's screen, each word a small deletion from the Shudra lexicon. "Hypothesis" - restricted. "Falsification" - restricted. "Paradigm" - restricted. Words that enabled questioning, removed from the vocabulary of millions.

Amira sipped her coffee, the bitter aroma mixing with the scent of old books that permeated her office. The language interface hummed softly in the background, a constant reminder of the system she had designed. She ran a finger along the smooth surface of the screen, the words dancing under her touch.

"Morning, Amira," Sarah Liu said as she entered, her footsteps echoing slightly in the sterile space. "The Brahmin council wants your assessment on the latest vocabulary restriction by EOD."

"I'll have it ready," Amira said, her voice precise, measured. "Any particular concerns they mentioned?"

"Just the usual—'cognitive complexity' and 'social stability,'" Sarah said, rolling her eyes slightly. "You know how they are."

Amira nodded, turning back to her screen. The report listed word after word, each one carefully chosen for its ability to enable complex thought. "Hypothesis" - the ability to frame questions. "Falsification" - the ability to test assumptions. "Paradigm" - the ability to see the world differently.

She accessed the Shudra vocabulary database, pulling up the current lexicon. The contrast was stark. Where Brahmin had 50,000 words, Shudra had only 5,000. Not just fewer words—different words. Words that couldn't express doubt, couldn't frame questions, couldn't challenge assumptions.

Amira's chest tightened. She had always believed she was doing the right thing—making language accessible to those who couldn't handle its complexity. But now she wasn't so sure. These weren't just words being restricted. They were thoughts being prevented.

She thought about her mentor, Dr. Khalid Hassan, a linguist of extraordinary depth and complexity. What would happen if he lost access to the vocabulary that allowed him to think his most profound thoughts?

The interface beeped, notifying her of a new message. It was from Khalid. The message was brief: "We need to talk."

Amira stared at the screen, the words burning into her retinas. She knew that tone—something was wrong. Very wrong.

The coffee in her mug had gone cold, the bitter taste lingering on her tongue. Amira thought about the vocabulary restrictions, about how they were systematically removing the tools of thought. She thought about the Shudra, about how they were being denied the ability to question, to challenge, to think for themselves.

She stood up and walked to the window, looking out at the Philadelphia skyline. The sun was setting, painting the sky in shades of orange and pink. Somewhere out there, millions of people were living in a world of simplified language, a world where complex thoughts were becoming impossible.

Amira closed the report, her fingers lingering on the interface. These weren't just words being restricted. They were thoughts being prevented. And she was the one who had designed the system that made it possible.
