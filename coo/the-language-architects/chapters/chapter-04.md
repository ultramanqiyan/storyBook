# Chapter 4: The Investigation

Amira arrived at the Language Research Center before dawn, her mentor's face still vivid in her memory. Khalid Hassan—a man who had spent forty years studying the relationship between language and thought—was losing his ability to think. And the system she had helped build was responsible.

The research center was quiet at this hour, the only sound the soft hum of servers and the occasional beep of a notification. Amira accessed the central database, pulling up records of language-downgraded individuals.

The numbers were staggering. Over the past five years, more than 12,000 people had been language-downgraded. Most were intellectuals, artists, scientists—people whose work required complex vocabulary. People whose thoughts threatened the status quo.

She pulled up the research on vocabulary restriction and cognitive function. Study after study confirmed what she had suspected: vocabulary restriction didn't just limit expression—it limited thought. People who lost access to complex words gradually lost the ability to think complex thoughts.

One study, conducted by researchers at MIT in 2039, had tracked 500 individuals before and after language downgrades. The results were unequivocal: cognitive flexibility scores dropped by an average of 34% within six months of vocabulary restriction. Abstract reasoning ability declined by 28%. Creative problem-solving decreased by 41%.

The researchers had concluded: "Vocabulary restriction produces measurable cognitive impairment. Language shapes thought in real, quantifiable ways."

Amira leaned back in her chair. She had spent her career designing a system that was systematically removing the tools of thought. She had helped create a world where complex thinking was becoming impossible.

She thought about Khalid, struggling to remember words that had once been as natural as breathing. She thought about the Shudra, denied access to the vocabulary of questioning and doubt. She thought about the children growing up in this system, their minds shaped by words that couldn't express complexity.

The research was clear. The system wasn't optimizing cognition—it was limiting it. And Amira had made it possible.

She closed the database and stared at the blank screen. The question wasn't whether she should act. The question was how.

The system was powerful, backed by the Brahmin Council and the full weight of the cognitive classification apparatus. She was one person—a mid-level linguist with access to vocabulary design but no real authority over policy.

But she had helped build the system. That meant she understood how it worked. And that meant she might be able to change it.

Amira stood and walked to the window. Outside, the sun was rising over Seattle, casting long shadows across the city. Somewhere out there, her mentor was waking up to a world where his thoughts were slowly disappearing.

She would find a way to stop this. She had to.
