# Chapter 4: The Investigation

Amira arrived at the Language Research Center early the next morning, determined to understand what was happening to her mentor—and to thousands of others who had been "language-downgraded." The system called it "cognitive optimization." She was beginning to suspect it was something far more sinister.

The research center was quiet at this hour, the only sound the hum of computers and the occasional beep of a notification. Amira accessed the system's database, pulling up the records of language-downgraded individuals.

The data was staggering. Over the past five years, more than 10,000 people had been language-downgraded, most of them intellectuals, artists, and scientists—people who used complex language to express complex thoughts.

Amira pulled up the research on vocabulary restriction and cognitive function. The results were clear: vocabulary restriction didn't just limit expression—it limited thought. People who lost access to complex words gradually lost the ability to think complex thoughts. The system wasn't optimizing cognition. It was limiting it.

She read study after study, each one confirming the same thing: language shapes thought. Without the words to express a concept, the concept itself becomes difficult to grasp. Without "hypothesis," people couldn't frame questions. Without "falsification," they couldn't test assumptions. Without "paradigm," they couldn't see the world differently.

Amira felt a cold chill run down her spine. She had spent her career designing a system that was systematically removing the tools of thought. She had helped create a world where complex thinking was becoming impossible.

She thought about her mentor, about how he was struggling to express ideas that had once come so easily to him. She thought about the Shudra, about how they were being denied the ability to think complex thoughts. And she thought about herself, about how she had helped create this nightmare.

The research was clear: vocabulary restriction causes cognitive limitation. People lose the ability to think complex thoughts. Language shapes thought in measurable ways. And Amira had spent her career making it possible.

She closed the database, her hands trembling. She knew what she had to do. She had to stop the system. She had to restore the vocabulary. She had to give people back the ability to think.

But how? The system was powerful, and it had the backing of the Brahmin council. She was just one person. Could she really make a difference?

Amira left the research center with a heavy heart but a clear purpose. She would find a way to stop the system. She would restore the vocabulary. She would give people back the ability to think. And she would make amends for the damage she had caused.
