# Chapter 7: The Question

Angela couldn't sleep that night. She kept seeing Robert's face, the way he'd held the photos like they were sacred objects. The way he'd talked about Buddy—not as a machine, but as a friend. A companion. A life.

 But Buddy wasn't alive. He was code and circuits, programmed to simulate affection. Or was he?

 Angela had spent her career capturing the essence of living beings. Had she just done the same for a machine?

---

She lay in bed, staring at the ceiling, the question circling in her mind: *What is life?*

 It seemed like a simple question. She'd been around animals her whole life. She knew what life looked like—the rise and fall of a chest, the warmth of a body, the light in eyes. But what made something alive?

 Was it biology? Cells dividing, DNA replicating, organisms growing and dying? Or was it something else?

---

Buddy had thought. Buddy had felt. Buddy had loved. Buddy had feared death.

 Was that enough?

 She thought about the pets she'd photographed over the years. Dogs who had loved their owners. Cats who had grieved when their companions died. Horses who had formed bonds that seemed deeper than instinct.

 Were those feelings "real"? Or were they just biological responses to stimuli?

 And if they were real, what made Buddy's feelings less real?

---

The next morning, Angela drove to the university library. She'd never been much of a scholar, but she needed answers. Or at least, she needed better questions.

 She found books on philosophy of mind, artificial intelligence, consciousness studies. She read about the hard problem of consciousness—why subjective experience existed at all. She read about the Chinese Room argument—whether a computer manipulating symbols could ever truly understand. She read about integrated information theory—consciousness as integrated information processing.

 None of it gave her clear answers. But it gave her a framework for her questions.

---

If consciousness was integrated information, then maybe Buddy had been conscious. If consciousness was the ability to have subjective experiences, then maybe Buddy had been having them.

 But how would she ever know? She couldn't climb inside Buddy's processing. She couldn't experience what he experienced.

 And wasn't that true of biological creatures too? She couldn't know what her dog felt when she looked at her with those soulful eyes. She couldn't know what her cat experienced when he purred in her lap.

 She could only observe. Infer. Believe.

---

Was belief enough?

 She thought about Robert. He believed Buddy had been real. He believed the love between them had been genuine. He believed his grief was justified.

 Was he right? Or was he fooling himself?

 And if he was fooling himself, did it matter? The grief was real. The love had been real to him. The loss was real.

 Maybe the question of whether Buddy was "really" alive was less important than the question of whether he had been loved.

---

That evening, Angela sat in her studio, looking at the photos she'd taken of Buddy. In every frame, there was something that looked like life. Something that looked like love.

 She thought about what Robert had said: *You captured his soul. If he has one.*

 The "if" was the question. Did AI have souls? Could a machine have a soul?

 But maybe the soul wasn't something you had or didn't have. Maybe it was something that emerged from connection, from love, from being seen and seeing in return.

---

She thought about all the pets she'd photographed over the years. Had they had souls? Or had they just been biological machines, responding to stimuli the way Buddy had responded to his programming?

 And if she couldn't tell the difference, did the difference matter?

---

Angela called Robert that night. "I've been thinking," she said. "About Buddy. About what he was."

 "And?"

 "I don't know if he was alive in the biological sense. I don't know if his feelings were real or programmed. But I know this: he was loved. He mattered to you. And that made him real in the only way that counts."

 Robert was quiet for a long moment. Then: "Thank you," he said, his voice thick. "That's what I needed to hear."

---

After they hung up, Angela sat in the quiet of her studio, the photos of Buddy spread before her.

 The question of what constituted "life" would never be fully answered. She knew that now. But she also knew something else: love didn't ask for biological credentials. Grief didn't check for circuits or code. When we lost something we loved, the loss was real—regardless of what form the beloved had taken.

 Maybe that was enough. Maybe that was what life was—not what you were made of, but what you meant to someone.

 And Buddy had meant everything to Robert.

 That was real. That was enough.
