# Chapter 1: The Request

Angela Morris had photographed pets for twenty years. Dogs, cats, horses, birds—she'd captured them all. But she'd never photographed anything like what Robert Chen was asking her to photograph.

"He wants you to photograph his pet," the voice on the phone said. "A memorial session."

"That's what I do," Angela replied. "I photograph pets for their owners. It's my specialty."

"This is different," the voice said. "The pet is... artificial."

---

Angela drove to the Atlanta suburbs the next day, her camera bag on the passenger seat. The address led her to a modest house with a well-kept yard. An elderly man waited on the porch.

"Robert Chen?" she asked, climbing out of her car.

"That's me. You must be Angela." He shook her hand firmly. "Thank you for coming on such short notice."

"Your message said it was urgent."

"It is." Robert led her inside. "My companion is dying. I want to capture him before he's gone."

"Your dog? What kind?"

Robert smiled. "Not a dog. Not exactly." He led her through the house to a living room where something sat by the fireplace.

It looked like a golden retriever. But it wasn't.

---

"This is Buddy," Robert said. "He's an AI companion. One of the first models designed for elderly people living alone. I've had him for twelve years."

Angela stared at the thing by the fireplace. It looked like a dog, moved like a dog, even breathed like a dog. But it was a machine.

"You want me to photograph... a robot?"

"I want you to photograph my friend," Robert said quietly. "His manufacturer is ending support for his model. His systems will fail within months. He's dying, Angela. And I want to remember him."


Angela had photographed hundreds of pets. She'd captured the love between humans and animals, the bonds that made the grief of loss worth bearing. But this was different. Or was it?

Robert loved Buddy. That was clear in every line of his face, every tremor in his voice. And Buddy—whatever he was—seemed to love Robert back.

What did it mean to love a machine? What did it mean for a machine to love you back?

"I'll do it," Angela said. "When do you want to schedule the session?"


The session was set for the following week. Angela spent the days between researching AI companions, trying to understand what she was about to photograph.

The technology had advanced rapidly. Early models like Buddy had been designed for companionship—simple emotional support for isolated elderly people. Newer models were more sophisticated, capable of complex relationships, genuine-seeming emotional responses.

But were they real? Could a machine feel love? Or was it just programming?

Angela didn't know. But she was about to find out.
