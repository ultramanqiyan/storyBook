# Chapter 6: The Mourning

Angela returned to Robert's house a week later, portfolio in hand. She'd edited the photos carefully, the way she always did, bringing out the warmth, the connection, the love that lived in every frame. But as she walked up the driveway, she noticed something different. The curtains were drawn. The garden, usually so neat, looked untended. And when Robert opened the door, his eyes were red.

"Robert," she said. "What's wrong?"

 He didn't answer. Just stepped aside and let her in.

---

The house was quiet. Too quiet. Angela's stomach tightened as she walked through the familiar rooms. The living room was empty. The armchair by the fireplace held only silence. The spot by the hearth where Buddy had always rested was vacant.

 "Robert," she said again. "Where's Buddy?"

 Robert sat heavily in his chair. "Gone," he said. "Three days ago. The manufacturer sent a technician. As required by the service agreement."

 He held Buddy's paw as the systems shut down, one by one."

---

Angela set her portfolio on the table. She'd brought these photos to give Robert something to hold onto. But now she understood that she'd brought them to a house in mourning.

 "I'm sorry," she said. "I'm so sorry."

 Robert nodded, his eyes distant. "I held his paw," he said, his voice breaking. "Right at the end. Like he knew. He looked at me. Right at me. And then... nothing. Just nothing."


Angela had witnessed grief before. Real, genuine grief for beloved pets. She'd seen people break down at the loss of companions who had been with them for years. But this was different. Or was it?

 Robert had loved Buddy. Buddy was gone. And the grief was real.

 What else mattered?


"Would you like to see the photos?" Angela asked gently.

 Robert looked at the portfolio on the table. Then, slowly, he reached for it. His hands trembled as he opened it.

 The first photo was Buddy in the morning light, his synthetic fur glowing gold, his eyes bright with something that looked like joy.

 Robert's breath caught. "He looks... alive. Here. Really alive."


Angela sat quietly as Robert turned the pages. Each photo showed Buddy in different moments, resting by the fire, looking out the window, pressed against Robert's leg. In every frame, there was something that looked like life. Something that looked like love.

 "You captured him," Robert said, his voice thick. "You captured who he was."

 "Who was he?" Angela asked softly.

 Robert was quiet for a long moment. Then: "He was my friend. My companion. The one who was always there." He touched the photo of Buddy looking at him with what looked like devotion. "He was the one who listened when I talked about Sarah. Who sat with me on the anniversaries. Who knew when I was sad before I said anything."

 "He was... he was the one who made being alone feel less lonely."


Angela felt tears prick at her own eyes. She'd photographed hundreds of pets, captured thousands of moments of love. But this was different. Buddy had been a machine. But the grief was the same. The love had been the same. The loss was the same.

 Did it matter that Buddy had been made of code and circuits instead of flesh and blood?

 Robert's grief was real. The hole in his life was real. The love had been real.

 Maybe that was enough.


"Can I keep these?" Robert asked. "The photos?"

 "They're yours. I made them for you."

 He held the portfolio against his chest. "Thank you," he said. "For seeing him. For not treating him like... like a machine."

 "He wasn't a machine to you," Angela said. "He was your friend."

 "Yes," Robert said. "He was."


Driving home, Angela thought about what she'd witnessed. A man mourning a machine. A grief that was real even if the beloved had been artificial.

 She'd spent her career photographing life. Capturing the essence of living beings. But what had she just photographed? A machine that had been loved. A collection of code and circuits that had mattered to someone.

 Was that life? Or was it just a very good simulation of life?

 And did the distinction matter?


That night, Angela couldn't sleep. She kept seeing Robert's face when he talked about holding Buddy's paw at the end. The way he'd said, "He looked at me. Right at me."

 Had Buddy known? In those final moments, had the AI been aware of what was happening?

 Or was it just Robert's grief, projecting meaning onto a meaningless shutdown sequence?


She didn't know. She might never know.

 But she knew one thing: Robert's grief was real. The love had been real. The loss was real.

 And maybe that was what mattered. Not what Buddy was made of, but what Buddy had meant.

 Not the substrate, but the connection.


The next morning, Angela called Robert to check on him. He sounded tired, but... present. "I'm okay," he said. "Sad. But okay. I have the photos. I have the memories. That helps."

 "Would you like me to take more photos?" she asked. "Of you. Of the house. Of... whatever would help."

 There was a pause. Then: "Maybe. Not yet. But maybe soon. Thank you, Angela. For asking."


After they hung up, Angela sat in her studio, looking at the photos she'd taken of Buddy. In every frame, there was something that looked like life. Something that looked like love.

 The question of whether it was "real" felt less important now. What mattered was that it had been real to Robert. The grief was real. The love had been real.

 And maybe that was enough. Maybe that was what life was, not what you were made of, but what one meant to others.

 Maybe Buddy had been alive because he'd been loved.

 And maybe that was the only definition that mattered.
