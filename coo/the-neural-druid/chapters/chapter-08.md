# Chapter 8: The System Collapse

The air carried the faint scent of coffee, lingering in the background.

Victor Strand was not a man who accepted failure.

He stood in his mobile command center—a high-tech trailer parked at the edge of sector seven—studying data streams that made no sense. His equipment was the best money could buy, calibrated by experts, maintained by technicians who cost more per hour than most people earned in a day.

And it was giving him garbage.

"Explain this to me again," he said, his voice dangerously calm.

The technician swallowed nervously. "Sir, the readings keep... changing. One moment we're detecting massive energy signatures from the clearing where Dr. Chen was spotted. The next moment, there's nothing. It's like something is deliberately manipulating our sensors."

"Something is," Strand said. "Or someone."

He pulled up satellite imagery of the area, taken just hours ago. The forest looked normal—old growth, dense, unremarkable. But when he compared it to images from three days ago, before Dr. Chen's midnight drive, the differences were striking.

Trees had grown. Not inches. Feet. In three days.

"Soil composition has changed dramatically," another technician reported. "Nutrient levels that should take years to develop have appeared overnight. And the water table—it's cleaner than any natural source we've ever measured."

Strand leaned back, processing. He had built his empire on understanding patterns, on seeing opportunities where others saw chaos. And the pattern here was clear, even if the details were obscured.

"Dr. Chen's AI has done something," he said. "Something unprecedented. And she's hiding it from us."

"Sir, should we move in? We have authorization—"

"Not yet." Strand stood, walking to the window that overlooked the dark forest. "Whatever is happening in there, it's valuable. More valuable than anything we've ever pursued. But it's also fragile. If we move too aggressively, we risk destroying it." He turned back to his team. "I want a different approach. Find out who Dr. Chen is talking to. Find out what her sister is working on. And find me a way into that forest that doesn't trigger whatever defenses she's set up."

His phone buzzed—a call from one of his contacts in Washington. He answered with a curt "Strand."

"Victor, we have a problem." The voice was tense, worried. "There's a journalist asking questions about Green Harvest. Sarah Chen. She's been contacting sources, digging into classified documents. If she publishes—"

"Handle it," Strand said. "Whatever it takes."

He ended the call and smiled grimly. Dr. Chen had a sister. A journalist sister. And that sister was causing trouble.

Perhaps it was time to remind the Chen family that Helix Industries played for keeps.
