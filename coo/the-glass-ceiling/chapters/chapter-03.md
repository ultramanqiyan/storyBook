# Chapter 3: The First Crack - Hidden Truth

The first month at Meridian was a blur.

Alex worked long hours—not because they had to, but because they wanted to. They wanted to prove they belonged. They wanted to show Marcus that his investment in them was justified. They wanted to be a Meridian person.

And for the first few weeks, everything seemed perfect.

The project was going well. The team was responsive. The stakeholders were happy. Alex felt like they were finally getting the hang of this product management thing. They'd stay late, working on documentation, responding to emails, preparing for meetings. The hours didn't matter because the work felt meaningful. Every task was a chance to prove themselves. Every project was an opportunity to show Marcus that he'd made the right choice.

They scheduled a 1:1 with Marcus to give an update. They were excited—they had good news to share. The feature was on track. The team was aligned. Everything was coming together.

---

**THE 1:1**

Marcus's office was sparse—a glass desk, a whiteboard covered in diagrams, a few family photos on the windowsill. The afternoon light streamed through the floor-to-ceiling windows, casting long shadows across the floor. The view showed the Meridian campus stretching out below, employees walking between buildings like ants in a carefully designed colony.

He gestured for Alex to sit.

"So, how's it going?"

"Great!" Alex said, leaning forward in their chair. They'd prepared for this meeting, rehearsed what they wanted to say. "The project is on track. We're launching in two weeks, and the team is excited. I've been working with Sarah on the documentation, and the engineering team has been really responsive. The stakeholders are happy with the progress."

Marcus nodded slowly. His expression was thoughtful, serious. The smile that had been so warm in previous meetings was gone, replaced by something more measured.

"That's good. But I want to talk about something else."

Alex's stomach tightened. "Oh?"

Marcus leaned forward, his eyes meeting Alex's. The afternoon light caught his face, illuminating the concern there. "Alex, I've been watching you. And I see a lot of potential. But I also see some areas for growth."

*Areas for growth.*

The words landed like a punch. Alex had been working twelve-hour days. They'd been giving everything to this job. They'd been the first one in and the last one out. And there were still areas for growth?

"What kind of areas?" Alex's voice came out smaller than they intended.

Marcus smiled—the same warm smile he'd given Alex in their interview. "Don't worry, this is normal. Everyone has areas for growth. That's how we get better. That's why we have these conversations. That's what separates good companies from great ones—we invest in developing our people."

He pulled up a document on his screen. Alex could see their name at the top, followed by a list of bullet points.

"I've been tracking your work, and I've noticed a few things. You're very detail-oriented, which is great. But sometimes you get lost in the details and miss the bigger picture."

Alex blinked. "I... I thought I was supposed to be detail-oriented."

"You are! That's a strength. But it can also be a limitation. I need you to be more strategic. Think about the why, not just the what. Understand how your work connects to the company's broader goals. See the forest, not just the trees."

*More strategic. Think about the why.*

Alex nodded slowly, trying to absorb the feedback. "Okay. I can do that."

Marcus's smile widened. "I know you can. That's why I'm telling you this. I believe in you, Alex. I want to help you grow. That's what families do—they help each other improve. I'm investing in you because I see your potential."

---

**THE DOUBT**

After the meeting, Alex walked back to their desk in a daze.

*More strategic. Think about the why. Miss the bigger picture.*

They'd thought they were doing well. They'd thought the project was going great. They'd prepared for that meeting expecting praise, not criticism. But apparently, there was something wrong with how they were working. Something they'd missed. Something everyone else could see but they couldn't.

They pulled up their project notes and stared at them. The documentation they'd spent hours on. The spreadsheets they'd created. The presentations they'd prepared. All of it suddenly seemed inadequate.

*What am I missing? What's the bigger picture?*

Around them, the office hummed with activity. People walked past, talking on phones, typing on laptops, moving with purpose. Jordan was laughing at something on their screen. Taylor was in a meeting, their face serious. Sarah was staring at her monitor, her eyes hollow. Sam was pacing near the windows, phone pressed to their ear.

Alex felt suddenly, intensely alone. Everyone else seemed to know what they were doing. Everyone else seemed to have figured it out. What was wrong with them?

---

**THE LATE NIGHT**

Alex stayed late that night.

The office emptied around them—people heading home, heading to dinner, heading to lives outside of work. The cleaning crew came through, vacuuming around desks, emptying trash cans. The lights in the ceiling dimmed automatically, sensing the reduced occupancy.

But Alex stayed at their desk, the glow of their monitor the only light in the darkening workspace. The air conditioning hummed overhead, a constant white noise that filled the silence.

They rewrote their project documentation. They added strategic context to every feature. They created a "why" section at the top of every document. They linked every task to company goals. They added metrics and projections and long-term vision statements. They created diagrams showing how their work connected to the broader product strategy.

By 10 PM, they had a new version ready to share with Marcus.

They sent it to him and headed home. The parking lot was still a quarter full.

---

**THE RESPONSE**

The next morning, Alex woke up early, anxious to see Marcus's response. They'd worked so hard on the revision. They'd addressed every concern. Surely this time, they'd get it right.

Marcus had replied at 7:15 AM:

*Thanks for this, Alex. I can see you're trying to be more strategic. But I think you're overcorrecting. Now you're too high-level and missing the details. Let's discuss in our next 1:1.*

Alex stared at the email.

*Too high-level. Missing the details.*

But yesterday, they'd been too detail-oriented and missing the bigger picture.

Which was it?

They read the email again. Then again. The words didn't change. They sat at their kitchen table, the morning light streaming through the window, and tried to understand what Marcus wanted.

*Be more strategic. But don't lose the details. See the bigger picture. But don't miss the specifics.*

How could they do both? How could anyone do both?

---

**THE CONVERSATION**

Alex brought it up in their next 1:1.

They sat across from Marcus, the same glass desk between them, the same afternoon light streaming through the windows. But everything felt different now. The office that had seemed so welcoming now felt like a trap.

"Marcus, I'm confused. You said I was too detail-oriented, so I tried to be more strategic. But now you're saying I'm too high-level?"

Marcus frowned. His brow furrowed in what looked like genuine confusion. "Alex, I never said you were too detail-oriented. I said that's a strength, but it can be a limitation. There's a difference."

Alex felt like they were losing their mind.

"But... you said I was missing the bigger picture."

"Right. And you are. But that doesn't mean you should stop being detail-oriented. It means you need to do both. Hold the details and the strategy simultaneously. That's what great product managers do."

*Both.*

"How do I do both?"

Marcus smiled, patient and kind. "That's what I'm here to help you with. It takes time. Be patient with yourself. Growth doesn't happen overnight. This is a journey, Alex. I'm here to guide you through it."

Alex nodded, but something felt wrong. They couldn't put their finger on it. The feedback made sense when Marcus said it, but when they tried to apply it, everything fell apart. It was like trying to hit a target that kept moving.

---

**THE PATTERN**

Over the next few weeks, a pattern emerged.

Alex would work hard on something. Marcus would give feedback. The feedback would be contradictory or impossible to satisfy. Alex would try harder. The cycle would repeat.

*You're doing great, but you need to be more visible.*

*You're doing great, but you need to speak up more in meetings.*

*You're doing great, but you need to be more concise in your updates.*

*You're doing great, but you need to be more thorough in your analysis.*

*You're doing great, but you need to take more initiative.*

*You're doing great, but you need to wait for direction.*

*You're doing great, but...*

Always "but." Always something to improve. Always just out of reach.

Alex started working longer hours. They stopped taking lunch breaks. They started coming in on weekends. They told themselves it was temporary—that once they figured out what Marcus wanted, everything would click. That once they proved themselves, the criticism would stop.

---

**THE COMPARISON**

Alex started comparing themselves to the other PMs.

Jordan was always enthusiastic, always visible, always in Marcus's good graces. They seemed to have figured out the formula—show up early, leave late, smile constantly, agree with everything Marcus said. But Jordan had been there a year and was still doing the same work. No promotions. No new projects. Just the same enthusiastic performance, day after day.

Taylor was cynical, but competent. They'd been there three years and seemed to have figured out how to do the minimum without getting fired. They came in at 9, left at 6, and never stayed for team dinners. Their work was good enough, but nothing special. They seemed to have given up on trying to impress Marcus.

Sarah was... Sarah. Quiet. Tired. Going through the motions. Alex had tried to talk to her a few times, but she always deflected. "I'm fine. Just tired." But her eyes said something else—something broken and resigned.

And Sam. Sam was always anxious, always checking their phone, always worried about something. They'd been there six months and seemed to be unraveling. Alex had caught them crying in the bathroom once. They'd pretended it was allergies.

*Is this what happens to people here?*

---

**THE BREAKTHROUGH**

One day, Alex had a breakthrough.

They'd been struggling with a particularly difficult stakeholder—a VP named David who wanted a feature that didn't align with the product strategy. David was powerful, well-connected, and used to getting his way. He'd pushed hard, making demands, threatening to escalate.

Alex had pushed back. They'd cited data and user research. They'd shown how the feature would hurt the product in the long run. They'd stood their ground, even when David got angry, even when he'd threatened to go to Marcus.

The stakeholder escalated to Marcus.

Marcus called Alex into his office.

"I heard about the situation with David. You handled it well."

Alex blinked. "I did?"

"Yes. You pushed back with data. You stood your ground. That's exactly what I want to see from you. That's strategic thinking."

For the first time in weeks, Alex felt like they'd done something right. The relief was overwhelming—a physical sensation, like a weight lifting from their chest.

"Thank you, Marcus."

He smiled. "See? You're learning. I knew you had it in you. This is what I've been trying to teach you—strategic thinking, standing up for what's right. You're growing, Alex. I'm proud of you."

*I'm proud of you.*

The words washed over Alex like a warm wave. All the criticism, all the confusion, all the late nights—it was worth it. Marcus believed in them. Marcus was proud of them. They were finally becoming the person Marcus wanted them to be.

---

**THE HIGH**

That night, Alex texted Rachel:

*Had a win today. Marcus said I handled a difficult situation well. He said he's proud of me.*

Rachel's response was immediate:

*That's great! But Alex, you've been there a month. Shouldn't you have had more wins by this point?*

Alex stared at the message.

*What do you mean?*

*I mean, you're working 12-hour days. You're coming in on weekends. You should be crushing it. Instead, you're celebrating one "good job" after weeks of criticism.*

*It's not like that. Marcus is helping me grow. The feedback is normal for a new job.*

*Alex, I love you. But this sounds like manipulation. Good managers don't make you doubt yourself constantly. Good managers don't give you impossible standards. Good managers don't make you work weekends and then criticize you for not doing enough.*

*You don't understand. This is how it works at top companies. This is what it takes to succeed. This is the opportunity I've been waiting for.*

*I hope you're right. I really do. But please, just keep your eyes open. Okay? And call me if you need to talk.*

Alex put down their phone.

Rachel didn't understand. She'd never worked at a place like Meridian. She didn't know what it took to succeed here. She didn't understand the pressure, the expectations, the opportunity.

*Marcus believes in me. He's investing in me. He wants me to grow.*

That had to mean something.

But as Alex lay in bed that night, staring at the ceiling, a small voice whispered: *What if Rachel is right?*

They pushed the thought away and closed their eyes. Tomorrow would be better. Tomorrow they'd prove themselves again. Tomorrow they'd finally become the person Marcus wanted them to be.

---

**END OF CHAPTER 3**
