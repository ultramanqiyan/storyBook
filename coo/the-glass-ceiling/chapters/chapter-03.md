# Chapter 3: The First Crack

The first month at Meridian was a blur.

Alex worked long hours—not because they had to, but because they wanted to. They wanted to prove they belonged. They wanted to show Marcus that his investment in them was justified.

They wanted to be a Meridian person.

**THE PROJECT**

Alex's first project was going well.

The feature enhancement was on track. The engineering team was responsive. The stakeholders were happy. Alex felt like they were finally getting the hang of this product management thing.

They scheduled a 1:1 with Marcus to give an update.

**THE 1:1**

Marcus's office was sparse—a desk, a whiteboard, a few family photos. He gestured for Alex to sit.

"So, how's it going?"

"Great!" Alex said. "The project is on track. We're launching in two weeks, and the team is excited."

Marcus nodded slowly. "That's good. But I want to talk about something else."

Alex's stomach tightened. "Oh?"

Marcus leaned forward. "Alex, I've been watching you. And I see a lot of potential. But I also see some areas for growth."

*Areas for growth.*

The words landed like a punch.

"What kind of areas?"

Marcus smiled—the same warm smile he'd given Alex in their interview. "Don't worry, this is normal. Everyone has areas for growth. That's how we get better."

He pulled up a document on his screen.

"I've been tracking your work, and I've noticed a few things. You're very detail-oriented, which is great. But sometimes you get lost in the details and miss the bigger picture."

Alex blinked. "I... I thought I was supposed to be detail-oriented."

"You are! That's a strength. But it can also be a limitation. I need you to be more strategic. Think about the why, not just the what."

*More strategic.*

Alex nodded slowly. "Okay. I can do that."

Marcus's smile widened. "I know you can. That's why I'm telling you this. I believe in you, Alex. I want to help you grow."

**THE DOUBT**

After the meeting, Alex walked back to their desk in a daze.

*More strategic. Think about the why. Miss the bigger picture.*

They'd thought they were doing well. They'd thought the project was going great. But apparently, there was something wrong with how they were working.

They pulled up their project notes and stared at them.

*What am I missing? What's the bigger picture?*

**THE LATE NIGHT**

Alex stayed late that night.

They rewrote their project documentation. They added strategic context to every feature. They created a "why" section at the top of every document.

By 10 PM, they had a new version ready to share with Marcus.

They sent it to him and headed home.

**THE RESPONSE**

The next morning, Marcus had replied:

*Thanks for this, Alex. I can see you're trying to be more strategic. But I think you're overcorrecting. Now you're too high-level and missing the details. Let's discuss in our next 1:1.*

Alex stared at the email.

*Too high-level. Missing the details.*

But yesterday, they'd been too detail-oriented and missing the bigger picture.

Which was it?

**THE CONVERSATION**

Alex brought it up in their next 1:1.

"Marcus, I'm confused. You said I was too detail-oriented, so I tried to be more strategic. But now you're saying I'm too high-level?"

Marcus frowned. "Alex, I never said you were too detail-oriented. I said that's a strength, but it can be a limitation. There's a difference."

Alex felt like they were losing their mind.

"But... you said I was missing the bigger picture."

"Right. And you are. But that doesn't mean you should stop being detail-oriented. It means you need to do both."

*Both.*

"How do I do both?"

Marcus smiled. "That's what I'm here to help you with. It takes time. Be patient with yourself."

**THE PATTERN**

Over the next few weeks, a pattern emerged.

Alex would work hard on something. Marcus would give feedback. The feedback would be contradictory or impossible to satisfy. Alex would try harder. The cycle would repeat.

*You're doing great, but you need to be more visible.*

*You're doing great, but you need to speak up more in meetings.*

*You're doing great, but you need to be more concise in your updates.*

*You're doing great, but...*

Always "but." Always something to improve. Always just out of reach.

**THE COMPARISON**

Alex started comparing themselves to the other PMs.

Jordan was always enthusiastic, always visible, always in Marcus's good graces. But Jordan had been there a year and was still doing the same work.

Taylor was cynical, but competent. They'd been there three years and seemed to have figured out how to do the minimum without getting fired.

Sarah was... Sarah. Quiet. Tired. Going through the motions.

And Sam. Sam was always anxious, always checking their phone, always worried about something.

*Is this what happens to people here?*

**THE BREAKTHROUGH**

One day, Alex had a breakthrough.

They'd been struggling with a particularly difficult stakeholder. The stakeholder wanted a feature that didn't align with the product strategy. Alex had pushed back, citing data and user research.

The stakeholder escalated to Marcus.

Marcus called Alex into his office.

"I heard about the situation with David. You handled it well."

Alex blinked. "I did?"

"Yes. You pushed back with data. You stood your ground. That's exactly what I want to see from you."

For the first time in weeks, Alex felt like they'd done something right.

"Thank you, Marcus."

He smiled. "See? You're learning. I knew you had it in you."

**THE HIGH**

That night, Alex texted Rachel:

*Had a win today. Marcus said I handled a difficult situation well.*

Rachel's response was immediate:

*That's great! But Alex, you've been there a month. Shouldn't you have had more wins by this point?*

Alex stared at the message.

*What do you mean?*

*I mean, you're working 12-hour days. You should be crushing it. Instead, you're celebrating one "good job" after weeks of criticism.*

*It's not like that. Marcus is helping me grow.*

*Alex, I love you. But this sounds like manipulation.*

*You don't understand. This is how it works at top companies.*

*I hope you're right.*

Alex put down their phone.

Rachel didn't understand. She'd never worked at a place like Meridian. She didn't know what it took to succeed here.

*Marcus believes in me. He's investing in me. He wants me to grow.*

That had to mean something.

**END OF CHAPTER 3**
