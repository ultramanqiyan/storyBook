# Chapter 5: The Isolation - First Step

By month four, Alex was alone.

Not literally—there were still people around. The team still sat nearby. The office still hummed with activity. But the connections they'd made in the beginning had faded. The enthusiasm had curdled into exhaustion. The "family" had become a collection of individuals, each fighting their own battles, each drowning in their own way.

---

**THE DISTANCE**

Alex noticed it first with their friends outside work.

They'd stopped responding to texts—not intentionally, but because every time they saw a notification, they were too exhausted to engage. They'd missed birthday parties, sending last-minute apologies that felt hollow even to them. They'd forgotten to call their parents for three weeks, and when they finally realized it, they felt a wave of guilt that they quickly buried under more work.

When they finally did call home, their mother's voice was strained with worry.

"Alex, I was worried about you. You never call anymore. Are you okay?"

"I'm sorry, Mom. Work has been crazy. We're in the middle of a big project."

"Work is always crazy. That's not an excuse. Your father and I miss you."

Alex wanted to argue, wanted to explain that this wasn't just normal busy—this was survival. But they didn't have the energy. They were too tired to explain, too tired to justify, too tired to do anything but apologize and promise to do better.

"I know. I'm sorry. I'll do better. I'll call more often."

But they didn't do better. Because every time they thought about reaching out, they remembered the project that wasn't moving the needle, the feedback that wasn't positive, the expectations they weren't meeting. They remembered Marcus's disappointed expression. They remembered the performance review that said "meeting expectations" like it was a failure.

*I'll call after this deadline.*

*I'll visit after this project.*

*I'll take a break after this quarter.*

But there was always another deadline. Another project. Another quarter. The finish line kept moving, and Alex kept chasing it.

---

**THE TEAM**

The team had changed too.

Jordan was still enthusiastic, but Alex noticed the cracks beneath the surface. The forced smiles that didn't reach their eyes. The late nights that had become routine. The desperate need to be visible, to be praised, to be seen. Jordan had been here a year, and they were still performing the same role, still chasing the same validation, still hoping that if they just worked hard enough, Marcus would finally give them what they promised.

Taylor had stopped pretending to care. They came in at 9, left at 6, and did the minimum required to not get fired. Their cynicism had calcified into a protective shell. They'd figured out the game—do enough to survive, but not enough to be noticed. It wasn't ambition. It was survival.

Sam had been put on a PIP. Alex found out by accident—Sam's screen was visible during a meeting, and the HR email was open. *Performance Improvement Plan.* The same words that had been haunting Alex's nightmares since their first review. Sam had stopped making eye contact with anyone. They spent their lunch breaks in their car, staring at nothing.

And Sarah. Sarah was barely present. She came in late, left early, and spent most of her time staring at her screen with dead eyes. The woman who had warned Alex about manipulation had become a ghost of herself—going through the motions, counting down the hours until she could escape.

"Are you okay?" Alex asked her once, catching her in the break room.

Sarah laughed. The sound was hollow. "No. But that's not new." She looked at Alex with something like pity. "Are you?"

Alex didn't know how to answer.

---

**THE PHYSICAL TOLL**

Alex started getting sick.

First, it was headaches—persistent, throbbing pain that started behind their eyes and spread across their skull. They took ibuprofen like candy, chasing it with coffee that had become their primary food group.

Then, it was insomnia. They'd lie in bed at night, their mind racing with all the things they hadn't done, all the feedback they hadn't addressed, all the ways they were falling short. When they finally did sleep, it was fitful, interrupted by anxiety dreams about work.

Then, it was a persistent cold that wouldn't go away. Their body ached constantly. Their skin broke out. Their hair started falling out in clumps in the shower.

They went to the doctor, who ran tests and found nothing physically wrong.

"You're run down," the doctor said, looking at Alex's chart with concern. "Your blood work is fine, but your stress hormones are through the roof. You need rest. You need to reduce your stress. You need to take care of yourself."

*Reduce stress. At Meridian.*

Alex laughed. The sound came out bitter. "That's not really an option. I have a job to do."

The doctor looked at them with something like sadness. "Then you need to make it an option. Your body is telling you something. Listen to it. Because if you don't, it will force you to stop. And that won't be on your terms."

But Alex didn't have time to listen to their body. They had a project to deliver. They had feedback to address. They had expectations to meet. They had a manager who was questioning whether they could handle the job.

---

**THE MISTAKE**

The mistake happened on a Thursday.

Alex was exhausted. They'd been working until midnight every night for two weeks, trying to get ahead of the feedback, trying to anticipate what Marcus would criticize before he could criticize it. Their brain was foggy. Their judgment was impaired. Their hands trembled slightly as they typed.

They sent an email to the wrong stakeholder.

It wasn't a catastrophic error—the information wasn't confidential, nothing was leaked. But it was embarrassing. The stakeholder replied with confusion, copying Marcus, asking why they'd received an email clearly meant for someone else. And Marcus found out.

---

**THE MEETING**

Marcus called Alex into his office the next morning.

The office felt different now. The glass desk that had once seemed modern and impressive now felt like an interrogation table. The floor-to-ceiling windows that had once inspired Alex now made them feel exposed.

"I heard about the email."

Alex's stomach dropped. "I'm sorry. I was tired. I made a mistake. It won't happen again."

Marcus nodded slowly. His expression was serious, disappointed. The warmth that had been there in the beginning was gone. "I understand. But this is concerning, Alex. You've been here four months, and you're still making basic errors. I need to know—can you handle this job?"

*Can you handle this job.*

The question hit Alex like a physical blow. They'd given everything to this job. They'd sacrificed their health, their relationships, their sanity. And Marcus was asking if they could handle it?

"Yes. I can. It was one mistake. I was tired."

"One mistake that shouldn't have happened. One mistake that reflects on me as your manager. One mistake that makes me question whether I made the right decision hiring you."

*Question whether I made the right decision.*

Alex felt tears forming. They blinked them back, refusing to cry in front of Marcus.

"I'm sorry. It won't happen again."

Marcus's expression softened slightly. "Alex, I want you to succeed. I've invested in you. I believe in your potential. But I need you to show me you can handle this. I need you to step up. Can you do that?"

"Yes. I can. I will."

Marcus smiled. "Good. I believe in you. Don't let me down."

---

**THE SPIRAL**

After the meeting, Alex spiraled.

*I made a mistake. I'm not handling the job. Marcus is questioning his decision. I need to work harder.*

They worked harder. Longer. They stopped taking breaks entirely. They stopped eating properly—just coffee and vending machine snacks. They stopped sleeping enough, catching naps in their car during lunch when they couldn't keep their eyes open.

*If I just work harder, I'll prove I can do this. If I just work harder, Marcus will see I'm worth it.*

But the harder they worked, the more mistakes they made. And the more mistakes they made, the harder they worked. The exhaustion created errors. The errors created shame. The shame created more work.

It was a cycle that couldn't end well.

---

**THE SISTER**

Rachel called on a Saturday evening.

Alex hadn't spoken to her in weeks. They'd been avoiding her calls, dreading the questions, not wanting to admit how bad things had gotten.

"Alex, I'm worried about you."

"I'm fine."

"You're not fine. I can hear it in your voice. You sound exhausted. You sound hollow. When did you last eat a real meal? When did you last sleep through the night?"

"I'm just busy. Work is demanding."

"Busy is one thing. This is something else. Alex, please. Tell me what's going on. I'm your sister. I love you."

Alex wanted to lie. Wanted to say everything was fine, that they were just tired, that it would get better. But they were too exhausted to lie. Too broken to pretend.

"I don't know what's happening," Alex admitted, their voice cracking. "I'm working so hard, but nothing I do is right. My manager keeps giving me feedback, but it doesn't make sense. It's contradictory. I try to fix one thing, and he criticizes me for something else. I feel like I'm losing my mind."

Rachel was quiet for a moment. When she spoke, her voice was careful, measured.

"Alex, this sounds like gaslighting."

*Gaslighting.*

The word landed like a stone in water.

"What do you mean?"

"I mean, your manager is telling you things that contradict each other. He's making you doubt your own perception. He's making you feel like you're the problem when you're not. That's what gaslighting is—making someone question their own reality."

"But... he believes in me. He said so. He invested in me. He wants me to succeed."

"Did he? Or did he say he believes in you, while making you feel like you're constantly failing? Did he say he wants you to succeed, while setting standards you can never meet?"

Alex didn't know how to answer. The questions cut through the fog in their brain, exposing things they'd been trying not to see.

"I... I need to think about this."

"Alex, I love you. And I'm scared for you. Please. Take care of yourself. Come home if you need to. We'll figure it out together."

---

**THE NIGHT**

Alex ended the call and stared at their phone.

Rachel's words echoed in their mind: *Gaslighting.* Was that what this was? Was that the key to understanding everything that had happened?

They wanted to deny it. Wanted to believe Marcus. Wanted to believe that if they just worked harder, everything would click into place.

But the evidence was mounting. The contradictory feedback. The moving goalposts. The isolation from everyone who might give them perspective. The physical toll their body was paying.

*I'm losing my mind,* Alex whispered to the empty room. *Or maybe I'm not. Maybe I'm finally seeing clearly.*

They pushed the thought away and tried to sleep. But sleep wouldn't come. The questions kept circling, demanding answers Alex wasn't ready to give.

---

**END OF CHAPTER 5**
