The next morning, Alex went to work with a different mindset.

He didn't stop feeling the anger or the grief. Those were still there, simmering beneath the surface. But he tried something new: he acknowledged them.

*I'm angry because I feel like my expertise is being dismissed. I'm grieving because something I valued is ending. That's okay. That's human.*

But he also tried something else. He started asking questions instead of defending.

"Taylor," he said, approaching the digital team leader, "can you show me how the programmatic platform works? I want to understand it better."

*It's not completely foreign,* he realized. *It's a different application of the same fundamentals.*


That afternoon, Alex sat in Dr. Wong's office and shared what he'd learned.

"I think I've been making it harder than it needs to be," he said. "I've been treating digital like it's a completely different world. But it's not. It's the same world, with different tools."

"That's a significant insight," Dr. Wong said. "What changed?"

"I stopped defending and started asking. Instead of trying to prove that print was valuable, I tried to understand why digital was effective." Alex paused. "And I realized something. The people who are good at digital aren't smarter than me. They just learned different tools. I can learn those tools too."

"Still scary." Alex smiled slightly. "But less impossible. Less like I'm starting from nothing. More like I'm adding to what I already know."

"That's the adaptation mindset," Dr. Wong said. "Not abandoning who you were. Becoming who you need to be next."

Alex nodded slowly. He wasn't there yet. He still felt the fear, the grief, the resistance. But for the first time, he could see a path forward.

Not a path back to what he'd lost. A path forward to something new.

And he was ready to start walking.
