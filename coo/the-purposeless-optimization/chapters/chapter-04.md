# Chapter 4: The Section

The recommendation came on a Tuesday morning.

Robert was reviewing the StoreBrain dashboard with his coffee, the way he did every day now. The system had flagged several items for his attention: a pricing adjustment on coffee beans, a staffing change for the weekend, an inventory order for the children's section.

And then, at the bottom of the list: "Discontinue poetry section. Floor space: 120 sq ft. Annual revenue: $2,340. Revenue per sq ft: $19.50. Store average: $47.20. Recommendation: Reallocate space to commercial fiction."

Robert stared at the notification.

The poetry section. His poetry section. The corner of the store where the light was soft, where Martha liked to browse, where the writing group met on Thursday evenings.

StoreBrain wanted him to get rid of it.

---

He walked to the poetry corner and stood there for a long time.

The section was small - just three bookcases, a comfortable chair, and a lamp. But it was one of his favorite parts of the store. He'd curated every title himself: classic poets like Whitman and Dickinson, contemporary voices like Mary Oliver and Billy Collins, local poets who had self-published their work.

The section wasn't profitable. StoreBrain was right about that. Poetry didn't sell the way commercial fiction did. Most people who browsed the poetry section didn't buy anything - they just read, or sat in the chair, or attended the writing group meetings.

But that was the point. The poetry section wasn't about revenue. It was about creating a space for something that mattered. Something that couldn't be measured in sales per square foot.

---

He rejected the recommendation.

StoreBrain responded with a gentle prompt: "Are you sure? Discontinuing the poetry section would increase annual revenue by an estimated $3,300. This would offset the underperformance of other sections and improve overall store profitability."

Robert rejected it again.

The system accepted his decision, but he could feel its disapproval. The dashboard showed a small red flag next to the poetry section - a reminder that it was underperforming, that it was costing him money, that it wasn't optimized.


That afternoon, Martha came in for her usual coffee.

She noticed the red flag on the dashboard - Robert had left the tablet on the counter - and her expression grew concerned.

"What's that about the poetry section?"

Robert explained the recommendation. How StoreBrain wanted him to discontinue it. How it wasn't profitable.

Martha's face softened. "Robert, the poetry section is one of the reasons I come here. It's not about the sales. It's about... the space. The possibility. The sense that this store values something beyond the bottom line."

"I know," Robert said. "I rejected the recommendation."

Martha smiled. "Good. Don't let the machine tell you what matters."


But the recommendation kept coming.

Every day, StoreBrain flagged the poetry section. Every day, Robert rejected it. But the system was persistent, and its arguments were compelling.

"Poetry section has underperformed for 6 consecutive months. Reallocating space would improve store efficiency by 4%."

"Customer traffic analysis shows low engagement with poetry section. Average dwell time: 8 minutes. Conversion rate: 12%. Store average: 34%."

"Competitor analysis shows similar stores have discontinued poetry sections. Market trend: declining poetry sales across all channels."

The data was clear. The poetry section was a drag on the store's performance. Getting rid of it would make the store more efficient, more profitable, more successful.


After two weeks of daily recommendations, Robert began to waver.

He told himself it was just one section. He could keep some poetry books in the general literature area. The writing group could meet somewhere else. Martha would understand.

And the store would be better. More efficient. More profitable.

Wasn't that the point of using StoreBrain in the first place? To make the store better?


He made the decision on a Thursday evening.

The writing group was meeting in the poetry corner - five people sitting in a circle, sharing their work. Robert watched them for a moment, then went to his office and approved the recommendation.

The poetry section would be discontinued. The space would be reallocated to commercial fiction.

He felt a hollow ache in his chest as he clicked "Approve." But he pushed it away. This was what optimization looked like. Making hard choices for the good of the whole.


The next day, he started moving the books.

He boxed up the poetry collection - Whitman, Dickinson, Oliver, Collins, and all the local poets who had trusted him with their work. He moved the comfortable chair to another part of the store. He took down the lamp.

By afternoon, the poetry corner was empty. Just a blank space where something had been.

Martha came in that evening. She walked to the poetry corner and stopped.

"Robert? What happened?"

"I had to make some changes," he said, not meeting her eyes. "The section wasn't performing. StoreBrain recommended -"

"StoreBrain recommended," Martha repeated, her voice flat. "And you listened."

"It wasn't just that," Robert said. "The data was clear. The section was costing me money. I had to make a business decision."

Martha looked at him for a long moment. Then she nodded slowly.

"I understand. It's your store. You have to do what's best for business."

She bought her coffee and left without another word.


That night, Robert couldn't sleep.

He lay in bed, thinking about the empty space where the poetry section had been. Thinking about Martha's face when she saw it. Thinking about the writing group that would have nowhere to meet next Thursday.

He told himself it was the right decision. The data was clear. The store would be better.

But something felt wrong. Something he couldn't name.


The next morning, StoreBrain had a new notification for him.

"Recommendation approved. Projected annual revenue increase: $3,300. Store efficiency improved by 4%. Excellent decision."

Robert stared at the notification. The system was congratulating him. Telling him he'd done the right thing.

But he didn't feel like he'd done the right thing. He felt like he'd lost something.

He just couldn't figure out what.
