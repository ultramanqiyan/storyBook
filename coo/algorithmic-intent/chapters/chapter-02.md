# Chapter 2: The Introduction

The IntentPredict system arrived at the community center on a Monday morning, delivered by a technician named Kevin who seemed far too enthusiastic about software for Dot's taste. He carried a laptop, a tablet, and a small camera device that he mounted on the wall of her office.

"The camera captures facial expressions and body language," Kevin explained, adjusting the angle. "The microphone picks up speech patterns and vocal cues. The system analyzes everything in real time and provides insights on the tablet."

Dot watched him work, her arms crossed. "And where does all this data go?"

"It's processed locally, on the laptop. Nothing leaves your office without your permission." Kevin smiled, as if anticipating her concerns. "Privacy is a big deal for us. The system is designed to work with you, not to spy on anyone."

"And if I don't want to use it for a particular case?"

"Totally up to you. The system is a tool, not a requirement. You decide when and how to use it."

Dot nodded slowly. She was still skeptical, but Kevin's matter-of-fact approach made the technology feel less threatening. It was just a camera and a computer. It couldn't replace her. It could only provide information.

"Okay," she said. "Show me how it works."

---

Kevin walked her through the interface. The tablet displayed a simple dashboard with options for starting a new session, reviewing past cases, and accessing the system's analysis tools.

"When you start a session, the system will begin recording and analyzing," Kevin explained. "It looks for patterns in speech, facial expressions, and body language that correlate with underlying intent. The analysis appears on the tablet in real time."

He pulled up a demo video. "This is a recorded session from one of our pilot communities. Watch the analysis panel."

The video showed a mediation session between two business partners. As they spoke, text appeared on the side of the screen: *Participant A: Stated concern is financial. Underlying intent is fear of betrayal.* *Participant B: Stated concern is workload. Underlying intent is desire for recognition.*

"The system identified these intents within the first five minutes," Kevin said. "The mediator was able to address the real issues immediately, and the case resolved in under an hour."

Dot studied the analysis. It was impressive, she had to admit. But something about it bothered her.

"How does the system know when it's wrong?" she asked.

"What do you mean?"

"I mean, how do you verify that the intent it identifies is actually what the person wants? People are complicated. They can want multiple things at once. They can want things they're not aware of. How does the system account for that?"

Kevin considered the question. "The system doesn't claim to know with certainty. It provides probabilities based on patterns. The final judgment is always yours. You're the mediator. The system just gives you information."

Dot nodded, but she wasn't entirely satisfied. The system claimed to identify intent, but intent was slippery. She'd spent fifteen years learning to read people, and she still got surprised sometimes. How could an algorithm be more accurate?

---

After Kevin left, Dot sat in her office, staring at the new equipment. The camera watched her from the wall, its small lens glinting in the afternoon light. The tablet sat on her desk, waiting for input. The laptop hummed quietly in the corner.

She felt a strange mixture of curiosity and resistance. Part of her wanted to try the system immediately, to see what it could do. Another part of her wanted to reject it entirely, to prove that human insight couldn't be replaced by algorithms.

But that wasn't fair, she reminded herself. The system wasn't replacing her. It was helping her. That's what the county had promised, anyway.

Her first appointment of the afternoon arrived—a dispute between two neighbors about a shared driveway. Dot had been mediating this kind of case for years. She knew the patterns, the arguments, the underlying dynamics.

She decided to try the system.

"Before we begin," she told the neighbors, "I'd like to try something new. The county has provided a tool that might help us understand the situation better. It's completely voluntary—do you mind if I use it?"

Both neighbors nodded, curious but unconcerned. Dot started the session on the tablet.

---

The mediation proceeded as these cases usually did. Mrs. Patterson claimed that Mr. Gonzalez was blocking the shared driveway with his work truck. Mr. Gonzalez claimed that Mrs. Patterson was parking too close to the entrance, making it difficult for him to navigate. Each had photographs, witnesses, and a firm conviction that they were right.

Dot listened, the way she always did. But this time, she also watched the tablet.

The system's analysis appeared gradually, updating as the conversation progressed:

*Patterson: Stated position is access. Primary intent is boundary maintenance. Secondary intent is respect acknowledgment.*

*Gonzalez: Stated position is fairness. Primary intent is practical necessity. Secondary intent is autonomy preservation.*

Dot studied the analysis. It was similar to what she would have concluded, but it had taken the system only ten minutes to identify patterns that usually took her an hour or more to uncover.

Mrs. Patterson didn't just want access to the driveway. She wanted Mr. Gonzalez to acknowledge that her property line was important, that she had rights he needed to respect. Mr. Gonzalez didn't just want fairness. He wanted to feel that he could make decisions about his own property without interference.

The surface dispute was about parking. The real conflict was about respect and autonomy.

---

Armed with this insight, Dot guided the conversation in a new direction.

"Mrs. Patterson," she said, "what would it mean to you if Mr. Gonzalez acknowledged your property line?"

The question caught Mrs. Patterson off guard. "I... well, it would mean he respected me. That he understood I have rights too."

"And Mr. Gonzalez, what would it mean to you if Mrs. Patterson acknowledged your need to use the driveway for your work?"

Mr. Gonzalez shifted in his chair. "It would mean she understood I have a business to run. That I'm not just some inconvenience."

The conversation shifted. For the next thirty minutes, Dot helped them negotiate not just parking arrangements, but mutual acknowledgment. By the end of the session, they had an agreement—and, more importantly, a new understanding of each other.

"Thank you," Mrs. Patterson said as they left. "I think we finally heard each other."

---

After they left, Dot sat at her desk, reviewing the session on the tablet. The system had been right. It had identified the underlying dynamics faster than she would have on her own. The mediation had been more efficient, more effective.

But something still nagged at her.

The system had told her what the neighbors wanted. But it hadn't told her how to address it. It hadn't suggested the question about acknowledgment. It hadn't guided the conversation toward resolution. That had been her.

The AI could see patterns. But it couldn't do the work of mediation. It couldn't sit with people, build trust, help them find their way to agreement. That was still her job.

Maybe that was the point. The system wasn't replacing her. It was giving her information she could use. The question was whether that information made her better at her job, or just faster.

She wasn't sure yet.

---

That evening, Dot walked home through the familiar streets of Millfield. The sun was setting, casting long shadows across the sidewalks. She nodded to the neighbors she passed, the people she'd known for years.

She thought about the AI, about the session, about the question she'd asked that had changed everything. The system had identified the intent. But she had known what to do with that information.

Maybe that was the division of labor. The AI could see patterns. She could understand meaning. The AI could identify what people wanted. She could help them face it.

It wasn't a replacement. It was a partnership.

She wasn't sure how she felt about that. But she was willing to keep trying.

The next day, she had three more appointments. She used the system for all of them. In each case, it identified underlying dynamics she might have missed or taken longer to find. In each case, the mediation was more efficient.

By the end of the week, Dot had to admit: the AI was helping. It wasn't perfect. It sometimes identified intents that seemed off, or missed nuances that she caught. But overall, it was making her better at her job.

The question was: what did that mean for her? For her skills, her experience, her fifteen years of learning to read people?

She didn't have an answer yet. But she was beginning to understand that the question wasn't going away.
