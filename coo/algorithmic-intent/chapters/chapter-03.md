# Chapter 3: The Prediction

The first case that truly tested the AI came three weeks into the pilot program. It was a dispute between a local restaurant owner and a former employee—a situation that seemed straightforward on the surface but grew more complicated the deeper Dot looked.

Maria Santos had owned Santos' Kitchen on Main Street for twenty years. Her former employee, Jake Morrison, had worked as a cook for five years before being fired last month. Jake claimed wrongful termination. Maria claimed he'd been stealing from the restaurant.

Dot had mediated dozens of employment disputes. Usually, they followed predictable patterns: the employee wanted acknowledgment or compensation, the employer wanted to protect their reputation or avoid setting a precedent. But something about this case felt different.

She started the session as she always did, with the AI running in the background. Jake and Maria sat across from each other, their body language rigid with months of accumulated tension.

"I gave five years to that restaurant," Jake said, his voice tight. "I worked weekends, holidays, overtime. And she fired me over some missing inventory that I had nothing to do with."

"The inventory didn't just walk away on its own," Maria countered. "You had access to the storage room. You had the keys. And the missing items were exactly the things you used at home."

"That's circumstantial. You have no proof."

"I have twenty years of experience. I know when something's not right."

---

Dot watched the tablet as the AI analyzed the conversation. The initial findings appeared:

*Jake: Stated position is wrongful termination. Primary intent is unclear—pattern suggests possible deception.*

*Maria: Stated position is theft. Primary intent is protection of business. Secondary intent is betrayal response.*

Dot frowned. The AI was flagging Jake's intent as "unclear"—something she'd rarely seen in the system. Usually, it identified primary and secondary intents with confidence. But Jake's patterns were apparently harder to read.

She guided the conversation forward, asking questions about the termination, the missing inventory, the history between them. As they talked, the AI continued to update its analysis:

*Jake: Deception indicators increasing. Possible hidden intent: concealing information about inventory.*

*Maria: Emotional intensity increasing. Possible hidden intent: something beyond theft concern.*

The AI was picking up something, but it wasn't clear what. Dot decided to dig deeper.

"Jake," she said, "can you tell me about your last few weeks at the restaurant? Before the termination?"

Jake shifted in his chair. "It was normal. Busy. Nothing special."

The AI flagged: *Deception indicators high. Statement contradicts behavioral pattern.*

"Maria, what was happening at the restaurant during that time?"

Maria hesitated. "It was... difficult. We were having financial problems. I was considering cutting back on staff."

The AI flagged: *New information. Relevance to dispute unclear.*

---

The session continued for another hour, with Dot asking questions and the AI providing analysis. By the end, a picture was beginning to emerge—but it wasn't the picture either party had presented.

According to the AI, Jake's deception indicators weren't about the theft. They were about something else—something he was hiding, but not what Maria thought. And Maria's emotional intensity wasn't just about the missing inventory. It was about something deeper.

Dot decided to take a risk.

"I'd like to try something," she said. "I'm going to share what the AI has identified, and I'd like both of you to tell me if it resonates."

Jake and Maria exchanged skeptical looks but nodded.

"The system suggests that there's more going on here than theft and termination. Jake, it's picking up that you might be hiding something—but not necessarily about the inventory. And Maria, it's suggesting that your response to this situation is about more than just business protection."

Silence.

"Jake," Dot said gently, "is there something else you're not telling me?"

Jake was quiet for a long moment. Then, his shoulders dropped. "I was looking for another job. Before I was fired. I had an interview lined up at the new place across town."

The AI flagged: *Hidden intent identified: departure preparation.*

"And the inventory?" Dot asked.

"I didn't steal anything. But I was... distracted. I might have made mistakes. Orders might have gotten mixed up. I wasn't paying attention like I should have been."

Maria stared at him. "You were planning to leave?"

"I was going to tell you. After the interview. But then..." He gestured vaguely. "This happened."

---

The AI had been right. Jake wasn't hiding theft—he was hiding his plans to leave. And Maria's response wasn't just about the missing inventory—it was about feeling betrayed by an employee she'd trusted.

"I thought you were loyal," Maria said, her voice cracking. "I thought you cared about the restaurant."

"I did. I do. But I needed more money. I have a kid on the way. I can't afford to stay at a place that's cutting back."

The conversation shifted. For the next hour, Dot helped them navigate the real issues: Maria's sense of betrayal, Jake's need for stability, the financial pressures that had brought them to this point. By the end, they hadn't resolved everything, but they had a new understanding.

"I'm sorry," Jake said as they prepared to leave. "I should have told you what was going on."

"I'm sorry too," Maria replied. "I should have asked before I accused."

---

After they left, Dot sat at her desk, reviewing the AI's analysis. The system had identified the hidden intent before she had. It had flagged Jake's deception and Maria's emotional intensity, pointing her toward questions she might not have thought to ask.

The AI had been right. And that was what troubled her.

She'd spent fifteen years developing her instincts, learning to read people, understanding the subtle cues that revealed what they really wanted. And now a machine could do it faster and, apparently, more accurately.

What did that mean for her? For her skills, her experience, her sense of professional identity?

She didn't have answers. But she was beginning to understand that the questions were more complicated than she'd initially thought.

The AI wasn't just a tool. It was a mirror, reflecting back her own limitations. And she wasn't sure she liked what she saw.

---

That evening, Dot walked home through the darkening streets. The town was quiet, the shops closed, the streetlights casting pools of yellow on the sidewalk. She thought about Jake and Maria, about the AI's analysis, about the future of her profession.

The system had helped. There was no denying that. It had identified patterns she might have missed, guided her toward questions she might not have asked. The mediation had been more efficient, more effective.

But efficiency wasn't the only thing that mattered. Mediation was about more than identifying intent. It was about building trust, creating space for difficult conversations, helping people find their way to resolution. The AI could see patterns. But it couldn't do the human work.

Or could it?

She thought about the future, about a world where AI could do everything she did—faster, cheaper, more accurately. What would happen to mediators like her? What would happen to the human element of conflict resolution?

She didn't know. But she was beginning to understand that the AI wasn't just changing her tools. It was changing her understanding of herself.

And that was something she hadn't expected.
