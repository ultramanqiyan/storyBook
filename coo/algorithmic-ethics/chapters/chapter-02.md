# Chapter 2: The System

The city council meeting ran late. The proposal was simple: an AI system that would analyze the community garden network and optimize resource allocation. Water, fertilizer, plot assignments—all would be determined by algorithm. "It will be more efficient," the representative said. "More fair." Marie wasn't sure fair could be computed.

The council chamber was packed. Gardeners, neighbors, community activists—all had come to hear about the new system that would supposedly transform how the gardens operated. Marie sat in the front row, her notebook on her lap, listening to the presentation with growing unease.

The representative from UrbanTech Solutions was polished and professional. She spoke with confidence about efficiency gains, cost savings, and improved outcomes. Charts and graphs appeared on the screen behind her, showing projected improvements in yield, water usage, and resource distribution.

"The current system relies on human judgment, which is inherently subjective and inconsistent," the representative explained. "Our AI platform will analyze data from all six gardens and make allocation decisions based on objective criteria. Every garden will receive exactly what it needs, no more and no less. It's the fairest possible approach."

Marie raised her hand. "What criteria will the AI use?"

"Excellent question. The system will optimize for yield per square foot, water efficiency, and cost-effectiveness. These are the metrics that matter for sustainable urban agriculture."

"What about community value? What about traditional knowledge? What about the gardens that serve purposes beyond food production?"

The representative smiled patiently. "Those are important considerations, and we can certainly include them as secondary factors. But the primary optimization targets must be quantifiable. That's how we ensure objectivity."

Marie felt a chill. Quantifiable. That was the problem. So much of what mattered in the gardens couldn't be quantified—the connections between neighbors, the preservation of traditions, the sense of purpose that gardening gave to elderly residents. These things were real, but they weren't measurable.

"Will the AI consider the age of gardeners?" someone asked from the back. "Some of our elderly residents can't produce as much as younger families."

"The system is designed to be fair," the representative replied. "It allocates resources based on potential yield, not personal characteristics. Age, background, and other factors are not part of the algorithm."

"But if yield is the primary criterion, won't that disadvantage gardeners who grow traditional crops that have lower yields?"

"The algorithm accounts for crop type and growing conditions. It's designed to optimize for what each plot can realistically produce."

Marie thought about Garden E—the small plot where elderly immigrants grew medicinal herbs and traditional vegetables. By yield standards, it was the least productive garden in the network. But it was also the most culturally significant, the most deeply connected to the community's history and traditions. How would an algorithm value that?

The meeting continued for hours. Questions were asked and answered, concerns raised and dismissed. The representative was skilled at addressing objections, always returning to the same points: efficiency, objectivity, fairness. The numbers were persuasive. The logic was sound. And slowly, Marie felt the room shifting toward acceptance.

She understood the appeal. The gardens were funded by the city, and the city wanted results. Yield reports, cost analyses, impact metrics—these were the language of municipal government. An AI system that could produce better numbers would be attractive to the council, regardless of what it might cost in human terms.

When the vote came, it wasn't even close. The council approved the AI system unanimously, with a few minor amendments. Marie would have input into the parameters, but the final decisions would be made by the algorithm. Implementation would begin next month.

After the meeting, Marie stood outside the council building, watching the crowd disperse. Some gardeners looked hopeful—they'd been promised a fairer, more efficient system. Others looked worried, though they couldn't say exactly why. Marie understood both reactions.

Ms. Chen, one of the gardeners from Garden C, approached her. "What do you think, Marie? Is this going to be good for us?"

Marie considered her answer carefully. "I think it will be different. The AI will make decisions based on data, not relationships. That might be more consistent, but it might also miss things that matter."

"Like what?"

"Like why people garden. It's not just about food. It's about community, tradition, connection. An algorithm can't see those things."

Ms. Chen nodded slowly. "My grandmother used to say that you can't measure a garden by its harvest alone. She said the best gardens grow more than vegetables—they grow people."

"Your grandmother was wise."

"She was. And she would have hated this." Ms. Chen smiled ruefully. "But maybe that's just old-fashioned thinking. Maybe we need to trust the technology."

Marie didn't know what to say. She wanted to believe that the AI would help, that it would make the gardens more efficient without losing what made them special. But she'd spent fifteen years learning that gardens were about more than efficiency. They were about people.

She walked home through the darkening streets, her mind turning over the implications of what had just happened. The gardens would change—that much was certain. But how? And would the changes be for better or worse?

She thought about Mrs. Rossi and her tomatoes, about Mrs. Nguyen and her herbs, about Darius and his pride in his first successful crop. These were the people the AI would affect. These were the lives that would be touched by its decisions.

Could an algorithm understand them? Could it see what Marie saw when she looked at the gardens—not just plots of land, but stories, traditions, connections? Could it value what couldn't be measured?

Marie didn't think so. But she would have to find a way to work with it. The decision had been made. The system was coming. And she would have to make sure that what mattered most—the community, the traditions, the people—didn't get lost in the optimization.

That night, Marie lay awake thinking about her mother. She remembered the early days, when the first garden was just a dream, a vacant lot that everyone said would never amount to anything. Her mother had fought for it, had believed in it when no one else did. She'd built something real, something human.

Now that legacy was being handed to an algorithm. And Marie didn't know if she could protect it.

She would try. That was all she could do. She would try to make the AI see what she saw, value what she valued. And if that wasn't possible, she would fight for the gardens the way her mother had fought—person by person, story by story, connection by connection.

The system was coming. But Marie Fontenot wasn't giving up.
