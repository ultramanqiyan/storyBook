# Chapter 7: The Question

Marie spent weeks thinking about what had happened. The AI had made the optimal decision. But optimal wasn't right. The question was: why? What was missing from the calculation? What couldn't be quantified?

She sat in her office, surrounded by the documentation she'd collected—photos of Garden E, testimonials from gardeners, records of what had been lost. The AI had analyzed Garden E and found it wanting. But Marie knew that the analysis had missed something essential.

The problem wasn't the algorithm. The algorithm was doing exactly what it was designed to do—optimize for efficiency. The problem was the parameters. The system measured yield, water usage, maintenance costs. It didn't measure community, tradition, human connection. Those things weren't in the data.

But they were real. Marie had seen them, felt them, witnessed their power. She'd watched Mrs. Rossi find purpose in her tomatoes. She'd seen Mr. Nguyen pass down knowledge that couldn't be found in books. She'd observed the morning gatherings that gave elderly gardeners a reason to get out of bed. These weren't imaginary values. They were as real as the vegetables the gardens produced.

So why couldn't the AI see them?

Marie started reading. Philosophy books about ethics—Aristotle on virtue, Kant on duty, Mill on utility. She read about the trolley problem, the famous thought experiment that asked whether it was right to sacrifice one life to save many. The AI had essentially made a trolley decision: sacrifice Garden E to benefit the larger network. By utilitarian logic, it was the right choice. The greatest good for the greatest number.

But something about that logic felt wrong. Marie thought about Mrs. Rossi, about Mr. Nguyen, about the twelve families who had lost their community. They weren't numbers in a calculation. They were people with lives, histories, relationships. Sacrificing them for the greater good might be efficient, but was it right?

She read about other ethical frameworks. Deontology—the idea that some actions were right or wrong regardless of consequences. Virtue ethics—the focus on character and flourishing rather than rules and outcomes. Care ethics—the emphasis on relationships and responsibilities rather than abstract principles.

None of these frameworks could be reduced to an algorithm. They required judgment, context, wisdom. They required understanding particular situations, not just applying universal rules. They required seeing people as individuals, not data points.

Marie began to understand the problem. The AI was a utilitarian calculator. It could add up benefits and costs, weigh outcomes, find the optimal solution. But it couldn't ask whether the optimization was for the right things. It couldn't question its own parameters. It couldn't see beyond what it was programmed to see.

That was the limitation of algorithmic ethics. The AI could tell you how to achieve a goal, but it couldn't tell you what goal to pursue. It could optimize for any parameter, but it couldn't choose which parameters mattered. It could calculate the most efficient path, but it couldn't decide where the path should lead.

Those were human decisions. And they couldn't be outsourced to an algorithm.

Marie thought about what had happened to Garden E. The AI had closed the garden because it was inefficient. But inefficiency wasn't inherently bad. Sometimes inefficiency was the price of other values—community, tradition, care. The question wasn't whether Garden E was efficient. The question was whether efficiency should be the primary goal.

The city council had decided that it should be. They'd approved a system that optimized for yield, cost-effectiveness, and resource utilization. Those were reasonable goals. But they weren't the only goals. And by making them the primary parameters, the council had implicitly decided that other values—community, tradition, accessibility—mattered less.

That was the real decision. Not the AI's calculation, but the human choice about what to calculate. The AI was just a tool for implementing that choice.

Marie realized that ethics wasn't about optimization. It was about values—and values couldn't be reduced to numbers. The AI could maximize efficiency, but it couldn't decide what efficiency was for. That was still a human decision. And maybe that was the point.

She thought about her mother, about the gardens she'd built. Her mother hadn't optimized for yield. She'd optimized for something harder to measure—human flourishing, community connection, cultural preservation. She'd understood that gardens weren't just about food production. They were about people.

Now Marie understood it too. The AI could help with the technical aspects of garden management—water allocation, fertilizer distribution, plot assignment. But it couldn't decide what the gardens were for. That was a question of values, not efficiency. And it required human judgment.

The question wasn't whether AI could make ethical decisions. The question was what role AI should play in ethical decision-making. And the answer, Marie was beginning to see, was that AI should support human judgment, not replace it.

The algorithm could calculate outcomes. But humans had to decide which outcomes mattered.

Marie closed her philosophy books and looked out the window at the remaining gardens. They were still there, still producing, still serving communities. But they were operating under a system that couldn't see what made them valuable.

That system could be changed. The parameters could be adjusted. But it would require convincing the city council that efficiency wasn't the only thing that mattered.

Marie had lost the battle for Garden E. But she hadn't lost the war. She'd learned something important about algorithmic ethics, about the limits of optimization, about what couldn't be quantified.

Now she would use that knowledge to fight for the remaining gardens. Not by attacking the AI, but by changing the parameters it used. Not by rejecting efficiency, but by insisting that other values mattered too.

The question of ethics would never be fully answered. Marie knew that now. But she also knew that the asking was important. The questioning. The refusal to accept that optimal was the same as right.

That was what humans did. That was what algorithms couldn't do. And that was why human judgment would always matter.
