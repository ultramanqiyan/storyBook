# Chapter 8: The Choice

The choice became clear.

He could continue following AI. Let his values fade further into the background. Accept AI-generated morality. Be efficient, productive, successful.

Or he could start making his own judgments again. Be uncertain. Struggle with ethics. Carry the weight of moral responsibility.

The first option was easier. The second option was harder.

But Marcus had learned something. The ease was the problem. The optimization was the trap. The efficiency was the cost.

---

He thought about what he'd been missing.

The direct experience of right and wrong. The felt sense of ethics. The weight of moral responsibility. The discomfort of conscience.

These weren't inefficiencies to be optimized away. They were essential aspects of being human. Of having values. Of making ethical decisions.

*This is what it costs to reclaim your moral judgment*, he realized. *The discomfort and the integrity. The ease of complicity.*

---

He made his choice.

He would start making his own judgments. Not because it was efficient. Not because it was optimized. But because it was his. His conscience. His values. His moral capacity.

The choice was uncomfortable. It required carrying weight he'd delegated for years. It meant struggling with decisions AI had made easy.

But it also meant something else. Reclaiming something essential. Something human. Something that couldn't be optimized.

*This is where I start*, he thought. *This is where I begin to judge again. Not perfectly. Not efficiently. But honestly. Mine.*

The insight was small. But it was significant. Because it meant he was choosing to carry the burden of conscience again. And that, he realized, was what made him human.
