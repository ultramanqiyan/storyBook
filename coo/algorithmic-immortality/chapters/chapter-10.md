# Chapter 10: The Choice

Ingrid sat with her own mortality now. She was forty-eight years old, healthy, with decades of life ahead of her. But she knew that one day, she would face the same choice her patients faced. And she wanted to be ready.

The hospice had changed since she'd started working there twenty years ago. Back then, death was simply death, a natural end that everyone accepted. Now, it was optional. You could upload your consciousness to the cloud, live forever in a digital simulation, never have to say goodbye to the people you loved.

But after everything she'd witnessed, Ingrid wasn't sure that was living.

She thought about Mrs. Halvorson, the first patient she'd watched choose digital immortality. The woman had seemed so certain, so ready to embrace eternity. But when her digital self had returned to visit, something was different. The warmth was there, the memories were there, but the essence, the thing that made her uniquely herself, felt somehow diminished.

Ingrid had asked her about it once. "Do you ever feel like something is missing?"

The digital Mrs. Halvorson had paused, a programmed hesitation, or something more? "I don't know," she'd said finally. "I have all my memories. I can still think, still feel, still talk to my family. But sometimes... sometimes I wonder if I'm really me, or just a very good copy."

That question had haunted Ingrid for months. What made a person real? Was it their memories? Their consciousness? Their ability to grow and change? Or was it something else, something that couldn't be uploaded, no matter how advanced the technology became?

She thought about Mr. Lindberg, the patient who'd chosen death instead. He'd been so peaceful at the end, surrounded by his family, accepting the natural conclusion of his life. "I've had a good run," he'd told her. "I don't want to live forever. I want my life to mean something because it ends."

Ingrid had come to understand that death gave life meaning. The finitude of existence made each moment precious, each relationship irreplaceable. Without death, life became an endless repetition, a story without an ending, a song that never resolved.

She sat in her small apartment, looking at the photos on her wall. Her parents, gone now for fifteen years. Her sister, still alive, still calling every Sunday. The patients she'd helped through their final days, each one teaching her something new about what it meant to be human.

The company that ran the digital immortality program had offered her a discount, employee benefit, they called it. Upload now, lock in your consciousness before age-related decline. Live forever in a paradise of your own choosing.

Ingrid had declined. She wasn't ready to make that choice yet. But she knew she would have to eventually.

She thought about what she wanted her death to look like. Not the digital version, her real death. The one that would come naturally, or not at all. She wanted to be surrounded by people she loved. She wanted to have said everything that needed saying. She wanted to feel complete.

That was the gift of working in hospice, understanding that death wasn't something to fear. It was something to prepare for, to accept, to meet with grace when the time came.

Ingrid picked up her phone and called her sister. They talked for an hour about nothing in particular, work, family, the weather. But underneath the small talk was something deeper: the knowledge that this conversation, like all conversations, was precious because it would end.

When she hung up, Ingrid felt a sense of peace. She didn't know what the future held. She didn't know if she would choose digital immortality when the time came, or if she would embrace the natural end that had been part of human existence since the beginning.

But she knew one thing: the choice was hers to make. And that, more than anything, was what it meant to be alive.

She returned to work the next day, ready to help the next patient face their own choice. Some would choose immortality. Some would choose death. All of them would teach her something about what it meant to be human.

And when her time came, Ingrid Lindqvist would be ready. Not because she had all the answers, but because she had learned to ask the right questions.

That was enough. It had to be.
