# Chapter 3: The Choice

Sarah was thirty-eight, dying of cancer, with two young children. When she talked about digital immortality, her eyes lit up. "I could watch them grow up," she said. "I could be there for their graduations, their weddings. I wouldn't have to leave them." Ingrid understood the appeal. But she also wondered: would the digital Sarah really be Sarah?

The room was bright with morning light, the pale Minnesota sun streaming through the windows. Sarah lay in her hospital bed, her face thin but her eyes fierce with determination. She had been diagnosed with ovarian cancer two years ago. The treatments had worked for a while, then stopped working. Now she had months, maybe weeks.

Her children were seven and nine, a boy and a girl who still believed their mother could fix anything. They visited every afternoon, bringing drawings and stories and the ordinary chaos of childhood. Sarah watched them with a mixture of love and grief, knowing she would miss everything.

"I've been reading about the upload," Sarah said, her voice stronger than it had been in days. "There's a clinic in Chicago. They've done over a hundred procedures. The success rate is ninety-two percent."

Ingrid sat beside her, the same position she had occupied for weeks. "What does your husband think?"

"He's scared. He doesn't know if it would really be me." Sarah paused. "But he also doesn't want to raise the kids alone. He wants them to have a mother, even if she's... different."

"And what do you think?"

"I think I don't want to die." Sarah's voice cracked. "I think I want to see Emma's first bra. I want to see Jack's first girlfriend. I want to help them with homework and yell at them about cleaning their rooms and watch them become adults."

Ingrid felt the familiar weight of her work. This was what she did, she helped people face impossible choices. But this one felt different. This wasn't about how to die. It was about whether to die at all.

"What if the upload doesn't capture everything that makes you you?"

"What if it does?" Sarah countered. "What if I can be there for my children? What if I don't have to miss everything?"

Ingrid didn't have an answer. She had spent twenty years helping people accept the limits of life. Now she was being asked to help someone transcend those limits.

"What about the cost?"

"We have insurance. It covers part of it. We'll figure out the rest." Sarah reached for Ingrid's hand. "I know you have doubts. I know it's not the same as... as dying well. But I'm not ready. I have too much to live for."

"Living and uploading aren't the same thing."

"Maybe not. But it's better than nothing." Sarah's eyes filled with tears. "I don't want to leave them. I can't bear the thought of them growing up without me. If there's any chance I can stay, any chance at all, I have to take it."

Ingrid held her hand, feeling the warmth, the pulse, the reality of a living person. In a few weeks, this body would be gone. But Sarah, or something like Sarah, might continue in digital form.

"Have you talked to the children about it?"

"Not yet. I don't know how to explain it." Sarah wiped her eyes. "How do you tell a seven-year-old that Mommy is going to live in a computer?"

"I don't know," Ingrid admitted. "But they'll need to understand. They'll need to know what's happening."

"I know. I'll figure it out." Sarah squeezed Ingrid's hand. "Will you be there? When I... when the upload happens? I don't want to be alone."

"Of course."

"And after? Will you visit me? In the digital environment?"

Ingrid hesitated. She had never visited a digital consciousness before. She wasn't sure she wanted to. But Sarah was her patient, her responsibility. And more than that, she was a person who needed support.

"I will," Ingrid said. "I'll visit."

Sarah smiled, the first real smile Ingrid had seen in weeks. "Thank you. That means more than you know."

The weeks that followed were filled with preparations. Sarah talked to her children, explaining in simple terms that Mommy was going to live in a special place where she could still see them and talk to them. They didn't fully understand, but they accepted it the way children accept things, as just another strange fact about a world that was often confusing.

Her husband struggled more. He wanted to support Sarah's decision, but he couldn't shake the feeling that the digital version wouldn't really be his wife. He talked to Ingrid about it late one night, after Sarah had fallen asleep.

"How will I know it's her?" he asked, his voice thick with fear. "What if it's just... a copy? A simulation that thinks it's Sarah?"

"I don't know," Ingrid said honestly. "I don't think anyone knows for sure."

"Then how can she do this? How can she ask me to... to love something that might not be her?"

"Because the alternative is losing her completely." Ingrid paused. "She's choosing hope over certainty. She's choosing the possibility of more time over the reality of none."

"That's terrifying."

"Yes. It is."

Ingrid watched Sarah's family prepare for the upload. The children drew pictures for their digital mother to see. The husband set up a special room in their house with screens and speakers for visits. And Sarah herself spent hours recording messages, writing letters, preparing for a form of existence that no one fully understood.

The night before the procedure, Ingrid sat with Sarah alone. The children were with their grandmother. The husband was in the waiting room, trying to process what was about to happen.

"Are you scared?" Ingrid asked.

"Yes." Sarah's voice was steady. "But I'm more scared of not trying. Of just... ending. Of missing everything."

"What do you think it will be like? After?"

"I don't know. The people who've done it say it feels like waking up from a long sleep. Like you were always there, just... somewhere else." Sarah paused. "I hope they're right. I hope I still feel like me."

"And if you don't?"

"Then at least I tried. At least I didn't just give up." Sarah looked at Ingrid with fierce determination. "I'm not ready to go. I have too much to live for. Even if living looks different than I expected."

Ingrid nodded. She still had doubts, deep, fundamental doubts about what digital immortality meant for death, for life, for identity. But Sarah had made her choice. And Ingrid would support her.

"I'll see you on the other side," Ingrid said.

"I hope so." Sarah smiled. "I really hope so."

The upload was scheduled for the next morning. Ingrid would be there, holding Sarah's hand as she had held hundreds of hands before. But this time, she wasn't helping someone die. She was helping someone choose not to die.

And she wasn't sure what that meant.
