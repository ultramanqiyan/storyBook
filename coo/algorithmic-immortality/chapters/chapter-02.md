# Chapter 2: The Option

Mr. Chen was the first patient Ingrid had cared for who chose digital immortality. "I'm not ready to go," he told her. "My granddaughter is having a baby next year. I want to see her grow up." The technology was new, expensive, and controversial. But for those who could afford it, death had become optional.

Ingrid had been Mr. Chen's hospice nurse for three months. He was seventy-two years old, a retired engineer who had spent his career building systems, solving problems, optimizing processes. He approached death with the same analytical mindset he had brought to everything else in his life, a challenge to be solved, a problem to be addressed.

He had terminal pancreatic cancer, had been given six months to live. But instead of accepting that timeline, he was exploring options.

"Tell me about the upload," Ingrid said, sitting beside his bed the way she had sat beside hundreds of beds before. This was her work, not to judge, but to accompany. Not to tell people what to choose, but to help them understand their choices.

Mr. Chen's eyes lit up with the same enthusiasm Ingrid had seen when he talked about his engineering work. "They can preserve my consciousness. Transfer it to a digital environment. I can continue to exist, interact with my family, watch my grandchildren grow up."

"How does it work?"

"They map my brain. Create a digital copy of my neural patterns. Upload that copy to a server environment." He spoke quickly now, the words tumbling over each other. "I can think, feel, remember everything I remember now. I can talk to my family through screens and speakers. I can watch videos, read messages, be there for birthdays and graduations."

Ingrid nodded slowly. She had heard about the technology, everyone had. It had been in development for decades, but only recently had it become available to the public. The cost was astronomical, hundreds of thousands of dollars, and the controversy was intense. Religious groups called it playing God. Philosophers debated whether the uploaded consciousness was really the same person. Scientists argued about the nature of consciousness itself.

But for people like Mr. Chen, none of that mattered. What mattered was more time.

"Have you talked to your family about this?"

"Yes. My daughter has concerns, she's not sure if the digital version will really be me. My son thinks it's unnatural. But my granddaughter..." His voice softened. "My granddaughter is pregnant. She wants me to meet her baby. She doesn't care if I'm digital or biological. She just wants me there."

Ingrid felt a familiar weight in her chest. This was the hardest part of her work, helping people navigate choices that had no right answers. She had helped patients choose between aggressive treatment and quality of life. She had helped families decide when to withdraw life support. She had sat with people as they made the most difficult decisions of their lives.

This was just another version of that.

"What does your doctor say?"

"He says it's my choice. He can't recommend it, he doesn't know if it really works. But he can't say no either."

"And what do you think?"

Mr. Chen was quiet for a moment. "I think... I think I want to try. I've spent my whole life solving problems, finding solutions. This is just another problem. And there's a solution available. Why wouldn't I take it?"

Ingrid nodded again. She couldn't tell him what to choose, that wasn't her role. But she could help him think through the implications.

"What if it doesn't work? What if the upload fails, or the result isn't... you?"

"Then I die. I was going to die anyway. At least this way, there's a chance."

"And if it does work? If the digital version is really you?"

"Then I get more time. More moments with my family. More experiences." He paused. "Isn't that worth the risk?"

Ingrid didn't have an answer. She had spent twenty years helping people die well. But she had never helped someone choose not to die. This was new territory.

"What about the cost?"

"I have the money. I worked hard my whole life, saved carefully. This is what I saved for."

"And your family? How will they feel, visiting a digital version of you?"

"They'll feel like I'm still here. That's the point." Mr. Chen reached for Ingrid's hand. "I know you have questions. I know it's strange. But I've thought about this for weeks. I've read everything I can find about the technology, the philosophy, the experiences of people who've done it. And I keep coming back to the same thing: I'm not ready to go."

Ingrid held his hand, the same gesture she had made hundreds of times. But this time, it felt different. She wasn't holding the hand of someone who was dying. She was holding the hand of someone who was choosing not to die.

"I'll support whatever you decide," she said. "That's my job."

"I know. And I appreciate it." Mr. Chen squeezed her hand. "I'm going to do it. The procedure is next week."

Ingrid watched Mr. Chen's family say goodbye, not to death, but to his physical form. His daughter cried, holding his hand as she had held since childhood. His son stood awkwardly, not sure how to feel about a father who wasn't dying but wasn't staying either. His granddaughter was the only one who seemed at peace with the decision.

"I'll see you soon, Grandpa," she said, looking at the screen where he would soon exist. "I'll introduce you to the baby."

In a week, he would "wake up" in a digital environment, his consciousness preserved. It wasn't death. But it wasn't life either. It was something new. And Ingrid wasn't sure if it was beautiful or terrifying.

She drove home that evening thinking about Mr. Chen. He had made his choice, not the choice she would have made, but his choice. And she had supported him. That was her job.

But something had shifted. For twenty years, she had helped people accept the end of their stories. Now she was helping people choose not to end. And she wasn't sure what that meant for her work, for death, for life itself.

The question haunted her: if death was optional, did it still have meaning?
