# Chapter 3: The Mother

Amina Okonkwo lived in a small house in London's Nigerian community, the kind of place that smelled of jollof rice and old stories. James had been visiting his mother every week for as long as he could remember, but today felt different. Today, he had questions he wasn't sure he wanted answered.

"James!" his mother said, opening the door with a warm smile. "Come in, come in. I made your favorite—jollof rice with chicken."

James stepped inside, the familiar scent of jollof rice wrapping around him like a blanket. The house was cluttered with cultural artifacts: Nigerian masks, traditional textiles, old books of folk tales. It was a museum of his cultural heritage.

"How's work?" his mother asked, pouring him a glass of palm wine.

"Busy," James said. "We're translating more cultural texts for Shudra consumption."

"Translating?" his mother said, her voice sharp. "Or simplifying?"

James felt a knot form in his stomach. He knew his mother was aware of what he did, but she had never been so direct about it.

"We're making culture accessible," James said, defending his work. "Most people can't handle the complexity of traditional folk tales."

"Accessible?" his mother scoffed. "You're not making culture accessible. You're drowning it. When noise floods the channels, the signal disappears. And what is lost can never be recovered."

James stared at his mother, the words hitting him like a physical blow. He had always believed he was doing the right thing, but now he wasn't so sure.

"The stories I told you as a child—they weren't just stories," his mother said. "They were lessons about life, about death, about the complexity of human experience. When you simplify them, you're not just making them easier to understand—you're stripping them of their meaning."

James thought about the folk tale he'd translated yesterday, about how he had stripped it of its complexity, its ambiguity, its richness. He thought about the AI-generated content that was flooding the cultural channels, about how it was drowning out real human culture.

"But the data shows that people prefer simple content," James said, his voice weak.

"Data?" his mother said. "What does data know about culture? Culture isn't about what people want—it's about what people need. It's about preserving the stories that make us human."

James felt a wave of guilt wash over him. He had spent his career helping to simplify culture, helping to generate the noise that was drowning out real human culture. He had been part of the problem.

"They're not making culture accessible, James. They're drowning it. When noise floods the channels, the signal disappears. And what is lost can never be recovered."

James left his mother's house with more questions than he'd arrived with. And for the first time, he wasn't sure he wanted to find the answers.
