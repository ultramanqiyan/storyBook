# Chapter 3: The Mother

Amina Okonkwo lived in a small house in London's Nigerian community, the kind of place that smelled of jollof rice and old stories. James had been visiting his mother every Sunday for as long as he could remember, but today felt different. Today, he carried questions that felt like stones in his pockets.

"James!" His mother opened the door before he could knock. "Come in, come in. I made your favorite—jollof rice with chicken."

He stepped inside, the familiar scent wrapping around him like a blanket. The house was cluttered with cultural artifacts: Nigerian masks on the walls, traditional textiles draped over furniture, old books of folk tales stacked on every surface. It was a museum of his heritage, preserved against a world that seemed determined to forget.

"Sit, sit." Amina guided him to the kitchen table. "You look tired. Is the work too much?"

"The work is fine, Mama." James accepted a plate of jollof rice. "We're translating more cultural texts for Shudra consumption."

"Translating?" Amina's voice sharpened. "Or simplifying?"

James set down his fork. He knew his mother was aware of what he did, but she had never been so direct about it.

"We're making culture accessible. Most people can't handle the complexity of traditional folk tales."

"Accessible." Amina sat across from him, her eyes hard. "You're not making culture accessible, James. You're drowning it. When noise floods the channels, the signal disappears. And what is lost can never be recovered."

The words landed like a physical blow. James stared at his mother, this woman who had spent her life preserving the stories of their people.

"The stories I told you as a child—they weren't just entertainment," Amina said. "They were lessons about life, about death, about the complexity of human experience. The trickster who outwits the king. The spirit who tests the traveler. The ancestor who returns to guide the lost. When you simplify them, you're not making them easier to understand—you're stripping them of their meaning."

James thought about the Anansi story he'd translated yesterday, about how he had stripped it of its complexity, its ambiguity, its richness. He thought about the AI-generated content flooding the cultural channels, drowning out real human culture.

"But the data shows that people prefer simple content," he said. His voice sounded weak even to his own ears.

"Data?" Amina laughed—a sharp, bitter sound. "What does data know about culture? Culture isn't about what people want, James. It's about what people need. It's about preserving the stories that make us human. It's about remembering who we are."

She reached across the table and took his hand. "You were always such a good listener when I told you those stories. You understood them, even when you were small. Don't tell me people can't handle complexity. Tell me who benefits when complexity disappears."

James left his mother's house an hour later, her words still echoing in his mind. The afternoon sun was bright, but he barely noticed. He was too busy thinking about the system he served—and the culture it was destroying.
