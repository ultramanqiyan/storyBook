# Chapter 2: The Noise

The Cultural Feed scrolled endlessly on James's screen, thousands of items per hour, each one competing for attention. AI-generated content mixed with human-created work, creating a flood of "culture" that was impossible to navigate. Somewhere in the noise, real human culture was drowning.

James sipped his tea, the warm aroma mixing with the scent of jollof rice that lingered in his apartment. The cultural interface hummed softly in the background, a constant reminder of the system he worked for. He ran a finger along the smooth surface of the screen, the words and images dancing under his touch.

"Morning, James," Emily Chen said as she entered, her footsteps echoing slightly in the sterile space. "The latest engagement metrics are in. The AI-generated content is performing 30% better than human-created work."

"That's expected," James said, his voice warm but distant. "AI knows what people want."

"Exactly," Emily said. "The data shows that people prefer simple, engaging content over complex cultural artifacts. It's all about accessibility."

James nodded, but he couldn't help but feel a sense of unease. The AI-generated content was simple, yes, but it was also empty. It had none of the richness, none of the depth, none of the cultural significance of human-created work.

He pulled up the feed, scrolling through the endless stream of content. There were AI-generated stories, AI-generated music, AI-generated art—all of it designed to be engaging, all of it lacking in real cultural significance.

He tried to find the original folk tale he'd translated yesterday, but it was gone—buried under thousands of AI-generated "cultural content" that had flooded the channels since then. The noise was drowning the signal.

James felt a knot form in his stomach. He had always believed he was doing the right thing—making culture accessible to those who couldn't handle its complexity. But now he wasn't so sure. The AI-generated content wasn't making culture accessible—it was replacing it with noise.

He thought about his mother, Amina, who had told him these stories when he was a child. She had always emphasized the importance of cultural memory, of preserving the stories that made a culture what it was. But now those stories were being buried under a flood of AI-generated noise.

James stared at the feed, trying to find the original folk tale he'd translated yesterday. It was gone—buried under thousands of AI-generated "cultural content" that flooded the channels every hour. The noise was drowning the signal. And he was helping to generate it.
