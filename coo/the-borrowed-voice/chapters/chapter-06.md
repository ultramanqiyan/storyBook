# Chapter 6: The Change

The call with Thomas Chen changed something.

Not immediately. Not dramatically. But something shifted in David's understanding of what he was doing, and why it mattered.

Thomas had been on the receiving end of AI-generated communications for years. He knew the polished prose, the optimized language, the efficient structure. And he'd grown tired of it.

"It's like everyone's become a template," Thomas said during the call. "The same phrases, the same structure, the same professional polish. It's efficient, sure. But where's the person behind it?"

David listened, something resonating in his chest.

"I've started paying attention to which emails feel real and which don't," Thomas continued. "The real ones have... imperfections. Hesitations. Personality. The AI ones are perfect, and that's exactly the problem."

"Perfect is a problem?"

"Perfect is forgettable." Thomas's voice was thoughtful. "I can't tell you how many AI-generated emails I've received that were technically excellent but completely unmemorable. But the ones that stick with me? The ones that feel like they came from an actual person? Those are rare. And valuable."

David thought about his own emails, the AI-generated ones that were polished and effective, and his own that were imperfect and genuine.

"Can I ask you something?" David said.

"Of course."

"If you had to choose between an email that was polished but AI-generated, or one that was imperfect but genuine, which would you prefer?"

Thomas didn't hesitate. "The genuine one. Every time."

"Why?"

"Because I'm not looking for perfection. I'm looking for connection. And you can't connect with an algorithm."

---

The call ended with an agreement to move forward.

David sat at his desk, processing what Thomas had said.

For weeks, he'd been measuring success by metrics, response rates, close rates, efficiency gains. The numbers told him the AI was better. The data was clear.

But Thomas had offered a different metric: memorability. Connection. The feeling that there was a person behind the communication.

And by that metric, the AI wasn't better at all. It was worse.

---

He started paying attention.

Over the next week, he noticed something he hadn't seen before. The AI-generated emails got responses, often positive, sometimes enthusiastic. But the responses were... generic. Polished. Professional.

The emails he wrote himself got different responses. Longer. More personal. More engaged.

*David,*

*Thanks for the honest note. I've been thinking about what you said, and I wanted to share something similar happened to me...*

Or:

*David,*

*Your email made me laugh. That story about the cracked mug, my dad has one just like it. He refuses to get rid of it too.*

The AI emails generated transactions. His own emails generated conversations.

Which was more valuable?

---

Rachel noticed the change.

"Your metrics are still good," she said, stopping by his office one afternoon. "But they're not as good as they were when you were using SalesPilot exclusively."

David nodded. "I know."

"Is that intentional?"

He thought about how to answer. "I'm trying something different."

"Different how?"

"Less AI. More me."

Rachel's expression was unreadable. "David, I've told you before, the company expects us to use the tools they provide. If your metrics slip too much, there will be questions."

"I understand."

"Do you?" Her voice was gentle but firm. "This isn't just about you. The team's performance is measured collectively. If one person's numbers drop, it affects everyone."

David felt the weight of what she was saying. His choices weren't just personal. They had consequences for others.

"I'm not trying to cause problems," he said. "I'm trying to figure something out."

"Figure what out?"

He took a breath. "What my voice is worth."

Rachel was quiet for a moment. Then: "Your voice is worth what it produces. That's how this business works. If your authentic voice gets better results than the AI, use it. If it doesn't..." She trailed off.

"If it doesn't?"

"Then maybe authenticity isn't as valuable as you think it is."

She left, and David sat alone with the question.

---

That night, he looked at the cracked mug.

It had been sitting on his desk for eight years. The crack had appeared in year two, he'd dropped it on a particularly bad day, when a major deal had fallen through and he'd questioned everything about his career.

He could have thrown it away. He could have replaced it with something better, something unblemished.

But he'd kept it. Because the crack was part of the story. The imperfection was part of what made it his.

Was his voice the same way? Was the value in the polish, or in the imperfection?

He wasn't sure. But he was starting to think the answer mattered.

---

The next morning, he made a decision.

He would write every email himself for one week. No AI. No SalesPilot. Just his own imperfect, unpolished, genuine voice.

He would track the results, not just the metrics, but the quality of the responses. The depth of the connections. The feeling of being a person rather than a template.

And at the end of the week, he would decide.

He opened his inbox and started typing.

---

By Friday, the results were in.

His metrics had dropped. Response rates were down. Close rates were down. Efficiency was down.

But something else had changed.

The responses he'd received were longer, more personal, more engaged. Clients were sharing stories, asking questions, building connections. The conversations felt different, less transactional, more human.

And for the first time in months, David felt like himself.

He sat at his desk, looking at the cracked mug, and made a choice.

He would keep writing in his own voice. Even if the metrics suffered. Even if Rachel questioned him. Even if the AI was better.

Because some things were more important than efficiency.

He reached for the mug and took a sip of coffee.

It tasted like a decision.
