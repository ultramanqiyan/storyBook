# Chapter 6: The Change

The call came on a Tuesday afternoon.

David was at his desk, reviewing the week's pipeline, when his phone rang. The caller ID showed Sarah Chen's name - his longest-standing client, the one who'd been with him for six years.

He answered with practiced warmth. "Sarah, good to hear from you. How are things?"

"David!" Her voice was bright, as it always was when they talked. "I wanted to follow up on the order we placed last month. Everything's going smoothly so far."

"That's great to hear." He pulled up her file on his screen. "How's the team adjusting to the new line?"

"Actually, that's what I wanted to talk to you about." She paused, and he could hear the smile in her voice. "I have to tell you, David - you've changed."

David felt something cold in his stomach. "Changed?"

"In a good way!" She laughed. "I've been working with you for six years now, and I've noticed a difference lately. Your emails are more... polished. More professional. Not that you weren't professional before, but there's a consistency now that I really appreciate."

David's grip tightened on the phone. "I see."

"It's funny," Sarah continued. "I was just telling my team about you. I said, 'You know how some salespeople are all over the place? One email is casual, the next is formal, you never know what you're going to get? David's not like that anymore. He's consistent. Reliable. You always know what you're going to get.'"

David felt the words like a physical blow. Consistent. Reliable. Always know what you're going to get.

She was describing the AI. She was describing the polished, professional communications that SalesPilot generated. She was describing the version of him that wasn't really him.

"I'm glad you've noticed the improvement," he said, his voice steady despite the turmoil inside. "I've been working on my communication style."

"Well, it shows," Sarah said warmly. "Keep it up. You're better than ever."

---

After the call, David sat at his desk, staring at the phone in his hand.

Sarah had noticed a change. She thought it was positive. She thought he had improved.

But the change wasn't him. The change was the AI. The improvement was the algorithm.

He opened his sent folder and pulled up his recent emails to Sarah. There they were - polished, professional, consistent. All of them generated by SalesPilot. All of them better than what he could have written himself.

She preferred the AI version. She thought it was an improvement.

And she was right. Objectively, the emails were better. More professional. More consistent. More effective.

But they weren't his.

---

He tried to write an email himself that afternoon.

It was a simple follow-up to a prospect - the kind of email he'd written thousands of times over his career. He opened a blank compose window and put his fingers on the keyboard.

*Dear Mr. Henderson,*

*I wanted to follow up on our conversation...*

He stopped. The words felt wrong. Stiff. Unnatural.

He tried again.

*Hi Mark,*

*Thanks for taking the time...*

No. That wasn't right either.

He stared at the blank screen, his fingers motionless. The words wouldn't come. His authentic voice - the one that had once flowed naturally - felt buried somewhere deep, inaccessible.

After twenty minutes of struggle, he opened SalesPilot and typed in the parameters: *Follow up with Mark Henderson, reference last conversation, suggest next steps, maintain professional warmth.*

The AI generated the email in seconds. It was perfect - polished, professional, effective.

He copied the text, pasted it into his compose window, and hit send.

Then he sat back in his chair, something hollow settling in his chest.


That night, he dreamed about mirrors.

In the dream, he was standing in front of a mirror, looking at his reflection. But the reflection wasn't quite right. It was him - his face, his features, his expression - but something was off.

The reflection was too smooth. Too polished. Too perfect.

He tried to touch his own face, but the reflection moved differently. It was a fraction of a second ahead of him, anticipating his movements before he made them.

He tried to speak, but the reflection spoke first. The words that came out were his - his voice, his cadence, his phrases - but they were better than what he would have said. More articulate. More professional. More effective.

He stared at the reflection, trying to find the imperfections that would make it real. But there were none. The reflection was flawless.

And then he realized: the reflection wasn't reflecting him. He was reflecting it.

He woke up gasping, his heart pounding.


The next morning, he arrived at the office early.

The building was quiet, the usual bustle of activity still hours away. He sat at his desk and stared at the SalesPilot interface, the cursor blinking in the compose window.

He thought about Sarah's words: *You've changed. In a good way.*

He thought about the metrics that proved the AI was better at being him than he was.

He thought about the authentic voice he'd lost, buried somewhere beneath months of polished, professional communications.

And he made a decision.

He would write an email himself. A real email. Not the parameters for an AI, but actual words, actual sentences, actual thoughts expressed in his own voice.

He opened a blank document and started typing.

*To whoever reads this,*

*I don't know when I lost my voice. I don't know when I started letting a machine speak for me. I don't know when I became the imposter of myself.*

*But I know it happened. I can feel it - the absence of something that used to be there. The missing weight of words that came from somewhere real.*

*I used to be good at this. Not perfect - never perfect - but genuine. Real. Myself.*

*Now I'm not sure what I am.*

He stopped. The words were rough, uneven, imperfect. Nothing like the polished prose that SalesPilot generated.

But they were his.

He stared at the document for a long time. Then he deleted it.

There was no one to send it to. No client who wanted to hear about his existential crisis. No colleague who would understand.

He was alone with his questions.


The week continued, and David went through the motions.

He generated emails with SalesPilot. He signed his name to communications he hadn't written. He maintained the professional consistency that clients like Sarah appreciated.

But inside, something was breaking.

He noticed it in small ways. The way his morning coffee didn't taste right. The way his cracked mug felt foreign in his hands. The way the Chicago skyline through his window looked like a picture of a city rather than a place where he lived.

He was becoming a spectator of his own life.


On Friday, Rachel stopped by his office.

"David, got a minute?"

He looked up from his screen. "Sure."

She settled into the chair across from his desk, her expression thoughtful. "I wanted to check in. Your numbers are still great - best quarter you've had in years. But I've been noticing something."

David felt a knot form in his stomach. "What's that?"

"You seem... distant lately. Not your work - your work is excellent. But you. Something's different."

David hesitated. He could tell her about Sarah's call, about the dreams, about the questions that had been haunting him. But what would he say? That he was troubled because his AI-generated emails were too good? That clients preferred the artificial version of him?

"I'm fine," he said instead. "Just been thinking about some things."

Rachel studied him for a moment. "You know you can talk to me, right? If something's bothering you."

"I know." He managed a smile. "Thanks, Rachel."

She nodded, but her expression suggested she wasn't entirely convinced. "Well, whatever you're doing, keep it up. The numbers don't lie."

She left, and David sat alone in his office.

The numbers don't lie.

But what were the numbers measuring? His success? Or the AI's?

He looked at the cracked mug on his desk - the one with the chip in the rim that he refused to fix. The mug was imperfect, but it was his. It had history, meaning, significance beyond its function.

His authentic voice was like that mug - imperfect, but genuine. The SalesPilot emails were like a perfect replica - flawless, but without the history.

But clients couldn't tell the difference. Sarah had praised the change. She thought he was better than ever.

And maybe he was. Maybe the AI version of him was objectively better - more professional, more consistent, more effective.

But it wasn't him.

And he wasn't sure anymore how to get back to what was.
