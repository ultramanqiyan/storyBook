# Chapter 2: The Polish

The next morning, David arrived at the office early.

He'd thought about SalesPilot all night - the way it had generated his email, word for word, as if it had crawled inside his head and found the exact words he would have chosen. The experience had left him unsettled, though he couldn't quite articulate why.

Wasn't this what technology was supposed to do? Make things easier? More efficient? He'd been writing sales emails for eight years. If a tool could do it faster, wasn't that a good thing?

He sat at his desk and opened his inbox. The usual mix of client inquiries, internal updates, vendor pitches. But one email caught his attention - a response from a prospect he'd been trying to reach for weeks.

*Mr. Kim,*

*Thank you for your persistence. I've been meaning to get back to you but haven't had the bandwidth. I'd be open to a conversation about your new product line. Can we schedule a call for next week?*

*Regards,*
*Michael Torres*
*Operations Director, Henderson Manufacturing*

David stared at the email. He'd been trying to reach Michael Torres for two months - polite follow-ups, value propositions, the usual sales approach. Nothing had worked. And now, out of nowhere, a response.

He checked his sent folder. The last email he'd sent to Torres was three days ago, before Rachel had shown him SalesPilot. It had been a standard follow-up - nothing special, nothing that should have triggered a response.

But here it was. A breakthrough.

He started typing a response. *Mr. Torres, thank you for getting back to me. I'd be happy to schedule a call...*

Then he stopped.

What would SalesPilot do?

The question appeared unbidden, and with it came a strange temptation. He'd seen what the AI could do - the polished, professional output that sounded exactly like him. Why not try it? Just this once. Just to see.

He opened the SalesPilot interface and typed in the parameters: *Respond to Michael Torres at Henderson Manufacturing, accept his request for a call, suggest times next week, maintain professional tone, express appreciation for his response.*

He clicked generate.

Text appeared on the screen.

*Michael,*

*Thank you for getting back to me - I know how busy things get, and I appreciate you making time to respond.*

*I'd be happy to schedule a call next week. I have availability Tuesday afternoon or Thursday morning, but I'm flexible based on your schedule. Just let me know what works for you.*

*Looking forward to the conversation.*

*Best,*
*David Kim*

David read it over. It was good - professional, warm, not pushy. Better than what he'd been typing, actually. More polished.

He hesitated, his finger over the send button. This wasn't his email. Not really. He'd provided the parameters, but the words had come from the AI.

But wasn't that what Rachel had said? The AI was amplifying him, not replacing him. Taking what he already did well and making it more efficient.

He hit send.

---

The response came within the hour.

*David,*

*Tuesday afternoon works. How about 3 PM? I'll send a calendar invite.*

*Thanks for being patient with my schedule. Looking forward to hearing what you have to say.*

*Michael*

David stared at the email. Two months of trying, and now - within an hour of using SalesPilot - he had a meeting scheduled.

He should have felt triumphant. This was what he'd been working toward. This was success.

Instead, he felt something else. Something he couldn't quite name.

He opened SalesPilot again and stared at the interface. The dashboard showed his activity - one email generated, one email sent, one response received. The metrics were impressive: response time, engagement rate, conversion probability.

But where was his voice in all of this?

He thought about the email to Sarah yesterday - the one he'd written himself, the one the AI had replicated perfectly. He thought about the genuine connection he felt with clients like her, the relationships built over years of real conversations.

And he thought about the email he'd just sent to Michael Torres - polished, professional, effective. But not really his.

Was that the trade-off? Efficiency for authenticity?

He closed the laptop and reached for his cracked mug. The coffee was the same office brew, the same routine, the same comfort it had always been.

But something had shifted. He could feel it.

---

The week continued, and David found himself using SalesPilot more often.

At first, it was just for the difficult emails - the ones that required careful wording, the ones where the stakes were high. The AI was good at those, generating polished prose that said exactly what needed to be said in exactly the right way.

But then he started using it for the routine emails too. The follow-ups, the confirmations, the standard communications that filled his days. Why struggle over wording when the AI could do it in seconds?

He told himself it was efficiency. He told himself it was smart. He told himself he was still in control - providing the parameters, reviewing the output, making the final decision to send.

But somewhere in the back of his mind, a small voice asked: *When was the last time you wrote something yourself?*

He ignored it.

By Friday, his metrics were up. Response rates had improved, meeting requests had increased, his pipeline was fuller than it had been in months. Rachel stopped by his office to congratulate him.

"Whatever you're doing, keep doing it," she said, her smile genuine. "The numbers look great."

David nodded, accepting the praise. But when she left, he opened his sent folder and scrolled through the week's communications.

Email after email after email. All of them professional, polished, effective. All of them signed with his name.

None of them written by him.

He closed the folder and stared out the window at the Chicago skyline. The city was moving out there, people making calls, writing emails, building relationships.

He was building relationships too. The metrics proved it. The numbers didn't lie.

But as he reached for his cracked mug, he noticed something. The coffee didn't taste quite right. The routine didn't feel quite the same.

He couldn't put his finger on what was different. But something was.

---

That night, he dreamed about voices.

In the dream, he was writing an email - typing words that appeared on the screen, one by one. But when he looked down at his hands, they weren't moving. The keyboard was typing itself, the words flowing without his input.

He tried to speak, to say something, but no sound came out. His voice was gone, replaced by something else - something polished and professional and not quite human.

He woke up in a cold sweat, his heart pounding.

The alarm clock said 5:47 AM. Outside his window, the Chicago skyline was just beginning to lighten, the first hints of dawn touching the tops of the buildings.

He lay in bed, staring at the ceiling, trying to shake off the dream.

It was just a dream. Just his subconscious processing the changes in his work. Nothing to worry about.

But as he got up and started his morning routine, he found himself thinking about the email he'd write today. And for the first time in years, he didn't feel the familiar anticipation - the sense of connection, of purpose, of genuine engagement.

He felt... nothing.

The cracked mug sat on his kitchen counter, waiting for the coffee to brew. He looked at it for a long moment.

Then he picked it up and took a sip.

The coffee tasted the same as always. But somehow, it wasn't quite enough.
