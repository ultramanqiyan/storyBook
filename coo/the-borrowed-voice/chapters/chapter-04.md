# Chapter 4: The Preference

The call with Sarah was scheduled for Thursday at 2 PM.

David spent the three days before it preparing, or trying to. He reviewed the proposal, checked the pricing, anticipated questions. But his mind kept drifting to the same thought: when he spoke to Sarah, would he be speaking as himself, or as the polished professional the AI had created?

He tried writing out talking points in his own voice. The words came slowly, awkwardly. Every sentence felt like a struggle against a current that wanted to carry him toward the smooth, efficient language of SalesPilot.

By Wednesday night, he had a page of notes that read nothing like him.

---

Thursday arrived.

David sat at his desk at 1:45 PM, staring at the phone. The cracked mug was beside him, filled with coffee he'd barely touched. The Chicago skyline stretched beyond his window, the afternoon light catching the glass of the buildings.

At 2:00 PM exactly, the phone rang.

He answered on the first ring. "David Kim."

"David, it's Sarah. Thanks for making time for this."

Her voice was warm, familiar, the voice of someone he'd worked with for six years. Someone who trusted him. Someone who had no idea that the emails she'd been responding to weren't really his.

"Of course," he said. "I've been looking forward to this."

The words came out smoothly. Too smoothly. He realized with a start that he'd unconsciously adopted the cadence of the AI-generated emails, the polished, professional rhythm that had become his default.

"Listen," Sarah said, "I wanted to talk through some details before we finalize. My team has questions about implementation timeline and support structure."

"Absolutely. Let me walk you through what we've put together."

He pulled up the proposal on his screen and began to explain. The words flowed easily, he'd prepared extensively, and the material was familiar. But as he talked, he noticed something strange.

He wasn't thinking about what to say. He was just... saying it. The sentences formed without conscious effort, each one polished and professional.

It was like listening to someone else speak.

---

The call went well. Better than well.

Sarah's questions were answered smoothly. Her concerns were addressed professionally. By the end of the conversation, she was ready to move forward.

"David, this has been really helpful," she said. "I appreciate you taking the time to walk me through everything."

"Of course. That's what I'm here for."

"I have to say," she continued, "I've been really impressed with your communication lately. The emails, this call, everything feels more... I don't know. Refined. Professional. It's clear you've been working on your approach."

David felt his stomach tighten. "Thank you. I appreciate that."

"I mean it. A lot of salespeople, after a few years, they get comfortable. They stop trying to improve. But you're clearly invested in getting better. That's rare."

He should have felt proud. The client was happy. The deal was closing. His metrics were improving.

Instead, he felt like a fraud.

"Sarah," he started, then stopped.

"Yes?"

He took a breath. This was the moment. He could tell her the truth, that his improved communication wasn't the result of personal growth, but of an AI tool that generated polished emails in his voice. That the professional she was impressed by wasn't really him.

But what would that accomplish? The deal would fall apart. The trust would evaporate. Six years of relationship building, gone.

"Thank you for saying that," he said finally. "I'll send over the final proposal by end of day."

"Perfect. Looking forward to it."

The call ended, and David sat in silence.

---

He used SalesPilot to write the proposal email.

The parameters were simple: *Send final proposal to Sarah Chen, express appreciation for the call, confirm next steps, maintain professional tone.*

The AI generated the email in seconds. Perfect. Polished. Professional.

He read it over, looking for something, anything, that felt like him.

There was nothing. Just efficient, effective communication.

He hit send.

---

That night, he dreamed about the cracked mug.

In the dream, he was sitting at his desk, reaching for the mug as he'd done every morning for eight years. But when he picked it up, the crack had disappeared. The mug was smooth, perfect, unblemished.

He tried to find the crack, the familiar imperfection that made it his, but it was gone. The mug was better now. More professional. More efficient.

But it wasn't his anymore.

He woke with a start, his heart pounding in the dark.

---

The next morning, he arrived at the office to find a response from Sarah.

*David,*

*The proposal looks great. We're ready to move forward. I'll have my team send over the contract details next week.*

*Thanks for all your work on this. It's been a pleasure working with you.*

*Best,*
*Sarah*

Another success. Another deal closed. Another client satisfied.

David stared at the email, trying to feel something, pride, satisfaction, relief.

But all he felt was hollow.

He opened SalesPilot and checked the history. The proposal email had been AI-generated. The follow-up emails had been AI-generated. The talking points for the call had been AI-generated.

Sarah had praised his improved communication. But she hadn't been praising him. She'd been praising the algorithm.

He closed the laptop and reached for his cracked mug.

It was empty, as it had been for days. He couldn't remember the last time he'd filled it.

---

Rachel stopped by his office that afternoon.

"David, I just saw the notification about the Chen deal. Congratulations! That's a significant win."

He nodded, not trusting himself to speak.

"Your numbers are up across the board," she continued. "Response rates, close rates, average deal size. Whatever you're doing with SalesPilot, it's working."

"Thanks."

She studied him for a moment. "Is everything okay? You seem... off."

David considered the question. Was everything okay? His metrics were great. His clients were happy. His career was thriving.

"I'm fine," he said. "Just tired."

Rachel nodded, accepting the answer. "Well, take care of yourself. And keep up the good work."

She left, and David sat alone in his office, staring at the empty mug on his desk.

---

That night, he tried to write something, anything, in his own voice.

He opened a blank document and stared at the cursor, waiting for words to come.

*Today I closed a deal with Sarah Chen. It was a good deal. The numbers were good. The client was happy.*

He read it over. The words were flat. Lifeless. Nothing like the polished prose that SalesPilot generated.

He tried again.

*I feel like I'm disappearing. The AI writes better than I do. The clients prefer the AI version. What happens when there's nothing left of me?*

He stared at the words. They were honest, at least. But they weren't polished. They weren't professional. They weren't the kind of thing he could send to anyone.

He deleted them and closed the document.

---

The next morning, he arrived at the office and opened his email.

A message from a new prospect was waiting.

*Mr. Kim,*

*I received your outreach last week. I'm interested in learning more about your services. Can we schedule a call?*

*Regards,*
*Jennifer Walsh*
*Procurement Manager, Atlas Industries*

David stared at the email. Another opportunity. Another chance to use the polished, professional voice that wasn't his.

He opened SalesPilot and started typing parameters.

Then he stopped.

His finger hovered over the keyboard. The cursor blinked, waiting for input.

He closed SalesPilot and opened a blank email.

*Jennifer,*

*Thanks for reaching out. I'd love to schedule a call. How does next Tuesday work for you?*

*Best,*
*David*

He read it over. It was simple. Direct. Not particularly polished.

But it was his.

He hit send before he could change his mind.

---

The response came within the hour.

*David,*

*Tuesday works. How about 2 PM? Looking forward to speaking with you.*

*Jennifer*

David stared at the email. A response. A positive response. To the email he'd written himself.

 A small step, perhaps. But it was a step forward.

He reached for his cracked mug, then realized it was still empty. He stood up and walked to the break room to fill it.

The coffee was the same office brew, the same taste. But as he took a sip, he noticed something different.

It tasted like his coffee again.

Not better. Not more polished. Just his.

He wasn't sure what that meant. But he knew it meant something.
