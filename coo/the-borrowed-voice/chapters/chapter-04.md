# Chapter 4: The Preference

The call came on a Thursday afternoon.

David was at his desk, reviewing the week's metrics, when his phone rang. The caller ID showed Sarah Chen's name - his longest-standing client, the one who'd been with him for six years, the one who appreciated his personal touch.

He answered with genuine warmth. "Sarah, good to hear from you. How are things?"

"David!" Her voice was bright, enthusiastic. "I wanted to thank you for that email you sent last week. The one about the new product line?"

David's mind went blank. He'd sent Sarah several emails recently - all of them generated by SalesPilot, all of them polished and professional. He couldn't remember which one she was referring to.

"The one where you talked about reliability," Sarah continued. "And shared that story about your other client? It was exactly what I needed to hear. I showed it to my team, and we've decided to move forward with the order."

David felt something cold in his stomach. The email she was describing - the story about the other client, the personal touch - that was the email he'd written himself. The one from weeks ago, before he'd started using SalesPilot regularly.

But he'd also sent her a SalesPilot-generated follow-up last week. Was she confusing the two?

"That's great news, Sarah," he said, his voice steady despite his confusion. "I'm glad the information was helpful."

"Helpful?" She laughed. "It was perfect. You know, David, that's what I've always appreciated about working with you. You don't just send the same templated stuff everyone else sends. You take the time to write something real."

David's grip tightened on the phone. "Thanks, Sarah. That means a lot."

"I mean it," she continued. "In this business, everyone's using those AI tools now. You know, the ones that write your emails for you? I can always tell when someone's using them. The emails are polished, but they're... empty somehow. You never send me that kind of thing. You always send the real deal."

David felt the words like a physical blow.

"Anyway," Sarah was saying, "I just wanted to call and thank you personally. The order's going through the system now. You should see the paperwork by end of week."

They talked for a few more minutes - about her team, about the industry, about the usual small talk that made their relationship feel genuine. David participated automatically, his mind elsewhere.

When the call ended, he sat at his desk, staring at the phone in his hand.

Sarah had praised him for sending "the real deal." She'd specifically contrasted his emails with AI-generated ones, saying she could always tell the difference.

But he hadn't written her an email himself in weeks.

He opened his sent folder and searched for Sarah's name. The emails appeared on the screen, one after another - all of them generated by SalesPilot, all of them polished and professional, all of them signed with his name.

He read through them, looking for the one Sarah had mentioned. The one with the story about his other client, the personal touch.

It wasn't there.

He expanded his search, going back further. Weeks, months. Looking for any email to Sarah that he'd written himself.

He found it - dated weeks ago, before SalesPilot had become his routine. The email he'd written that first morning, the one the AI had replicated perfectly in Rachel's demo.

But that wasn't the email Sarah was talking about. She'd said "last week."

He scrolled through the SalesPilot-generated emails again, reading each one carefully. And then he saw it.

An email from last week, generated by the AI. It mentioned reliability. It included a story about another client.

It was the email Sarah was praising.

But he hadn't written it. The AI had.

---

He sat with the realization for a long time.

Sarah couldn't tell the difference. His longest-standing client, the one who valued his personal touch, the one who specifically said she could always identify AI-generated emails - she couldn't tell that his recent communications had been generated by a machine.

What did that mean?

He thought about what Rachel had said: *The AI learns your style over time. It can write in your voice.*

But this was more than writing in his voice. This was writing in his voice so convincingly that even people who knew him well couldn't tell the difference.

Was that success? Or was that something else?

He opened SalesPilot and stared at the interface. The dashboard showed his metrics - response rates, engagement scores, client satisfaction. Everything was up. Everything was better.

But Sarah's words echoed in his mind: *You don't just send the same templated stuff everyone else sends. You take the time to write something real.*

He hadn't taken the time. He hadn't written anything real. The AI had done it for him, and Sarah had praised him for it.

Was the praise really for him? Or was it for the algorithm that had learned to mimic him?

---

That afternoon, he tried an experiment.

He had a follow-up email to write for another client - a routine communication, nothing special. He decided to write it himself, without SalesPilot.

He opened a blank compose window and put his fingers on the keyboard.

*Dear Mr. Patterson,*

*I wanted to follow up on our conversation from last week...*

He stopped. The words felt awkward, forced. He tried to continue, but the sentences wouldn't flow. His authentic voice - the one that had once come naturally - felt rusty, unused.

After fifteen minutes of struggle, he had a rough draft. It was genuine, yes, but also uneven. Less polished than the SalesPilot versions. Less professional.

He sent it anyway.

The response came the next morning.

*David,*

*Thanks for the follow-up. I have to say, this email felt different from your usual ones. Not bad, just... different. Everything okay?*

*Best,*
*Tom Patterson*

David stared at the response. Different. Not bad, but different.

The client had noticed. The change in his voice - from AI-polished to authentically rough - had been noticeable enough to prompt concern.

He thought about Sarah's praise. She hadn't noticed the difference. But Patterson had.

Why?

He pulled up both emails - the one to Sarah and the one to Patterson. He compared them side by side.

The email to Sarah had been generated by SalesPilot. It was polished, professional, effective. It had included a story about another client - a story the AI had constructed from his past communications.

The email to Patterson had been written by him. It was genuine, uneven, less polished. It had included a personal observation - something he'd actually thought, actually felt.

Both emails had been signed with his name. Both emails had represented him to his clients.

But only one of them was really his.


The question haunted him for the rest of the week.

What did it mean to write in your own voice? What did it mean to be authentic? If the AI could generate emails that clients couldn't distinguish from his real ones, was there even a difference?

He thought about the cracked mug on his desk - the one with the chip in the rim that he refused to fix. The mug was imperfect, but it was his. It had history, meaning, significance beyond its function.

His authentic emails were like that mug - imperfect, but genuine. The SalesPilot emails were like a perfect replica - flawless, but without the history.

But here was the thing: clients couldn't tell the difference. Sarah, who valued his personal touch, had praised an AI-generated email. Patterson, who noticed something different, had questioned his authentic one.

What did that mean about authenticity? What did that mean about his voice?

He didn't have answers. But the questions were becoming impossible to ignore.


On Friday, Rachel stopped by his office.

"David, got a minute?"

He looked up from his screen. "Sure."

She settled into the chair across from his desk, her expression thoughtful. "I wanted to check in. Your numbers are great - best quarter you've had in two years. But I've noticed something."

David felt a knot form in his stomach. "What's that?"

"You seem... distracted lately. Not your work - your work is excellent. But you. Something's different."

David hesitated. He could tell her about his experiment, about Sarah's call, about the questions that had been haunting him. But what would he say? That he was troubled because his AI-generated emails were too good? That clients couldn't tell the difference between him and an algorithm?

"I'm fine," he said instead. "Just been thinking about some things."

Rachel studied him for a moment. "You know you can talk to me, right? If something's bothering you."

"I know." He managed a smile. "Thanks, Rachel."

She nodded, but her expression suggested she wasn't entirely convinced. "Well, whatever you're doing, keep it up. The numbers don't lie."

She left, and David sat alone in his office, the cracked mug on his desk, the SalesPilot interface glowing on his screen.

The numbers don't lie.

But what were the numbers measuring? His success? Or the AI's?

He didn't know anymore.


That night, he dreamed about the mug.

In the dream, he was holding it - the cracked rim against his lips, the familiar weight in his hand. But when he took a sip, the coffee didn't taste right. It was too smooth, too perfect, without the slight bitterness he'd grown used to.

He looked down at the mug. The crack was gone. The rim was smooth, flawless, perfect.

But it wasn't his mug anymore. It was a perfect copy, without the history, without the meaning, without the story that made it his.

He tried to find the crack - the imperfection that made it real - but it was gone. The mug was better now. Flawless.

But it wasn't his.

He woke up with tears in his eyes, not sure why.
