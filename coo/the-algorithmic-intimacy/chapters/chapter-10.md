# Chapter 10: The Visit

Sarah decided to visit Patricia Wells without any preparation.

It was a Wednesday morning, and she had a gap in her schedule. Patricia had been her client for seven years - a divorced mother of two, concerned about retirement, always appreciative of Sarah's personal attention.

Normally, Sarah would review the WealthGuide profile before any client interaction. She would study the notes, memorize the details, prepare the talking points.

But today, she wanted to try something different.

She drove to Patricia's house in the suburbs, the Boston skyline fading in the rearview mirror. The neighborhood was quiet, tree-lined, the kind of place where people raised families and planned for the future.

She parked in front of Patricia's house and sat for a moment, her hands on the steering wheel.

What did she remember about Patricia?

Divorced. Two children - a son and a daughter. The son was in college, she thought. The daughter was still in high school. Patricia worked in healthcare administration. She was worried about having enough saved for retirement.

The details came, but slowly. Imperfectly. Nothing like the comprehensive profile WealthGuide would have provided.

But they were hers.

She walked up to the door and knocked.

Patricia answered after a moment, her expression shifting from curiosity to surprise. "Sarah? What are you doing here?"

"I was in the neighborhood," Sarah said, which was technically true. "I wanted to check in, see how you're doing. Do you have a few minutes?"

Patricia's surprise shifted to something warmer. "Of course. Come in."


They sat in Patricia's living room, cups of tea on the coffee table.

Patricia's house was comfortable, lived-in. Family photos lined the walls - Patricia with her children at various ages, Patricia with her parents, Patricia with friends. Sarah studied the photos, trying to connect them to the woman sitting across from her.

"So," Patricia said, "what brings you by? Is something wrong with my portfolio?"

"No, nothing like that," Sarah said. "I just... wanted to connect. Not as your advisor, necessarily. Just as... me."

Patricia's expression softened. "That's nice. I can't remember the last time someone did that."

They talked. Not about finances, not about portfolios, but about life. Patricia's son was finishing his junior year at Boston University. Her daughter was applying to colleges. Patricia herself was thinking about a career change - maybe something less stressful, more fulfilling.

"I've been doing healthcare administration for twenty years," Patricia said. "I'm tired. I want to do something that matters."

"What would that be?" Sarah asked.

Patricia smiled. "I've been thinking about teaching. Like my mother. She was a third-grade teacher for thirty years."

Sarah felt something shift inside her. Third-grade teacher. Margaret had been a third-grade teacher too.

"That sounds wonderful," Sarah said. "What's holding you back?"

"Money, mostly," Patricia admitted. "Teaching doesn't pay what administration pays. And I have two kids to put through college."

They talked about the financial implications, but not in the abstract way Sarah would have discussed them with WealthGuide's talking points. They talked about trade-offs, about what mattered, about what Patricia wanted from her life.

The conversation meandered. It wasn't efficient. It wasn't polished. But it was real.


After an hour, Sarah stood to leave.

"Thank you for coming by," Patricia said, walking her to the door. "I can't remember the last time I had a conversation like that. It felt... genuine."

Sarah smiled. "It felt genuine to me too."

"Sarah," Patricia said, her expression turning serious. "Can I tell you something?"

"Of course."

"I've noticed a change in you lately. Over the past few months, you've been more efficient, more on top of things. But also... more distant. Like you were there, but not really there."

Sarah felt something tighten in her chest. "I'm sorry. I didn't realize it was noticeable."

"It was," Patricia said. "But today felt different. Today felt like the old Sarah. The one who actually listened, who actually cared."

She paused. "I missed her."

Sarah felt tears prick at her eyes. "I missed her too."


She drove back to the office, the Boston skyline growing larger in the windshield.

The visit had been imperfect. She had forgotten to ask about Patricia's retirement timeline. She had missed the opportunity to discuss a 529 plan adjustment. She had been present, but professionally incomplete.

But Patricia had noticed the difference. Patricia had appreciated the genuine connection.

Maybe that was the trade-off. Efficiency for authenticity. Polish for presence.

Maybe it was worth it.


Back at the office, she sat at her desk and stared at the WealthGuide interface.

There were notifications waiting - birthdays, anniversaries, follow-ups. The system had been working while she was gone, preparing communications, scheduling calls, managing relationships.

She looked at the notifications and felt something different. Not the hollow resignation she'd felt for months, but a quiet resolve.

She would keep using WealthGuide. It was too useful to abandon. But she would use it differently. As a tool, not a replacement. As a reference, not a script.

She would try to remember. To be present. To care.

Not perfectly. Not efficiently. But genuinely.


That evening, she stayed late at the office.

The financial district was quiet, the streets empty. She sat at her desk and stared at the Boston skyline, the harbor glinting in the fading light.

She thought about Margaret, who had trusted her, valued her, called her a friend. She thought about Patricia, who had missed the "old Sarah." She thought about all the clients she had served over eighteen years, and the ones she had yet to serve.

She didn't have answers. She didn't know if her approach would work, if she could maintain her practice while being genuinely present, if the trade-offs were sustainable.

But she knew one thing: she was going to try.


She opened a blank document and started typing.

*I don't know if this will work. I don't know if I can reclaim what I've lost. But I'm going to try.*

*I'm going to remember my clients. Not just their data points, but their faces, their voices, their stories.*

*I'm going to be present in my conversations. Not just executing scripts, but actually listening, actually caring.*

*I'm going to use WealthGuide as a tool, not a replacement. As a reference, not a script.*

*It won't be efficient. It won't be polished. But it will be real.*

*And maybe that's enough.*

She saved the document and closed her laptop.


The Boston skyline was dark now, the lights of the city twinkling like stars. The harbor was invisible in the night, but she could feel its presence, a constant beyond the changing city.

Sarah Mitchell had been an investment advisor for eighteen years. She had built her practice on relationships, on genuine care, on being present for her clients.

She had almost lost that. Almost let the efficiency seduce her into becoming a ghost in her own professional life.

But she was finding her way back.

The question was still there, unanswered: What did it mean to care?

But she was living her way toward the answer.

 One client at a time. One conversation at a time. One genuine moment at a time.

 A small beginning. But it was hers.
