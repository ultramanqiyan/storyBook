# Chapter 7: The Empty Memory

The days after Margaret's funeral passed in a blur.

Sarah went through the motions. She met with clients, delivered WealthGuide's recommendations, signed her name to communications she hadn't written. The work continued. The metrics stayed strong.

But inside, something had broken.

---

She started trying to remember.

It happened on a Wednesday morning, sitting at her desk before the first meeting. She opened her client list and tried to recall each person without looking at the WealthGuide profiles.

Robert Chen. Tech entrepreneur. Anxious about volatility. Daughter in San Francisco.

David and Lisa Morrison. Early retirement. Inheritance. Son in college.

Patricia Wells. Divorced. Two children. Concerned about retirement.

The names came, but barely. Like reading from a script she'd memorized but didn't understand. The details were there - the data points, the financial information, the WealthGuide notes - but the people were missing.

She tried to remember their faces. The way they smiled. The sound of their voices. The stories they'd shared.

Nothing came.

---

The realization settled in like a weight.

She couldn't remember her clients. Not the way she used to. Not the way that mattered.

Eighteen years of building relationships. Twelve years with Margaret. Six years with Robert. Eight years with the Morrisons. And she couldn't recall their faces without looking at photographs.

What had happened to her?


She opened WealthGuide and scrolled through the client profiles.

Each one was a wealth of information - birthdays, anniversaries, children's names, career changes, health concerns. Everything she needed to know about each client, organized and accessible.

But the information felt clinical. Like reading medical charts instead of knowing patients. Like studying data instead of understanding people.

She thought about Margaret, who had said Sarah was more than just an advisor. She was a friend.

But had she been? Had she really known Margaret? Or had she just been executing the AI's instructions, showing up for meetings, delivering the system's insights?

She couldn't remember the last time she'd had a genuine conversation with Margaret. Not a WealthGuide-guided conversation, but a real one. The kind where she wasn't checking boxes or following AI suggestions.

She couldn't remember.


That afternoon, she had a meeting with Robert Chen.

WealthGuide had prepared the usual briefing - portfolio performance, market concerns, suggested talking points. Sarah reviewed the notes and felt nothing.

Robert arrived on time, his expression the usual mix of confidence and anxiety. They sat in her office, the Boston skyline visible through the window.

"Sarah," he said, "I've been thinking about my daughter. She's thinking about graduate school, and I want to make sure I can support her financially."

Sarah nodded, her mind racing through the WealthGuide notes. Had there been anything about graduate school? She couldn't remember.

"That's wonderful," she said. "Tell me more about her plans."

Robert talked about his daughter's interests, the programs she was considering, the financial implications. Sarah listened, but she was distracted. She was trying to remember if WealthGuide had flagged this, if she should have known, if she was failing.

"Sarah?" Robert's voice broke through her thoughts. "Are you okay? You seem... distant."

She blinked. "I'm sorry, Robert. I'm fine. Just thinking about your situation."

He studied her for a moment. "You know, you've been different lately. More efficient, more on top of things. But also... less present. Does that make sense?"

The words hit her like a physical blow. Less present.

"I'm sorry," she said again. "I've had a lot on my mind."

Robert nodded, but his expression was concerned. "If there's anything you need to talk about, I'm here. You've always been there for me."

She managed a smile. "Thank you, Robert. I appreciate that."

But inside, she was crumbling. He had noticed. He had felt the difference. The efficiency, the organization, the on-top-of-things - and the absence behind it all.


That evening, she stayed late at the office.

The financial district was quiet, the streets empty. She sat at her desk and stared at the WealthGuide interface.

She pulled up Margaret's profile one more time. The photo smiled from the corner of the screen - the same warm expression Sarah had seen for twelve years.

She tried to remember Margaret without the photo. The way her eyes crinkled when she smiled. The sound of her voice. The stories about her third-grade students.

The memories came, but faintly. Like echoes of something that had once been vivid.

She thought about all the times WealthGuide had reminded her of Margaret's birthday. All the AI-generated communications she'd signed. All the meetings where she'd followed the system's script.

Had any of it been real? Had she ever really known Margaret? Or had she just been performing the role of caring advisor?


The question haunted her.

She opened her desk drawer and pulled out a stack of old files - the paper ones she'd kept before everything went digital. There were notes from client meetings, handwritten reminders, photographs that clients had given her over the years.

She found Margaret's file and opened it.

Inside, there were photos. Margaret at her retirement party. Margaret with her students. Margaret and Harold on their wedding day.

There were handwritten notes. "Margaret mentioned her mother is ill - follow up." "Margaret's birthday - March 15." "Ask about Harold's health."

Sarah stared at the notes. They were in her own handwriting. She had written them, years ago, before WealthGuide. Before the system had taken over.

She tried to remember writing them. The meetings where she'd taken these notes. The conversations that had prompted these reminders.

Nothing came.


The realization was devastating.

She had lost something essential. Not just her memory of Margaret, but her memory of herself. The person who had written these notes, who had cared enough to write them, who had been present enough to notice - that person was gone.

In her place was someone else. Someone who relied on algorithms, who executed instructions, who performed care instead of feeling it.

She wasn't sure when the change had happened. It had been gradual, like erosion. Slow enough that she hadn't noticed until it was too late.

But now she saw it clearly. She had become a ghost in her own professional life. A delivery mechanism for AI-generated intimacy.

And she didn't know how to get back.


That night, she dreamed about Margaret.

In the dream, they were sitting in Sarah's office, having a meeting. Margaret was talking about her students, about the joy of teaching, about the children whose lives she had touched.

Sarah tried to listen, but she couldn't focus. She kept looking at the WealthGuide interface, checking the notes, following the script.

Margaret noticed. "Sarah, are you there? Are you listening?"

Sarah tried to answer, but no words came. She was there, but not there. Present, but absent.

Margaret's face began to fade, her voice growing distant. "I thought you cared," she said. "I thought you remembered."

Sarah woke up with tears on her face, the dream still vivid in her mind.


The next morning, she arrived at the office early.

The Boston skyline was bright through the window, the harbor glinting in the morning light. She sat at her desk and stared at the WealthGuide interface.

There were notifications waiting - birthdays, anniversaries, follow-ups. The system had been working while she slept, preparing communications, scheduling calls, managing relationships.

She looked at the notifications and felt nothing.

She had become what she feared most: efficient, successful, and hollow.

The question was: what would she do about it?
