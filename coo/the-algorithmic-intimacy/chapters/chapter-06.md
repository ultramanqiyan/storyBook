# Chapter 6: The Loss

The call came on a Tuesday afternoon.

Sarah was at her desk, reviewing WealthGuide's recommendations for the week, when her phone rang. The caller ID showed an unfamiliar number.

"Sarah Mitchell," she answered.

"Ms. Mitchell, this is Patricia Sullivan." The voice was strained, thick with emotion. "Margaret's sister-in-law."

Sarah felt something cold in her stomach. "Patricia. What's wrong?"

"It's Margaret." Patricia's voice cracked. "She passed away this morning. A heart attack. It was... it was sudden."

Sarah sat frozen at her desk. Margaret. Her longest-standing client. Twelve years of meetings, conversations, birthday calls.

"I'm so sorry," she managed. "I'm so, so sorry."

"Thank you," Patricia said. "I know she thought highly of you. She always said you were more than just her advisor. You were a friend."

The words hit Sarah like a physical blow. A friend.

"Is there anything I can do?" Sarah asked. "Anything at all?"

"There will be a memorial service next week," Patricia said. "I'll send you the details. And... I know this is unusual, but Margaret mentioned in her will that she'd like you to handle some charitable bequests. She didn't have family, but she wanted to leave something to the school where she taught."

"Of course," Sarah said. "I'll take care of it."

They said their goodbyes, and Sarah hung up the phone.

---

She sat at her desk, staring at the wall.

Margaret was gone. The woman who had been her client for twelve years, who had shared stories about her third-grade students, who had talked about her late husband Harold with such love.

Sarah tried to remember Margaret's face. The way her eyes crinkled when she smiled. The warmth of her voice. The photo of Harold she kept on her desk.

But the memories felt distant. Fuzzy. Like looking at a photograph through fog.

She opened WealthGuide and pulled up Margaret's profile.

The photo smiled from the corner of the screen - the same warm expression Sarah had seen for twelve years. Below the photo, a wealth of information:

*Client since: 2012*
*Portfolio value: $847,000*
*Last meeting: 23 days ago*
*Communication preference: In-person*
*Notes: Retired teacher. Widowed. No children. Values personal relationship.*

Sarah stared at the notes. Retired teacher. Widowed. No children. Values personal relationship.

The information was accurate. But it felt clinical. Like reading a Wikipedia entry about someone who had just died.

She closed her eyes and tried to remember Margaret without the screen. The stories she'd told. The way she'd laughed. The sound of her voice on the phone last week, thanking Sarah for remembering her birthday.

The memories came, but barely. Like fragments of a dream she couldn't quite hold onto.

---

That evening, she stayed late at the office.

The financial district was dark, the streets empty. She sat at her desk and pulled up Margaret's communication history.

There were dozens of entries - meeting notes, phone calls, emails. Each one documented with precision. Dates, topics, outcomes.

Sarah scrolled through them, looking for something real. Something that would bring Margaret back to her.

*March 15: Birthday call. Client appreciative.*
*November 3: Anniversary of Harold's death. Client emotional. Discussed estate planning.*
*July 22: Quarterly review. Client concerned about market volatility. Reassured.*
*January 10: In-person meeting. Client mentioned health concerns (arthritis). Discussed long-term care options.*

The notes were accurate. Comprehensive. Professional.

But they weren't memories. They were data points.


She tried to remember the last time she'd seen Margaret in person.

WealthGuide said it was 23 days ago. A quarterly review meeting. Sarah had followed the AI's recommendations, discussed portfolio performance, suggested some adjustments.

But what had they talked about? What had Margaret said? What had she looked like?

Sarah couldn't remember.

She had the notes. WealthGuide had documented everything. But the actual experience - the human connection, the warmth, the presence - was gone.


The memorial service was on Saturday.

Sarah drove to the church where Margaret had been a member for forty years. The building was old, beautiful, filled with light streaming through stained glass windows.

She sat in the back pew, watching as people filed in. Former students, now grown. Fellow teachers, retired like Margaret. Neighbors from her community.

They spoke about her with love. About her dedication to teaching. About her kindness. About the way she'd touched their lives.

Sarah listened, feeling like an imposter.

These people had known Margaret. Really known her. They remembered her laugh, her stories, her warmth.

Sarah had been her advisor for twelve years. And she couldn't even remember her face without looking at a photograph.


After the service, Patricia approached her.

"Ms. Mitchell," she said. "Thank you for coming."

"Please, call me Sarah." She paused. "I'm so sorry for your loss."

Patricia nodded. "Margaret spoke of you often. She said you were one of the few people who really listened to her. Who remembered the things that mattered."

Sarah felt the words like a knife.

"I should have done more," she said. "I should have... been there more."

"You were there when she needed you," Patricia said. "That's what matters."

But Sarah knew the truth. She hadn't been there. WealthGuide had. The system had remembered Margaret's birthday, suggested the talking points, prompted the calls.

Sarah had just shown up.


That night, she couldn't sleep.

She lay in bed, staring at the ceiling, thinking about Margaret. About the twelve years they'd known each other. About the relationship she thought they'd had.

But what kind of relationship was it, really? She couldn't remember Margaret's face without a photograph. She couldn't recall their conversations without checking the notes. She couldn't even be sure what Margaret had meant to her.

The system had managed the relationship. Sarah had just been the interface.

And now Margaret was gone, and Sarah couldn't even grieve properly because she wasn't sure she had really known her.


The next morning, she arrived at the office early.

The Boston skyline was bright through the window, the harbor glinting in the morning light. She sat at her desk and stared at the WealthGuide interface.

There was a notification waiting:

*Client status update: Margaret Sullivan - DECEASED. Recommend: Send condolence message to family. Review estate documents. Process charitable bequests.*

Sarah stared at the notification. The system had already moved on. It was already processing the next steps, handling the logistics, managing the aftermath.

But Sarah was still stuck on what she'd lost.

Not just Margaret. Not just a client. But something deeper. Something she couldn't quite name.

She had thought she was building relationships. She had thought she was caring for people. She had thought she was making a difference.

But now she wasn't sure. Now she wondered if she had just been going through the motions. Performing care instead of feeling it. Executing the AI's instructions instead of being present.

Margaret had thought Sarah was her friend. Margaret had thought Sarah listened, remembered, cared.

But had she? Or had she just been following the script?


She sat with the question for a long time.

The WealthGuide interface glowed on her screen, waiting for her to acknowledge the notification. To send the condolence message. To process the estate.

But she couldn't. Not yet.

She closed her eyes and tried one more time to remember Margaret. Not the data points, not the notes, not the WealthGuide profile.

The real Margaret. The woman who had taught third grade for thirty years. The woman who had loved her husband Harold for forty-seven years. The woman who had no children but considered her students her family.

The memories came, faint and fragmented. A smile. A laugh. A story about a difficult student. A photograph of Harold on a desk.

It wasn't much. But it was something.

And for now, that was all she had.
