# Chapter 2: The Dopamine

Three months into using FocusZone, Marcus noticed something addictive.

Every time the AI suggested a goal, he felt a small rush of satisfaction. Every time it optimized a priority, he felt a tiny hit of accomplishment. Every time it determined a direction, he felt a moment of clarity.

The feedback loop was immediate and rewarding.

---

He checked his productivity metrics.

Goals completed: up 40%. Priorities optimized: up 35%. Progress made: up 50%.

The numbers were impressive. But beneath the metrics, something felt different.

*I'm accomplishing more*, he thought. *But I'm not... choosing any of it.*

---

The dopamine hits came regularly.

FocusZone would suggest a goal, and he'd feel motivated. It would optimize a priority, and he'd feel productive. It would determine a direction, and he'd feel directed.

The satisfaction was real. But the source was external.

*This is what AI-assisted motivation feels like*, he realized. *Not wanting things yourself—letting AI want them for you. Not choosing directions—letting AI choose them.*

---

He tried an experiment.

He turned off FocusZone for an hour. Just one hour of direction without AI assistance.

The experience was disorienting.

Every goal felt uncertain. Every priority felt arbitrary. Every direction felt unclear.

*This is what it feels like to have no will*, he realized. *This is what I've become. Someone who can't want anything without AI support.*

He turned FocusZone back on. The relief was immediate. The dopamine returned.

*This is what dependency feels like*, he thought. *Not needing AI to work—needing it to want. Needing it to care. Needing it to have direction.*
