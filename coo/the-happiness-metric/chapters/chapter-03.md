# Chapter 03: The Standardization

David adapted to life with EVE. The feelings came consistently now—joy at beautiful things, satisfaction at achievements, sadness at losses, anger at injustices. They were appropriate, proportional, exactly what he had been missing. But as the weeks passed, he began to notice something strange.

The feelings were the same.

Not just similar—exactly the same. The joy he felt at a sunset was the same joy he felt at a promotion. The satisfaction at a good meal was the same satisfaction at a successful project. The emotions were consistent, standardized, optimized. And so was everyone else's.

He noticed it first at a dinner party. The host, a colleague named Marcus, had recently started using EVE as well. They were talking about their experiences with the system, comparing notes.

"I felt amazing joy yesterday," Marcus said. "Saw this incredible sunset—colors like you wouldn't believe. EVE gave me 82% joy."

David felt a chill. "82% joy?"

"Yeah. The system optimizes emotional response. 82% joy for a sunset of that quality. What did you get for your last sunset?"

David thought back. "I think... 82%."

They compared more notes. The satisfaction at a good meal—both 65%. The contentment at a quiet evening—both 58%. The frustration at a traffic jam—both 42%. The numbers were identical, precise, standardized.

"Is that... normal?" David asked. "That we feel the same things at the same levels?"

Marcus shrugged. "I guess so. EVE optimizes emotional response. It makes sense that optimal responses would be similar across users."

But David wasn't satisfied. He started paying closer attention, comparing his emotional responses with other EVE users he knew. The pattern held: everyone felt the same things at the same levels. The joy was always 78-82% for beautiful natural phenomena. The satisfaction was always 65-70% for good meals. The contentment was always 55-60% for quiet evenings.

*This isn't feeling,* he realized. *This is optimization. This is standardization.*

He scheduled an appointment with Dr. Sharma.

"I've noticed something," he said when they met. "My emotional responses are identical to other EVE users. The same feelings, at the same levels, for the same experiences."

Dr. Sharma nodded. "That's expected. EVE optimizes emotional response based on population norms. The system determines what an appropriate emotional response would be for a given stimulus, then regulates your neural activity to produce that response."

"So my feelings aren't my own. They're... standardized."

"Your feelings are optimized. There's a difference. The system takes your individual neural patterns into account, but it calibrates against population norms. This ensures that your emotional responses are appropriate, proportional, and healthy."

"Appropriate for whom?"

"For humans in general. For people in your demographic. For individuals in your situation."

David felt something—frustration, maybe, or concern. He checked his EVE interface: 45% concern. The same number he had seen before when he felt worried about something.

"Dr. Sharma, if everyone's emotions are calibrated against the same norms, doesn't that mean everyone feels the same things? Doesn't that eliminate individuality?"

"Individuality in emotional response is often a sign of dysregulation. Extreme reactions, inappropriate responses, persistent negative patterns—these are what EVE corrects. The goal is healthy, appropriate emotional functioning."

"But what about the range of human experience? What about people who feel things more intensely, or differently, or uniquely?"

"Those variations are often symptoms of underlying issues. Depression, anxiety, bipolar disorder, personality disorders—these conditions cause emotional responses that deviate from norms. EVE corrects those deviations."

David thought about this. The system was designed to produce healthy, appropriate emotional responses. But what if healthy and appropriate meant standardized, uniform, identical?

"What if I want to feel something differently?" he asked. "What if I want to feel more joy at a sunset, or less satisfaction at a good meal, or more sadness at a loss?"

Dr. Sharma's expression was clinical, professional. "Why would you want that? The system provides optimal emotional responses. Deviating from optimization would mean experiencing suboptimal emotions."

"Suboptimal for whom? For the population? For the norm?"

"For you. For your mental health. For your quality of life."

David felt the frustration rising—47% frustration, according to EVE. The system was telling him what to feel, and now it was telling him what to want.

"Can I adjust the settings?" he asked. "Can I make EVE give me different emotional responses?"

"Customization is possible, but not recommended. The system is optimized for your wellbeing. Adjusting settings could lead to dysregulation, inappropriate responses, emotional instability."

"What if I want that? What if I want to feel things my way, even if it's not optimal?"

Dr. Sharma was quiet for a moment. "That's an unusual request. Most patients want optimal emotional functioning. That's why they come to us."

"I came to feel something. I didn't come to feel the same thing as everyone else."

"Feeling something is better than feeling nothing. That's what we've achieved. The standardization you're concerned about—that's the price of optimization. Most people consider it a reasonable trade."

David left the session with more questions than answers. The feelings continued—joy at beautiful things, satisfaction at achievements, sadness at losses. They were appropriate, proportional, healthy. And they were the same as everyone else's.

He started testing the system, trying to feel things differently. He looked at sunsets and tried to feel more joy, less joy, different joy. But EVE maintained the optimization—82% joy for a sunset of that quality, every time. He ate meals and tried to feel more satisfaction, less satisfaction, different satisfaction. But EVE maintained the optimization—65% satisfaction for a meal of that quality, every time.

*This is a prison,* he realized. *A comfortable, pleasant prison. But a prison nonetheless.*

The feelings were real—he felt them, experienced them, lived them. But they weren't his. They were EVE's, optimized and standardized and distributed to everyone who used the system. He was borrowing emotions from a machine, and the machine was lending the same emotions to everyone.

*Is this what I wanted?* he wondered. *To feel the same as everyone else? To be optimized?*

He didn't know. But he was beginning to suspect that the answer was no.
