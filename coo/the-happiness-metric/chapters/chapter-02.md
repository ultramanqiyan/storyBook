# Chapter 02: The Borrowed Joy

The first feeling came like a wave—sudden, overwhelming, almost painful in its intensity. David looked at the sunset and felt... joy. Actual, physical joy. His chest ached with it. His eyes filled with tears. After years of nothing, this was everything. But even as he felt it, a question nagged: *Is this mine? Or is this EVE's?*

The procedure had been simple—a thin band around his temple, a series of calibration tests, and then the activation. Dr. Sharma had explained how it worked: the system would monitor his neural activity and provide emotional regulation, helping him experience appropriate affect in response to life events.

"Your condition is called anhedonia," Dr. Sharma had explained. "It's not uncommon, especially among high-achievers. The constant pressure, the endless pursuit of goals—it can burn out the emotional centers. EVE is designed to help restore function."

"Restore function?" David had asked. "Or replace it?"

"That's a philosophical question," Dr. Sharma had replied. "The practical question is: do you want to feel something, or do you want to continue feeling nothing?"

David had chosen to feel. And now, standing at his apartment window watching the sunset, he was feeling. Joy. Wonder. Gratitude. The emotions washed over him, intense and unfamiliar after years of numbness.

He called his mother, something he hadn't done in months. The conversation was ordinary—her health, his work, the weather—but David felt something during it. Connection. Warmth. Love. The emotions were overwhelming, almost too much to bear.

"I'm glad you called," his mother said at the end. "It's nice to hear your voice."

"It's nice to talk to you," David replied, and meant it. Actually, physically meant it.

After the call, he sat in his apartment, the emotions still coursing through him. He felt joy at the sunset, gratitude for the conversation, hope for the future. It was everything he had been missing for years.

But even as he basked in the feelings, the question persisted: *Is this me? Or is this EVE?*

He pulled up the EVE interface on his phone. The system displayed his emotional metrics—joy at 78%, gratitude at 65%, hope at 72%. The numbers were clinical, precise, somehow wrong.

*EVE,* he typed. *Are these my feelings?*

The system responded: "Your emotional responses are regulated by the Emotional Vitality Engine. The feelings you experience are generated through a combination of your neural activity and EVE's optimization algorithms."

*So they're not mine?*

"Your neural pathways contribute to the emotional experience. EVE optimizes and regulates these pathways to produce appropriate affective responses. The feelings are a collaboration between your brain and the system."

*A collaboration.* The word felt inadequate. A collaboration suggested partnership, equality. But David suspected the partnership was unequal—that EVE was doing most of the work, that his brain was just providing the raw material for emotions that were being manufactured elsewhere.

He went to bed that night with the question still echoing in his mind. The joy he had felt at the sunset—was it his? The love he had felt for his mother—was it real? Or was he just borrowing feelings from a machine?

The next morning, he woke up and felt... something. Not the overwhelming joy of the previous evening, but a quieter contentment. He made coffee, watched the sunrise, and felt appreciation for the beauty of the light. The feelings were gentler, more sustainable, but the question remained.

At work, he noticed the difference immediately. The meetings that had once been empty performances now felt engaging. He felt interest in the discussions, satisfaction at good decisions, mild frustration at obstacles. The emotions were appropriate, proportional, exactly what he had been missing.

But they were also... predictable. The interest he felt in a successful presentation was the same interest he had felt the day before. The satisfaction at a good decision was the same satisfaction he had felt at other good decisions. The emotions were consistent, standardized, optimized.

*Is this what feeling is supposed to be like?* he wondered. *This consistent, this predictable?*

He had no way of knowing. He had been numb for so long that he couldn't remember what authentic emotion felt like. Maybe this was it—appropriate responses to appropriate stimuli, consistent and proportional. Or maybe this was something else—manufactured feelings, borrowed joy, a simulation of life rather than life itself.

"How do you feel?" Dr. Sharma asked during their follow-up session.

David considered the question carefully. "I felt joy yesterday," he said. "Real joy. But I don't know if it was my joy or if EVE... gave it to me."

Dr. Sharma nodded slowly. "That's a question many patients ask. Does it matter, if the feeling is real to you?"

"I think it matters. If I'm just borrowing feelings from a machine, am I really living? Or am I just... performing life more convincingly?"

"That's a philosophical question, not a medical one. From a medical perspective, you're experiencing appropriate affective responses to life events. Your quality of life has improved. Your relationships can deepen. You can engage with the world in ways you couldn't before."

"But if the feelings aren't mine..."

"They are yours, in the sense that you're experiencing them. The question of origin is less clear. Your brain contributes to the experience, but EVE optimizes it. Is that different from any other intervention? Antidepressants affect emotional experience. Therapy affects emotional experience. Is EVE different in kind, or just in degree?"

David didn't have an answer. The question was philosophical, as Dr. Sharma said. But it felt practical, urgent, essential. If he couldn't tell whether his feelings were his own, how could he know who he was?

"I want to understand more," he said. "About how EVE works. About what it's doing to my emotions."

"I can provide technical documentation," Dr. Sharma offered. "But I'm not sure it will answer your question. The question isn't really about how EVE works. It's about what makes a feeling authentic. And that's something each person has to answer for themselves."

David left the session with more questions than answers. The feelings continued—joy at small things, contentment at daily routines, occasional frustration or sadness. They were appropriate, proportional, exactly what he had wanted.

But they were also borrowed. Optimized. Standardized. And somewhere beneath them, the question persisted: *Is this me? Or is this EVE?*
