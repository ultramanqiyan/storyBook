# Chapter 05: The Test

The invitation came on a Tuesday morning, three months after my fall. The International Climbing Federation wanted me to compete in the World Championship—the most prestigious event in the sport.

I stared at the email, my heart racing. The World Championship. I had dreamed of this since I was a child, had trained for it my entire career. But now, with RISK-AI still damaged and my climbing still uncertain, the invitation felt like a trap.

"You should do it," Marcus said, when I showed him the email. "It's the perfect test."

"Test of what?"

"Of whether you've actually recovered. Of whether you can climb without the system. Of whether you're ready to trust yourself."

I nodded, the words settling into my mind. He was right. This was what I had been working toward—the chance to prove that I could climb without AI assistance, that I could trust my own judgment, that I was more than a vessel for optimized decisions.

"I'll do it," I said.

---

The training was brutal. I spent every day at the gym, working through routes, developing my intuition, learning to read the rock without data. My body had healed, my shoulder was strong, my endurance was returning. But my mind was still the challenge.

I had to learn to compete without RISK-AI—to face routes I had never seen, to make decisions in real time, to trust my judgment under pressure. It was terrifying.

"What if I fail?" I asked Marcus one evening, after a particularly frustrating session.

"Then you fail," he said simply. "Failure is part of climbing. It's part of life. The question isn't whether you'll fail—it's whether you'll have the courage to try anyway."

The words stayed with me. I had spent eight years optimizing for success, avoiding failure at all costs. But maybe failure wasn't the enemy. Maybe it was the teacher.

---

The competition was in Switzerland, two months away. I trained every day, pushing my body and mind to their limits. I climbed routes I had never seen, made decisions without data, learned to trust my intuition even when it was uncertain.

It was the hardest training I had ever done. But it was also the most real.

I thought about RISK-AI often—the system that had guided me for eight years, the voice that had become my second self. The doctors said it could be repaired, that I could have it back if I wanted. But I wasn't sure I did.

The system had made me successful, but it had also made me dependent. It had optimized my performance, but it had atrophied my judgment. It had given me perfect execution, but it had taken away my ability to think.

I wasn't sure I wanted that back.


The night before I left for Switzerland, I sat in my apartment, staring at the mountains through my window. The peaks were dark against the sky, their faces mysterious and unknowable.

I thought about the climb that had started all this—the Sentinel, the fall, the broken body and damaged system. I had almost died on that mountain, had lost everything I thought I was. But I had also found something: the ability to trust myself.

The competition tomorrow would test that ability. I would face routes I had never seen, make decisions without guidance, climb without optimization. I might fail. I might fall. I might embarrass myself in front of the world.

But I would do it as myself—not as a vessel for AI decisions, but as a climber who had learned to think again.


The flight to Switzerland was long and uncomfortable. I sat in my seat, my mind racing through scenarios, my body tense with anticipation. The other competitors were probably training with their AI systems, optimizing their routes, calculating their probabilities.

I would have none of that. I would have only my intuition, my judgment, my hard-won ability to read the rock.

It wasn't much. But it was mine.


The competition venue was massive—a state-of-the-art climbing facility with walls that stretched toward the ceiling, routes that challenged even the best athletes. I walked through the space, my eyes scanning the problems, my mind already working.

The other competitors were there, their AI systems humming in their ears, their faces focused and calm. They looked like I used to look—optimized, efficient, certain.

I felt a flash of envy, quickly suppressed. I had chosen this path. I had chosen to climb without the system. Now I had to live with the consequences.


The opening ceremony was a blur of lights and music and speeches. I stood with the other athletes, my heart pounding, my mind racing. Tomorrow, the competition would begin. Tomorrow, I would find out if I could really do this.

That night, I lay in my hotel bed, staring at the ceiling. The silence in my ear was familiar now—no longer oppressive, just quiet. I had learned to live with it, had learned to fill it with my own thoughts.

Tomorrow, those thoughts would be tested. Tomorrow, I would climb without a net.

I closed my eyes and tried to sleep, but the anticipation was too strong. I was terrified. I was excited. I was ready.

Or at least, I hoped I was.
