# Chapter 04: The Recovery

The climbing gym was empty at 6 AM, the usual crowd of early risers not yet arrived. I stood at the base of the bouldering wall, my hands chalked, my shoulder aching, my mind racing.

This was my first attempt to climb without RISK-AI. The system was still damaged, still silent, still unable to guide me. I was on my own.

I looked up at the route, a simple bouldering problem, rated V2, well within my capabilities even injured. But without the system, it felt impossible. I didn't know where to place my hands, didn't know which holds were solid, didn't know the sequence.

I didn't know anything.

---

I started climbing, my movements awkward and uncertain. My hands found holds, but I couldn't tell if they were good. My feet placed themselves, but I couldn't judge the stability. Every move felt like a guess, every position felt precarious.

Halfway up, I froze. My body trembled, my mind blank. I didn't know what to do next.

"Come on," I whispered to myself. "You've done this a thousand times."

But I hadn't. I had executed routes a thousand times, following the system's guidance. I had never actually climbed, not really, not independently, not with my own judgment.

I downclimbed slowly, my face burning with shame.

---

The gym was filling up by the time I finished my third attempt. Other climbers moved through the space with confidence, their bodies flowing over the walls with ease. I watched them, trying to understand what I was missing.

"First time climbing without AI?"

I turned to see an older man, maybe fifty, his face weathered from years of outdoor climbing. He was watching me with something like sympathy.

"How did you know?"

"Your movements," he said. "They're uncertain. Like you're waiting for someone to tell you what to do."

I nodded, the words hitting too close to home. "I've been climbing with RISK-AI for eight years. I don't know how to do it without the system."

The man smiled, but it wasn't cruel. "I remember when those systems first came out. Everyone said they would make climbing safer, more accessible. And they did, in some ways. But they also took something away."

"What?"

"The ability to read the rock." He gestured to the wall behind me. "Climbing isn't just about strength or technique. It's about judgment, about looking at a wall and knowing, without being told, where to go. That's what the system does for you. And that's what you've lost."


The man's name was Marcus, and he had been climbing for thirty years, long before AI assistance existed. He offered to help me relearn what I had lost.

"It won't be easy," he warned. "You've spent eight years letting a machine do your thinking. That's a lot of dependency to undo."

"I know," I said. "But I have to try."

We started with the basics. Marcus showed me how to read a route, how to look at a wall and see the lines, the sequences, the possibilities. He taught me to trust my body, to feel the holds, to develop the intuition that RISK-AI had replaced.

"Close your eyes," he said, as I stood at the base of a route. "Feel the rock. What does it tell you?"

I tried, but there was nothing. Just silence, emptiness, the absence of the system's guidance.

"I don't feel anything," I admitted.

"That's because you've stopped listening," Marcus said. "The system has been talking for so long that you've forgotten how to hear your own voice."


The weeks that followed were brutal. I climbed every day, struggling through routes that should have been easy, falling on problems I had once executed without thought. My body healed slowly, my shoulder regaining strength, my ribs finally mending. But my mind was the real challenge.

I had to learn to think again, not the optimized thinking of AI guidance, but the messy, intuitive thinking of actual judgment. I had to learn to trust myself, to make decisions without data, to feel the rock instead of analyzing it.

It was the hardest thing I had ever done.


The breakthrough came on a Tuesday, six weeks after I started training with Marcus. I was working on a V4 bouldering problem, a route that had been frustrating me for days. I stood at the base, studying the holds, trying to see the sequence.

And then, without warning, I saw it. Not data, not calculation, not probability. Just... knowing. A sense of where to go, how to move, what the rock was telling me.

I started climbing, my body flowing through the sequence. The moves felt right, not optimized, not calculated, but right. I reached the top, my hands finding the final hold, my body settling into position.

I had done it. Not because RISK-AI told me how, but because I had figured it out myself.

The feeling was like nothing I had ever experienced. Pride, yes, but something more, wonder. The joy of actually knowing, of actually trusting, of actually climbing.


"That's it," Marcus said, watching my face. "That's what you've been missing."

I stared at my hands, chalk-dusted and trembling. "I felt it. The rock. I knew what to do without being told."

"That's intuition," Marcus said. "That's judgment. That's what climbing is supposed to be."

I nodded, the words settling into my mind. This was what I had lost, the ability to know without being told, to feel without being guided, to trust without being assured.

This was what I was fighting to recover.


That night, I sat in my apartment, the silence in my ear no longer oppressive. RISK-AI was still offline, still damaged, still unable to guide me. But for the first time, I didn't mind.

I was learning to climb again. Not the optimized climbing of AI assistance, but the real climbing, the kind that came from intuition, from judgment, from the deep knowing that Marcus had talked about.

The path ahead was still hard. I had months of recovery ahead, years of dependency to undo. But I had found something essential along the way: the ability to trust myself.

The system could be repaired. RISK-AI could come back online. But I wasn't sure I wanted it to.

For the first time in eight years, I was climbing for myself, not for the data, not for the metrics, not for the optimization. But for the joy of actually knowing.
