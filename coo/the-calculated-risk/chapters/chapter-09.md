# Chapter 09: The Struggle

The resistance met every week, in a gym that had been converted from an old warehouse. The space was rough, the equipment was basic, but the community was real.

I trained there every day, working with other climbers who had rejected RISK-AI, developing my intuition, learning to trust my judgment. The work was hard—harder than anything I had done with the system. But it was also more meaningful.

"Your technique is improving," Marcus said one evening, watching me work through a difficult route. "But your judgment is still developing."

"What do you mean?"

"You're still thinking too much. Analyzing, calculating, trying to optimize without the system. What you need to do is feel."

"Feel?" I asked, confused.

"Intuition isn't about thinking," Marcus explained. "It's about knowing without knowing why. It's about trusting your body, your instincts, your deep understanding of the rock. The system taught you to think your way through routes. Now you need to learn to feel your way."

---

The training was frustrating. I had spent eight years thinking through routes—analyzing holds, calculating sequences, optimizing movements. Now I had to unlearn all of that and develop a completely different approach.

I practiced every day, trying to feel the rock instead of analyzing it. Some days were better than others. Some days, I would stand at the base of a route and just know—without thinking, without calculating—where to go. Other days, I would freeze, my mind blank, my body uncertain.

The struggle was real. But so was the progress.

---

The competition invitations started coming again—smaller events, local competitions, nothing like the World Championship. But they were something.

I competed in a regional event in Colorado, placing fifth—not my best result, but my best performance. I had climbed without the system, trusted my judgment, and done well.

The feeling was different from the optimized victories I had achieved with RISK-AI. It wasn't about the result; it was about the process. I had actually climbed, actually thought, actually trusted myself.

That was worth more than any trophy.

---

The months passed. I continued training, continued competing, continued developing my intuition. My results were inconsistent—sometimes I placed well, sometimes I didn't. But my judgment was improving.

I could feel it in the way I approached routes, in the decisions I made, in the trust I had in myself. The system had atrophied my intuition, but it was coming back.

---

The breakthrough came on a Saturday morning, six months after I had rejected RISK-AI. I was working on a route that had been frustrating me for weeks—a complex problem that required both strength and creativity.

I stood at the base, studying the holds, trying to see the sequence. But instead of analyzing, instead of calculating, I just... felt.

My body moved without thinking, my hands finding holds, my feet placing themselves, my body flowing through the sequence. It wasn't optimized, wasn't efficient, wasn't perfect. But it was right.

I reached the top, my hands finding the final hold, my body settling into position. The feeling was like nothing I had ever experienced—not the hollow victory of optimized success, but the deep satisfaction of genuine achievement.

I had done it. Not because RISK-AI told me how, but because I had figured it out myself.

---

"That's it," Marcus said, watching my face. "That's what you've been working toward."

I nodded, the words settling into my mind. This was what I had lost when I started using the system—the ability to know without being told, to feel without analyzing, to trust without calculating.

This was what I had been fighting to recover.

---

The resistance grew. More climbers joined our community, drawn by word of mouth, by the promise of something the system couldn't provide. We trained together, competed together, supported each other through the challenges.

I wasn't just climbing for myself anymore. I was climbing for everyone who had been made dependent by RISK-AI, for everyone who had lost their intuition, for everyone who wanted to find their way back.

The struggle continued. The system was still powerful, the industry still dominant, the odds still against us. But we were fighting back, one route at a time.

---

That night, I sat in my apartment, staring at the mountains through my window. The peaks were dark against the sky, their faces mysterious and unknowable.

I thought about the journey that had brought me here—the fall on the Sentinel, the months of recovery, the decision to reject RISK-AI, the struggle to develop my intuition. It had been the hardest year of my life.

But it had also been the most meaningful.

I had found something essential, something the system had tried to take away. I had learned to trust myself, to feel the rock, to know without being told.

The path ahead was still uncertain. My career was still in jeopardy, my future still unclear. But I had something more important than certainty: I had my judgment back.

And that was everything.
