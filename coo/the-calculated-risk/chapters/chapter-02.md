# Chapter 02: The Fall

*Eight weeks earlier.*

The climb was supposed to be routine. RISK-AI had calculated a 96.2% probability of success, the route was well within my capabilities, and the conditions were optimal. There was no reason for anything to go wrong.

But it did.

---

The mountain was called The Sentinel, a 2,000-foot granite face in the Colorado Rockies. I had climbed it a dozen times before, always with RISK-AI guiding my moves, always with perfect execution. This time was supposed to be no different.

I started early, the morning light painting the rock in shades of gold and orange. The cameras were rolling, my sponsor's drone hovering overhead, capturing every move for the content they would later monetize.

"Route conditions optimal," RISK-AI said in my ear. "Weather window: 8 hours. Proceed with standard protocol."

I began to climb.

---

The first 1,500 feet went smoothly. My hands found the holds, my feet placed themselves with precision, my body moved through the sequences the system had calculated. This was what I did—what I had always done. Perfect execution of a perfect plan.

But at 1,600 feet, something changed.

"Anomaly detected," RISK-AI said, its voice calm but urgent. "Rock stability compromised at current position. Recommend immediate retreat to last secure point."

I looked down at the hold my right hand was gripping. It looked solid—the same as every other hold on this route. But RISK-AI's sensors could detect things I couldn't: micro-fractures, stress patterns, the subtle signs of impending failure.

I began to downclimb, moving carefully back to the last secure position.

Then the rock gave way.


The fall was silent at first—just the sickening sensation of weightlessness, the world spinning around me. Then came the sound: rock breaking, rope singing, the desperate whir of my auto-belay trying to arrest my descent.

I hit the ledge hard, my body crumpling against stone. Pain exploded through my shoulder, my hip, my head. The world went white, then black, then white again.

"Emergency protocol initiated," RISK-AI said, its voice steady despite the chaos. "Vital signs critical. Initiating medical distress beacon. Help is on the way."

I lay on the ledge, my body broken, my mind reeling. This wasn't supposed to happen. The system had calculated the risk. The probability was in my favor. I had followed the protocol.

So why was I lying on a ledge, my blood pooling on the rock?


The rescue took four hours. I drifted in and out of consciousness, RISK-AI's voice a constant presence in my ear, updating me on my vitals, the rescue progress, the probability of survival.

"Survival probability: 94.7%. Rescue team ETA: 47 minutes. Remain calm. Help is coming."

I wanted to laugh. Remain calm? I was lying on a mountain ledge, my body broken, my career possibly over. How was I supposed to remain calm?

But I didn't laugh. I just lay there, staring at the sky, wondering what had gone wrong.


The hospital was a blur of white walls and concerned faces. My parents arrived first, their expressions shifting from fear to relief when they saw me alive. Then came my sponsor, their representative already calculating the impact on my content schedule.

"The fall will affect your metrics," he said, as if that was what mattered. "We'll need to adjust your content calendar. But the footage is dramatic—should perform well on the platforms."

I stared at him, unable to speak. My body was broken, my career in jeopardy, and he was talking about content metrics?


The doctor came later, her expression serious. "You're lucky," she said. "The fall could have killed you. As it is, you have a broken collarbone, three broken ribs, and a concussion. You'll need surgery on the shoulder, and rehabilitation will take months."

"Months?" The word came out as a whisper.

"At least six," the doctor said. "Possibly longer. And there's something else." She hesitated, as if unsure how to proceed. "The impact damaged your neural interface. RISK-AI may not function properly until it's repaired."

The words struck with devastating force. RISK-AI was damaged? Without it, I couldn't climb. Without it, I couldn't calculate risk. Without it, I was nothing.


That night, I lay in my hospital bed, staring at the ceiling. The silence in my ear was deafening—no RISK-AI, no data, no guidance. Just me and my thoughts.

I tried to remember what climbing had felt like before the system. The memory was distant, faded. There had been something—some feeling of intuition, of instinct, of knowing without knowing why. But I couldn't reach it.

The woman's words from the party echoed in my mind: "Risk is supposed to be felt, not calculated."

I had dismissed her then. But now, lying in a hospital bed with a broken body and a damaged system, I wondered if she had been right.

What if the system had made me weaker, not stronger? What if optimization had cost me something essential?

I didn't know. But I was going to find out.
