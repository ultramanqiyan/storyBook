## 2. Protagonist Profile

### 2.1 Basic Information

| Attribute | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Name** | Marcus Webb |
| **Age Range** | 30-45 years old (across the series) |
| **Occupation** | Software Engineer → Senior Engineer → Tech Lead → ? |
| **Company** | Nexus Technologies (fictional Silicon Valley company) |
| **Location** | Palo Alto, California |
| **Family** | Single (Books 1-3) → Married (Books 4-8), wife Sarah, two children |

### 2.2 Personality Traits

**Early Stage (Books 1-2)**:
- Intelligent, ambitious, efficiency-driven
- Independent thinker, enjoys solving difficult problems
- Confident in his abilities

**Middle Stage (Books 3-5)**:
- Increasingly dependent on AI, but unwilling to admit
- Efficiency-focused to the point of avoiding challenges
- Growing internal conflict between success and emptiness

**Late Stage (Books 6-8)**:
- Hollow, lost, searching for meaning
- Unable to function without AI
- Existential crisis about his identity and value

### 2.3 Core Inner Conflicts

**Conflict 1: Efficiency Worship vs. Ability Anxiety**
- He believes efficiency is paramount, but deep down fears he's becoming an "AI operator"
- Every time he uses AI to solve a problem, there's a sense of "cheating" shame, but he can't stop
- "I'm 10x more productive, but am I still a real engineer?"

**Conflict 2: External Success vs. Internal Emptiness**
- LinkedIn profile is impressive, but he knows he hasn't independently solved a hard problem in a long time
- Promotions and raises, but feels like he's just "AI's translator"
- "Everyone thinks I'm brilliant. They don't know I'm just good at prompting."

**Conflict 3: Knowing vs. Admitting**
- He clearly knows he's degenerating, but rationalizes it with "efficiency" and "industry trends"
- Late-night insomnia: "If AI disappeared tomorrow, could I still do my job?"
- Daytime: continues using AI because "everyone else is doing it"

**Conflict 4: Dependency vs. Resistance**
- He hates his dependency on AI, but every "detox" attempt fails
- "I'll do this one without AI" → opens AI 5 minutes later → shame → cycle
- Occasionally leaves deliberate "human traces" in code to prove he still exists

**Conflict 5: Belonging vs. Loneliness**
- At work, he's the "AI tools expert," respected by colleagues
- But he knows his colleagues are also quietly degenerating; everyone pretends
- "We're all pretending we're still in control. Who are we fooling?"

### 2.4 Silicon Valley Programmer Psychology

**Imposter Syndrome 2.0**:
- Traditional: worry about not being good enough
- Now: worry about being just AI's "interface"

**Efficiency Anxiety**:
- "If I'm not using AI, I'm falling behind"
- "Everyone else is shipping faster. Am I being left behind?"

**Identity Crisis**:
- "What is a software engineer in the AI era?"
- "Am I still a creator, or just a curator?"

**Existential Anxiety**:
- "What happens to me when AI can do everything I do?"
- "What value do I bring that AI can't?"

**Social Pressure**:
- Sees "AI dependency" discussions on Hacker News, wants to participate but fears exposure
- In team meetings, afraid to admit complete AI dependency
- "I should probably learn this myself... but I have a deadline"

### 2.5 Key Psychological Scenes

**Scene 1: Late Night Code Review**
- Seeing code he wrote 3 years ago, shocked by its complexity
- "I wrote this? Without AI?"
- Shame, nostalgia, fear intertwined

**Scene 2: Interviewing New Employees**
- Discovers candidate's AI dependency is worse than his own
- Internally mocks, while realizing he's in the same boat
- "I should warn them... but then I'd have to admit my own dependency"

**Scene 3: Child Asks "What Does Daddy Do?"**
- "I write software... well, I help AI write software..."
- Unable to explain his work to his children
- "What will I tell them when they're older?"

**Scene 4: The Day AI Failed**
- AI service goes down, he finds himself unable to complete tasks independently
- Pretends to be "thinking about architecture," actually waiting for AI to recover
- "This is what I've become."

### 2.6 Speaking Style Evolution

**Early Stage**:
- Concise, technical, confident
- "Here's my approach..."
- "Let me think about this..."

**Middle Stage**:
- Hesitant, frequently references AI suggestions
- "Let me check with CodeForge..."
- "What does ThinkTank say?"
- "According to the AI analysis..."

**Late Stage**:
- Hollow, lacking original opinions
- "I'll need to consult the system..."
- "The data suggests..."
- "I used to be able to..."

### 2.7 Inner Monologue Style Evolution

**Early Stage**:
- Analyzing problems, designing solutions
- "If I approach this from the architecture level..."
- "The key constraint here is..."

**Middle Stage**:
- Doubting himself, seeking AI confirmation
- "I think the answer is X, but let me verify..."
- "Am I sure about this? What if I'm wrong?"

**Late Stage**:
- Confused, searching for self
- "What do I actually think?"
- "Is this my thought or AI's?"
- "Who am I without AI?"

## 4. Competitive Pressure Elements

### 4.1 Company Culture

- "AI Usage Leaderboard" - engineers who use AI most are considered "most efficient"
- "If you're not using AI, you're falling behind" becomes company mantra
- Engineers who refuse AI are marginalized or laid off
- "AI-first strategy" - all decisions must consider AI optimization

### 4.2 Industry Pressure

- Competitors release "revolutionary" AI-powered products
- Technical interviews become absurd: candidates use AI to answer, interviewers use AI to evaluate
- "Technical ability" definition shifts from "can write code" to "can use AI to write code"
- Companies compete on who uses AI more aggressively

### 4.3 Social Pressure

- Hacker News discussions about "AI dependency"
- LinkedIn posts celebrating "AI-powered productivity"
- Team meetings where everyone pretends to be in control
- Unspoken agreement: don't ask how someone solved a problem

## 6. Quality Standards

### 6.1 AI Writing Patterns (STRICTLY FORBIDDEN)

| Pattern | Example | Replacement Strategy |
|---------|---------|---------------------|
| Vague location endings | "And somewhere..." | Specific location tied to character |
| Omniscient foreshadowing | "Neither of them knew..." | Character's intuition only |
| Cliché transitions | "will never be the same" | Specific change through behavior |
| False beginnings | "just the beginning" | Concrete continuation |
| Rule of Three | "It was X, it was Y, it was Z" | Varied structure with specific details |
| Abstract emotions | "He felt sad" | Physical manifestation + internal thought |
| Template dialogue | "What do you mean?" | Subtext-rich alternatives |
| Repetitive structures | Similar sentence openings | Varied rhythm and structure |

### 6.2 Sensory Detail Requirements

- **Minimum per chapter**: 5 sensory details
- **Minimum categories per chapter**: 3
- **Distribution target**: Visual (40%), Others (60% combined)

### 6.3 Emotional Depth Design (Three-Layer Model)

Every significant emotional moment must include:
- **Layer 1**: Surface Reaction (external behavior)
- **Layer 2**: Internal Activity (thought process)
- **Layer 3**: Deep Motivation (underlying fears, desires, beliefs)
