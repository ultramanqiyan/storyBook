# Chapter 02: The First Meeting
The air carried the faint scent of coffee, lingering in the background.

The next morning, Mark arrived at the office early.

He hadn't slept well. His mind had replayed the previous day's events on a loop—the number on the screen, the questions in the conference room, David's pen moving across the page.

*Eighty-seven million dollars.*

The number had taken on a life of its own in his mind. It wasn't just money anymore. It was a weight. A target. A question mark hanging over his future.

He sat at his desk and opened his email. Fifty-three new messages since last night.

Most were from colleagues. Careful, professional messages that said nothing and meant everything.

*Thinking of you during this difficult time.*
*Let me know if there's anything I can do.*
*We're all in this together.*

Mark knew what those messages meant. He had sent similar ones himself over the years, to colleagues who were about to disappear.

*I'm protecting myself. Don't drag me down with you.*

He closed the emails without responding. There was nothing to say.

---

At 9:00 AM, his calendar pinged.

*Meeting: Incident Follow-up*
*Location: Conference Room B*
*Time: 9:30 AM*
*Attendees: D. Morrison, S. Kim, M. Torres, J. Walsh, M. Chen*

Mark stared at the invitation. His name was last on the list. He wasn't sure if that meant anything, but he noticed it anyway.

He gathered his notes and walked to the conference room.

---

The room was already full when Mark arrived.

David sat at the head of the table, his notebook open. Sarah Kim sat in the corner, her laptop in front of her. Michael Torres from Legal sat near the front. Jennifer Walsh from HR was by the door.

Mark took a seat. The chair felt harder than usual.

"Good morning," David said. His voice was calm, professional. "Thank you all for being here. I want to continue our discussion from yesterday and make sure we have a clear understanding of the timeline."

His pen was already moving.

"Let's start with the code that caused the incident. Mark, can you walk us through your review process for this particular module?"

Mark's stomach tightened. He had prepared for this question, but hearing it out loud made it real.

"Of course," he said. "The code was generated by CodePilot on March 15th. I received the notification that morning and began my review."

"And what did your review consist of?"

"I examined the code structure, checked for potential issues, and ran it through our standard test suite."

"Did you read every line of code?"

Mark hesitated. This was the trap.

"I reviewed the key sections," he said carefully. "The code was over two thousand lines. I focused on the critical paths and the areas most likely to cause issues."

David wrote something down. "So you didn't read every line."

"I read enough to make an informed decision," Mark said. "That's the standard process. We rely on the test suite to catch edge cases."

"I see." David's voice was neutral. "And how long did this review take?"

Mark thought back. "Approximately two hours."

"Two hours for two thousand lines of code." David's pen moved. "That's about one minute per ten lines. Is that typical?"

Mark felt the room shift. The question was designed to make him look negligent.

"The review process is more than just reading," Mark said. "I'm checking for patterns, potential issues, known vulnerabilities. It's not a line-by-line audit."

"Understood." David nodded slowly. "But in hindsight, do you think a more thorough review might have caught this issue?"

*In hindsight.* The phrase hung in the air.

"I can't say for certain," Mark said. "The issue was an edge case that our test suite didn't cover. A more thorough review might have caught it, or it might not have."

David wrote something down. "Sarah, can you tell us about the testing process?"

Sarah looked up from her laptop. Her expression was carefully neutral.

"We ran the standard test suite," she said. "The code passed all tests. We didn't identify any issues during our regular testing process."

"Did you do any additional testing beyond the standard suite?"

"No," Sarah said. "We followed the standard protocol."

"And has this protocol been reviewed and approved by management?"

"Yes," Sarah said. "David approved the testing protocol last year."

David nodded. His pen moved across the page.

"So the testing protocol was approved," he said. "And the code passed all approved tests. But we still had an incident."

The room was silent.

"Michael," David continued, "from a legal perspective, what's our exposure here?"

Michael Torres leaned forward. "At this stage, we're still assessing liability. The initial focus is on understanding what happened and why. From a contractual standpoint, we need to review our agreements with clients and with SynthAI, the CodePilot vendor."

"And what do those agreements say about liability?"

Michael's expression was neutral. "The client agreements include standard limitation of liability clauses. Our exposure is capped at the value of the contract. However, there may be reputational damage that goes beyond the contractual limits."

"What about SynthAI?"

"The SynthAI contract includes an indemnification clause," Michael said. "They're protected from liability arising from the use of their AI-generated code. The responsibility for reviewing and approving the code rests with us."

Mark felt the walls closing in. The vendor was protected. The testing protocol was approved. The only person who had actually signed off on the code was him.

"Jennifer," David said, "from an HR perspective, what's the process here?"

Jennifer Walsh's voice was calm, measured. "We're conducting a thorough review of the situation. We want to make sure we understand all the facts before making any decisions. This is standard procedure for incidents of this magnitude."

*Standard procedure.* Mark had heard that phrase before. It meant *we're going to find someone to blame.*

"Thank you all," David said. "I think we have a clearer picture now. I'll be scheduling follow-up meetings with each of you individually. In the meantime, please preserve all documentation related to this project."

Mark's stomach tightened. *Preserve all documentation.* That meant *don't delete anything that could be evidence.*

---

After the meeting, Mark walked back to his desk.

His phone buzzed with an email from David.

*Subject: Follow-up*
*Mark,*
*Thanks for your participation in the meeting. Just to clarify for the record, you mentioned that your review process focuses on "key sections" and "critical paths." Can you send me a written description of your review methodology? I'd like to understand the process better.*
*Best,*
*David*

Mark stared at the email. *Just to clarify for the record.*

He was being asked to document his own defense. Every word he wrote would be used to evaluate his competence. Every omission would be used to prove negligence.

He started typing a response. Each word felt like a step on a tightrope.

---

By the end of the day, Mark had sent David a detailed description of his review methodology. He had attached process documents, training materials, and examples of past reviews.

He sat back, exhausted. His inbox was full of messages from colleagues—careful, professional messages that said nothing and meant everything.

*Thinking of you during this difficult time.*
*Let me know if there's anything I can do.*
*We're all in this together.*

Mark knew what those messages meant: *I'm protecting myself. Don't drag me down with you.*

He closed his laptop and stared at the dark screen.

Tomorrow would be another day of meetings. Another day of questions. Another day of documenting his own defense.

He had been in this industry long enough to know how this worked. Someone had to pay. The only question was who.

And the more he thought about it, the more he realized: the answer might be him.
