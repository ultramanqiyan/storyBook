# Chapter 5: The First Step

Friday morning, David stared at his email inbox.

Forty-seven unread messages. Each one a decision. Each one an opportunity to check with someone, to seek validation, to avoid the terrifying act of trusting himself.

He took a breath. Dr. Webb's words echoed in his mind: *Start small. Make one decision this week without checking with anyone.*

One decision. He could do that.

His eyes landed on a message from a junior consultant on his team: "David, should we use the standard methodology for the Henderson analysis, or adapt it based on their industry?"

The question was simple. Routine. The kind of thing David would normally forward to Jennifer, or ask Marcus about, or research for hours before responding with a hedged answer.

But today, he would try something different.

He read the question again. Henderson was in manufacturing. The standard methodology was designed for retail. Adapting it would make sense, but it would also require more work from the team.

His instinct said: adapt it. The analysis would be better. The client would get more value.

*But what if I'm wrong? What if the standard methodology is fine and I'm overcomplicating things?*

The familiar doubt crept in. His fingers hovered over the keyboard, ready to type a question to Marcus. Ready to seek the validation that had always been his safety net.

He pulled his hands back.

*One decision. That's all.*

He typed: "Adapt the methodology for manufacturing. Use the framework we developed for the Thompson account as a starting point. Let me know if you hit any roadblocks."

He read it twice. His heart pounded. His palms were sweaty.

He hit send.

Then he sat back and waited for the catastrophe.

---

Nothing happened.

The world didn't end. His inbox didn't explode with angry replies. The junior consultant responded with a simple "Thanks, David. We'll get started."

David stared at the response. That was it? No pushback? No one telling him he was wrong?

The feeling that washed over him was unfamiliar. A lightness in his chest. A quieting of the constant noise in his head.

*Maybe I can do this.*

---

The next test came an hour later.

A client email: "David, we need to reschedule next week's workshop. Can you do Wednesday instead of Tuesday?"

David's old instinct was to check the team calendar, confirm with everyone, make sure the new date worked for all stakeholders. But the question was straightforward. Wednesday worked. He knew it worked.

He typed: "Wednesday works. I'll send a revised calendar invite."

Send.

Again, nothing catastrophic. The client replied: "Perfect. Thanks for the quick response."

David felt something shift inside him. A tiny crack in the wall of fear that had surrounded him for years.

---

By afternoon, he'd made five decisions without checking with anyone.

Each one felt like a small rebellion. Each one brought a flicker of the lightness he'd felt that morning. And each one had gone fine. Not perfectly—there were follow-up questions, clarifications needed—but fine. Normal. The way work was supposed to be.

His phone buzzed. A text from Rachel: "Coffee tomorrow? Same place?"

He smiled. He hadn't told her about his experiment, but somehow she'd known to check in.

---

The Meridian call was rescheduled for Monday.

David spent the weekend preparing, but differently than before. Instead of rehearsing every possible objection, instead of trying to anticipate every question, he focused on what he actually believed.

The operations-first approach made sense. The data supported it. Jennifer might have mentioned finance, but that was a month ago, before the new analysis was complete. The situation had changed. The recommendation should change too.

He practiced saying it out loud in his apartment: "I believe operations should come first because..."

The words felt strange in his mouth. Not because they were wrong, but because he was claiming them. Owning them. Not hedging or qualifying or waiting for someone else's approval.

*I believe.*

When had he last said those words about anything work-related?

---

Monday morning, David walked into the conference room with a different feeling in his chest.

Not confidence—not yet. But something closer to readiness. A willingness to stand behind his own judgment, even if it was wrong.

The video call connected. Patricia's face appeared, along with the rest of the Meridian team.

"David," Patricia said. "Let's try this again. What are you recommending?"

David took a breath. His heart was still pounding. His palms were still sweaty. But this time, he didn't let the fear stop him.

"I'm recommending we start with operations," he said. "I know Jennifer mentioned finance last month, but the new analysis shows operations will have the fastest impact on customer experience. The data is clear."

"And if we disagree?" one of the executives asked.

David felt the old impulse to back down, to hedge, to say "let me check." He pushed through it.

"Then we can discuss it. But I believe in this recommendation. I've done the analysis. I'm prepared to defend it."

The room was quiet. David's heart hammered in his chest.

Patricia leaned forward. "Finally," she said. "That's what we've been waiting for."

David blinked. "What?"

"A real recommendation. Not 'let me check with my team.' Not 'I'll get back to you.' An actual position." She almost smiled. "We can work with this."

The conversation that followed was challenging. The executives pushed back on timelines, questioned assumptions, asked for more detail. But David held his ground where he believed in his position, and conceded where he saw their point. He was present in the meeting in a way he'd never been before—not as a passive conduit for other people's decisions, but as a consultant with his own judgment.

By the end of the call, they had a plan. Not a perfect plan, but a real one. And Patricia's final words stayed with him: "Good work, David. Keep this up."

---

Tuesday morning, David met Rachel at the coffee shop.

"I made decisions," he said, before she'd even sat down. "Real ones. Without checking with anyone."

Rachel smiled. "How did it feel?"

"Terrifying. And then... liberating." He shook his head. "I didn't realize how much energy I was spending on second-guessing until I stopped."

"That's the thing about self-trust," Rachel said. "It's not about being right all the time. It's about being present in your own life. Making choices instead of waiting for permission."

David nodded slowly. "The client meeting went well. They actually listened to me. Engaged with my ideas. It was like... they'd been waiting for me to show up."

"They probably were." Rachel sipped her coffee. "Clients hire consultants for judgment, not just analysis. When you don't trust yourself, you're not giving them what they paid for."

The observation hit David hard. All this time, he'd thought he was being careful. Thorough. Responsible. But really, he'd been withholding. Holding back the very thing that made him valuable.

"I have a long way to go," he said. "I still feel the fear. Still want to check."

"That's normal. Self-trust isn't a switch you flip. It's a muscle you build." Rachel met his eyes. "But you've taken the first step. That's the hardest part."

David looked out the window at the rainy Chicago street. The same view he'd seen a week ago, when he'd felt completely lost. But today, the city looked different. Not threatening. Full of possibility.

"Thank you," he said. "For seeing me. For saying something."

Rachel smiled. "Someone did it for me once. Now it's your turn to pay it forward."

She gathered her things. "Keep going. One decision at a time."

David watched her leave, then turned back to his coffee. The warmth spread through him—not just from the cup, but from something deeper.

He had a long way to go. But for the first time, he believed he could get there.

One decision at a time.
