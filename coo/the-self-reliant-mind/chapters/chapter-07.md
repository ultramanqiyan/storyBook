# Chapter 7: The Practice

Three weeks after the Pella crisis, David sat in his Monday morning meeting and realized something had changed.

The partner leading the discussion asked for recommendations on a client's market expansion strategy. David's hand went up before he'd even thought about it.

"I'd suggest we focus on the Midwest region first," he said. "The data shows stronger brand recognition there, and the logistics infrastructure is already in place. We can expand to the coasts in phase two."

The partner nodded. "Makes sense. David, lead the analysis."

Just like that. No checking in. No second-guessing. No desperate search for validation. Just a recommendation, made confidently, accepted on its merits.

The practice had started small.

After the Pella success, David had made a commitment to himself: every day, at least one decision made without seeking validation. Not reckless decisions窶破ust normal, everyday choices that he would have previously deferred to others.

Some days it was easy. An email sent without review. A timeline proposed without checking. A client question answered directly instead of with "let me get back to you."

Other days it was harder. A decision with real consequences. A recommendation that could be wrong. A moment when the old fear crept back, whispering that he wasn't qualified, that he'd be exposed, that he should check first.

But he'd learned to push through. To recognize the fear as an old habit, not a warning. To trust the judgment he'd built over years of training and experience.

And slowly, the practice had become a pattern. The pattern had become a habit. The habit had become... him.

*This is what Dr. Webb talked about. The fear of being wrong. But being wrong isn't the end. It's part of the process.*

He picked up the phone and called the client.

"I've reviewed the data, and the strategy isn't performing as expected. I take responsibility for that recommendation. Here's what I think we should do next..."

The conversation was difficult. The client was frustrated. But David didn't retreat into defensiveness or blame. He owned the decision, acknowledged the results, and proposed adjustments.

By the end of the call, they had a new plan. Not a perfect plan, but a path forward.

And David walked away with something more valuable than a perfect track record: the knowledge that he could handle being wrong. That a mistake wasn't a catastrophe. That self-reliance meant owning your failures as well as your successes.


The junior consultant found him in the coffee room the next day.

"David? Do you have a minute?"

"Sure. What's up?"

She stopped, looking embarrassed. "Sorry. I know you're busy."

David closed his laptop. "No, I understand. I used to be exactly where you are."

Amanda's eyes widened. "Really? But you're so... confident. You always know what to say in meetings."

"I didn't used to be." David leaned back in his chair. "A few months ago, I couldn't send an email without checking with three people first. I was paralyzed by the fear of being wrong."

"What changed?"

David thought about the question. "I learned that self-trust isn't about being right all the time. It's about being willing to stand behind your own judgment. Even when you might be wrong."

"But how do you know when to trust yourself?"

"You don't know. You choose." David met her eyes. "And then you learn from what happens. Every decision is practice. The good ones and the bad ones."

Amanda was quiet for a moment. "That sounds terrifying."

"It is. At first." David smiled slightly. "But then it becomes liberating. Because you realize that you don't need permission to have an opinion. You don't need validation to be a professional. You just need to be willing to own your choices."

"Even the wrong ones?"

"Especially the wrong ones. That's how you learn." He paused. "Try this: make one decision today without checking with anyone. Something small. See how it feels."

Amanda nodded slowly. "One decision?"

"One decision. That's all."

She left looking slightly less anxious. David watched her go, feeling a strange sense of recognition. He'd been her. And now, somehow, he was the one giving advice.


That evening, David met Rachel at their usual coffee shop.

"You're different," she said, after they'd ordered. "More settled."

David considered this. "I think I'm getting used to it. The self-reliance thing. It's becoming... normal."

"That's how it happens. Not a dramatic transformation. Just practice, day after day, until it becomes who you are."

"I helped a junior consultant today. She was struggling with the same things I used to struggle with."

Rachel smiled. "That's the next phase. Once you've learned something, you teach it. That's how it spreads."

David nodded slowly. "I didn't expect it to feel this way. Like I'm part of something bigger. Not just fixing myself, but... passing it on."

"That's the thing about self-reliance," Rachel said. "It sounds like it's about being alone. But really, it's about being strong enough to help others. To stand on your own feet so you can lend a hand to someone else."

The observation resonated with David. He'd spent so long focused on his own struggle that he hadn't seen the bigger picture. Self-reliance wasn't isolation. It was the foundation for connection.


Walking home that night, David thought about how far he'd come.

Three months ago, he'd been paralyzed by Jennifer's absence. Unable to make a decision without validation. Terrified of being exposed as a fraud.

Now, he made decisions every day without checking. He'd handled a major client crisis alone. He'd owned a mistake and moved forward. He'd helped someone else on the same path.

The fear wasn't gone. It probably never would be completely. But it was quieter now. Manageable. A background noise instead of a deafening roar.

He pulled out his phone and texted Dr. Webb: "Practice is working. It's becoming habit."

The response came quickly: "That's the goal. Not perfection. Progress. Keep going."

David smiled and put his phone away. The city lights glittered around him, full of possibility.

He was still learning. Still growing. Still practicing.

But for the first time, he trusted that he would get there.

One decision at a time.
