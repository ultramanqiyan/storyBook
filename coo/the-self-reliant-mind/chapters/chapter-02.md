# Chapter 2: The House of Cards

The email arrived at 7:47 AM, before David had even poured his first coffee.

From: HR Department
Subject: Jennifer Walsh - Leave of Absence

"Team, Jennifer has taken an unexpected personal leave of absence, effective immediately. She will be out for the next several weeks. During her absence, David Chen will serve as acting lead on her client engagements. Please direct all questions to David or reach out to HR for urgent matters."

David read the message three times, his coffee forgotten in his hand.

Jennifer was out. Indefinitely. And he was covering her clients.

The panic started in his chest and spread outward. He'd worked with Jennifer for two years. She'd been his safety net, his validator, the person who told him whether his work was good enough. Without her, he was... what?

Exposed.

His phone buzzed. A text from Marcus: "Congrats on the acting lead role. You got this."

David stared at the message. *Got this?* He didn't have anything. He had a list of clients he didn't know how to manage alone and a terrifying absence where his safety net used to be.

He walked past Jennifer's office on the way to his desk. Her door was closed, the lights off. Her calendar, usually packed with back-to-back meetings, showed empty space for the next three weeks. The sight of it made his stomach clench.

*What am I supposed to do?*

---

By noon, David had forty-three unread emails.

Client requests. Team questions. Project updates. Each one felt like a demand he couldn't meet. He'd opened the first few, started drafting responses, then stopped. What if he said the wrong thing? What if Jennifer would have handled it differently? What if he made a decision that turned out to be wrong?

His phone rang. The caller ID showed one of Jennifer's key clients.

"David Chen," he answered, his voice steadier than he felt.

"David, it's Patricia from Meridian. We need to move forward on the organizational restructuring recommendations. Jennifer was supposed to walk us through the implementation plan this week. Can you handle that?"

The implementation plan. David knew the plan—he'd helped build it. But presenting it alone? Making recommendations without Jennifer's approval?

"I can definitely help with that," he said, his mouth dry. "Let me... let me pull together the materials and get back to you."

"When can we expect the call?"

David's mind raced. He needed time to prepare. Time to review. Time to... what? Check with someone who wasn't there?

"Tomorrow afternoon?" he suggested.

"Tomorrow works. Two o'clock. We'll need concrete recommendations, David. Not more analysis. We're ready to move."

The line went dead. David stared at his phone.

*Concrete recommendations. Without Jennifer.*

---

The rest of the day was a blur of hesitation and half-starts.

A team member asked about project priorities. David said he'd "check on that" and never got back to her.

A client emailed with a question about methodology. David drafted three different responses and deleted all of them, unable to commit to an answer.

His inbox grew. His to-do list expanded. His chest tightened.

By late afternoon, he'd accomplished almost nothing. Every decision felt like a minefield. Every choice carried the weight of potential failure. Without Jennifer's validation, he couldn't tell good from bad, right from wrong.

Marcus stopped by his desk again. "How's it going? You look like you haven't moved all day."

"I'm fine," David said automatically. "Just... a lot to catch up on."

"Need any help?"

The offer was genuine. David could see it in Marcus's face. But asking for help felt like admitting failure. Like proving he couldn't handle the responsibility.

"I've got it," he said. "Thanks."

Marcus hesitated, then walked away. David watched him go, feeling the familiar isolation of his own making.


The Meridian call the next day was worse than David had imagined.

He'd prepared. He'd reviewed the implementation plan six times. He'd written out talking points and rehearsed them in front of his bathroom mirror. But when the video conference started and six faces appeared on his screen, his preparation evaporated.

"David, thanks for joining," Patricia said. "We're eager to hear your recommendations on the restructuring timeline."

David cleared his throat. "Yes, of course. So, based on our analysis, we're recommending a phased approach..."

He walked through the plan, just as he'd practiced. But with every sentence, he felt the weight of uncertainty pressing down. Was this right? Would Jennifer have said this differently? What if he was missing something?

"So you're recommending we start with operations?" one of the other executives asked.

David hesitated. "Yes, that's... that's what the analysis suggests."

"But what about the finance team? They're equally affected."

The question threw him. The finance team wasn't in the original plan. Had he missed something? Should he have anticipated this?

"Let me... let me look into that and get back to you," he said.

Patricia's expression shifted. "David, we were hoping for answers today. Jennifer always came to these meetings prepared to make recommendations."

"I understand, but..."

"We've been working with your firm for two years. We pay premium rates for premium advice. Right now, I'm not feeling the premium."

The call ended with vague promises to follow up. David sat alone in the conference room, the silence deafening.

*They're right. I'm not premium. I'm not even adequate. I'm just... lost.*


That night, David stayed late.

The office was empty. Most people had left hours ago, their work done, their decisions made. David sat at his desk, surrounded by papers and open browser tabs, trying to figure out where everything had gone wrong.

He'd done the work. He knew the material. He'd been on these projects for months. But without Jennifer's approval, he couldn't trust his own judgment.

His phone buzzed. His sister again: "How's the new responsibility?"

David typed: "I'm drowning."

Then he deleted it. He didn't want to admit it. Didn't want to say out loud that he was failing.

He typed instead: "Challenging. Learning a lot."

The lie felt bitter in his mouth.

He opened his email and stared at the growing list of unread messages. Each one was a decision he couldn't make. A question he couldn't answer. A responsibility he couldn't shoulder.

*How did I get here?*

He'd always been a high performer. Top of his class in college. Fast promotions in his early career. Praise from clients and colleagues alike. But somewhere along the way, he'd built his confidence on a foundation of other people's approval. And now that foundation was gone.

The house of cards he'd been living in had collapsed. And he was standing in the wreckage, not knowing how to rebuild.

He closed his laptop and leaned back in his chair. The office lights hummed overhead. Outside the window, the city glittered with a million lives that seemed to know what they were doing.

*What's wrong with me? Why can't I trust myself?*

The question hung in the air, unanswered. But somewhere in the asking, David felt a shift. Not a solution—nothing so simple. But a recognition. A crack in the wall of his denial.

He couldn't keep living like this. Something had to change.

He just didn't know what.
