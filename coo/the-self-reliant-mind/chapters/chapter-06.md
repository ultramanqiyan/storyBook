# Chapter 6: The Hard Test

The call came at 4:47 PM on a Friday.

David was packing up for the weekend, his mind already shifting toward the evening ahead—a dinner with friends, maybe a movie, the first real relaxation he'd allowed himself in weeks. His progress with self-reliance had been steady. Small decisions, growing confidence. The Meridian account was back on track. Dr. Webb had noted improvement in their last session.

Then his phone rang.

The caller ID showed a number he didn't recognize, but the area code was familiar—one of his manufacturing clients in Wisconsin.

"David Chen."

"David, it's Karen from Pella Manufacturing." The voice was tight, urgent. "We have a situation. The line supervisor just walked out. Half the production team is threatening to follow. We need to implement the organizational changes now—tonight—or we're going to lose the entire second shift."

David's stomach dropped. The Pella account was one of Jennifer's key clients, one he'd inherited when she left. The organizational changes had been planned for next quarter—a careful, phased approach that he'd helped design.

"Karen, the timeline we discussed—"

"I know what we discussed. But that was before this." Her voice cracked slightly. "The supervisor was the linchpin. Without him, the whole team is ready to walk. If we don't act tonight, we'll have a production shutdown by morning. That's millions of dollars. Jobs. Everything."

David felt the familiar surge of panic. This was beyond anything he'd handled alone. A decision this big, this urgent, with this much at stake—

"I need to consult with—"

"There's no one to consult with, David. Jennifer's gone. Your firm's other partners are in meetings or traveling. I've tried." Karen's voice was desperate now. "You're it. You're the consultant on this account. We need an answer now. Not tomorrow. Not after you check with your team. Now."

The silence on the line was deafening. David could hear his own heartbeat, feel the cold sweat breaking out on his palms.

*This is it. The test. Do I trust myself or not?*

---

He put Karen on hold and stood up, pacing his empty office.

The old patterns screamed at him. Find someone. Anyone. Email the partners. Call Dr. Webb. Text Rachel. Get validation. Get permission. Get someone else to make this call.

But he knew, even as the thoughts raced through his mind, that there wasn't time. Karen was right. By the time he tracked down a partner, the second shift would be gone. The damage would be done.

He thought about the plan he'd helped design. The phased approach. The careful timeline. But that plan had assumed stability, assumed the current team would stay in place through the transition.

The situation had changed. The plan needed to change with it.

*What do I actually believe is the right thing to do?*

He closed his eyes and forced himself to think clearly. The supervisor had been the linchpin—Karen's word. That meant the team trusted him, followed him. If he was gone, the team would follow whoever stepped into his role. But there was no one ready to step in. No one the team knew.

Unless...

The organizational plan called for a team lead structure—promoting from within, creating distributed leadership instead of a single supervisor. It was supposed to happen in phase three. But what if they accelerated it? What if they implemented the team lead structure tonight, with the most respected workers taking immediate leadership roles?

It was risky. The workers weren't trained for it. The structure wasn't fully designed. A hundred things could go wrong.

But the alternative—doing nothing—was guaranteed failure.

*Do I trust myself?*

The question hung in the air. David thought about the small decisions he'd made over the past week. The emails he'd sent without checking. The client calls where he'd held his ground. The growing feeling that he was capable of more than he'd believed.

This was bigger. Scarier. More visible.

But it was the same principle. Trust his judgment. Make a decision. Stand behind it.

He picked up the phone.

---

"Karen, I'm going to recommend we accelerate the team lead structure."

"Tonight?"

"Tonight. We identify the most respected workers on each shift—people the team already trusts. We promote them immediately, give them authority to make decisions, and commit to training and supporting them going forward."

Karen was silent for a moment. "That's not in the plan."

"No. The plan assumed we'd have time to prepare. The situation has changed." David felt his voice steadying as he spoke. "But the principle is sound. Distributed leadership is what we were moving toward anyway. We're just doing it faster than we planned."

"What if it doesn't work? What if the team rejects it?"

"Then we'll adjust. But I believe this gives us the best chance of keeping the team together tonight. And if we do nothing, we lose them for sure."

He heard Karen take a breath on the other end. "You're sure about this?"

The old David would have hedged. Would have said "I think" or "It's my recommendation" or "We should consider." The new David—the one he was still becoming—chose differently.

"Yes. I'm sure. I've worked with your team for six months. I know their dynamics. I believe this will work."

Another pause. Then: "Okay. Send me the details. We'll implement tonight."

---

The next four hours were a blur.

David worked from his office, his dinner forgotten, creating the accelerated implementation plan. He identified the potential team leads based on his observations from site visits. He drafted the communication for the workers. He created a support structure for the new leads—check-ins, resources, escalation paths.

By 9 PM, he was on a video call with Karen and the remaining supervisors, walking through the plan.

"The key is authenticity," he told them. "Don't pretend this is how we planned it. Tell the team the truth—the supervisor left, we need leadership now, and we're trusting them to step up. People respond to honesty."

At 10 PM, the announcement was made to the second shift.

At 10:30, David got a text from Karen: "They're staying. The new leads are stepping up. The shift is running."

At midnight, another text: "First shift is briefed. They're on board. Production is continuing."

David sat back in his chair, exhausted, and let out a breath he felt like he'd been holding for weeks.

It had worked. Not perfectly—there would be adjustments needed, problems to solve, training to do. But the immediate crisis was averted. The team was intact. Production was running.

And he'd done it alone. Not because no one else was available, but because he'd chosen to trust himself.

---

Saturday morning, David slept in for the first time in months.

When he finally checked his phone, there were messages waiting. A voicemail from one of the partners: "David, heard about Pella. Good work. We'll discuss on Monday."

An email from Karen: "David, thank you. I know this wasn't in the plan, but you made the right call. The team is actually more engaged than they were before. We might have stumbled onto something better than the original approach."

And a text from Rachel: "Heard you had a big night. How are you feeling?"

David smiled. He typed back: "Like I passed a test I didn't know I was taking."

Then he sat back and let the feeling wash over him. Not pride, exactly. Something quieter. A deep sense of rightness. The recognition that he was capable of more than he'd believed.

He'd trusted himself in a hard moment. And it had worked.

Not because he was perfect. Not because he had all the answers. But because he'd been willing to stand behind his own judgment.

That was self-reliance. Not the absence of fear, but the choice to act anyway.

And it felt like freedom.
