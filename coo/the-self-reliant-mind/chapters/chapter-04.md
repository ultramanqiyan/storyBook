# Chapter 4: The Resistance

Thursday morning, David walked into the Meridian meeting with his recommendations printed and ready.

He'd practiced the presentation in his apartment the night before, standing in front of his bathroom mirror, watching his own face for signs of doubt. The recommendations were solid. He knew they were solid. He'd done the analysis, reviewed the data, considered the alternatives.

But as he sat in the conference room, waiting for the video call to connect, the old feelings crept back.

*What if I'm wrong? What if they hate it? What if Jennifer would have done it differently?*

His phone sat on the table in front of him, face up, notifications visible. Every few seconds, his eyes drifted to the screen, looking for... what? Permission? Validation? A safety net that wasn't there?

The call connected. Six faces appeared on the screen.

"David, good to see you," Patricia said. "Let's get right to it. What are you recommending?"

"Based on our analysis," he began, "we're recommending a phased approach. Starting with operations, then expanding to finance in phase two, and finally addressing the technology teams in phase three."

"Why operations first?" one of the executives asked.

David's heart rate spiked. This was the question. The one he'd anticipated. The one he had an answer for.

"Operations has the highest impact on customer experience," he said. "The data shows that improving operations efficiency will have the fastest ROI and create momentum for the broader changes."

"That's different from what Jennifer mentioned last month," Patricia said. "She was leaning toward starting with finance."

The comment hit David like a physical blow. Jennifer had said something different? He hadn't known that. Had he missed something? Was his entire recommendation wrong?

"I... I wasn't aware of that conversation," he said, his voice faltering. "Let me... let me reconsider and get back to you."

Patricia's expression shifted. "David, we need answers today. We can't keep pushing this back."

"With what? Jennifer isn't here." Patricia's voice was sharp. "You're the consultant. Give us a recommendation."

"David." Patricia's voice cut through. "Do you believe in your recommendation or not?"

The question hung in the air. Did he? He'd spent hours preparing it. He'd reviewed the data. He'd thought it through. But now, faced with the possibility that Jennifer might have disagreed, he couldn't trust himself.

"I need to verify some things," he said finally. "Can we reconvene tomorrow?"

The call ended with frustrated sighs and vague agreements. David sat alone in the conference room, his printed recommendations spread across the table in front of him.

He'd failed. Again.

---

That afternoon, HR sent an email: "Given the challenges with the Meridian account, we're assigning an executive coach to support David during Jennifer's absence. Please contact Dr. Marcus Webb to schedule a session."

David stared at the message. An executive coach. Because he couldn't handle the job on his own.

The shame burned hot in his chest. He was thirty-two years old. A senior consultant. And he needed a coach to tell him how to do his job.

But he scheduled the appointment anyway. Because what choice did he have?

---

"So. HR tells me you're struggling with some decisions."

The understatement made David wince. "I'm... having trouble operating without my manager. She's out indefinitely, and I'm covering her clients. I keep second-guessing myself."

"Second-guessing." Dr. Webb nodded. "What does that look like?"

"I prepare recommendations, but when it's time to present them, I freeze. I can't commit. I keep looking for someone to tell me I'm doing it right."

"And when there's no one to tell you?"

David's throat tightened. "I fall apart."

Dr. Webb was quiet for a moment. "What are you afraid will happen if you make a decision and it's wrong?"

The question was simple, but it cut to the core. David had been asking himself the same thing for days.

"Everyone will know I'm not as good as they think I am," he said quietly. "They'll see I'm a fraud."

"And then what?"

"What do you mean?"

"After they see you're a fraud. What happens next?"

David thought back. He'd made mistakes. Missed deadlines. Given wrong information. Each time, he'd been terrified of the consequences. Each time, the consequences had been... mild. A conversation. A correction. A learning moment.

"Nothing catastrophic," he admitted. "I got feedback. I fixed it. We moved on."

The words landed like a weight. David felt tears prick at his eyes, which surprised him. He hadn't cried in years.

"I've spent my whole career trying to be perfect," he said. "Trying to never make a mistake. Never show weakness. And now I can't function without someone telling me I'm okay."

"That's the trap of perfectionism," Dr. Webb said. "It sounds like a high standard. But really, it's a prison. You can't be perfect, so you can't trust yourself. And you can't trust yourself, so you need someone else to validate you. And when that person is gone..."

"You're lost." David finished.

"Exactly." Dr. Webb leaned forward. "Here's what I want you to consider: What if the goal isn't to be perfect? What if the goal is to be real? To make decisions, some of which will be wrong, and to learn from them? What would that look like?"

David stared at the floor. The idea was terrifying. And also... liberating.

"I don't know if I can do that," he said.

---

That night, David sat in his apartment, thinking about the conversation.

One decision. Without checking. Without validation.

His phone sat on the coffee table, silent. No new messages. No one to tell him what to do.

He thought about Rachel, the independent consultant he'd met. She'd said self-reliance wasn't about being alone. It was about being the authority on your own choices.

He thought about Dr. Webb's question: What if the goal is to be real?

He thought about Meridian, and Patricia's frustrated face, and the recommendation he still believed in.

Tomorrow, he would try again. Not because he was sure he was right. But because he was tired of being sure he was wrong.

One decision. That's all.

He could do that.

Couldn't he?
