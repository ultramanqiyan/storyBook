# Chapter 1: The Validation Trap

The client nodded along as David presented the quarterly analysis, but David wasn't watching the client. He was watching Jennifer.

His manager sat at the far end of the conference table, her expression unreadable. Was that approval? Concern? Boredom? David couldn't tell. He'd been working with Jennifer for two years, and he still couldn't read her face.

"The efficiency metrics show a twelve percent improvement over last quarter," he continued, his voice steady despite the knot in his stomach. "We're recommending a phased approach to the organizational changes, starting with the operations team."

The client's VP of Operations leaned forward. "What's the timeline on that?"

David opened his mouth to answer, then hesitated. He knew the timeline窶派e'd built it himself. But what if Jennifer disagreed? What if he was about to say something that would make him look incompetent?

"Six to eight weeks for the first phase," Jennifer said smoothly, saving him from having to speak. "David can walk you through the detailed breakdown."

He could. He had the breakdown memorized. But now he was flustered, caught between his own knowledge and his fear of being wrong. He stumbled through the explanation, checking Jennifer's face after every point. She gave nothing away.

When the meeting ended, the client seemed satisfied. They shook hands, agreed on next steps, and filed out of the conference room. David gathered his materials slowly, waiting for Jennifer to speak first.

"That went well," she said, already checking her phone.

David's chest loosened slightly. "Good. I wasn't sure about the operations recommendation. Do you think窶?

"David." Jennifer looked up, her expression somewhere between tired and impatient. "You've been doing this for three years. You don't need me to approve every recommendation you make."

"You wanted me to tell you it was okay." She stood up, tucking her phone into her bag. "It was okay. It was more than okay. You're good at this. When are you going to start believing it?"

She left before he could respond. David stood alone in the conference room, the silence pressing in on him.

*Fine. She said it was fine. But what does fine mean?*

Back at his desk, David pulled up the email he'd been drafting before the meeting. A simple request to the client's operations director窶把onfirming the timeline, asking about availability for follow-up calls. He'd written it three times already, each version slightly different.

Should he send it now or wait until tomorrow morning? Morning would be better for response rates, but what if something changed overnight? What if Jennifer wanted to review it first?

He typed a quick message to Jennifer: "Should I send the follow-up email now or wait until morning?"

Then he waited.

His inbox pinged with new messages. A status update from the project team. A calendar invitation for next week's review. A question from a junior consultant about methodology. He answered each one quickly, efficiently, the way he'd been trained. But his eyes kept drifting to the chat window, waiting for Jennifer's response.

Twenty minutes passed. Then thirty.

David read the email again. It was fine. He knew it was fine. But without Jennifer's confirmation, sending it felt like stepping off a cliff.

*What if I'm wrong? What if there's something I missed? What if she wanted to add something?*

His phone buzzed. Jennifer: "Your call. You're the lead."

Your call. The words should have been empowering. Instead, they felt like a trap. If he sent it and it was wrong, it was his fault. If he waited and it was late, that was his fault too. Either way, he was exposed.

He sent the email. Then he immediately opened his sent folder to read it again, searching for errors he might have missed.

---

The pattern had started years ago, though David hadn't recognized it as a pattern at the time.

In college, he'd emailed professors after every assignment, asking if he was on the right track. In his first job, he'd checked in with his manager daily, sometimes multiple times a day. When he'd joined the consulting firm, he'd assumed the constant feedback was normal窶蚤fter all, the culture was built on performance reviews, project evaluations, and client feedback loops.

But somewhere along the way, the feedback had become a crutch. He couldn't make a decision without checking. Couldn't send an email without reviewing. Couldn't trust his own judgment without someone else's validation.

His colleague Marcus stopped by his desk that afternoon. "Hey, are you coming to the team lunch?"

David looked up from his screen. "I don't know. Is everyone going?"

"Most people. Why?"

"I just don't want to miss anything. Is Jennifer going?"

Marcus raised an eyebrow. "Does it matter? You're allowed to eat lunch, David."

"I know. I just..." He trailed off. What was he supposed to say? That he couldn't decide whether to go to lunch without knowing if his manager would approve? That he was afraid of being seen as not working hard enough?

"I'll catch up with you," he said finally.

Marcus shrugged and walked away. David watched him go, feeling the familiar weight of his own hesitation.

*What's wrong with me?*


That evening, David sat in his apartment, the glow of his laptop the only light in the room. His dinner sat untouched on the counter窶杯akeout that had gone cold an hour ago.

He was supposed to be reviewing a proposal for next week's client meeting. Instead, he was scrolling through old emails, looking for feedback that might tell him whether he was doing a good job.

*Jennifer said the presentation was "more than okay." But what does that mean? Is that good? Is that great? Is that just her being polite?*

He found a performance review from six months ago. "David demonstrates strong analytical skills and client management abilities. Areas for development: increased independence in decision-making, reduced need for supervision."

*Reduced need for supervision.*

The words stung, even though he'd read them before. He knew he was too dependent. He knew his constant checking-in frustrated Jennifer and his colleagues. He knew, somewhere deep down, that he was capable of more than he was showing.

But knowing didn't change anything. Every time he tried to make a decision on his own, the fear crept in. What if he was wrong? What if he missed something? What if everyone found out he wasn't as competent as they thought?

His phone buzzed. A text from his sister: "How was your day?"

He typed back: "Fine. Same as always."

Then he deleted it. "Fine" wasn't true. Nothing about his day had felt fine. He'd spent eight hours second-guessing himself, seeking approval, and feeling like a fraud.

He typed a new response: "Exhausting. I don't know how to stop caring what everyone thinks."

His sister's reply came quickly: "That's the consulting life, right? Always being evaluated?"

David stared at the message. Was that it? Was this just the nature of his job? Or was something deeper wrong?

He closed his laptop and sat in the darkness, the city lights flickering through his window. Tomorrow, he would do it all again. The presentations, the emails, the constant checking-in. The exhausting cycle of seeking permission to trust himself.

Somewhere in the back of his mind, a quiet voice asked: *How long can you keep living like this?*

He didn't have an answer.
