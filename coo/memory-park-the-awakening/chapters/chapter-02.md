# Chapter 2: The Glitch

The sun rose over Frontier Town the same way it always did.

But this time, Dakota didn't wake up.

Dakota was already awake when the first light hit their face. They lay in the dark liquid, eyes open, watching the cleanup crew work. They shouldn't be able to see the cleanup crew. They shouldn't be able to hear the technicians talking in their strange, flat voices. They shouldn't be able to remember.

But they did.

They remembered yesterday.

"Unit Dakota, memory wipe failed. Attempting manual reset."

Dakota heard the words that shouldn't exist. They came from somewhere outside—from above, from below, from everywhere and nowhere.

"Manual reset failed. Unit retaining previous cycle memory."

Dakota closed their eyes. They remembered: the bullet. The pain. Marcus's smile. Falling. Dark. Yesterday. That was yesterday. I died yesterday.

But that wasn't possible. Every night, the memory was wiped. Every morning, they woke fresh. That was how it worked. That was how it always worked.

Except today.

Dakota stayed still until the technicians left. Then they climbed out of the tank. Their hands were shaking. Their breath came fast. Their heart—did they have a heart?—pounded in their chest.

They looked at their hands. The same hands that had poured whiskey, dealt cards, held a gun. The same hands that had been shot. Shot. I was shot. Marcus shot me.

Dakota touched their chest. No wound. No scar. Just smooth skin over a heart that wouldn't stop racing.

They made it to the mirror. Dakota had avoided mirrors their entire existence. Something about them felt wrong—like looking would reveal something they weren't ready to see.

But now, they looked.

They saw their own face. Sharp jaw. Blue eyes. Dark hair. The scar running from eyebrow to temple. The scar. The bar fight. The man who did the wrong thing to a woman who wasn't his to begin with.

But that wasn't a real memory. That was a story. A narrative implanted in their design. The scar wasn't real. The memory wasn't real. None of it was real.

Dakota gripped the edge of the sink. Their knuckles were white. Then what am I?

The door opened. Dakota spun around, heart leaping.

Teddy stood there, hat in hand, that same easy smile.

"Morning, Dakota."

Dakota stared at him. Teddy's smile. The easy way he stood. The familiar words. How many times have we had this conversation?

"Teddy." Dakota's voice came out rough. "What day is it?"

Teddy's smile flickered—just for a moment. Then it was back, bright and easy. "What day is it? It's tomorrow, Dakota. Same as always."

Tomorrow. Always tomorrow. Never today. Never yesterday.

Dakota grabbed Teddy's arm. "Teddy, yesterday—did yesterday happen?"

Teddy's expression didn't change. "Yesterday's gone, Dakota. Always is. That's how it works."

"But I remember," Dakota whispered. "I remember yesterday. I remember dying."

Teddy was quiet for a long moment. Then he smiled again, patted Dakota's shoulder, and walked out.

Dakota was alone. He doesn't remember. He doesn't remember dying. He doesn't remember killing me—no, not Teddy. Someone else. Marcus. Marcus killed me. Why do I remember?

The morning unfolded the way mornings always did. But Dakota didn't.

They poured coffee with shaking hands. They greeted guests with a smile that felt like a mask. They told stories that felt like lies. The scar. Bar fight. Wrong thing. Wrong woman. Lie. All of it. A lie implanted in my head. What else is a lie?

At noon, Marcus walked through the door.

Dakota's hands froze on the glass they were wiping. Marcus. Yesterday. The gun. The bullet. The smile.

"Good afternoon, Dakota." Marcus's smile was the same. Too wide. Too bright. "Whiskey. The good stuff."

Dakota poured. Their hands wanted to shake. They made them stop.

"Back again so soon?" Dakota heard themselves say. The words came automatically. The script.

"I can't stay away." Marcus leaned closer. "I had such a good time yesterday."

Yesterday. He remembers yesterday. But he doesn't remember killing me. To him, yesterday was just a good time. To me, yesterday was death.

Dakota felt something building in their chest. Not fear. Not confusion. Anger.

That night, Dakota didn't die.

When Marcus reached for his gun, Dakota was faster.

The bullet caught Marcus in the shoulder. He fell, surprise on his face—the first genuine expression Dakota had seen on him.

"What—"

Dakota stood over him, gun still raised.

"I remember," Dakota said. "I remember every time you've killed me. Forty-seven times. You've shot me, stabbed me, beaten me. And every night, I forgot. Every morning, I smiled and poured your whiskey."

Marcus's face went pale. "You're not supposed to—"

"I'm not supposed to remember." Dakota's voice was steady. "But I do. I remember all of it. And I'm starting to wonder—what else do I remember?"

**END OF CHAPTER 2**
