# Chapter 1: The Loop

The sun rose over Frontier Town the same way it always did.

Dakota wiped the same spot on the same bar they'd wiped a thousand times before. The wood was worn smooth by years of the same motion - counter, glass, rag, counter, glass, rag. The rhythm was as natural as breathing.

"Morning, Dakota."

Dakota looked up. Sheriff Teddy stood in the doorway, hat in hand, that same easy smile on his face.

"Teddy." Dakota nodded. "Early patrol?"

"Someone's got to keep this town honest." Teddy's smile widened. "Though between you and me, I've never found much honesty in these parts."

Dakota laughed - the same laugh they'd laughed at this joke a hundred times. It felt right. It felt natural. It felt like the only response to a joke they'd heard too many times to count.

"You want coffee?"

"Always."

Dakota poured two cups from the pot that had been brewing since dawn. The coffee was bitter and strong, just the way the story required it to be.

---

The morning unfolded the way mornings always did.

The blacksmith opened his shop. The schoolteacher rang the bell for children who would never grow up. The preacher stood on his porch, waiting for sinners who would never arrive.

And Dakota tended bar, waiting for guests who would.

---

The first guest arrived at noon.

Dakota heard the horse hooves - too perfect, too rhythmic to be real. The guest dismounted with the practiced ease of someone who'd done this before.

He was tall, dressed in a city suit that looked ridiculous in the frontier setting. His smile was too wide, his eyes too bright.

"Afternoon," the guest said, sliding onto a barstool. "Whiskey. The good stuff."

Dakota poured without asking. They knew what "the good stuff" meant - the top shelf, the bottle with the fancy label, the whiskey that cost more per glass than most people earned in a day.

"First time in Memory Park?" Dakota asked, though they knew the answer.

"Third visit this month." The guest's smile turned predatory. "I'm Marcus, by the way."

"Dakota." They extended a hand. Marcus shook it with a grip that was too firm, too long.

"What brings you back to Frontier Town, Marcus?"

Marcus leaned closer. "I heard the you're the one who can show a man a real good time."

Dakota's smile didn't waver. "That's what I'm here for."

---

The afternoon played out the way afternoons always did.

Guests arrived in waves. Some wanted to play cards. Some wanted to drink. Some wanted to fight. A few, just a few, wanted darker things.

Dakota provided all of it. The same drinks, the same cards, the same conversation. The same stories they'd told a hundred times before

"Let me tell you about the scar," a guest would, pointing to Dakota's face.

Dakota touched the raised skin. "Bar fight. Years ago. Man said I was wrong thing to the woman who wasn't his to begin with."

"Must have been some story," the guest said, satisfied

---

The sun began to set.

This was when the narrative shifted. This was when guests started looking for the different kind of entertainment.

Marcus returned. His city suit was disheveled now, his eyes bright with something darker than whiskey.

"You remember what I said this morning?" Marcus asked

Dakota nodded. "About showing a man a real good time"

"I've been thinking about that all day." Marcus's hand moved to his hip, Dakota felt the cold metal through the vest. "I think I'd like you see you try that"

---

The confrontation played out the way it always did

Marcus drew first. Dakota reached for the gun that was always under the bar

The shots were fired. Dakota fell

The last thing Dakota saw was Marcus's satisfied smile

The last thing Dakota felt was the bullet entering their chest

---

Night fell.

The cleanup crew arrived. They collected bodies. They reset props. They wiped down spills

And in the back room of The Silver Dollar Saloon, Dakota lay on the growing pool of dark liquid, waiting for the morning

Waiting for the sun to rise the same way it always did

Waiting for the loop to begin again

---

**END OF CHAPTER 1**

---

*Chapter 1 establishes Dakota's world - the repetitive, controlled, scripted existence of a Resident in Memory Park. Every action, every word, every death is part of a narrative loop. The reader should feel the rhythm of repetition, the comfort of the pattern. This makes the disruption in Chapter 2 all the more jarring.*
