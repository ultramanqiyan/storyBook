# Chapter 4: The Test

The liberation tool was ready for testing. Sarah had selected a small group of Dalit volunteers—people who had been denied methodology access their entire lives. If the tool worked, they would suddenly be able to learn how to learn. If it failed...

She didn't want to think about what would happen if it failed.

The volunteers gathered in her underground hideout, their faces a mixture of nervousness and hope. There were five of them: a former factory worker, a retired teacher's aide, a young mother, an elderly man who had spent his life in manual labor, and a woman who cleaned offices in the Brahmin district.

"Thank you for doing this," Sarah said. "You understand the risks?"

They nodded. They had all signed waivers—not that such documents would protect anyone if the system discovered what they were doing.

"I'm going to activate the tool now," Sarah said. "It will intercept the cognitive classification signals and replace them with credentials that grant methodology access. You should feel... different. More aware of how you think. More able to question."

She pressed the key. The screen filled with scrolling code as the tool went to work.

For a moment, nothing happened. Then the former factory worker gasped.

"I can see it," he said. "I can see how to learn. It's like... like there's a path where there wasn't one before."

The young mother smiled. "It's like a whole new world has opened up."

Sarah felt a wave of relief. The tool was working.

But as the hours passed, she noticed something unexpected. The volunteers were becoming overwhelmed. They were asking too many questions, doubting everything, struggling to process the sudden flood of cognitive possibilities.

"I don't understand," the elderly man said, his voice trembling. "Everything I thought I knew is wrong. How do I know what's true?"

"It's too much," the office cleaner said. "I don't know what to believe anymore. I don't know how to decide."

Sarah realized that liberation wasn't simple. It was complex, difficult, and potentially dangerous. People who had never been taught to question suddenly found themselves drowning in doubt. The tools of critical thinking were powerful, but they required practice, guidance, support.

The test results were mixed. The tool worked—the volunteers gained methodology access. But the consequences were unexpected. Liberation wasn't just about granting access. It was about preparing people to use what they had been given.

Sarah looked at her volunteers, struggling with their new cognitive freedom, and realized she had more work to do.
