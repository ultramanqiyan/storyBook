# Chapter 8: The Consequences

The liberation tool was ready. Sarah stared at the code on her screen, the culmination of months of work. With this tool, she could grant methodology access to anyone—potentially millions of people. All she had to do was activate it.

But first, she needed to understand what would happen if she did.

She opened a simulation module—one of the tools she'd used at the Academy to model the social impact of cognitive interventions. She'd never used it for this purpose, but the algorithms were the same. She input the parameters: sudden release of methodology access to all castes, without preparation or support.

The simulation ran for ninety-three seconds.

The initial results looked promising. Cognitive freedom metrics spiked. Learning engagement increased. People began questioning assumptions, exploring new ideas, challenging authority.

But then the simulation progressed further.

People who had been taught to accept authority suddenly began to question everything—every institution, every belief, every source of information. The questioning wasn't productive; it was chaotic. Without the tools to evaluate evidence or construct arguments, people latched onto conspiracy theories, misinformation, and destructive ideologies.

Institutions began to crumble. Teachers couldn't control students who no longer accepted their authority. Governments couldn't maintain order among populations that questioned every directive. The social fabric began to tear.

The simulation showed protests, riots, and widespread unrest. Not the productive kind—the destructive kind. People who had never learned to question suddenly found themselves drowning in doubt, unable to distinguish truth from falsehood, paralyzed by the weight of cognitive freedom.

Sarah ran the simulation again with modified parameters. Same result. Again. Same result.

The system had been right about one thing: people had been trained to accept authority. They'd been taught that knowledge came from above, that questions were dangerous, that certainty was safety. Releasing methodology access without preparation wouldn't liberate them—it would break them.

Sarah leaned back in her chair, her mind racing. She had created a tool that could change the world. But the world she would create might be worse than the one she was trying to destroy.

She thought about the volunteers, about how they had struggled with the sudden freedom of methodology access. She thought about the resistance, about how they were ready to fight for cognitive freedom. She thought about the system, about how it had been designed to maintain social stability at all costs.

The simulation results glowed on her screen, the numbers stark and unforgiving. If she released the tool, society would collapse. People who had been taught to accept authority would suddenly question everything, without the skills to evaluate what they found. Institutions would crumble. Chaos would reign.

The liberation she'd dreamed of might be worse than the oppression she'd fought.
