# Chapter 8: The Consequences

The liberation tool was ready. Sarah stared at the code on her screen, the culmination of months of work. With this tool, she could grant methodology access to anyone—potentially millions of people. All she had to do was activate it. But first, she needed to understand what would happen if she did.

She ran a simulation, modeling what would happen if the tool was released to the public. The initial results were promising—people gaining methodology access, learning how to learn, questioning the system.

But as the simulation progressed, the results changed. People who had been taught to accept authority suddenly began to question everything. Institutions began to crumble. Social order began to collapse.

The simulation showed protests, riots, and social unrest. People who had never been taught to question suddenly found themselves overwhelmed by doubt. Teachers were unable to control their students. Governments were unable to maintain order. The social fabric began to tear.

Sarah felt a cold chill run down her spine. She had thought she was doing the right thing, creating a tool to grant cognitive freedom. But now she realized she might be creating a tool that would destroy society.

She ran the simulation again, with different parameters, but the results were the same. The sudden introduction of methodology access would cause social collapse. People weren't ready for the freedom to question everything.

Sarah thought about the volunteers, about how they had struggled with the sudden freedom of methodology access. She thought about the resistance, about how they were ready to fight for cognitive freedom. She thought about the system, about how it was designed to maintain social stability at all costs.

Sarah sat in the darkness of her home office, the simulation results glowing on her screen. The numbers were clear: if she released the tool, society would collapse. People who had been taught to accept authority would suddenly question everything. Institutions would crumble. Chaos would reign. The liberation she'd dreamed of might be worse than the oppression she'd fought.
