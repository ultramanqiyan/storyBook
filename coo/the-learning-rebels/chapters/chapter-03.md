# Chapter 3: The Father

Marcus Chen lived in a small apartment in San Francisco's Mission District, the kind of place that smelled of old books and fresh coffee. Sarah had been visiting her father every week since his exile from the Academy, but today felt different. Today, she needed advice about something she couldn't discuss with anyone else.

"Sarah!" Her father opened the door before she could knock. "Come in. I made your favorite—dark roast with a dash of cinnamon."

She stepped inside, the familiar scent wrapping around her. The apartment was cluttered with scientific journals, textbooks, and the half-finished projects that filled her father's days since the system had stripped him of his credentials.

"How's the work?" he asked, pouring her a cup.

"I think I've found a way to bypass the cognitive barriers." Sarah took the coffee, warming her hands on the mug. "A tool that can grant methodology access to anyone."

Her father's smile faded. "Sarah, you know what happened to me when I challenged the system."

"I know." She set down the mug. "But this is different. You preserved science—existing knowledge. I'm giving people the ability to create new knowledge. To learn how to learn."

"And what happens when they do?" Marcus sat across from her, his eyes serious. "What happens when people who have never been taught to question suddenly find themselves with the tools to do so?"

Sarah thought about the Shudra and Dalit castes, about how they had been denied methodology access their entire lives. "They'll be able to think for themselves. They'll be able to challenge the system."

"And what if they challenge everything? What if the social order collapses?"

Sarah's stomach tightened. She had thought about this, but she hadn't fully confronted the consequences. "The social order is built on oppression. Maybe it deserves to collapse."

"Maybe." Her father's voice was gentle. "But collapse is messy. People get hurt. The innocent along with the guilty."

They sat in silence for a moment. Outside, the sounds of the Mission filtered through the window—traffic, voices, the distant rumble of BART.

"I don't have answers, Sarah. Only questions." Marcus reached across the table and took her hand. "When I stood where you're standing, I chose to preserve truth. I don't know if I was right. I only know I couldn't have lived with myself if I hadn't tried."

Sarah thought about her father's choice, about how he had paid for it with his career, his reputation, his standing in the community. But she also thought about the science he had preserved—the knowledge that would be there for future generations, even if he never got credit for saving it.

"What would you do?" she asked.

"I would do what you're doing." His grip tightened on her hand. "I would try. Because the alternative is to let the system win. And I couldn't bear that."

Sarah left her father's apartment an hour later, the afternoon sun warm on her face. If he could make that choice, so could she. She would finish the liberation tool. She would grant methodology access to those who had been denied it. She would challenge the system, whatever the cost.
