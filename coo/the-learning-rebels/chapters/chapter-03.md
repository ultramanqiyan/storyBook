# Chapter 3: The Father

Marcus Chen lived in a small apartment in San Francisco, the kind of place that smelled of old books and fresh coffee. Sarah had been visiting her father every week since his exile, but today felt different. Today, she needed his advice about something she wasn't sure she could discuss with anyone else.

"Sarah!" her father said, opening the door with a warm smile. "Come in, come in. I made your favorite—dark roast with a dash of cinnamon."

Sarah stepped inside, the familiar scent of books and coffee wrapping around her like a blanket. The apartment was cluttered with old scientific journals, textbooks, and a half-finished project on the kitchen table.

"How's the project going?" her father asked, pouring her a cup of coffee.

"It's coming along," Sarah said. "I think I've found a way to bypass the cognitive barriers."

Her father's smile faded. "Sarah, you know what happened to me when I tried to challenge the system."

"I know," Sarah said. "But this is different. This isn't just preserving science. This is granting people the ability to learn how to learn."

"And what happens when they do?" her father asked. "What happens when people who have never been taught to question suddenly find themselves with the tools to do so?"

Sarah thought about the Shudra and Dalit castes, about how they had been denied methodology access their entire lives. "They'll be able to think for themselves," she said. "They'll be able to question the system."

"And what if they question everything?" her father asked. "What if the social order collapses?"

Sarah felt a knot form in her stomach. She had thought about this, but she hadn't fully considered the consequences. "The social order is based on oppression," she said. "It deserves to collapse."

"Maybe," her father said. "But collapse is messy. People get hurt."

They sat in silence for a moment, the only sound the hum of the coffee maker.

"I don't have answers, Sarah," her father said. "Only questions. When I stood where you're standing, I chose to preserve truth. I don't know if I was right. I only know I couldn't have lived with myself if I hadn't tried."

Sarah thought about her father's choice, about how he had paid for it with his career, his reputation, his freedom. But she also thought about the science he had preserved, about how it would be there for future generations.

"What would you do?" she asked.

"I would do what you're doing," her father said. "I would try. Because the alternative is to let the system win."

Sarah left her father's apartment with more resolve than she'd arrived with. If he could make that choice, so could she. She would finish the liberation tool. She would grant methodology access to those who had been denied it. She would challenge the system, no matter the cost.
