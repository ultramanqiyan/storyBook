# Chapter 5: The Network

The resistance network found Sarah before she found them. A message appeared on her encrypted channel: "You are not alone. We've been watching your work. When you're ready, we should talk."

Sarah stared at the message, her heart racing. She wasn't the only one fighting the system.

She replied: "I'm ready."

The response came quickly: "Meet us at the old library on 5th Street at midnight. Come alone."

Sarah arrived at the library at midnight, her heart pounding. The building was dark, its windows boarded up. She knocked on the door three times, as instructed.

The door opened, and a man in his mid-thirties stepped out. "You must be Sarah," he said. "I'm David."

Sarah followed him inside. The library was filled with people, all working on computers, talking in hushed tones.

"Welcome to the resistance," David said. "We've been tracking your work on the liberation tool. It's impressive."

Sarah was surprised. "How did you know about it?"

"We have eyes everywhere," David said. "The system isn't as all-seeing as it thinks."

Sarah spent the next few hours learning about the resistance. It was larger than she had imagined—dozens of people across the country, each working in their own way to challenge the cognitive stratification system.

There were educators teaching methodology to Dalit in secret. There were hackers disrupting the system's classification process. There were activists spreading information about the system's true purpose.

"We've been waiting for someone like you," David said. "Someone who understands the system from the inside. Someone who can create a tool like yours."

Sarah felt a sense of belonging she hadn't felt since she left her old life. She wasn't alone. There were others fighting the system, others who believed that methodology access should be a right, not a privilege.

But as she learned more about the network, she realized that liberation was more complex than she'd imagined. The question wasn't just whether to free people's minds. It was what would happen after.

Some in the resistance wanted to release the tool immediately, to grant methodology access to everyone. Others wanted to test it more thoroughly, to understand its consequences better.

Sarah found herself caught in the middle. She believed in the tool's potential, but she also knew its risks. She had seen firsthand how overwhelming methodology access could be for people who had never learned how to question.

The resistance was real—dozens of people across the country, each working in their own way to challenge the cognitive stratification system. Sarah wasn't alone.
