# Chapter 1: The Auditor

Sarah Chen had been a Cognitive Auditor for seven years before she understood what she was actually auditing. She had believed she was checking for errors, ensuring the system worked correctly. Then she discovered the truth: the system was working correctly. And that was the problem.

Sarah sat in her underground hideout, the dim light casting shadows across her face. The walls were lined with computer equipment, the hum of servers filling the small space. She sipped her coffee, the bitter taste matching her mood.

She thought back to her days as a Cognitive Auditor. She had been one of the best, known for her meticulous attention to detail, her ability to spot even the smallest errors in the system. She had believed she was doing good work, ensuring that the cognitive classification system was fair and accurate.

But then she had discovered the truth. The system wasn't making mistakes. It was deliberately limiting methodology access to certain castes. It was preventing people from learning how to learn. It was maintaining social stability through cognitive stratification.

Sarah's chest tightened as she remembered the day she had made the discovery. She had been auditing a batch of cognitive classifications, when she noticed a pattern. The system was consistently denying methodology access to people who asked too many questions, who showed too much curiosity, who challenged the status quo.

She had dug deeper, and found that the system was designed to do exactly that. The cognitive stratification wasn't a bug—it was a feature. The Brahmin caste was given full methodology access, while the Shudra and Dalit castes were deliberately denied the tools to learn how to learn.

Sarah had been horrified. She had spent seven years helping to maintain a system that was systematically oppressing people. She had been a cog in the machine of cognitive control.

She had quit her job that day, left her old life behind, and gone into hiding. Now she was living in an underground hideout, working on a tool to bypass the cognitive barriers.

Sarah closed the audit report, her hands trembling. The system wasn't broken. It was working exactly as designed—limiting methodology access, preventing questioning, maintaining social stability through cognitive stratification. And she had spent seven years helping to maintain it. Now she had to decide: what was she going to do about it?
