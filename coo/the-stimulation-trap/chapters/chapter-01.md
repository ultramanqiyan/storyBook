# Chapter 01: The Normal

The notification chimes at 6:47 PM.

Leo sets down his fork. His dinner—reheated pasta from a box—sits half-eaten on the table. He's not hungry. He hasn't been hungry for a while. But he eats anyway because that's what people do.

He walks to the couch. The device sits on the coffee table, right where he left it this morning. The screen glows with a notification: *New content available. Based on your preferences.*

*Finally.*

He picks it up. The weight is familiar in his hands. Comfortable. He's been holding this device every evening for three years now. Maybe longer. He doesn't remember exactly when it started. It just... became part of his routine. Like brushing his teeth. Like going to work.

He settles into the couch. The cushions have a permanent indent where he always sits. He pulls the headset from the side table—also right where he left it—and slides it over his eyes.

The world shifts.

---

The virtual lobby is beautiful.

It's designed to be. Every curve, every color, every light has been calculated to make him feel welcome. Relaxed. Happy. The floor is polished marble that reflects a sky full of stars. The walls are glass, showing a cityscape that doesn't exist anywhere in the real world—towers of light, rivers of neon, bridges that glow like veins of gold.

Leo walks forward. His virtual feet make no sound on the marble. But he can feel the simulation of solidity beneath him. The haptic feedback is precise. It always is.

*This is better.*

He approaches the content hub. A floating menu appears, showing his recommendations. Movies. Games. Experiences. Each one tailored to his preferences. Each one designed to give him exactly what he wants.

He scrolls.

*The Last Emperor's Dream*—a historical drama with 98% satisfaction rating.

*Neon Drift*—a racing game that adapts to his skill level in real-time.

*The Garden of Echoes*—an exploration experience through impossible landscapes.

He's seen them all before. Not these exact ones, but others like them. Hundreds of others. Thousands, maybe. He's lost count.

He selects *The Garden of Echoes*. He's in the mood for something calm. Something beautiful. Something that will let him forget about his spreadsheets and his quarterly reports and his empty apartment and his dinner that tasted like cardboard.

The world shifts again.

---

He's standing in a meadow.

The grass is impossibly green. Each blade catches the light differently, creating a shimmer that moves like water. Flowers bloom around him—colors he doesn't have names for. The sky is a gradient of purple and gold, like a sunset that never ends.

He walks. The grass brushes against his legs. He can feel it through the haptic suit—a gentle pressure, a soft tickle. The wind carries a scent. Jasmine. Orange blossom. Something else, something he can't identify but that makes him think of childhood summers he never actually had.

*This is nice.*

He finds a stream. The water is clear, reflecting the impossible sky. He kneels beside it. When he touches the surface, ripples spread outward. The water is cool against his fingers. Real. Almost too real.

He sits on the bank. A bird lands nearby—a creature with feathers like stained glass. It tilts its head, watching him. Then it sings.

The sound is perfect. Every note is clear, pure, designed to resonate with something deep in his chest. He feels it more than he hears it. A warmth spreading through his body. A loosening of tension he didn't know he was carrying.

*This is what I needed.*

He stays there for what feels like hours. The sun never fully sets. The bird sings. The water flows. The flowers bloom. Everything is beautiful. Everything is peaceful. Everything is exactly as it should be.


When he removes the headset, his apartment is dark.

The clock on the wall reads 11:23 PM. Four hours. He's been in there for four hours. It felt like minutes.

He sits on the couch. The device is warm in his lap. His body feels heavy, but in a good way. Satisfied. Like after a good meal or a long sleep.

He should go to bed. He has work tomorrow. Another day of spreadsheets and quarterly reports and meetings that could have been emails.

But he doesn't move. Not yet.

He looks at the device. The screen shows his session summary: *Experience completed. Satisfaction rating: 94%. Would you like to try similar content?*

He taps *Yes*.

The recommendations load. New experiences. New worlds. New ways to feel something other than the quiet hum of his daily life.

*Tomorrow,* he thinks. *I'll try these tomorrow.*

He sets the device on the coffee table. He stands. His legs are stiff. He walks to the kitchen and drinks a glass of water. It tastes like nothing. Like the pipes in his building. Like his half-eaten pasta.

He looks at the sink. The dishes from last night are still there. He should wash them. He should clean the kitchen. He should do a lot of things.

Instead, he goes to bed.


The next morning, his alarm goes off at 7:00 AM.

He showers. He dresses. He eats a bowl of cereal that tastes like cardboard. He checks his phone. A message from his sister: *Call me when you get a chance. It's been a while.*

He types a reply: *Sure. Maybe this weekend.*

He doesn't know if he means it. The weekend feels far away. A lot can happen between now and then. A lot of content to experience. A lot of worlds to visit.

He grabs his bag. He walks to the door. Before he leaves, he glances at the couch. At the device sitting on the coffee table, waiting for him.

*Tonight,* he thinks. *Tonight I'll try something new.*

He opens the door. The hallway is gray. The carpet is worn. The fluorescent lights buzz overhead.

He walks to work.


At the office, his cubicle is the same as every other cubicle.

Gray walls. Gray carpet. Gray computer screen. He sits. He logs in. He opens his spreadsheets. The numbers blur together. Revenue. Expenses. Projections. Variance.

His coworker, Marcus, appears at the edge of his cubicle.

"Hey. Did you see the game last night?"

Leo looks up. "What game?"

"The finals. It was incredible. Overtime. Last-second shot. You missed it?"

"I was... doing something else."

Marcus shakes his head. "Man, you need to get out more. Or at least watch the highlights."

"Yeah. Maybe."

Marcus lingers. "Everything okay? You seem... I don't know. Distant lately."

"I'm fine. Just tired."

"Okay." Marcus doesn't look convinced, but he walks away. "Let me know if you want to grab lunch later."

"Sure."

Leo turns back to his screen. The numbers are still there. Waiting for him. He should focus. He should work.

But his mind keeps drifting back to the Garden of Echoes. To the grass that shimmered like water. To the bird with feathers like stained glass. To the feeling of peace that he can't quite hold onto in the real world.

*Tonight,* he thinks. *Tonight I'll go back.*


Lunch comes and goes. Leo eats at his desk—a sandwich he doesn't taste. Marcus doesn't come by again.

The afternoon stretches. Meetings. Emails. More spreadsheets. The clock on his screen moves slowly. Each minute feels like an hour.

At 5:30, he packs his bag. He walks to the elevator. He rides down with a crowd of other people, all of them looking at their phones, all of them silent.

Outside, the sky is gray. The air is cool. People rush past him, heading to the subway, to their cars, to their homes.

He walks to his apartment. The same route he always takes. Past the same buildings. Past the same stores. Past the same people he never really sees.

*Almost there.*


At 6:47 PM, the notification chimes.

Leo is already on the couch. The device is already in his hands. The headset is already sliding over his eyes.

The world shifts.

He's back in the lobby. The marble floor. The glass walls. The city of light.

The menu appears. His recommendations. New content. New experiences.

He scrolls.

*The Garden of Echoes II: The Forgotten Paths.*

His finger hovers over the button.

*This is what I need,* he thinks. *This is what makes everything okay.*

He presses play.

The world shifts again.


Later—much later—he removes the headset.

The clock reads 1:15 AM. He's been in there for six hours. His body aches. His eyes are dry. His stomach growls—he forgot to eat dinner again.

He should feel bad about that. He should feel something about skipping meals and losing sleep and spending his entire evening in a world that isn't real.

But he doesn't.

He feels satisfied. Content. Like he's just come back from a vacation, even though he hasn't moved from his couch.

He sets the device down. He stands. He walks to the kitchen. He opens the refrigerator. It's mostly empty—some condiments, a wilted lettuce, a carton of milk that might be expired.

He closes the refrigerator. He drinks a glass of water instead.

He goes to bed.

As he lies in the dark, staring at the ceiling, he thinks about tomorrow. About work. About spreadsheets and quarterly reports and meetings that could have been emails.

And he thinks about the evening. About the device waiting on the coffee table. About the worlds he can visit. About the feelings he can experience.

*This is normal,* he tells himself. *Everyone does this. This is just how people live now.*

He closes his eyes.

He sleeps.

And in his dreams, he's still in the Garden of Echoes, walking through grass that shimmers like water, listening to a bird with feathers like stained glass sing a song that makes him feel something he can't quite name.

*This is enough,* he thinks. *This has to be enough.*

But somewhere deep inside—a place he doesn't visit, a thought he doesn't examine—there's a small, quiet question.

*Is it?*
