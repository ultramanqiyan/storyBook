# Chapter 08: The Descent

The knocking doesn't stop.

Leo lies in bed. The headset is beside him on the pillow. He's been wearing it for... he doesn't know how long. Hours. Days. Time has lost meaning.

The knocking continues.

"Leo! Open the door! Please!"

It's a woman's voice. He knows this voice. He should know this voice.

*Sarah,* he thinks. *My sister.*

The name comes to him slowly. Like pulling something through thick water.

"Leo! I'm not leaving until you open this door!"

He considers ignoring her. Staying in bed. Putting the headset back on.

But something stops him. A small, distant memory. Of a time when people mattered. When relationships mattered. When he was someone who cared about things other than the feeling.

He gets up.

---

The door opens.

Sarah stands in the hallway. Her eyes are red. Her face is pale. She looks like she hasn't slept in days.

Behind her are two other people. Older. A man and a woman. They look familiar.

*Parents,* Leo thinks. *My parents.*

The word feels foreign. Distant. Like it belongs to someone else.

"Leo." Sarah's voice breaks. "Oh god. Look at you."

He looks down at himself. He's wearing the same clothes he's been wearing for... he doesn't know how long. There are stains. Wrinkles. His arms are thin. Thinner than he remembers.

"When's the last time you ate?" The woman—his mother—steps forward. Her eyes are wet. "When's the last time you slept? When's the last time you did anything?"

Leo tries to answer. He can't. He doesn't remember.

"We've been calling you for weeks," the man—his father—says. "Sarah came by. You wouldn't open the door. We were about to call the police."

"Leo, please." Sarah reaches for him. "Let us help you."

Leo looks at her hand. At the concern in her eyes. At the love that he should feel but doesn't.

He steps back.

"I'm fine."

"You're not fine." Sarah's voice is hard now. Angry. "You're falling apart. You've lost weight. You've lost your job—I called your office, Leo. They fired you two weeks ago. You didn't even tell us."

Fired. The word should mean something. It doesn't.

"Leo." His mother steps forward. "Please. Come with us. We'll figure this out. Together."

Leo looks at them. At his family. At the people who love him.

He knows he should feel something. Gratitude. Love. Shame. Something.

But he doesn't.

All he feels is the absence. The emptiness. The need for the headset.

"I can't."

"Can't what?" Sarah asks.

"I can't... I can't leave. I can't stop. I can't..."

He trails off. He doesn't know how to explain. He doesn't have words for what's happening to him.

"Leo." His father's voice is gentle. "Whatever it is. Whatever you're going through. We're here. We love you. We're not going to abandon you."

*Love.*

The word should mean something. It doesn't.

"I know," he says. Because that's what he's supposed to say. "I know."

---

They stay for hours.

They clean his apartment. They make him food. They sit with him on the couch and talk about things he doesn't understand. Family events he doesn't remember. People he doesn't recognize.

He nods at the right moments. He smiles when he's supposed to smile. He pretends to be present.

But inside, he's counting the minutes.

*When can I put the headset back on?*

*When can I feel something again?*

*When will they leave?*

He hates himself for thinking these things. He knows he should want them here. He knows he should be grateful.

But he can't feel it. He can't feel anything.

Except the absence. The emptiness. The need.


That night, they make him promise.

"Promise you'll eat," his mother says.

"I promise."

"Promise you'll sleep."

"I promise."

"Promise you'll call us tomorrow."

"I promise."

"Promise you'll stay away from that thing." Sarah points to the headset on his nightstand. "Promise you'll give yourself a chance to heal."

He hesitates.

"Leo. Please."

"I promise."

They leave.


He breaks all the promises.

He doesn't eat. He doesn't sleep. He doesn't call.

And the headset... he can't stay away from it. He tries. He sits on the couch and stares at the wall and tries to think about something else.

But his mind keeps drifting. To the feeling. To the only thing that makes him feel alive.

*Just once,* he thinks. *Just once more. Then I'll stop.*

He picks up the headset.

He puts it on.


The world shifts.

The feeling comes.

But it's weaker now. Dimmer. Like a light that's slowly dying.

He tries to hold onto it. To make it last.

But it slips away.

Seconds. That's all he gets. Seconds of something that used to last minutes.

He takes off the headset.

He stares at the wall.

*This is it,* he thinks. *This is what Kane warned me about. The burnout. The end.*

He should feel scared. He should feel desperate.

But he doesn't.

He just feels... tired.


The next day, Sarah comes back.

She has a bag with her. Clothes. Food. A laptop.

"I'm staying," she says. "I'm not leaving you alone."

"You don't have to do that."

"I know. I'm doing it anyway."

She sets up on his couch. She makes him eat. She makes him talk.

He tries. He really tries.

But he can't feel it. The connection. The love. The gratitude.

It's all just... words. Actions. Empty gestures.

"I'm going to fix this," Sarah says. "We're going to fix this. Together."

Leo nods. He wants to believe her.

But he knows the truth.

There's nothing left to fix.


That night, when Sarah is asleep on the couch, Leo gets up.

He walks to his room. He closes the door. He picks up the headset.

He puts it on.

The world shifts.

The feeling comes.

Seconds. Dim. Weak.

But it's something. It's the only thing.

He holds onto it as long as he can.

Then it's gone.


He does this every night.

He waits for Sarah to fall asleep. He sneaks into his room. He puts on the headset.

He chases the feeling.

It comes in shorter and shorter bursts. Weaker and weaker.

But he can't stop.

Because without it, there's nothing. Just the gray. Just the emptiness. Just the slow, steady descent into nothing.


Two weeks later, Sarah finds him.

He's on the floor of his room. The headset is beside him. He doesn't remember falling.

"Leo!"

She's shaking him. Her voice is panicked.

"Leo, can you hear me? Leo!"

He opens his eyes. He sees her face. The fear. The desperation.

He should feel something. Guilt. Shame. Concern.

He doesn't.

"I'm fine," he says.

"You're not fine." Her voice breaks. "You're not fine, Leo. You're dying. Can't you see that? You're killing yourself with this... this thing."

He looks at the headset on the floor. At the black visor. At the promise of feeling.

"I know."

"Then stop. Please. Stop."

He looks at his sister. At the tears streaming down her face. At the love that he can't feel.

"I can't."

"Why? Why can't you?"

He tries to explain. To find the words.

"Because without it... there's nothing. I don't feel anything. I don't care about anything. I'm just... empty."

Sarah stares at him. Then she picks up the headset.

"Give me that."

"No—"

She throws it against the wall. It cracks. The visor shatters.

Leo watches it fall to the floor. Broken. Useless.

He should feel angry. He should feel devastated.

But he doesn't.

He just feels... nothing.


The next day, his parents come.

They take him to a hospital. A psychiatric ward. A place with white walls and soft voices and people who tell him it's going to be okay.

He sits in a chair. He answers questions. He takes pills.

He doesn't feel any of it.


The doctor is kind. Patient. Understanding.

"Your neural pathways have been severely damaged," she says. "The direct stimulation has burned out your receptors. Your ability to feel pleasure, joy, connection—all of it has been compromised."

"Can it be fixed?"

"We don't know. The brain is resilient. It can heal. But this kind of damage..." She pauses. "It may take years. It may never fully recover."

Leo nods. He should feel scared. He should feel devastated.

But he doesn't.

"So what do I do?"

"You wait. You heal. You learn to live with less." She looks at him. "And you stay away from anything that stimulates those pathways. Forever. If you go back to it, you'll burn out what's left. And then... there will be nothing."

*Nothing.*

He's already living in nothing. He's been living in nothing for months.

"Okay," he says.


He stays in the hospital for three weeks.

He goes to therapy. He takes medication. He talks to other patients who are struggling with their own demons.

He doesn't feel any of it.

But he pretends. He smiles. He nods. He says the right things.

Because that's what people do. That's what's expected.

And maybe, if he pretends long enough, it will become real.


When he's released, Sarah is waiting.

She drives him to their parents' house. To his childhood bedroom. To a life he doesn't recognize.

"I'm going to take care of you," she says. "For as long as you need."

"Thank you."

He means it. He thinks he means it.

But he can't feel it.


That night, he lies in his childhood bed.

The ceiling is familiar. The walls are familiar. The sounds of the house are familiar.

But he doesn't feel at home.

He doesn't feel anything.

He closes his eyes.

He thinks about the feeling. About the joy. The peace. The connection. The meaning. The love.

All of it gone.

All of it lost.

He thinks about the headset. About the chase. About everything he sacrificed.

And he thinks about what Kane said.

*Most people who choose that path... they don't usually come back.*

He's back. In a way. In his parents' house. With his family. With people who love him.

But he's not really back.

He's just... here. Existing. Waiting.

For what, he doesn't know.


The next morning, he wakes up.

The sun is coming through the window. The birds are singing. His mother is making breakfast downstairs.

It should feel like a fresh start. Like hope. Like possibility.

It doesn't.

It just feels like another day.

He gets up. He gets dressed. He goes downstairs.

His mother smiles at him. "Good morning, sweetheart. How did you sleep?"

"Fine."

He sits at the table. He eats the food she puts in front of him. He answers her questions. He pretends to be present.

But inside, he's thinking about something else.

About a forum post he saw once. About a new technology. About a way to feel again.

He shouldn't look for it. He knows he shouldn't.

But he can't stop thinking about it.

*Maybe there's something else. Something new. Something that will work.*

He knows it's a trap. He knows it's the same cycle.

But he can't help it.

He's already planning his next descent.
