# Chapter 4: The Young - Those Who Cannot Pay

David's apartment was on the third floor of a building that had seen better decades. The elevator was broken—again—and Robert climbed the stairs slowly, his knees protesting each step. The hallway smelled of old carpet and stale cooking, and the fluorescent lights flickered in a way that made him think of hospitals.

He hadn't seen his son in three months. Not since David had lost his job as a software developer, a casualty of the same efficiency initiative that was now spreading through Morrison & Associates. They'd spoken on the phone, exchanged texts, but Robert had made excuses to avoid visiting. Work was busy. Margaret needed help with something. The drive was too long.

The truth was simpler and more painful: he didn't want to see what his son's life had become.

He knocked on the door. Waited. Heard footsteps inside.

David opened the door, and Robert felt his chest tighten.

His son looked older than twenty-eight. There were shadows under his eyes that spoke of sleepless nights, a hollowness to his cheeks that suggested missed meals. He was wearing a t-shirt that had once been black but had faded to gray, and his hair was longer than Robert had ever seen it.

"Dad." David's voice was flat. "You didn't call first."

"I wanted to surprise you."

David stepped aside. "Come in."

The apartment was small—a studio with a kitchenette, a bed pushed against the wall, a desk covered in computer equipment. The blinds were drawn, blocking out what would have been a view of the parking lot. A stack of unopened mail sat on the counter.

Robert walked to the center of the room and didn't know where to look. This was his son's life. This was what thirty years of parenting, of college tuition, of hopes and dreams had produced.

"Sit down," David said, gesturing to the only chair. He sat on the edge of his bed. "What brings you here?"

"I was in the area."

"Mom said you were running some numbers. That you were worried about something."

Robert looked at his son. David had always been perceptive, even as a child. He'd inherited Margaret's ability to read people, to sense the emotional currents beneath the surface of words.

"Your company," Robert said. "The layoffs. How are you holding up?"

David laughed—a short, bitter sound. "How do you think? I apply for jobs. I get rejection emails. Sometimes I don't even get that. Just silence." He gestured at the computer on his desk. "I spend eight hours a day filling out applications. Most of them are screened by AI before a human ever sees them. The AI decides I'm not a match. The AI decides I'm not worth interviewing."

"Have you tried—"

"Networking? Yes, Dad. I've tried networking. Everyone I know is in the same boat. We're all competing for the same few jobs, and the jobs are disappearing faster than we can apply for them."

Robert thought about the efficiency initiative at Morrison & Associates. About the thirty percent of the workforce that would be cut. About all the other companies doing the same thing.

"David, I—"

"You know what the worst part is?" David interrupted. "The worst part is that I helped build this. I wrote code for the systems that are now replacing people like me. I optimized the algorithms that screen resumes. I made myself obsolete."

The words hung in the air between them. Robert thought about his own calculations, his own spreadsheets, his own numbers that told a story he didn't want to read.

"Your mother and I have savings," he said. "We can help you—"

"I don't want your money, Dad."

"It's not about what you want. It's about—"

"It's about what?" David stood up, his hands clenching at his sides. "What is it about? You getting to feel like a good father? You getting to pretend that everything's okay because you can write a check?"

Robert felt the words like a physical blow. He stood slowly, his knees protesting again.

"I came here because I was worried about you. Because I wanted to see how you were doing. Because—" He stopped. Because what? Because he felt guilty? Because the numbers he'd run showed a future where his son had nothing?

"Because you wanted to make sure I wasn't going to ask you for money," David said. "Because you wanted to protect your retirement."

"That's not—"

"Isn't it?" David's voice cracked. "You've spent your whole life planning, Dad. Calculating. Running the numbers. You know exactly how much you need to retire. You know exactly how long your savings will last. And you know that if you have to support me, those numbers change."

Robert didn't have an answer. The truth was, he had thought about it. He'd run the calculations. He'd wondered how much it would cost to help David, how it would affect his retirement plans, whether he could afford to be generous.

He'd reduced his son to a line item in a spreadsheet.

"I'm sorry," he said. "I'm sorry I didn't call. I'm sorry I didn't visit. I'm sorry I—" He stopped. What was he sorry for? For being who he was? For thinking the way he'd always thought?

David sat back down on the bed. His shoulders slumped. "I'm sorry too. I didn't mean to snap at you. It's just—" He ran a hand through his hair. "It's hard, Dad. It's really hard. I feel like I did everything right. I went to college. I got good grades. I got a job. I worked hard. And now—" He gestured at the apartment, at the closed blinds, at the stack of unopened mail. "Now I'm here. And I don't know how to get out."

Robert sat in the chair. The plastic was cracked, and a sharp edge pressed into his thigh. He welcomed the discomfort. It was something real, something physical, something that wasn't a number on a screen.

"Tell me," he said. "Tell me what it's like."

And David did. He talked about the applications, the rejections, the silence. He talked about the interviews where he could tell the interviewer was going through the motions, already knowing they wouldn't hire him. He talked about the gig work he'd picked up—delivering food, driving for rideshare apps—that barely covered his rent. He talked about the friends who'd stopped calling, the family gatherings he avoided because he couldn't stand the questions about work.

He talked about the shame.

Robert listened. He didn't offer advice. He didn't run calculations. He just listened.

When David finished, the apartment was quiet. The fluorescent light in the kitchenette buzzed faintly. Outside, a car alarm went off, then stopped.

"I visited Mom last week," David said. "She made me take home leftovers. She always makes too much food when I come over. I think she knows I'm not eating enough."

"She worries about you."

"I know. I worry about her too. About both of you." David looked at his father. "Dad, what happens to you and Mom if the pension system fails? If Social Security runs out? What happens to everyone your age who did everything right and still ends up with nothing?"

Robert thought about the numbers. The ratios. The projections. The spiral.

"I don't know," he said. "I honestly don't know."

They sat in silence for a long moment. Then David stood and walked to the kitchenette. "You want some coffee? It's not very good, but it's warm."

"Sure."

David busied himself with the coffee maker, and Robert watched his son. The boy he'd raised, the man he'd become. A generation that had done everything right and still ended up here, in a studio apartment with broken blinds and a stack of unopened mail.

"David," he said. "I want to help. Not because of the numbers. Not because I'm calculating how it affects my retirement. Because you're my son. Because I love you. Because—" His voice caught. "Because I don't know what else to do."

David turned around. His eyes were wet. "I know, Dad. I know."

They drank their coffee in silence. It wasn't very good, but it was warm.

When Robert left an hour later, David hugged him at the door. It was awkward—neither of them had ever been good at physical affection—but it was real.

"Call Mom," David said. "She worries."

"I will."

Robert walked down the stairs, past the broken elevator, through the hallway that smelled of old carpet and stale cooking. He got in his car and sat in the parking lot for a long moment.

His phone buzzed. A text from Margaret: *How is he?*

He typed: *Struggling. But he's our son. We'll figure it out.*

He put the phone down and started the car. As he pulled out of the parking lot, he thought about the numbers. The ratios. The projections. The spiral.

But he also thought about David. About the shame in his voice. About the way he'd hugged his father at the door.

Some things couldn't be calculated. Some things couldn't be reduced to numbers on a spreadsheet.

Robert drove home in the fading afternoon light, and for the first time in weeks, he felt something that wasn't fear or calculation or dread.

He felt love.

It wasn't a number. It couldn't be quantified or projected or optimized.

But it was real. And right now, that was enough.
