# Chapter 2: The News - When Efficiency Became a Layoff

The email from the night before still glowed in Robert's mind: "All staff meeting, 9:00 AM, main auditorium. Attendance mandatory."

He closed his email and looked around the office he'd occupied for the past fifteen years. The same desk he'd negotiated for when he made partner, the same chair that had molded to his back over thousands of hours, the same window that looked out over the parking lot where he'd watched seasons change for four decades.

The office felt different this morning. Or maybe he was different.

Margaret had noticed it at breakfast. "You're quiet," she'd said, and he'd nodded without explaining. How could he explain? The number from last night still sat in his chest like a stone. Three months. Three months and he'd walk away from this building for the last time.

Now, staring at the mandatory meeting email, he felt a flicker of something he couldn't name. Premonition, perhaps. Or just the natural anxiety of a man who'd spent his career preparing for the worst.

He gathered his tablet and walked to the auditorium.

The halls were quieter than usual. He passed cubicles where people sat with the particular stillness of those waiting for bad news. A few glanced up as he walked by, their eyes searching his face for clues he didn't have.

The auditorium was filling when he arrived. He took his usual seat near the back, a habit from years of partnership meetings where he'd learned that distance gave perspective. Around him, colleagues settled into chairs, their conversations hushed and fragmented.

"As many of you know, Morrison & Associates has been a leader in our industry for over sixty years. We've weathered recessions, adapted to technological changes, and maintained our commitment to our people." He paused. "But the world is changing faster than ever before."

"Over the next six months, Morrison & Associates will be implementing a comprehensive efficiency initiative. This initiative will integrate advanced AI systems into our core operations, allowing us to serve our clients faster, more accurately, and at lower cost."

The word *efficiency* hung in the air like smoke.

"What does this mean for our workforce?" Thomas continued, as if reading the question on every face in the room. "It means change. Significant change. We will be reducing our staff by approximately thirty percent over the coming year."

A collective intake of breath. Someone near the front made a sound that might have been a sob.

"Let me be clear: this is not a reflection on the quality of our people. You are talented, dedicated, and hardworking. But the nature of work is changing, and we must change with it."

The screen shifted again, showing a timeline. *Phase 1: Assessment. Phase 2: Implementation. Phase 3: Transition.*

"Department heads will be meeting individually with affected employees over the next two weeks. If you are not contacted for a meeting, your position is secure for the current phase. However, I want to be transparent: this is an ongoing process. Future phases may involve additional restructuring."

Robert's hands were cold. He looked around the room and saw faces he'd known for decades. Janet from Accounting, who'd started the same year he had. Marcus in IT, who'd fixed his computer more times than he could count. The young analysts in the front row, some of them barely out of college, their careers ending before they'd truly begun.

"Questions will be answered in departmental meetings this afternoon. HR will be available for anyone who needs support. This is a difficult day, but I believe it's necessary for the long-term health of our organization."

Thomas stepped back from the podium. The screen went dark. The lights came up.

No one moved.

Then, slowly, the room began to stir. People stood, gathered their things, moved toward the exits. The silence was broken by fragments of conversationŌĆöshocked, angry, afraid.

Robert remained in his seat.

He thought about the number. The seven digits that represented forty years of work. The three months until retirement.

He thought about the young analysts in the front row. About David, who'd been laid off six months ago and still hadn't found steady work. About the world his son was inheriting, a world where efficiency meant something different than it had when Robert was young.

*Efficiency*, he thought. *Such a clean word for such a messy thing.*

He stood and walked out of the auditorium, past the cubicles where people sat staring at their screens, past the conference rooms where meetings were already being scheduled, past the break room where someone was crying.

In his office, he sat at his desk and looked out the window. The parking lot was half-empty. It was only 9:30 AM, but it felt like the end of something.

His phone buzzed. A text from Margaret: *How was the meeting?*

He stared at it for a long moment. Then he typed: *Fine. Just a company update. Nothing to worry about.*

He put the phone down and watched the empty parking spaces, the spaces that would only grow emptier in the months to come.

Three months. Would it be enough?
