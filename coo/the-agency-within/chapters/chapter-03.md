# Chapter 3: The Spark

The regret from the missed opportunity lingered for days. Riley completed assigned tasks, attended meetings, maintained the routine. But something had shifted. The comfort of waiting had been replaced by a restless dissatisfaction.

Thursday afternoon, Riley found herself standing outside Angela Washington's office. Angela was a senior director, someone Riley had admired from a distance. Her presence in meetings was commanding but not intimidating. She spoke with purpose, acted with confidence, seemed to move through the world with an ease that Riley had never known.

The door was open. Angela looked up from her computer.

"Riley, right? Can I help you?"

The words came before Riley could second-guess them. "I was wondering if you had a few minutes. I wanted to ask you something."

Angela's expression shifted to curiosity. "Of course. Come in."

The office was warm and organized. Angela gestured to a chair across from her desk.

"What's on your mind?"

Riley sat, suddenly aware of how unusual this was. Approaching a senior director without an agenda. Asking for time without a specific request. This wasn't how Riley operated.

"I saw you in the leadership meeting yesterday," Riley began. "You proposed that new approach to the quality metrics. It was... impressive. You just spoke up, offered an idea, and everyone listened."

Angela smiled slightly. "Thank you. But I suspect there's more on your mind than flattery."

Riley took a breath. "How do you do that? How do you just... speak up? Take initiative? I've been here two years, and I've never once volunteered for anything. I wait to be assigned. I complete tasks. I meet expectations. And I'm watching opportunities pass me by."

The admission was more honest than Riley had intended. But Angela's expression remained open.

"That's a good question. And it's one I asked myself, about fifteen years ago." Angela leaned back in her chair. "I used to be like you. Waiting for direction. Afraid to overstep. Convinced that my job was to do what I was told."

"What changed?"

"I realized that waiting is a choice. A choice to let others determine your path. A choice to be reactive instead of proactive." Angela paused. "I call it agency. The power to act. To decide what needs to happen and make it happen."

"But how do you know what to do? What if you're wrong?"

"You don't know. You're sometimes wrong. That's part of it." Angela's voice was gentle but direct. "Agency isn't about being right all the time. It's about taking ownership of your choices. Including the choice to try something that might not work."

Riley absorbed this. "I've always thought my job was to wait for direction. That taking initiative was... overstepping."

"That's a common belief. Especially for people who are good at following instructions." Angela smiled. "But here's the thing. The people who advance, who create impact, who build careers worth having, they're not waiting for permission. They're looking for opportunities to contribute. And when they don't see opportunities, they create them."

"How do I start?"

Angela considered the question. "Start small. Look for problems that need solving. Offer solutions instead of just identifying issues. Take one action each day that you weren't told to take."

"That sounds terrifying."

"It is, at first. But the fear diminishes with practice. And the rewards are significant." Angela leaned forward. "Riley, I've noticed your work. You're capable. Organized. Detail-oriented. You could be doing much more than coordinating other people's projects. But no one's going to hand you that opportunity. You have to take it."

The conversation continued for another twenty minutes. Angela shared stories of her own journey, the small initiatives that had built her reputation, the failures that had taught her lessons, the gradual development of confidence that came from action.

Walking back to the desk, Riley felt something unfamiliar. Not certainty, but possibility. The beginning of a different way of seeing.

The rest of the afternoon was spent differently. Instead of waiting for the next assignment, Riley looked at the current projects with new eyes. What problems existed? What improvements could be made? What small actions could be taken without permission?

The answers were there, if Riley looked for them. The project tracking system was inefficient. The meeting scheduling process was cumbersome. The status reports could be more informative.

These weren't dramatic opportunities. They weren't the patient experience initiative that Derek had claimed. But they were something. Places where Riley could act instead of wait.

That evening, the apartment didn't feel as small. The questions that had haunted Riley for weeks now had a different quality. Not just what was wrong, but what could be done.

Agency. The power to act. The choice to stop waiting.

Riley didn't know if this would work. Didn't know if the fear could be overcome. But for the first time, there was a direction. A possibility. A spark.

The morning would come, and with it, the chance to try something different. Not a dramatic transformation, but a small step. One action that wasn't assigned. One choice to act instead of wait.

The waiting game wasn't over. But maybe it didn't have to be the only game Riley played.
