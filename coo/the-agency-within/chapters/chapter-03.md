# Chapter 3: The Spark

Two days after the missed opportunity, Riley sat at their desk, unable to focus. The usual tasks waited—spreadsheets to update, reports to format—but they felt distant, irrelevant. The question that had been circling since Monday wouldn't leave: What would it take?

Riley had never asked for help before. Not like this. Not about career, about growth, about the gnawing sense that something was wrong. But the regret was still fresh, and the pattern was becoming impossible to ignore.

The company directory was open on the screen. Riley scrolled through the names, looking for someone—anyone—who might have answers. And then the cursor stopped on a name that felt right: Angela Washington, Senior Director of Strategic Initiatives.

Riley had never spoken to Angela directly. She was three levels up, in a different department, someone Riley had only observed from a distance. But there was something about the way Angela moved through the office—confident, purposeful, always in motion toward something she had decided mattered.

Without giving time for second thoughts, Riley typed a short email.

*Hi Angela, I'm Riley Thompson, a project coordinator on the operations team. I was wondering if you might have 15 minutes to chat. I'm struggling with something in my career, and I'd value your perspective. No pressure, but if you have time, I'd be grateful.*

Riley hit send before the doubt could surface.

The reply came within an hour.

*Riley, of course. I admire anyone who reaches out. How about Thursday at 2? My office is on the fifth floor, corner suite. Looking forward to it.*

Thursday was tomorrow. Riley stared at the confirmation, suddenly aware of what had just happened. A meeting. With a senior director. About something real.

The familiar voice of caution surfaced: What are you doing? You don't just email senior directors. You don't ask for career advice from people you've never met. You wait for the proper channels, the right introduction, the appropriate moment.

But Riley had been waiting for three years. And the moment hadn't come.

Angela's office was different from what Riley expected. The walls were lined with photos—family gatherings, team celebrations, what looked like a marathon finish line. Books filled the shelves, but they weren't the usual business titles. Philosophy. History. Biographies of people who had changed things.

Angela herself stood by the window, looking out at the Boston skyline. She was in her early fifties, with short gray hair and the kind of presence that filled a room without trying. When she turned, her eyes were warm but sharp, taking in Riley with a single glance.

"Riley. Come in, sit down." She gestured to a chair across from her desk. "I'm glad you reached out."

"Thank you for making time." Riley sat, suddenly aware of how nervous they were. "I'm not sure this is... I mean, I've never done this before."

"Reached out to someone you didn't know for advice?" Angela smiled. "Most people don't. That's why I said yes. The fact that you asked tells me something about you already."

Riley blinked. "What does it tell you?"

"That you're ready to stop waiting." Angela settled into her own chair, her posture relaxed but attentive. "So tell me. What's going on?"

The words came slowly at first, then faster. Riley told Angela about the missed opportunity, about the pattern of waiting, about the comfortable cage of reactivity that had become a prison. Angela listened without interrupting, her expression thoughtful.

When Riley finished, there was a moment of silence. Then Angela nodded slowly.

"You're describing something I see all the time. Good people, capable people, stuck in a pattern of waiting. Waiting for permission, waiting for invitation, waiting for someone to notice they're ready." She leaned forward. "Can I tell you what I've learned about waiting?"

Riley nodded.

"Waiting feels safe. It feels like the right thing to do—like you're being respectful, being professional, not overstepping. But here's what waiting actually is: it's giving away your power. Every time you wait for someone else to decide, you're telling yourself that your judgment doesn't matter. That your initiative doesn't matter. That you need external validation before you can act."

Riley felt the words land like a physical blow. "That's... exactly what it feels like. Like I need permission to matter."

"Agency is the opposite of that." Angela's voice was firm but kind. "Agency is the recognition that you have the power to act. Not because someone gave you permission, but because you're a human being with judgment and capability. You decide what needs to happen, and you make it happen."

"But what if I'm wrong? What if I overstep? What if I try something and fail?"

Angela smiled. "Then you learn. That's how growth happens. Not by waiting for someone to tell you the right answer, but by trying and adjusting. Every successful person I know has a trail of mistakes behind them. The difference is, they didn't let the fear of mistakes stop them from acting."

Riley sat with this. The idea was simple, but it felt revolutionary. Agency wasn't about being perfect. It was about being willing.

"Can I ask you something personal?" Riley said.

"Of course."

"How did you learn this? Were you always... like this? Confident, proactive?"

Angela laughed, a warm sound that filled the office. "God, no. I spent the first decade of my career waiting. Waiting for my boss to notice me, waiting for opportunities to land in my lap, waiting for someone to tell me I was ready. I was good at my job, but I was invisible. Just like you."

"What changed?"

"A mentor said something that stuck with me. She said: 'Angela, no one is coming. No one is going to tap you on the shoulder and say it's your turn. If you want something, you have to reach out and take it.'" Angela's eyes were serious now. "It was the hardest thing I'd ever heard. And the most liberating."

The meeting lasted forty-five minutes. By the time Riley stood to leave, something had shifted—a small crack in the wall of waiting, a glimpse of what might be on the other side.

"Riley," Angela said as they reached the door. "Can I give you one more piece of advice?"

"Please."

"Start small. Agency isn't about taking on the world tomorrow. It's about one small action that you choose. One thing you initiate. One moment where you stop waiting and start acting. The rest builds from there."

Riley nodded. "Thank you. Really. I don't know how to repay this."

"You don't repay it. You pay it forward. Someday, someone will reach out to you the way you reached out to me. And you'll help them." Angela smiled. "That's how it works."

Riley walked back through the office, the conversation echoing in their mind. Agency. Ownership. The power to act.

The words felt strange, unfamiliar. But also... possible. For the first time, Riley could imagine a different way of being. Not waiting for the world to happen, but happening to the world.

What would that look like? What would it feel like to stop asking for permission and start taking action?

Riley didn't have answers yet. But the question had changed. It wasn't "What would it take to break out?" anymore.

It was "What's stopping me?"

In the space where the old answers used to live, something new was forming. A spark. A possibility. A different way forward.

Riley returned to their desk, sat down, and looked at the familiar screen. The spreadsheets and reports were still there. But they looked different now. Not the whole world, just a small part of it.

And Riley was starting to wonder what the rest of the world might look like, if only they had the courage to reach for it.
