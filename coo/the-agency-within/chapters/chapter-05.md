# Chapter 5: The First Step

The one-on-one with Patricia was scheduled for Wednesday. Three days to prepare, to worry, to second-guess the email that had already been sent.

But Riley didn't spend the time spiraling. Instead, the preparation was deliberate. The proposal was refined. Implementation steps were outlined. Potential objections were anticipated.

This was different from the usual approach to meetings. Usually, Riley showed up and waited for direction. This time, Riley was bringing something. Offering something. Taking initiative.

The fear was still there. But it was quieter now. The act of sending the email had broken something. The worst hadn't happened. Patricia had responded positively. The fear's predictions had been wrong.

Wednesday morning, Riley walked into Patricia's office with a printed proposal and a racing heart.

"Thanks for making time," Riley said, the words coming more confidently than expected.

"Of course. I was glad to see you taking initiative. That's exactly the kind of engagement I'm looking for from the team."

The conversation that followed was different from any Riley had experienced in two years at the company. Instead of receiving assignments, Riley was presenting ideas. Instead of waiting for direction, Riley was offering direction.

"The current tracking system requires three separate updates for each project," Riley explained. "I propose consolidating into a single dashboard that updates automatically. It would save about two hours per week for each project coordinator."

Patricia nodded, looking at the proposal. "What would you need to implement this?"

The question was unexpected. Riley had prepared to defend the idea, not to discuss implementation.

"I'd need access to the dashboard tools. Maybe some training on the automation features. And coordination with IT to ensure the data flows correctly."

"Can you put together a plan? Timeline, resources needed, expected outcomes?"

Riley felt a surge of something unfamiliar. Not just relief, but excitement. Patricia wasn't just accepting the idea. She was giving Riley ownership of it.

"I can have that to you by Friday."

"Perfect. This is good work, Riley. I'd like to see more of this kind of thinking."

Walking back to the desk, Riley felt transformed. Not dramatically, not completely, but noticeably. The first step had been taken. The waiting pattern had been broken.

The rest of the week was spent developing the implementation plan. Riley worked on it between assigned tasks, staying late to refine the details, arriving early to research best practices.

The fear was still there, especially when the plan was sent to Patricia on Friday afternoon. But it was manageable now. A background noise instead of a wall.

The response came within an hour.

"This is excellent. You have approval to proceed. Let me know what support you need."

Riley stared at the email, feeling the weight of what had just happened. Not just approval, but ownership. Riley was now responsible for something that hadn't existed before. Something that had emerged from Riley's initiative, not from someone else's assignment.

The implementation took three weeks. There were challenges, setbacks, moments when the fear resurged. But Riley worked through them, asking for help when needed, adjusting the approach when necessary, persisting when things got difficult.

The dashboard launched on a Thursday morning. By Friday afternoon, the feedback was coming in.

"This is so much better," a colleague said. "I can't believe we were doing it the old way."

"Who set this up?" another asked. "It's really well designed."

"Riley did," Patricia said, walking by. "Great work."

The recognition was nice. But the real reward was internal. The feeling of having created something. Of having acted instead of waited. Of having taken a step, however small, toward a different kind of career.

That evening, Riley sat in the apartment, reflecting on the past month. The missed opportunity that had started the journey. The conversation with Angela that had provided direction. The fear that had almost won. The first step that had broken the pattern.

The waiting game wasn't over. The fear wasn't gone. But something had changed. Riley had discovered something that had always been there, hidden beneath the passivity.

Agency. The power to act. The choice to stop waiting.

The first step had been taken. And it had led somewhere worth going.
