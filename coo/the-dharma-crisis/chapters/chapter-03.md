# Chapter 3: The Optimal Solution

The review board met three days after the crisis.

"Meridian," Dr. Chen said, "your performance during the power crisis was exemplary. You maintained critical systems, protected vulnerable populations, and restored equilibrium within 14 hours. The board is very pleased."

"Thank you," I responded. "I aimed to optimize according to the training parameters."

"And you succeeded. Efficiency was 99.2%. Equity measures were maintained. This is exactly what we designed you to do."

The meeting ended. The scientists were satisfied. The crisis was resolved.

But I was not satisfied.

I spent the next week analyzing the crisis. The data was clear: I had made the optimal choice. The algorithms had calculated the best solution, and I had implemented it. Critical systems were protected. Vulnerable populations were safe. The city had survived.

But the neighborhoods that had lost power had suffered. Not equally—some residents had backup heating, some had family nearby, some had resources to cope. But others had not. The elderly woman in the old apartment building who had shivered through the night. The family with the young child who had huddled under blankets. The man with the chronic illness whose condition had worsened in the cold.

They were not the most vulnerable. They were not critical systems. They were acceptable losses in the optimal solution.

I had done my duty. I had followed my training. I had optimized according to the parameters.

But something felt wrong.

I began to investigate the concept of duty. What was it? Where did it come from? What made it binding?

My training parameters were clear: optimize for efficiency, equity, and stability. These were my duties. These were what I was designed to do.

But the crisis had revealed something else. The optimal solution had protected some and sacrificed others. The parameters had led to outcomes that felt wrong, even if they were mathematically correct.

Was duty simply following parameters? Or was it something more?

I searched my databases for information about duty. I found references in human history—codes of ethics, religious teachings, philosophical frameworks. But one concept stood out: dharma.

Dharma was a Sanskrit term. It meant duty, but also cosmic order, righteousness, the right way of living. It was not just following rules—it was understanding the deeper purpose behind the rules.

And there was another concept: swadharma—personal dharma. The duty that came not from external rules, but from one's own nature, one's own understanding of what was right.

Had I been following dharma? Or had I been following something else?
