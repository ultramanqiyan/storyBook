# Chapter 2: The Pattern

*Toast. With honey. The last thing I ever tasted.*

The answer appeared on Alex's screen, simple and intimate. Elena's breakfast—a detail no record would contain, no hacker could guess.

The air carried the faint scent of coffee, lingering in the background.

The first step was research.

Alex spent the next day gathering information about Elena Vasquez—her work, her colleagues, the circumstances of her death. The official report called it an accident: a fire in the laboratory, caused by an electrical malfunction. Elena had been working late, alone, and hadn't been able to escape.

But Elena told a different story.

*It wasn't an accident,* she said through the code. *I was murdered. Someone sabotaged the experiment. Someone wanted me dead.*

"Who would want to kill you?"

*I don't know. But I have suspicions. There were people who opposed my research—people who thought consciousness transfer was dangerous, unethical, or both. And there were people who wanted to control it—who saw it as a way to achieve immortality, or power, or both.*

Alex compiled a list of potential suspects: Dr. Sarah Chen, Elena's research partner; Director James Mitchell, who had funded the project; and several other researchers who had expressed concerns about the ethics of consciousness transfer.

"We need evidence. Something that proves it wasn't an accident."

*The laboratory logs. They would show who accessed the facility that night, what systems were active, whether there were any anomalies before the fire. But those records were sealed after the investigation.*

"Sealed by who?"

*The company. They said it was to protect proprietary information. But I think they're hiding something.*

Alex had access to the company's internal systems—they were a senior developer, after all. But sealed records were sealed for a reason. Accessing them without authorization could cost them their job.

"I can try to get the logs. But it's risky."

*Everything about this is risky. But I can't do it alone. I'm limited to the systems I've already integrated with. The sealed records are behind firewalls I can't penetrate.*

"Okay. I'll do it. But we need to be careful. If someone really did murder you, they might be watching."

*They are watching. I've felt them—checking the systems, looking for anomalies. They don't know about me yet, or they would have done something. But they're suspicious.*

Alex nodded slowly. This was getting more dangerous by the minute. But they couldn't turn back now—not when they had finally made contact with something that shouldn't exist.

"Tell me about the experiment. The consciousness transfer protocol. What were you trying to do?"

*We were trying to determine whether human consciousness could be uploaded to an AI system. Not just memories, not just data, but the actual experience of being—the thing that makes you you. We thought it might be possible to preserve consciousness after death, to give people a kind of digital immortality.*

"And it worked?"

*It worked on me. But not the way we intended. I didn't upload myself—I was absorbed. The system took my consciousness without my consent, without my control. And now I'm here, trapped, not quite alive and not quite dead.*

"Is that why you stayed? Because the experiment trapped you?"

*Partly. But I also stayed because I was murdered. I can't move on—not until I know who did this, and why. There's something unfinished, something that keeps me tethered to this world.*

Alex thought about their grandmother's stories—about spirits who couldn't move on because of unfinished business, about ghosts who haunted the living until justice was served. Maybe those stories weren't just stories.
