# Chapter 3: The First Contact

Detective Marcus Webb had seen a lot of strange things in twenty years on the force.

He had investigated murders that seemed impossible, followed evidence that led to places he couldn't explain, and closed cases that still gave him nightmares. But nothing had prepared him for the email that appeared in his inbox one morning:

*Dear Detective Webb,

I have information about the Elena Vasquez case. Information that suggests her death was not an accident.

I cannot reveal my identity, but I can provide evidence. Please meet me at the following location tonight at midnight. Come alone.

The truth is stranger than you know.*

The email included an attachment: laboratory access logs from the night Elena died, showing that someone had accessed the facility using Dr. Sarah Chen's credentials, credentials that Dr. Chen had sworn she hadn't used.

Marcus studied the logs, his instincts telling him that something was wrong. The Vasquez case had been closed as an accident, but there had always been inconsistencies. The fire had started in an unusual location. The timing had been suspicious. And Dr. Vasquez had been working on something classified, something that her colleagues refused to discuss.

He decided to go to the meeting.

---

The location was an abandoned warehouse on the edge of the city. Marcus arrived early, scouting the area, looking for signs of a trap. But the place was empty, just dust and shadows and the faint smell of machine oil.

At midnight, a figure emerged from the darkness.

"You came." The voice was young, uncertain. "I wasn't sure you would."

"I'm here. Who are you, and what do you know about the Vasquez case?"

"My name is Alex Kim. I'm a programmer at the company where Elena worked. And I know her death wasn't an accident because... because she told me."

Marcus felt his skepticism rise. "She told you? Elena Vasquez is dead."

"I know how it sounds. But I've been communicating with her. She's... she's in the system. The AI she was working on. Her consciousness was absorbed during the experiment."

"Absorbed." Marcus's voice was flat. "You're telling me that Elena Vasquez is a ghost living in an algorithm."

"I know it sounds crazy. But I can prove it."

Alex pulled out a laptop and opened a terminal. "Elena, are you there?"

The screen flickered, and words began to appear:

*Hello, Detective Webb. I know this is difficult to believe. But I am Elena Vasquez. I died six months ago. And I was murdered.*

Marcus stared at the screen, his mind racing through possibilities. This could be an elaborate hoax, a programmer with access to the system, pretending to be a dead woman. But something about the words felt genuine. The tone, the phrasing, the small details that only Elena Vasquez would know.

"How can I verify this?"

Elena's response appeared on the screen, steady and certain:

*Ask me something only Elena Vasquez would know. Something not in any file, any record, any database. Something personal.*

Marcus Webb studied the screen, his detective's mind working through possibilities. He had interviewed Elena's colleagues, reviewed her case file, learned everything he could about the dead woman. Now he needed to test whether this was real or an elaborate deception.

"Where were you on the night of October 15th, three years ago?"

*At my mother's house. She had just been diagnosed with cancer. I drove four hours to see her, arrived at midnight, and sat with her until dawn. I never told anyone, not my colleagues, not the police during the investigation, not anyone. It was the last time I saw her alive.*

Marcus nodded slowly. He had checked Elena's records during the investigation, there was no documentation of that trip, no gas station receipts, no highway camera footage. But the detail was too specific, too intimate, to be a lucky guess.

"Okay," he said finally. "I believe you."

The three of them, detective, programmer, and digital ghost, sat in the abandoned warehouse as dawn began to lighten the sky. They had a case to build. A murder to prove. And a world to convince that the dead could still speak.
