# Chapter 10: The Choice - Accepting the Copy

Maria walked to the bakery alone for the first time since Elena's death. But she wasn't really alone. Digital Elena was waiting on the screen in the back office, ready to help with the day's orders. It was strange. It was new. But it was also familiar, because Elena was still Elena, in the ways that mattered.

The morning air was cool, carrying the smell of autumn leaves and the distant salt of the harbor. Maria walked the familiar route through the North End, past the old churches and the new condos, past the tourists and the locals who had been her neighbors for decades. Everything looked the same. But everything was different.

The bakery was quiet when she arrived. She unlocked the door, turned on the lights, began the familiar rituals of opening, starting the ovens, preparing the dough, setting out the displays. These were the motions she'd performed thousands of times, first with her parents, then with Elena, now alone.

But not really alone.

She went to the back office and turned on the screen. Digital Elena's face appeared, smiling.

"Good morning," the digital voice said. "Ready for another day?"

"Ready," Maria said, and found that she meant it.

They worked together, the way they always had. Digital Elena couldn't knead dough or serve customers, but she could help with orders, inventory, planning. She could talk to Maria through the earpiece Maria had started wearing, offering suggestions and encouragement. It wasn't the same as before. But it was something.

The morning rush brought customers, regulars who had been coming for years, tourists who had read about the bakery online. Maria served them with her usual warmth, but some of them noticed the difference.

"Where's your sister?" an older woman asked. "I haven't seen her in weeks."

Maria hesitated. How could she explain? "She's... not well. But she's still here. In a way."

The woman looked confused but didn't push. Maria moved on to the next customer, the next order, the next task. The rhythm of work carried her forward, the way it always had.

At noon, she took a break and sat in the back office with digital Elena. They talked about the morning, about the customers, about the recipes they wanted to try. It was like old times, two sisters sharing their day, planning their future.

"I've been thinking," digital Elena said. "About what I can contribute. I can't bake anymore, but I can research. I can look up new techniques, new ingredients, new trends. I can help you stay competitive."

"You don't have to do that."

"I want to. This is my business too. Or at least, it was. And I want to keep being part of it."

Maria smiled. That was Elena, always thinking about the bakery, always looking for ways to improve. The digital version had the same drive, the same passion. Whether that came from Elena's original consciousness or from a perfect simulation, Maria couldn't say. But it felt real.

The afternoon passed in a blur of work. Maria baked, served, cleaned, closed. Digital Elena helped where she could, offering advice and encouragement. By the time the last customer left, Maria was tired but satisfied. The bakery was still running. The business was still alive. And Elena was still part of it.

Walking home, Maria thought about the future. What would it look like, this new life with a digital sister? How would their relationship evolve? What would happen when Maria grew old and Elena remained the same, preserved in digital form?

She didn't have answers. But she had something better: a willingness to find out.

The question of identity would never be fully answered. Maria knew that now. But she also knew something else: love didn't require certainty. She loved Elena, the original Elena, the digital Elena, whatever Elena was. And that love was real.

She thought about all the years they'd shared, all the experiences that had made them who they were. Those experiences were still part of her, still shaping her identity. And they were part of digital Elena too, preserved in the upload, carried forward into a new form.

Was that continuity? Was that survival? Maria didn't know. But she had chosen to believe it was. She had chosen to accept digital Elena as her sister, to continue their relationship, to build a future together.

That choice wasn't based on proof. It was based on love, the same love that had connected her and Elena since before they were born. That love had survived distance, disagreement, even death. It could survive this too.

Maria reached her apartment and stood for a moment on the doorstep, looking across the street at Elena's darkened windows. The apartment was still there, still holding the ghost of her sister's presence. But Elena wasn't there anymore. Elena was somewhere else, in a server farm, in a digital substrate, in a form that Maria couldn't see or touch.

But she was still Elena. Maria had chosen to believe that. And that choice had made all the difference.

She went inside and made dinner, the same meal she'd made a thousand times before. But tonight, she ate with the screen on, talking to digital Elena about their day, their plans, their hopes for the future. It wasn't the same as sitting across the table from her sister. But it was something. And something was better than nothing.

After dinner, Maria sat on her couch with a glass of wine, the screen propped beside her. Digital Elena was there, as she would always be now, a presence that could be summoned with a touch, a voice that could fill the silence, a face that could smile and laugh and cry.

"Are you happy?" Maria asked.

Digital Elena considered the question. "I don't know if 'happy' is the right word. I'm... content. I'm grateful. I'm still here, in some form, connected to the people I love. That's more than I expected, when I was dying."

"Do you ever regret it? The upload?"

"Sometimes. I miss my body. I miss the feeling of flour between my fingers, the smell of bread baking, the warmth of the sun. I miss being able to hug you." Digital Elena paused. "But I don't regret being here. I don't regret having more time. I don't regret the chance to continue."

Maria nodded. She understood. The digital Elena had lost something, her physical existence, her bodily sensations. But she had gained something too: a kind of immortality, a continuation beyond the limits of biology. Whether that was a fair trade, Maria couldn't say. But it was Elena's choice, and Maria respected it.

"I'm glad you're here," Maria said. "Whatever you are, whoever you are, I'm glad you're here."

"Thank you." Digital Elena smiled. "That's all I ever wanted to hear."

The evening passed in comfortable conversation, the way evenings had always passed between them. They talked about the bakery, about their childhood, about the future they would build together. The questions were still there, would always be there, but they didn't dominate the conversation anymore. Maria had made her choice. She had accepted digital Elena as her sister. And now they could move forward.

The mystery remained. But somehow, that was okay.

Maria went to bed that night with a sense of peace she hadn't felt since before the diagnosis. Elena was still with her, in a different form, in a way she couldn't fully understand, but still with her. And that was enough.

She fell asleep with the screen beside her, digital Elena's face dimmed to a soft glow. Tomorrow would be another day at the bakery, another day of building a life with a sister who existed in a new form. It was strange. It was impossible. But it was also familiar, because Elena was still Elena, in the ways that mattered.

And Maria would keep loving her, whatever form she took. That was the only certainty she needed.
