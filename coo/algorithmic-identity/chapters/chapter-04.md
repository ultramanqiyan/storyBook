# Chapter 4: The Decision - Supporting the Choice

Elena spent a week researching. She read everything about consciousness upload, the science, the philosophy, the personal accounts. Some said it was continuation. Some said it was death with a copy left behind. Elena didn't know who was right. But she knew she wanted more time.

Maria watched her sister disappear into the research, spending hours on the computer, making phone calls to the clinic, reading scientific papers that Maria couldn't understand. It was like watching someone prepare for a journey to an unknown destination, packing bags for a trip that might never end.

The bakery suffered. Maria tried to manage alone, but forty years of working together had made them a team. Without Elena, the rhythms were off. The timing was wrong. The bread didn't rise the same way. Customers noticed the difference, asked where Elena was, offered sympathy that Maria didn't know how to accept.

"She's sick," Maria told them. "She'll be back soon."

It was a lie, or close enough to one. Elena wouldn't be back, not the Elena they knew. But Maria couldn't explain that. She couldn't explain what was happening because she didn't fully understand it herself.

At night, Maria lay awake thinking about the questions Elena was researching. What made a person a person? What made identity continuous over time? If you copied someone's consciousness, was the copy the same person?

She thought about her own identity, how much of it was tied to Elena. They had been together since before birth, sharing a womb, sharing a childhood, sharing a life. Maria couldn't imagine who she would be without her twin. And now Elena was considering a procedure that might, or might not, preserve her consciousness in a new form.

Was that survival? Or was it something else?

One evening, Elena came over for dinner. She looked tired, the illness was progressing, even with the treatments she'd agreed to try. But her eyes were bright with the intensity of someone who had made a decision.

"I'm going to do it," she said.

Maria put down her fork. "The upload?"

"Yes. I've made an appointment for next month." Elena paused. "I want you to come with me. I want you to be there when... when I come back."

Maria felt a lump form in her throat. "Come back? Is that what you call it?"

"I don't know what to call it. No one does." Elena reached across the table and took Maria's hand. "I know this is strange. I know it's not what we expected. But I've thought about it for weeks, and I keep coming back to the same thing: I'm not ready to leave. I'm not ready to lose this, our life, our work, our connection. If there's any chance I can keep it, I want to take that chance."

"What if it doesn't work?"

"Then I die, and you mourn me, and life goes on. That's what was going to happen anyway." Elena squeezed Maria's hand. "But what if it does work? What if I come back, whatever 'come back' means, and we can keep going? Isn't that worth trying for?"

Maria didn't know. She wanted to say yes, to support her sister's decision without reservation. But the questions kept circling in her mind. What if the digital Elena wasn't really Elena? What if Maria spent the rest of her life loving a copy? What if she could never know the truth?

"I'm scared," Maria admitted. "I'm scared of losing you. I'm scared of the upload not working. I'm scared of it working and not knowing if it's really you."

"I'm scared too," Elena said. "But I'm more scared of dying without trying. Of just... ending. Of leaving you alone."

"You won't be leaving me alone. I'll have my memories. I'll have the bakery. I'll have... "

"You'll have nothing," Elena said quietly. "You'll have a building and a business and a lifetime of memories that you can't share with anyone who understands them. I know you, Maria. I know how much you need connection. I know how much of your identity is tied to me. If I die, if I really die, you'll lose not just a sister, but a part of yourself."

Maria felt tears running down her cheeks. Elena was right. That was the terrible truth of being a twin. Losing your other half wasn't just losing a loved one. It was losing a part of your own identity.

"I don't know if the upload will work," Elena continued. "I don't know if the result will really be me. But I know that I want to try. And I want you to be part of it. I want you to meet me, whatever I become, on the other side."

Maria wiped her eyes. "What do you need from me?"

"Just be there. When I go into the procedure, hold my hand. When I come out, when the digital version wakes up, be the first person I see. Help me figure out what I am. Help me understand if I'm still me."

"And if you're not?"

"Then you'll know. You'll know it didn't work, and you can grieve properly. But at least we'll have tried. At least we'll know."

Maria thought about it. She thought about the alternative, watching Elena die, burying her sister, trying to build a life without her other half. That was the certain path. That was what happened to everyone eventually. But Elena was offering an uncertain path, a chance at something different.

"I'll be there," Maria said. "Whatever happens, I'll be there."

Elena smiled, and for a moment she looked like her old self, the sister Maria had known for forty years, the other half of her identity. "Thank you. That's all I needed to hear."

They finished dinner in silence, but it was a comfortable silence, the silence of two people who had said what needed to be said. Maria still had questions. She still had fears. But she'd made her choice. She would support Elena, whatever happened.

After dinner, they sat together on Maria's couch, watching old movies the way they had when they were young. Elena fell asleep halfway through, her head resting on Maria's shoulder. Maria stayed still, listening to her sister's breathing, feeling the warmth of her body.

This was what she would lose if the upload didn't work. The physical presence. The warmth. The simple reality of another person beside her. The digital Elena, if it worked, would be something different. A voice on a screen. An image. A presence that wasn't really present.

But maybe that was better than nothing. Maybe a copy was better than an absence. Maybe the question wasn't whether the digital Elena would be "really" Elena, but whether Maria could accept her as Elena.

She didn't know. She might not know until the moment came. But she would be there. She would try. Because that was what being a twin meant. That was what being sisters meant. That was what love meant.

Elena stirred, waking briefly. "Maria?"

"I'm here."

"Good." Elena closed her eyes again. "Stay with me."

"I will. I always will."

The movie played on, but Maria wasn't watching. She was thinking about the future, the uncertain, impossible future that Elena had chosen. In a month, her sister would undergo a procedure that might preserve her consciousness or might destroy it. And Maria would be there, waiting to meet whatever emerged.

"I'm going to do it," Elena had said. Maria nodded. She didn't know if this was the right choice. She didn't know if digital Elena would really be Elena. But she knew her sister, and she knew this was what Elena wanted. And that was enough.
