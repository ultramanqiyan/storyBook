# Chapter 1: The Twin - Two Halves of One

Maria Rossi rolled out the dough while Elena measured the flour. They moved in perfect synchronization—reaching for the same tool, anticipating each other's needs, speaking in half-sentences that only the other understood. Being a twin was like having another self.

The bakery had been theirs for twenty years, ever since they'd taken over from their parents. Rossi's Panetteria sat on a corner in Boston's North End, a neighborhood that still felt like the Italy their grandparents had left behind. The morning rush was just beginning—regulars stopping for coffee and pastry, tourists drawn by the smell of fresh bread, locals who had been coming here for generations.

Maria formed the dough into loaves while Elena prepared the fillings for the cornetti. They didn't need to speak; each knew what the other was doing, what needed to be done next. It had always been this way between them—a connection that went beyond words, beyond thought, into something deeper.

"More sugar in the apricot," Maria said, not looking up from her work.

"I know," Elena replied. "I can taste it too."

They laughed together, the same laugh they'd shared since childhood. People often couldn't tell them apart—same dark hair, same brown eyes, same dimpled smile. But Maria had always known which one she was, just as Elena had always known which one she was. Being a twin didn't mean being the same. It meant being two who shared a beginning.

The morning passed in a blur of work. Maria worked the counter while Elena managed the ovens. They switched places without discussing it, each moving to where she was needed. Customers remarked on it sometimes—the way the Rossi twins seemed to read each other's minds. Maria would smile and say it was just practice. But she knew it was more than that.

At noon, they took their break together, as they always did. They sat at the small table in the back, sharing a plate of pasta and a bottle of wine. Elena had made the sauce that morning—her special recipe with the secret ingredient she'd never written down. Maria had baked the bread. They ate in comfortable silence, the way people who have known each other forever can eat without needing to fill the space with words.

"You're coughing again," Maria said finally.

Elena waved her hand dismissively. "It's nothing. Allergies."

"It's been two months."

"I'll make an appointment." Elena took a sip of wine. "Stop worrying."

Maria wanted to push, but she knew her sister. Elena didn't like to talk about things that frightened her. And something about this cough—the way it had lingered, the way Elena tried to hide it—frightened Maria more than she wanted to admit.

Instead, she changed the subject. "Did you hear from Marco?"

Their younger brother had moved to California years ago, chasing a tech career that had made him rich and distant. He called on holidays, sent gifts on birthdays, but he wasn't really part of their lives anymore. Not the way they were part of each other's.

"He sent an email," Elena said. "Something about a new project. I didn't really understand it."

"He should visit."

"He's busy."

"We're busy too. We still make time for family."

Elena smiled. "You're the one who always says family isn't about time. It's about connection."

"Connection requires presence."

"Then he should visit." Elena shrugged. "But he won't. He's made his choice."

Maria thought about their brother—how different he was from them, how he'd never understood the bond they shared. He'd grown up in the same house, with the same parents, eating the same meals. But he'd never been a twin. He'd never known what it was like to have another person so deeply woven into your identity that you couldn't tell where you ended and they began.

The afternoon brought more customers, more work, more of the rhythm that had defined their lives for decades. Maria lost herself in the familiar motions—kneading dough, shaping loaves, serving customers. Elena worked beside her, as she always had, as she always would.

But Maria noticed the coughs. The way Elena tried to suppress them. The slight pallor beneath her sister's olive skin. The pauses between movements that had once been seamless. Something was wrong. She could feel it the way she felt everything about Elena—in her bones, in her blood, in the place where their connection lived.

At five o'clock, the last customer left. Maria began cleaning the counter while Elena started the final batch of bread for tomorrow. They worked in silence, the comfortable silence of two people who had spent a lifetime together.

"Maria," Elena said finally.

"Yes?"

"I made an appointment. For next week."

Maria's hands stilled on the counter. "Good."

"Will you come with me?"

"Of course." There was no question. There was never any question. Where one went, the other followed. It had been that way since they were born, two months premature, holding each other's hands in the incubator. The nurses had said they'd never seen anything like it—twins who couldn't bear to be separated, who calmed each other's cries, who seemed to draw strength from each other's presence.

That bond had only grown stronger over the years. They'd shared a room until they were thirty, when Elena had finally moved across the street to her own apartment. They'd opened the bakery together, worked side by side every day, built a life that was as much shared as individual. Maria couldn't imagine existing without Elena. She wasn't sure she would know who she was.

The bakery closed at six. Maria and Elena walked home together, as they had every day for forty years. They talked about tomorrow's orders, about the new flour supplier, about nothing in particular. They didn't talk about Elena's persistent cough, or the appointment she had next week. Some things were too frightening to name.

At the corner where they always parted—Elena to her apartment across the street, Maria to the house they'd grown up in, the house she'd inherited when their parents passed—they stopped.

"See you tomorrow," Elena said.

"Tomorrow," Maria agreed.

They hugged, the way they always did, holding on a moment longer than necessary. Maria felt the thinness of her sister's frame, the slight tremor in her hands. She wanted to say something—to name the fear that lived in her chest, to demand that Elena tell her the truth. But she knew her sister. Elena would talk when she was ready. And until then, Maria would wait.

She watched Elena cross the street and disappear into her building. Then she went inside her own home, alone with her thoughts and her fears and the terrible certainty that something was coming, something that would change everything.

That night, Maria lay in bed, unable to sleep. Across the street, she could see the light in Elena's window. Her twin was awake too. They'd shared everything for forty years—a womb, a childhood, a business, a life. And now Maria felt, with a certainty she couldn't explain, that everything was about to change.

She didn't know what the appointment would reveal. She didn't know what the future held. But she knew one thing: whatever came, she would face it with Elena. Because that was what being a twin meant. That was what being sisters meant. That was what love meant.

The light in Elena's window went out. Maria closed her eyes and tried to sleep, tried not to think about tomorrow, tried not to imagine a world without her other half.
