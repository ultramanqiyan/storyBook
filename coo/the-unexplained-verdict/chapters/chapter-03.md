# Chapter 3: The Distance

The stomach tightening stopped coming.

Jennifer noticed it first on a Tuesday, processing a claim that should have triggered the familiar sensation, a complex fraud case, the kind that used to make her body react before her mind caught up. She read through the file, analyzed the data, made her decision.

But her stomach stayed quiet.

*That's strange*, she thought. *I used to feel these.*

She tried to remember the last time her body had reacted to a claim, the last time her shoulders had tensed on a difficult case, the last time her chest had tightened on a denial, the last time her hands had trembled on a signature. The memories were there, but they felt distant, like stories she'd heard about someone else.

*When did I stop feeling my work?*

The question haunted her through the afternoon. She sat at her desk, the fluorescent lights humming overhead, the heating system clicking in the walls. Her colleagues worked around her in their own bubbles of concentration, each person processing their own queues, each person trusting their own algorithms.

She processed claims with her usual efficiency, approve, deny, investigate, but the physical sensations that had once accompanied each decision were absent. Her body was calm, her breathing was even, her hands were steady. By every metric that mattered, she was doing her job well.

But she felt like a spectator.

*This is what efficiency looks like*, she told herself. *This is what Marcus wants. This is what the algorithm enables.*

But the words rang hollow. She'd become a claims agent because she wanted to help people, because she believed in the weight of each decision, because she trusted her body to tell her when something was wrong.

Now her body was silent, and she wasn't sure what that meant.

---

She tried an experiment.

The next morning, she deliberately ignored the ClaimSmart assessment on her first ten claims. She read each file carefully, studied the evidence, tried to form her own judgment before looking at the algorithm's verdict.

The results were humbling.

Her assessments matched ClaimSmart's only 60% of the time. On three claims, she would have made the wrong decision, approving a fraudulent claim, denying a legitimate one, missing a pattern the algorithm had caught. On four others, she was uncertain, unable to reach a verdict without the data's guidance.

*I'm getting worse*, she realized. *Or the algorithm is making me worse.*

The thought was terrifying. She'd spent twelve years building her expertise, developing the judgment that made her good at her job. Now, after two years of relying on ClaimSmart, that expertise was fading, like a muscle that had atrophied from disuse.

*What have I done to myself?*

She stared at the claims on her screen, the algorithm's verdicts glowing beside her own uncertain assessments. The gap between them was visible now, measurable. The AI was better than her. Faster, more accurate, more consistent.

*So what am I for?*

The question sat in her chest like a stone. She had no answer.

---

She didn't tell anyone.

Not Marcus, who praised her efficiency. Not her colleagues, who were all using the same system. Not her husband, who wouldn't understand why she was upset about being good at her job.

Instead, she kept processing claims, kept signing verdicts, kept pretending that nothing was wrong.

But inside, she was screaming.

The distance grew with each passing day. She felt further from her work, further from the people behind the claims, further from the reasoning behind the verdicts, further from the agent she used to be. The algorithm made decisions, she signed them, and the gap between understanding and action widened.

*This is what cognitive outsourcing feels like*, she thought. *Not a dramatic surrender. Just a slow, quiet emptying.*

She tried to fight it. She started taking notes by hand, recording her observations before looking at the ClaimSmart assessment. She forced herself to explain each verdict out loud, as if she were talking to a client. She walked through the office parking lot on her lunch break, trying to feel the ground beneath her feet, trying to reconnect with something real.

But the distance remained, a hollow space where her expertise used to live.

---

One evening, she found herself staring at a claim she'd processed that morning.

It was a simple case, auto accident, clear liability, straightforward approval. She'd signed it without hesitation, trusting the algorithm's assessment, confident in the verdict.

But now, looking at the file again, she couldn't remember the claimant's name.

*Was it Patterson? Peterson? Something with a P.*

She scrolled through the file, searching for the name. Patricia Wilson. 34 years old. Single mother, two children, driving a 2015 Honda Civic. The accident had been a rear-end collision, the other driver clearly at fault. The claim had been approved, the payment processed.

*Patricia Wilson*, she thought. *I approved her claim this morning, and I can't remember her name.*

The realization hit her like a physical blow. She'd processed dozens of claims that day, signed dozens of verdicts, made dozens of decisions that affected people's lives. And she couldn't remember a single one.

*This is what I've become*, she thought. *A signature. A rubber stamp. A name on a document.*

The distance was complete.

---

That night, she sat at her kitchen table while her husband watched television in the other room. The house was warm, the lights were soft, the life she'd built was all around her. But she felt disconnected from it, as if she were watching herself from a great distance.

"Jennifer?" Her husband's voice came from the living room. "You okay in there?"

"I'm fine," she called back automatically. "Just thinking about work."

"Work's going great, right? You got that commendation last month."

She had. A certificate, a mention in the company newsletter, a small bonus. Recognition for her efficiency, her accuracy, her numbers.

"Yeah," she said. "It's going great."

But the words tasted like ash in her mouth. She had built a career on understanding, on judgment, on the ability to see what the data didn't show. Now she was being rewarded for the opposite, for trusting the data completely, for surrendering her judgment, for becoming a rubber stamp.

*What do I do now?* she wondered. *How do I get back what I've lost?*

The questions had no answers. But she kept asking them, turning them over in her mind like stones in a river.

---

The next morning, she arrived at the office early, before anyone else. The building was quiet, the parking lot nearly empty. She walked through the silent corridors to her office, the fluorescent lights flickering on as she passed.

She sat at her desk and stared at the ClaimSmart interface. The queue was already there, waiting for her attention. Claims from overnight, claims from other time zones, claims that needed decisions.

But for the first time in months, she didn't want to sign them.

She wanted to understand them.

She opened the first claim and began to read, not skimming, not scanning, but actually reading. The claimant's story. The evidence. The factors. The human reality behind the data.

It took her twenty minutes to process that first claim. Twenty minutes of careful attention, of deliberate thought, of trying to rebuild the muscle she'd let atrophy.

Her assessment matched the algorithm's. The claim was legitimate. She signed it.

But this time, the signature meant something different. This time, she understood why.

*This is how I start*, she thought. *One claim at a time. One decision at a time. One step back toward the professional I used to be.*

The distance was still there. The gap between her expertise and the algorithm's was still visible. But for the first time in months, she was trying to close it.

And that, she thought, was something.
