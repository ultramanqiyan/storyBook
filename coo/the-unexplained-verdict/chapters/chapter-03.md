# Chapter 3: The Distance

The stomach tightening stopped coming.

Jennifer noticed it first on a Tuesday, processing a claim that should have triggered the familiar sensation—a complex fraud case, the kind that used to make her body react before her mind caught up. She read through the file, analyzed the data, made her decision.

But her stomach stayed quiet.

*That's strange*, she thought. *I used to feel these.*

She tried to remember the last time her body had reacted to a claim—the last time her shoulders had tensed on a difficult case, the last time her chest had tightened on a denial, the last time her hands had trembled on a signature. The memories were there, but they felt distant, like stories she'd heard about someone else.

*When did I stop feeling my work?*

---

The question haunted her through the afternoon.

She processed claims with her usual efficiency—approve, deny, investigate—but the physical sensations that had once accompanied each decision were absent. Her body was calm, her breathing was even, her hands were steady. By every metric that mattered, she was doing her job well.

But she felt like a spectator.

*This is what efficiency looks like*, she told herself. *This is what Marcus wants. This is what the algorithm enables.*

But the words rang hollow. She'd become a claims agent because she wanted to help people, because she believed in the weight of each decision, because she trusted her body to tell her when something was wrong.

Now her body was silent, and she wasn't sure what that meant.

---

She tried an experiment.

The next morning, she deliberately ignored the ClaimSmart assessment on her first ten claims. She read each file carefully, studied the evidence, tried to form her own judgment before looking at the algorithm's verdict.

The results were humbling.

Her assessments matched ClaimSmart's only 60% of the time. On three claims, she would have made the wrong decision—approving a fraudulent claim, denying a legitimate one, missing a pattern the algorithm had caught. On four others, she was uncertain, unable to reach a verdict without the data's guidance.

*I'm getting worse*, she realized. *Or the algorithm is making me worse.*

The thought was terrifying. She'd spent twelve years building her expertise, developing the judgment that made her good at her job. Now, after two years of relying on ClaimSmart, that expertise was fading—like a muscle that had atrophied from disuse.

*What have I done to myself?*


She didn't tell anyone.

Not Marcus, who praised her efficiency. Not her colleagues, who were all using the same system. Not her husband, who wouldn't understand why she was upset about being good at her job.

Instead, she kept processing claims, kept signing verdicts, kept pretending that nothing was wrong.

But inside, she was screaming.


The distance grew.

Each day, she felt further from her work—further from the people behind the claims, further from the reasoning behind the verdicts, further from the agent she used to be. The algorithm made decisions, she signed them, and the gap between understanding and action widened.

*This is what cognitive outsourcing feels like*, she thought. *Not a dramatic surrender. Just a slow, quiet emptying.*

She tried to fight it. She started taking notes by hand, recording her observations before looking at the ClaimSmart assessment. She forced herself to explain each verdict out loud, as if she were talking to a client. She walked through the office parking lot on her lunch break, trying to feel the ground beneath her feet.

But the distance remained—a hollow space where her expertise used to live.


One evening, she found herself staring at a claim she'd processed that morning.

It was a simple case—auto accident, clear liability, straightforward approval. She'd signed it without hesitation, trusting the algorithm's assessment, confident in the verdict.

But now, looking at the file again, she couldn't remember the claimant's name.

*Was it Patterson? Peterson? Something with a P.*

She scrolled through the file, searching for the name. Patricia Wilson. 34 years old. Single mother, two children, driving a 2015 Honda Civic.

*Patricia Wilson*, she thought. *I approved her claim this morning, and I can't remember her name.*

The realization hit her like a physical blow. She'd processed dozens of claims that day, signed dozens of verdicts, made dozens of decisions that affected people's lives. And she couldn't remember a single one.

*This is what I've become*, she thought. *A signature. A rubber stamp. A name on a document.*

The distance was complete.
