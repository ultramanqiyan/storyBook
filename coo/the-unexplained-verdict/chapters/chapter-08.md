# Chapter 8: The Pretense

The files sat on her desk like accusations.

Jennifer picked up the first one - a claim from three months ago, her signature at the bottom, her name attached to a decision she couldn't explain. She opened it and began to read, really read, for the first time in months. The details were there: the claimant's story, the damage assessment, the AI's verdict. But beneath all of it was her signature, her name, her pretense of understanding.

The Henderson claim. Water damage from a burst pipe. Approved.

She remembered signing it. She remembered the AI's green verdict, the confidence score, the efficiency of the process. What she didn't remember was reading it. What she didn't remember was thinking about it. What she didn't remember was making a decision.

She had simply signed.

She opened the next file. The Kowalski claim. Auto accident, liability disputed. Approved. Her signature was there, bold and confident, attached to a decision the AI had made. Had she read it? Had she considered the evidence? Had she weighed the factors?

She couldn't remember.

The third file. The Ramirez claim. Property damage, claim denied. Her signature again, attached to a rejection she couldn't explain. Why had this claim been denied? What were the reasons? What was the reasoning?

She had no idea.

Jennifer worked through the files one by one, each signature a reminder of what she'd become. David Chen's claim. Mrs. Patterson's denial. The Martinez fraud she couldn't explain. Each one bore her name, her mark, her pretense of understanding.

The stack grew as she worked through the morning. Files she'd processed weeks ago, months ago - each one a decision she'd signed without reading, a verdict she'd attached her name to without understanding. The AI had made the calls. She had provided the signature. That was the arrangement. That was what the system wanted. That was what she'd become.

She paused on a claim from four months ago - one of the first she'd processed entirely through the AI. The claimant was a small business owner, like Maria Martinez. The damage was similar - water, structural, the kind of claim that came through every day. The AI had approved it. She had signed it.

Had it been fraud?

She didn't know. She would never know. The claim was closed, the money paid, the file archived. If it had been fraud, if the AI had been wrong, there was no way to go back and check. She had signed her name to a decision she couldn't defend, and now that decision was part of her record, part of her career, part of who she was.

The weight of it settled in her chest.

She had been doing this job for twelve years. For the first six, before the AI, she had read every claim. She had considered every factor. She had built explanations she could defend, reasoning she could articulate. Those signatures meant something. They represented actual decisions, actual judgments, actual professional work.

Then the AI had arrived, and for a while she had still participated. She had still read the claims, still considered the evidence, still questioned the verdicts, still added her own analysis to the AI's conclusions. The signatures from that period had meaning. They represented decisions she had participated in making.

But somewhere along the way, the participation had faded. The reading had become skimming. The consideration had become acceptance. The questioning had stopped entirely. She had gone from being a partner in the decision-making process to being a rubber stamp.

And she hadn't noticed.

That was the worst part. She hadn't noticed the change. She had thought she was still doing her job - still making decisions, still exercising judgment, still being the professional she'd always been. But the truth was written in these files, in signatures she couldn't explain, in decisions she couldn't defend.

She had been pretending. Pretending to understand. Pretending to decide. Pretending to be the person she used to be.

The heat rose to her face as the realization settled in. She had been lying. Not deliberately - she hadn't set out to deceive anyone. But the effect was the same. Every signature on these files was a lie. Every "I can explain this" was a lie. Every moment of professional confidence was a lie.

She had been lying to her clients. Lying to her colleagues. Lying to herself.

The files spread across her desk, each one a testament to her pretense. She thought about David Chen, sitting across from her, asking his simple question: *Why?* She thought about the confidence with which she'd promised to explain, the professional assurance she'd projected. She had believed it herself - believed that she could articulate the reasoning, that the knowledge was there if she just took the time to access it.

But the knowledge wasn't there. The knowledge had never been there. The AI had made the decision, and she had signed her name to it, and somewhere along the way she had convinced herself that this was the same thing as understanding.

It wasn't.

The horror of it settled in - not just that she couldn't explain, but that she'd pretended she could. Every client meeting, every phone call, every "I can walk you through the factors" - all of it a lie. She had been lying to them, and she had been lying to herself.

She thought about the Martinez claim, sitting open on her screen, fraudulent and approved. She had known it was fraud - her body had known, her instincts had screamed. But she couldn't articulate why. She couldn't defend her knowledge. She couldn't explain what she knew.

And that was the trap. That was what the cognitive outsourcing had cost her. Not just the ability to make decisions, but the ability to defend them. Not just the capacity for judgment, but the capacity for explanation. She had surrendered her professional identity to a machine, and in return she had received... what? Efficiency? Speed? A number on a dashboard that counted her signatures?

The weight of it pressed down on her shoulders. She had done this to herself. No one had forced her. No one had tricked her. She had walked into this trap with her eyes open, choosing efficiency over understanding, choosing speed over depth, choosing the easy path over the right one.

And now she had to face what she'd become.

Jennifer sat with the truth for a long time. The files lay spread across her desk, the Martinez claim glowed on her screen, the office hummed around her with the sounds of people doing their jobs. She was supposed to be doing her job too - processing claims, signing verdicts, maintaining her numbers.

But for the first time in months, she couldn't. The pretense had cracked. The lie had been exposed. She saw herself clearly now - not the professional she'd thought she was, but the rubber stamp she'd become.

The question was: what came next?

She couldn't undo what she'd done. The signatures were already on the files, the decisions already made. She couldn't go back and read the claims she'd skimmed, couldn't add the reasoning she'd skipped, couldn't become the person she used to be.

But she could choose what to do now.

The thought was small, almost invisible beneath the weight of her shame. But it was there. She had made choices that led her here. She could make choices that led her somewhere else.

The Cleveland sky was visible through her window, gray but brightening at the edges. The lake wind carried that familiar smell of industry and water. She couldn't undo what she'd done - the signatures she'd given without reading, the explanations she'd faked, the trust she'd betrayed. But she could choose what to do next.

The question was no longer "what have I become?" The question was "what will I do now?"

---

The afternoon light had shifted by the time Jennifer moved. Her colleagues had come and gone from her peripheral vision, the office filling and emptying with the rhythm of the workday. The queue had grown longer, claims piling up, the system waiting for her to process them.

But she hadn't moved. She had sat with the files, with the truth, with the weight of her own complicity.

She had done this to herself. That was the hardest truth to accept. The AI hadn't taken her judgment - she had given it away. The system hadn't stolen her expertise - she had surrendered it. Every time she signed without reading, every time she approved without thinking, every time she chose efficiency over understanding, she had been making a choice.

And those choices had led her here. To this desk, these files, this moment of reckoning.

She picked up the Martinez file again. The AI's verdict still glowed green on her screen, still confident, still certain. The system had spoken. The system had decided. And she had attached her name to a decision she couldn't defend.

But she could change that. Not for the past - the past was fixed, the signatures already signed. But for this claim. For this moment. For whatever came next.

She would report it. She would flag it for investigation. She would tell someone what she knew - not with the articulation she should have had, but with the honesty she owed to herself and her clients.

And then she would try to remember how to think.

The Cleveland sky was turning gold through her window, the late afternoon light catching the edges of the buildings. She had become a rubber stamp. She had pretended to understand. She had lied to everyone, including herself.

But she was done pretending.

Jennifer closed the files on her desk and turned back to her screen. The Martinez claim waited, fraudulent and approved, the AI confident in its verdict. She would report it. She would flag it for investigation. And when they asked why - when they demanded an explanation - she would have to tell the truth.

She didn't have a good explanation. But she was going to try to find one.

The files sat on her desk like accusations. And for the first time in months, Jennifer was ready to face them.
