# Chapter 8: The Give and Take

The message appeared in the Slack channel on a Wednesday afternoon.

"Hi everyone. I'm new here. I joined the collective last week but I'm feeling really overwhelmed. Everyone seems to know each other already and I feel like I don't belong. Is it normal to feel this way?"

Maya stared at the message, the words striking a chord deep inside her.

The name on the message was "Lisa Chen." Maya didn't recognize it—must be someone who'd joined recently. But she recognized the feeling. The desperate loneliness. The sense of being an outsider looking in.

Two months ago, that had been her.

She started typing a response, then stopped. What could she say? She was still new herself. Still learning. What did she have to offer?

But then she thought about Tomás, who had welcomed her without hesitation. About Rachel, who had answered her 1 AM call without complaint. About all the people who had shown up for her when she'd needed them.

They hadn't waited until they felt qualified. They had just helped.

Maybe that was the point.

She typed: "Lisa, I felt exactly the same way when I first joined. Two months ago, I was completely alone. This community changed my life. Can we grab coffee sometime? I'd love to help you find your place here."

She hit send before she could second-guess herself.

---

Lisa turned out to be a UX designer who had just moved to Denver from Seattle.

She'd left behind a corporate job, a community of friends, and a life that had felt stable. Now she was freelancing in a new city, knowing no one, drowning in the isolation that came with starting over.

"I thought I was doing something wrong," Lisa said, her coffee cup clutched in both hands. "Everyone at the meetup seemed so connected. I felt like I was crashing someone else's party."

Maya nodded. "I know exactly what you mean. When I first walked into that room, I almost turned around and left. Everyone seemed so comfortable, so... together."

"What changed?"

"I stayed." Maya smiled. "And I let myself be honest. I told people I was struggling. I asked for help when I needed it. And I realized that everyone there had felt the same way at some point. The connection wasn't something they had—it was something they'd built."

Lisa's expression shifted. "So it's not just me being awkward?"

"It's all of us being awkward together." Maya laughed. "That's what community is. A bunch of awkward people showing up for each other anyway."

---

Over the next few weeks, Maya found herself doing something she'd never expected.

She was helping people.

Not in a formal way—not as a mentor or a leader or anything that felt too big for her. Just in small, everyday ways. Answering questions in the Slack channel. Offering to review someone's portfolio. Meeting new members for coffee and helping them feel welcome.

Each time she helped someone, she felt something unexpected: joy.

Not the hollow satisfaction of professional achievement. Not the temporary relief of a problem solved. Something deeper. The feeling of being useful. Of mattering to someone. Of being part of something bigger than herself.

"You're really good at this," Rachel told her one afternoon. They were having their weekly coffee date, a ritual that had become one of Maya's favorite parts of the week.

"Good at what?"

"Making people feel welcome. I've noticed you with the new members. You have a way of helping them feel like they belong."

Maya shrugged, embarrassed. "I just remember how it felt to be new. How scary it was. I want to make it easier for them."

"That's exactly what community needs." Rachel smiled. "People who remember what it's like to be on the outside, and who are willing to open the door for others."

---

The realization came during a meetup a few weeks later.

Maya was sitting in the circle, listening to a new member share her struggles with finding clients. The woman was clearly overwhelmed, her voice shaking as she talked about the financial pressure she was under.

Before Maya could think about it, she found herself speaking. "I went through something similar last year. A client crisis that almost broke me. But I reached out to this group, and they helped me through it. You don't have to do this alone."

After the meeting, the woman approached her. "Thank you for sharing that. I've been feeling like such a failure. Hearing that other people have been through the same thing... it helps."

"Can I give you my number?" Maya asked. "If you ever want to talk, or need help with anything, I'm here."

The woman's eyes filled with tears. "That would mean so much. Thank you."

Walking home that night, Maya thought about what had just happened.

She'd spent three years believing she had nothing to offer. That she was too broken, too alone, too much of a mess to be of use to anyone. But here she was, helping people. Making a difference.

Not because she was perfect. Not because she had all the answers. But because she'd been through the darkness and was willing to show others the way out.

*This is what community is,* she thought. *Not just receiving support. Giving it too. Not just being helped. Helping others.*

The cycle of connection. The give and take. The beautiful, messy, human web of people showing up for each other.

She was part of it now. Really part of it. Not just taking, but giving. Not just receiving, but contributing.

And it felt like coming home.

---

That night, Maya sat in her apartment and looked at her phone.

The messages were there—the ones she'd sent and received over the past few weeks. Lisa thanking her for the coffee. The new member grateful for her number. A dozen small exchanges that had added up to something bigger.

She thought about Tomás, who had started this community. Who had opened the door for so many people. Who had shown Maya what connection could look like.

She thought about Rachel, who had answered her desperate 1 AM call. Who had become a real friend. Who had taught her that vulnerability wasn't weakness.

She thought about all the people who had helped her, and all the people she was now helping in return.

*This is what I was missing,* she realized. *Not just people in my life. But purpose. Contribution. Being part of something.*

She opened her laptop and started an email to her mom.

"Hey Mom," she typed. "I wanted to tell you about something. I've found a community here. Real people who care about each other. And I'm not just receiving their help—I'm helping them too. I think... I think I'm finally becoming the person I wanted to be."

She hit send, feeling the weight of the words settle in her chest.

She wasn't fixed. Wasn't perfect. Wasn't suddenly surrounded by dozens of best friends.

But she had people. And she had purpose. And she had a place.

That was more than enough.
