# Chapter 6: The Risk

The email arrived at 11:47 PM on a Friday.

Maya had been getting ready for bed, her mind already shifting to weekend mode, when her phone buzzed with a notification. She glanced at it, expecting a client approval or a calendar reminder.

Instead, she saw: "URGENT: Issue with delivered files - need immediate response."

Her heart sank. She opened the email and read the message from her largest client—the one who had been talking about bringing design in-house, the one who provided a third of her income.

"Maya, we've discovered a significant issue with the files you delivered last month. The print vendor is saying the color profiles are wrong, and the entire print run is unusable. We need to understand what happened and how you're going to fix this. Please call me first thing tomorrow morning."

Maya stared at the screen, her mind racing. The print run. Thousands of dollars. Potentially her biggest client lost.

And she had no idea what had gone wrong.

---

She didn't sleep.

Instead, she sat at her computer, pulling up the files she'd delivered, checking and rechecking the color profiles. Everything looked correct on her end. But clearly something had gone wrong somewhere, and she had no idea where to start looking.

The old patterns kicked in immediately. *I have to fix this myself. I can't let anyone know I messed up. I have to figure this out alone.*

But another voice—the one that had been growing stronger over the past few weeks—whispered: *You don't have to do this alone.*

She thought about Rachel. About Tomás. About the collective she'd just started to be part of.

*They could help. Someone there might know what went wrong. Someone might have been through this before.*

But asking for help meant admitting she'd made a mistake. Meant showing vulnerability. Meant risking rejection.

*What if they think I'm incompetent? What if they decide I'm not worth knowing? What if—*

She stopped herself. This was exactly what Dr. Chen had talked about. The fear of being seen as flawed keeping her from the connection she needed.

She looked at the clock. 1:23 AM. Too late to call anyone. But she could send a message.

She opened the freelancer group's Slack channel and typed: "Hey, has anyone ever dealt with a print color profile issue? I'm in a bit of a crisis and could use some advice."

Her finger hovered over the send button.

*This is stupid. They're going to think I'm an idiot. I should just figure this out myself.*

But she remembered the loneliness she'd felt for so long. The isolation that had almost broken her. The relief of being seen by Rachel and not being rejected.

She hit send.

Then she waited, heart pounding, for a response that might not come.

---

At 1:31 AM, her phone buzzed.

Rachel: "Oh no! What happened? I've dealt with print issues before. Can you call me?"

Maya's eyes filled with tears. She hadn't expected anyone to be awake. Hadn't expected anyone to respond. Hadn't expected the immediate offer of help.

She called Rachel, her voice shaking as she explained the situation.

"Okay, first of all, take a breath," Rachel said. "This is fixable. Print issues happen to everyone. You're not the first designer to have a color profile problem, and you won't be the last."

"But the client—"

"The client is upset, but they want a solution more than they want to blame you. Focus on fixing the problem, and the relationship will follow." Rachel's voice was calm, steady. "Now, tell me exactly what files you sent and what specifications the printer asked for."

For the next hour, Rachel walked Maya through the troubleshooting process. They identified the issue—a mismatch between Maya's working color space and the printer's requirements. They developed a plan for fixing the files and communicating with the client.

By 3 AM, Maya had a clear path forward. Not a perfect solution—the client would still be unhappy about the delay—but a solution.

"Thank you," Maya said, her voice thick. "I don't know what I would have done without you."

"That's what we're here for," Rachel said. "And hey—next time, don't wait until 1 AM to ask for help. We're a community. Use us."

---

The next morning, Maya called the client.

She explained what had happened, took responsibility for the error, and outlined the steps she was taking to fix it. The client was frustrated but appreciated her honesty and quick response.

"We'll need the corrected files by Monday," they said. "And we'll need you to cover the cost of the reprint."

"Of course," Maya said. "I'll have everything to you by Sunday night."

After the call, she sat back in her chair, exhausted but relieved. The crisis wasn't over—she still had work to do, money to pay—but she wasn't drowning anymore.

Her phone buzzed. A message from Tomás in the Slack channel: "Heard you're dealing with a print issue. Let me know if you need help with the rework. I've got some bandwidth this weekend."

Another message from Jake: "I've been through something similar. Happy to review your files before you send them back if you want a second set of eyes."

Another from a designer Maya had never spoken to: "Color profiles are tricky. Here's a checklist I use to make sure everything is correct before sending to print."

Maya stared at the messages, feeling something crack open inside her.

These people—most of whom she barely knew—were offering to help. Not because they had to. Not because she was paying them. But because that's what community did.

*This is what it feels like,* she thought. *Being supported. Not being alone.*

---

By Sunday night, Maya had delivered the corrected files.

The client confirmed receipt and grudgingly acknowledged that the new files looked good. The relationship was damaged but not destroyed. Maya would have to work to rebuild trust, but she had a chance.

She sat in her apartment, looking at the messages still filling her phone. Tomás had reviewed her files. Jake had checked her color profiles. Rachel had talked her through the client conversation. Three other people had offered encouragement and advice.

She'd spent three years believing she had to do everything alone. Three years building walls instead of bridges. Three years drowning in isolation because she was too afraid to ask for help.

And in one weekend, she'd learned something that changed everything.

She didn't have to do it alone. No one did. That was the whole point of community.

She pulled out her phone and typed a message to the group: "Thank you all so much for your help this weekend. I honestly don't know what I would have done without you. I'm so grateful to be part of this community."

The responses came quickly:

"Anytime, Maya. That's what we're here for."

"Glad we could help!"

"You'd do the same for us."

Maya smiled, feeling the warmth spread through her chest.

She would do the same for them. That was the other side of community—not just receiving help, but giving it. Not just being supported, but supporting others.

She was still learning. Still scared sometimes. Still fighting the old patterns that told her to hide, to protect herself, to stay alone.

But now she had something she'd never had before.

People.

And that made all the difference.
