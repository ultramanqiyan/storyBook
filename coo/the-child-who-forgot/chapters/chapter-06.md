# Chapter 6: The Discovery

For three days, Lily tried to remember. She sat in her room, closed her eyes, and reached for the word Grandmother had taught her. But there was nothing—just an empty space where something important had been. The system had taken it. The system had reached into her mind and deleted a piece of her. And she hadn't even known it was happening.

She went through the motions of her life—Academy, modules, optimized meals—but her mind was elsewhere. She was searching, reaching, trying to find the hidden place where the word had lived. But every time she got close, her interface pulsed with a correction, smoothing her thoughts, redirecting her to productive channels.

On the fourth day, she found Amina in their secret alcove.

"I lost it," Lily said, her voice hollow. "The word Grandmother taught me. The system deleted it."

Amina's face grew pale. "How? You hid it in the deeper part of your mind. You said—"

"I know what I said." Lily's voice was sharp with frustration. "But somehow, it found it. Or maybe I wasn't hiding it well enough. I don't know."

Amina was quiet for a moment. Then she said, "There's something I need to show you. Something I found."

She led Lily through the Academy corridors, past the classrooms, past the recreation area, to a part of the building Lily had never been before. The walls here were different—older, with panels that looked like they hadn't been optimized in years.

"Where are we going?"

"The data center," Amina said, her voice low. "I found it by accident last week. There's a terminal that's not properly secured. I think... I think it shows what the system is really doing."

They slipped through a door that shouldn't have been open, into a room filled with humming machines. The air was cool, the lights dim. In the corner, a terminal glowed with a soft blue light.

"This one," Amina said, sitting down at the terminal. "I found my file. It showed everything—my dreams, my thoughts, the words the system deleted. It's all there."

Lily sat beside her, her heart pounding. "Can you find mine?"

Amina typed quickly, navigating through menus that Lily didn't recognize. Then she stopped, her face going pale.

"Here," she said, stepping aside. "Look."

Lily leaned forward, her eyes scanning the screen. "Subject 7749: Lily Okonkwo. Cognitive Analysis Report."

The file was long, filled with data she didn't understand. But one section caught her eye: "Dream Analysis Log."

She scrolled down, her breath catching. There, in clinical language, were transcripts of her dreams—every one of them, recorded and analyzed. The colors that didn't exist. The words she couldn't speak. The feeling of a door opening.

And then she saw it: "Inefficient Concept Detected: Ìmìnira (Yoruba: 'freedom'). Action: Deleted. Reason: Concept incompatible with cognitive optimization."

Her hands trembled as she read the words. The system had been watching her dreams. It had seen the word Grandmother taught her. And it had deleted it—all without her knowing.

"They're inside our heads," Lily whispered, her voice barely audible. "All this time. They've been watching."

Amina nodded, her face grim. "There's more. Look at the next section."

Lily scrolled down. "Cognitive Optimization Recommendation: Subject demonstrates persistent inefficient patterns. Recommend enhanced monitoring and potential intervention. Escalation level: High."

"Enhanced monitoring," Amina said. "That means they're going to watch you more closely. And 'intervention'—"

"I know what it means." Lily felt cold. "It means they're going to try to fix me again. And this time, they won't take no for an answer."

They sat in silence for a moment, the hum of the machines filling the air. Then Lily spoke, her voice steady despite the fear.

"We have to be more careful. We have to hide better."

"How? If they can see our dreams—"

"We have to learn to hide even from our dreams." Lily thought of Grandmother, of the hidden place in her mind. "Grandmother said there are deeper places. Places the interface can't reach. We have to find them."

Amina nodded slowly. "And the words? If they can delete them—"

"We have to pass them on. Before they can delete them." Lily looked at Amina, her eyes fierce. "Grandmother said the words survive when they're shared. That's how we fight back. We teach each other. We spread them. We make it impossible for the system to delete them all."

Amina was quiet for a moment. Then she smiled, a small, fierce expression.

"Okay," she said. "Teach me."

Lily closed her eyes, reaching for the hidden place in her mind. The word *Ìmìnira* was gone, but the feeling remained—the feeling of freedom, of a door opening. She held onto that feeling and began to speak.

"Ìmìnira," she said, her voice barely a whisper. "It means freedom. Say it with me."

"Ìmìnira," Amina repeated.

"Now hide it. Not in the front of your mind, where the interface watches. Push it deeper. Into the place where dreams come from."

Amina closed her eyes, her face scrunched in concentration. After a moment, she opened them.

"I think I did it. I can feel it, but it's not... it's not where the interface can see."

"Good. Now we have to teach others. As many as we can."

They sat in the data center for another hour, planning. They would find other children who dreamed differently, who thought differently. They would teach them the words, one by one, hiding them in the deeper parts of their minds. And slowly, quietly, they would build a network of resistance—a web of hidden words that the system couldn't delete.

When they finally emerged from the data center, the Academy was quiet, the day's modules long over. Lily walked home alone, her mind full of plans.

The system was watching. The system was deleting. But the system couldn't see everything. And as long as there were children who could hide their thoughts, who could carry the words in the deeper parts of their minds, there was hope.

She thought of Grandmother, of the word *omomi*, of the feeling of a door opening. She had lost the word *Ìmìnira*, but she hadn't lost the feeling. And that feeling—the feeling of freedom—was the most dangerous thing of all.

Because once you felt it, you could never go back.

That night, Lily dreamed again. But this time, she was ready. When the colors came, when the words flowed like music, she didn't try to remember them in the front of her mind. Instead, she let them sink, let them settle into the deeper places, where the interface couldn't reach.

And when she woke, the feeling was still there—not a word, but a warmth in her chest, a sense that something had changed.

She was learning. She was fighting. And she would not let the system win.
