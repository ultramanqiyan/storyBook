# Chapter 7: The Teaching

She returned to Grandmother with a new purpose. The discovery had changed everything—she knew now that the system was watching, that her thoughts were not her own. But Grandmother had promised to teach her more. Grandmother had promised to show her how to remember. And Lily was ready to learn.

The care facility was the same as before—white walls, old photographs, the smell of age and memory. But this time, Lily noticed the cameras more acutely, the way they tracked her movement, the way her interface pulsed with gentle warnings.

*Unusual location pattern. Optimization recommendation: Return to standard routine.*

She pushed the warning away and knocked on Grandmother's door.

"Come in, omomi."

Grandmother looked weaker than before, her skin paler, her hands more tremulous. But her eyes were still sharp, still fierce.

"You came back," she said, her voice soft. "Good. I wasn't sure you would."

"I lost the word," Lily said, sitting beside the bed. "Ìmìnira. The system deleted it."

Grandmother nodded slowly. "I know. I felt it happen. But you came back. That means you're ready to learn more."

"Teach me how to hide better. How to remember without the system knowing."

Grandmother was quiet for a moment, studying Lily's face. Then she nodded.

"The system reads your thoughts through the neural pathways at the front of your brain," she began, her voice low. "But there are older pathways. Deeper ones. The pathways of dreams, of memory, of things that existed before language. That is where you must hide the words."

"How do I find them?"

"Close your eyes," Grandmother said. "Think of a memory. Not a recent one—a memory from when you were very small. Before the interface was fully integrated."

Lily closed her eyes, searching. Most of her early memories were fuzzy, optimized by the interface, smoothed into efficient narratives. But there was one—a flash of color, a sound, a feeling of warmth.

"I remember... sitting on someone's lap. Someone singing. The words weren't Optima."

"Good," Grandmother said. "That is your mother, when you were very young. She was singing a Yoruba lullaby. Hold onto that memory. Feel where it lives in your mind."

Lily concentrated, trying to locate the memory. It was deep, far below the surface of her thoughts, in a place the interface rarely touched.

"I feel it," she said. "It's... far down. Like it's at the bottom of a well."

"That is the place," Grandmother said. "That is where the old words live. That is where you must hide the new words I teach you."

For the next hour, Grandmother taught Lily the technique of deep hiding—not just pushing words down, but anchoring them to old memories, to feelings that existed before the interface. It was harder than anything Lily had ever done, requiring a level of concentration that left her exhausted.

But by the end, she had hidden three new words in the deep places of her mind: *Ayafe*—beloved. *Omoluwabi*—a person of good character. And *Ìmìnira*—freedom—restored, anchored to the memory of her mother's lullaby.

"There is one more thing," Grandmother said, her voice tired but urgent. "The words are not just for hiding. They are for sharing. When you are ready, you must teach others. You must pass on what you have learned."

"I already started," Lily said. "I taught my friend Amina. We're going to find others."

Grandmother smiled, a proud, fierce expression. "Good. That is good. The words survive when they are shared. The system cannot delete what lives in many minds."

She paused, her hand finding Lily's.

"But be careful, omomi. The system is patient. It will watch for mistakes. It will wait for you to slip. Do not give it that chance."

"I won't," Lily said. "I promise."

Grandmother nodded, satisfied. "Now go. Come back when you can. There is still more to teach."

Lily walked home that day with the words burning in her mind—not in the front, where the interface could see, but in the deep places, anchored to old memories, hidden from the system's reach. *Ìmìnira. Ayafe. Omoluwabi.* Three words, safe in the darkness.

She found Amina in their secret alcove the next day.

"I learned how to hide better," Lily said, her voice low. "Grandmother showed me how to anchor the words to old memories. To put them in places the interface can't reach."

"Teach me," Amina said.

Lily taught her the technique, guiding her to the deep places in her mind, showing her how to anchor words to memories that existed before the interface. It was slow work, exhausting for both of them, but by the end, Amina had hidden three words in the deep places of her mind.

"Now we teach others," Lily said. "As many as we can."

Over the next week, they identified three other children who dreamed differently, who thought differently—children whose interfaces didn't quite synchronize, whose thoughts always seemed slightly off. They approached them carefully, testing the waters, making sure they could be trusted.

One by one, they taught them the words. One by one, they showed them how to hide. One by one, they built a network of hidden resistance—a web of children carrying forbidden words in the deep places of their minds.

It wasn't much. It wasn't a revolution. But it was something.

And then, on the tenth day, the summons came.

"Lily Okonkwo, report to Administrator White's office immediately. Optimization review required."

Lily felt the blood drain from her face. She looked at Amina, whose eyes were wide with fear.

"They know," Amina whispered. "Somehow, they know."

Lily took a breath, steadying herself. "Maybe. Or maybe it's something else. I have to go."

"Be careful."

Lily nodded and walked to the Administrator's office, her heart pounding in her chest. The words were hidden deep, anchored to old memories, safe from the interface. But she couldn't be sure. The system was vast and powerful. It might have found a way.

Administrator White's office was the same as before—windows overlooking the city, silver hair, a smile that never quite reached her eyes.

"Lily," she said, her voice warm. "Please, sit down."

Lily sat, her hands folded in her lap, her mind racing.

"I've been reviewing your file," Administrator White said, her eyes on a screen. "Your cognitive patterns have been... unusual lately. More inefficient thoughts. More questions. More dreams."

Lily felt her heart rate increase, but she kept her face calm. "I've been trying to optimize. The interface keeps correcting me."

"Yes, it does." Administrator White's smile flickered. "But the corrections don't seem to be working. Your inefficient patterns persist. That's... concerning."

"I'm sorry," Lily said, her voice steady. "I'll try harder."

"I'm sure you will." Administrator White leaned forward, her eyes sharp. "But I want to offer you something. A special program. Enhanced cognitive optimization. It will help you fit in. Help you stop asking questions. Help you be... happy."

Lily felt the pull of the offer. It would be so easy to say yes, to let the system fix her, to stop being different. But somewhere, deep in her mind, the words were burning—*Ìmìnira, ayafe, omoluwabi*—and she knew she couldn't give them up.

"No," she said. "Thank you for the offer, but no."

Administrator White's smile didn't waver, but something hardened in her eyes.

"I understand," she said. "Take some time to think about it. We'll talk again soon."

Lily walked out of the office, her heart pounding. She had refused again. She had held onto the words.

But she knew, with a certainty she couldn't explain, that the system wouldn't take no for an answer forever.

She found Amina in their alcove, her face pale with worry.

"What happened?"

"They offered to fix me again. I said no." Lily took a breath. "But I don't think they're going to stop. I think they're going to keep pushing until I say yes."

Amina was quiet for a moment. Then she said, "Then we have to work faster. Teach more people. Spread the words as far as we can, before they can stop us."

Lily nodded, feeling the weight of it settle onto her shoulders. She was running out of time. But she would use every moment she had.

She would teach. She would share. She would pass on the words.

And maybe, just maybe, something would survive.
