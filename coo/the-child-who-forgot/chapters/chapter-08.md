# Chapter 8: The Choice

The summons came three days after her last lesson with Grandmother. A soft chime at her neural interface, a pleasant voice: "Lily Okonkwo, please report to Administrator White's office. Optimization opportunity."

The words sounded kind, helpful even. But Lily had learned to hear the danger beneath the kindness.

She walked through the Academy corridors, her heart pounding, her mind racing. The words were hidden deep—*Ìmìnira, ayafe, omoluwabi*—anchored to old memories, safe from the interface. But she couldn't shake the feeling that something was wrong.

Administrator White's office was white and soft, designed to feel welcoming. The woman behind the desk smiled warmly.

"Lily. Thank you for coming. We've noticed some... irregularities in your cognitive patterns."

Lily sat in the chair across from her, her hands folded in her lap. "I've been trying to optimize."

"I know you have." Administrator White's voice was gentle. "But the optimizations don't seem to be working. Your dreams are still unusual. Your thoughts still stray from approved patterns. And your social interactions have become... concerning."

Lily felt a chill. "Concerning?"

"You've been spending time with certain children. Children who, like you, demonstrate inefficient cognitive patterns. Children who ask questions." Administrator White leaned forward. "We've been monitoring your interactions, Lily. We know what you're doing."

Lily's heart stopped. They knew. Somehow, they knew.

"I don't know what you mean," she said, her voice steady despite the fear.

Administrator White smiled, a patient expression. "You've been teaching them. Words from the old language. Concepts that have been optimized away." She paused, her eyes sharp. "We can't see the words themselves—they're hidden, somehow, in a part of your mind the interface doesn't reach. But we can see the patterns. The changes in behavior. The questions."

Lily felt the blood drain from her face. They couldn't see the words, but they could see the effects. They knew something was happening, even if they didn't know exactly what.

"We want to help you, Lily," Administrator White said, her voice soft. "This path you're on—it leads nowhere good. The old words, the old ways—they're gone for a reason. They caused conflict, suffering, inefficiency. Why would you want to bring them back?"

Lily thought of Grandmother, of the colors in her room, of the words that flowed like music. She thought of the feeling of freedom, of a door opening, of stepping into a room she didn't know existed.

"Because they matter," she said, her voice barely a whisper. "Because they're part of who we are. Because without them, we're not... we're not human."

Administrator White's smile flickered. "Human is an inefficient concept, Lily. We've moved beyond it. We've become something better—something optimized, something efficient, something... happy."

"But are we?" Lily heard herself ask. "Are we happy? Or are we just... optimized to think we are?"

The silence stretched. Administrator White's face had gone still, her eyes fixed on Lily with an intensity that made her want to shrink back.

"You're very perceptive," she said finally. "More perceptive than most. That's part of the problem, you know. Your mind doesn't work the way it should. It sees things, questions things, that others don't."

"Is that bad?"

"In an optimized world? Yes." Administrator White leaned back, her voice becoming clinical. "Your neural pathways are atypical. The interface can't fully integrate with your cognitive processes. It's a rare condition—maybe one in a million—but it means you'll always be... different."

Lily felt something shift inside her. A condition. A defect. That's how they saw it.

"We can fix it," Administrator White continued. "A procedure. Completely safe, completely painless. It will optimize your neural pathways, allow the interface to integrate fully. You'll fit in. You'll be happy. You'll stop asking questions that have no answers."

Lily thought about it. Really thought about it. The offer was tempting—a way to stop being different, to stop being afraid, to fit in at last. She could say yes, let them fix her, and never have to worry about the words, the dreams, the questions.

But then she thought of Grandmother, fading in her care facility, holding onto the old words like precious stones. She thought of Amina, her eyes wide with hope and fear. She thought of the other children, carrying forbidden words in the deep places of their minds.

She thought of *Ìmìnira*—freedom—and what it meant to choose.

"No," she said.

Administrator White's smile didn't waver, but something hardened in her eyes. "I understand. Take some time to think about it. We'll talk again soon."

Lily walked out of the office, her heart pounding. She had refused again. She had held onto the words.

But she knew, with a certainty she couldn't explain, that the system wouldn't take no for an answer forever.

She found Amina in their alcove, her face pale with worry.

"They know," Lily said, her voice barely a whisper. "They can't see the words, but they know something is happening. They offered to fix me."

"What did you say?"

"I said no." Lily took a breath. "But they're going to keep pushing. We have to work faster. Teach as many people as we can, before they stop us."

Amina nodded, her eyes fierce. "I'll find more children. Children who dream differently. Children who ask questions."

"Be careful. They're watching us now."

"I know." Amina's hand found Lily's, a brief, human contact that the interface couldn't optimize. "But we have to keep going. The words are too important."

Lily nodded, feeling the weight of it settle onto her shoulders. She had made her choice. She had refused the fix. Now she had to live with the consequences—or die with them.

That night, Lily dreamed again. But this time, the dream was different. There was a door, standing open, and beyond it was a room filled with colors—real colors, not the gray and white of her world. And in the center of the room stood Grandmother, her hand extended, waiting.

"Come, omomi," she said, her voice like music. "There is more to show you."

Lily stepped through the door, and the world exploded into color.

She woke with a gasp, the words burning in her mind—*Ìmìnira, ayafe, omoluwabi*—and a new word, one she had never heard before, echoing in the deepest part of her consciousness.

*Ìbàjé.*

She didn't know what it meant. But she knew it was important.

She would ask Grandmother. She would learn. And she would pass it on.

Before it was too late.
