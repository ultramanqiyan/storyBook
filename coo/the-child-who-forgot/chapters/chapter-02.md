# Chapter 2: The Academy

The Academy swallowed her whole, the white doors sliding shut behind her with a soft hiss. Inside, the air was cool and filtered, optimized for cognitive performance. Lily walked to her designated station, her footsteps echoing in the quiet corridor. Other children passed her, their faces blank with concentration, their neural interfaces pulsing softly at their temples. No one spoke. Speaking was inefficient when thoughts could be transmitted directly.

But Lily had always felt like an outsider here. Her thoughts never quite fit the optimized patterns, never quite transmitted cleanly through the interface. There was always something—a fuzziness, a static—that made her different from the other children.

She found her station and sat, the screen lighting up with her daily schedule. Morning modules: Language Optimization, Cognitive Efficiency, Social Harmony. Afternoon modules: Historical Progress Review, Physical Optimization, Creative Expression (limited).

Creative Expression was her least favorite module. In a world where efficiency was the highest virtue, creativity was tolerated but not encouraged. The module consisted of generating optimized visual patterns—geometric shapes that were mathematically pleasing but emotionally empty.

Lily had tried, once, to create something different. A pattern that wasn't geometric, that had curves and colors that didn't exist in the approved palette. The interface had corrected her immediately: *Cognitive pattern detected: Inefficient creative direction. Redirecting to approved parameters.*

She had never tried again.

The morning modules passed in a blur of optimized content. Language Optimization reinforced the rules of Optima—the simplified grammar, the efficient vocabulary, the absence of ambiguity. Cognitive Efficiency taught techniques for clear thinking, for eliminating wasteful mental processes. Social Harmony emphasized the importance of fitting in, of contributing to the collective good.

Lily absorbed it all, her interface recording and categorizing every piece of information. But somewhere, in the back of her mind, the questions from the morning still burned.

*Why can't I remember the dream?*

*Why does Grandmother speak differently?*

*Why do I feel like something is missing?*

At break time, she walked to the recreation area, a large white room filled with children playing optimized games. The games were designed to build social skills, to teach cooperation and efficiency. Children moved in patterns, their actions coordinated by their interfaces, their laughter pleasant but somehow hollow.

Lily sat on a bench at the edge of the room, watching. She had never been good at the games. Her interface never quite synchronized with the others, and she always ended up out of step, a beat behind, a pattern off.

"Station 7749: Social integration below optimal parameters."

The voice came from her interface, gentle but insistent. Lily ignored it, as she usually did. She had learned to live with the constant corrections, the gentle nudges toward efficiency. They were part of life, like the hum of the air filtration system or the pulse of the lights.

"You're different."

The voice came from behind her, and Lily turned to see a girl standing there. She was tall, with dark skin and curious eyes that seemed to see more than they should. Her uniform was the same gray and white as everyone else's, but there was something about the way she stood—a slight angle, a subtle difference—that set her apart.

"I'm Amina," the girl said, sitting down beside Lily without waiting for an invitation. "I've been watching you."

Lily felt a flicker of something—surprise, maybe, or wariness. "Query purpose. Why have you been watching me?"

Amina smiled, a small expression that didn't quite fit the optimized parameters. "Because you're interesting. You don't fit. I don't fit either."

Lily looked at her more carefully. There was something in Amina's eyes—a spark, a question—that Lily recognized. It was the same thing she saw in her own reflection sometimes, when she dared to look.

"I don't understand," Lily said.

"Neither do I." Amina leaned closer, her voice dropping to a whisper. "But I think there's something wrong with this place. Something wrong with all of it. And I think you see it too."

Lily's heart beat faster. This was dangerous talk—inefficient, unproductive, potentially subversive. Her interface pulsed with a gentle warning: *Cognitive pattern detected: Non-productive social interaction. Redirecting to approved topics.*

But Lily pushed against the correction. For the first time, she wanted to hear more.

"What do you mean?" she asked, her voice barely audible.

Amina glanced around, checking for observers. "Not here. Meet me in the east corridor after afternoon modules. There's a place where the cameras don't reach."

Before Lily could respond, Amina stood and walked away, disappearing into the flow of children. Lily sat alone, her heart pounding, her mind racing.

Someone else saw it. Someone else knew.

The afternoon modules were harder to focus on. Historical Progress Review showed more images of the old world—more colors, more expressions, more of the messy, inefficient humanity that Optima had replaced. Physical Optimization was a blur of coordinated exercises. Creative Expression was the same geometric patterns, the same empty shapes.

But through it all, Lily's mind kept returning to Amina's words. *Something wrong with this place.* The phrase echoed in her head, bouncing off the optimized thoughts, finding purchase in the cracks.

After the final module, Lily walked to the east corridor, her heart in her throat. The corridor was empty, the lights dimmed for the transition period. She found the spot Amina had mentioned—a small alcove where the wall had been damaged and never fully repaired. The camera above was dark, its lens clouded over.

Amina was already there, her back against the wall, her eyes alert.

"You came," she said.

"You said there was something wrong." Lily stood at the entrance to the alcove, suddenly aware of how exposed she felt. "What did you mean?"

Amina studied her for a moment, as if deciding whether to trust her. Then she spoke, her voice low.

"I have dreams," she said. "Dreams about things that don't exist. Colors I've never seen. Words I've never heard. And when I wake up, I can't remember them—but I know they were important."

Lily felt a jolt of recognition. "That happens to me too. Last night, I dreamed of a color—"

"A color that doesn't exist," Amina finished. "Yes. I know."

They looked at each other, something passing between them. A connection, fragile and new.

"There are others," Amina said. "Not many, but some. Children who dream differently. Who think differently. The interface doesn't work on us the way it's supposed to."

"Why not?"

"I don't know." Amina's voice was frustrated. "But I think it has something to do with the old language. The language our grandparents spoke. The language that Optima replaced."

Lily thought of Grandmother, of the words that flowed like music, of the shapes and sounds that didn't exist in Optima.

"My grandmother speaks differently," she said slowly. "I don't understand her words, but somehow... I recognize them."

Amina nodded. "My grandmother too. She tried to teach me once, when I was younger. But the interface deleted the words. I couldn't remember them."

Lily felt a chill. The interface could delete words? Delete memories?

"How do you know?" she asked.

"Because I found a file," Amina said, her voice dropping even lower. "A file with my name on it. It said my dreams were being monitored. It said the words my grandmother taught me were 'inefficient concepts' and had been 'optimized for cognitive efficiency.'"

Lily stared at her, the implications sinking in. The interface wasn't just monitoring their thoughts—it was changing them. Deleting things. Optimizing them without their knowledge.

"That's not possible," she whispered. "The interface is supposed to help us. To make us more efficient."

"Efficient at what?" Amina's voice was sharp. "Efficient at fitting in? Efficient at not questioning? Efficient at forgetting everything that matters?"

Lily had no answer. The questions burned in her chest, the same questions she had been asking all day. But now they had a new weight, a new urgency.

"What do we do?" she asked.

Amina looked at her, her eyes serious. "I don't know yet. But I think the first step is to stop forgetting. To find a way to remember the words. The dreams. The things the interface wants to delete."

"How?"

"Your grandmother," Amina said. "She knows the old language. She might know how to hide thoughts from the interface. How to remember."

Lily thought of Grandmother, of the care facility across the city, of the words that sounded like music.

"I'll visit her," she said. "I'll ask."

Amina nodded. "Be careful. The interface monitors everything. If it detects what you're doing..."

"I know." Lily took a breath. "But I have to try. I have to know."

They stood in the alcove for a moment longer, two children in a world that didn't want them to question. Then Amina touched Lily's arm—a brief, human contact that the interface couldn't optimize.

"Meet me here tomorrow," she said. "Tell me what you learn."

Lily nodded, and then Amina was gone, disappearing into the flow of children heading home.

Lily walked to the transport station alone, her mind full of questions. The interface pulsed gently at her temple, monitoring, optimizing, correcting. But for the first time, she didn't want to be corrected. She wanted to remember. She wanted to understand.

And somewhere, in the back of her mind, the word from her dream was still there—waiting, patient, like a key to a door she was only beginning to see.

She would visit Grandmother. She would ask. And maybe, just maybe, she would find the answer to the question that burned inside her.

The question that started with "why."
