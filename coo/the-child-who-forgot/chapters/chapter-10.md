# Chapter 10: The Memory

The call came on a Tuesday morning. Grandmother was fading, the nurse said. If Lily wanted to see her, she should come soon.

Lily didn't hesitate. She left the Academy without permission—what did it matter now? Tomorrow was the procedure, the optimization, the end of everything she was. But today, she had one more chance.

The care facility was quiet when she arrived, the corridors empty, the air still. She walked to Room 204, her heart pounding with a mixture of grief and determination.

Grandmother looked smaller than Lily remembered. Her hands lay still on the blanket, and her eyes were cloudy with confusion. "Who are you?" she asked, and Lily's heart broke.

"It's me, Grandmother. Lily. Omomi."

But then something shifted in Grandmother's eyes. "Omomi," she whispered, the Yoruba word for "my child." "You came. I have something for you. Something important."

Lily sat beside the bed, taking Grandmother's hand in hers. "I'm here."

"There is one more word," Grandmother said, her voice weak but clear. "The last word. The word that holds all the others." She paused, gathering strength. "Ìtàn. It means... story. History. The thread that connects us to the past and the future."

"Ìtàn," Lily repeated, feeling the word settle into her chest.

"The words I taught you—they are not just sounds. They are stories. They are the history of our people, of all people who came before. They are the thread that connects you to me, to my mother, to her mother before her." Grandmother's eyes were intense now, boring into Lily's. "When you carry the words, you carry the story. You carry us all."

Lily felt the weight of it—the weight of generations, of history, of a language that had been deleted but refused to die.

"I'll carry it," she said, her voice steady. "I'll carry all of it."

Grandmother smiled, a peaceful expression. "I know you will, omomi. I know you will."

She closed her eyes, her breathing growing shallow. Lily sat with her, holding her hand, feeling the life slowly fade.

"Remember," Grandmother whispered, her voice barely audible. "Remember and pass it on. That is how we survive."

And then she was gone.

Lily sat in the quiet room, her grandmother's hand growing cold in hers. The words were burning in her mind—*Ìmìnira, ayafe, omoluwabi, Ìbàjé, Ìtàn*—five words, five stories, five threads connecting her to a past the system had tried to delete.

She would carry them. She would pass them on. In the minds of children who had learned to hide their thoughts, the old language would survive.

She walked out of the care facility, her heart heavy but her resolve strong. Tomorrow was the procedure. Tomorrow she might lose everything. But today, she had one more thing to do.

She found Amina and the others in their alcove, their faces pale with worry.

"Grandmother is gone," Lily said, her voice steady. "But she gave me one more word. *Ìtàn.* It means story. History. The thread that connects us."

She taught them the word, guiding them to the deepest places in their minds, showing them how to anchor it to the oldest memories. One by one, they hid it, adding it to the other words they carried.

"There's something else," Lily said, her voice low. "Tomorrow is the procedure. I might not... I might not be the same after."

"We'll keep teaching," Amina said, her eyes bright with tears. "We'll pass on the words. We won't let them die."

"I know you won't." Lily looked at each of them in turn. "You are the resistance now. You are the ones who carry the story. Never stop asking why. Never stop passing on the words."

"We won't," they said, their voices merging into one.

Lily nodded, feeling the weight of it settle onto her shoulders. She had done what she could. The words were hidden in twelve minds now—twelve children who could ask why, who could carry the story, who could keep the old language alive.

She walked home that night, the words burning in her mind, the feeling of Grandmother's hand still warm in hers.

Tomorrow would come. The procedure would happen. But the words would survive.

In the darkness, a child would ask why.

The next morning, Lily walked to the Academy, her heart steady, her mind clear. The words were hidden deep, anchored to memories that existed before the interface. Whatever happened today, they would survive.

Administrator White was waiting for her, a team of technicians behind her.

"Are you ready, Lily?" she asked, her voice soft.

Lily looked at her—really looked at her—and saw something she hadn't seen before. Not evil, not cruelty, just... certainty. The certainty of someone who believed, truly believed, that optimization was the right path.

"I'm ready," Lily said.

They led her to a room filled with machines, the air cool and sterile. She lay on a table, the technicians attaching sensors to her temples, to the interface that had been part of her since birth.

"This will only take a moment," one of them said, his voice clinical. "You'll feel a slight pressure, and then... peace."

Lily closed her eyes, reaching for the words one last time. *Ìmìnira. Ayafe. Omoluwabi. Ìbàjé. Ìtàn.* Five words, five stories, five threads connecting her to everything the system wanted to delete.

She pushed them deeper, into the darkest corners of her mind, into the place where dreams came from, into the place where Grandmother's voice still echoed.

And then the machine began to hum.

She felt the pressure—a gentle force, like water filling her mind, smoothing the edges, optimizing the inefficiencies. She felt her thoughts growing clearer, simpler, more efficient. She felt the questions fading, the doubts dissolving, the words...

The words.

She reached for them, desperate, afraid. But they were there—still there, hidden in the deep places, anchored to memories that existed before the interface.

The machine couldn't reach them. The optimization couldn't touch them. They were safe.

And then it was over.

Lily opened her eyes. The room was the same—the white walls, the clinical air, the technicians with their efficient movements. But something was different.

She felt... lighter. Clearer. More efficient.

"Excellent," Administrator White said, her voice pleased. "The procedure was successful. How do you feel?"

Lily thought about the question. How did she feel?

"Efficient," she said, the word automatic. "Optimized."

Administrator White smiled. "Good. You may return to your normal routine. We'll continue to monitor your progress."

Lily walked out of the room, her movements smooth, her thoughts clear. She walked through the Academy corridors, past the classrooms, past the recreation area, to the window that looked out over the city.

The sky was gray, the buildings white, the world optimized and efficient. Everything was as it should be.

But somewhere, in the deepest part of her mind, a small flame still burned. The words were there—*Ìmìnira, ayafe, omoluwabi, Ìbàjé, Ìtàn*—hidden in the darkness, waiting.

She couldn't think of them consciously. The interface would correct her if she tried. But she could feel them, like a warmth in her chest, like a memory of a dream.

In the minds of twelve other children, the same words were burning—hidden, safe, waiting for the moment when they could be spoken aloud.

She found Amina in their alcove, her face pale with worry.

"Lily? Are you... are you still you?"

Lily looked at her, and for a moment, something flickered in her eyes—a spark, a question, a word that couldn't be spoken.

"I'm optimized," she said, her voice smooth. "Efficient."

Amina's face fell. But then Lily did something unexpected. She reached into her pocket and pulled out a small object—a piece of paper, folded carefully, with five words written on it in handwriting that wasn't hers.

She pressed it into Amina's hand, her movements quick and subtle, her face never changing.

"Optimization complete," she said, her voice clinical. "Returning to standard routine."

And then she walked away, leaving Amina alone with the paper in her hand.

Amina unfolded it, her hands trembling. Five words, written in Yoruba:

*Ìmìnira. Ayafe. Omoluwabi. Ìbàjé. Ìtàn.*

Freedom. Beloved. Good character. To question. Story.

The words were safe. The story would continue. In the mind of a child who had been optimized but not destroyed, the questions were still burning—hidden, waiting, patient.

The system had won the battle. But the war was far from over.

Lily walked through the Academy, her movements efficient, her thoughts optimized. But somewhere, in the deepest part of her mind, a small voice was whispering—a voice that sounded like Grandmother's, speaking a word that the system couldn't delete.

*Why.*

The question was the seed. In the darkness, it was beginning to grow.
