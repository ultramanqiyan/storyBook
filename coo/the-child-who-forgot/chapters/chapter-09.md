# Chapter 9: The Resistance

For a week, Lily waited for the consequences. Every chime of her neural interface made her heart race. Every glance from a teacher felt like an accusation. But nothing happened—or at least, nothing obvious. Administrator White's offer still hung in the air, patient and persistent. "We'll talk again soon."

Lily knew it wasn't over. But she also knew she couldn't go back to the way things were. She had made her choice. Now she had to act on it.

She found Amina in their alcove, her face drawn with worry.

"They haven't called me back yet," Lily said. "But they will. We have to work faster."

"I found two more," Amina said. "Children who dream differently. I think they're ready."

"Teach them. Use the deep hiding technique. Anchor the words to old memories."

Amina nodded. "What about you?"

"I'm going to visit Grandmother. There's a new word in my dreams. *Ìbàjé.* I need to know what it means."

The care facility was the same as always—white walls, old photographs, the smell of age and memory. But when Lily reached Room 204, she found Grandmother weaker than before, her skin almost translucent, her breathing shallow.

"Omomi," Grandmother whispered, her hand finding Lily's. "You came."

"Of course I came." Lily sat beside the bed, her heart aching. "Grandmother, there's a new word in my dreams. *Ìbàjé.* What does it mean?"

Grandmother's eyes sharpened, a flicker of the old fire. "You dreamed it? The word came to you in a dream?"

"Yes. What does it mean?"

Grandmother was silent for a long moment. Then she spoke, her voice barely a whisper.

"Ìbàjé. It means... to spoil, to ruin, to make wrong. But it also means... to question. To ask why something is the way it is." She paused, her grip tightening on Lily's hand. "It is the most dangerous word of all. Because once you ask why, you can never go back."

Lily felt the word settle into her chest, a small, heavy weight. "Why is it dangerous?"

"Because it is the word that starts everything. The word that begins resistance. The word that opens the door." Grandmother's eyes were intense now, boring into Lily's. "The system can delete words for freedom, for love, for beauty. But if it deletes the word for asking why, it deletes the possibility of change itself."

"And that's why they deleted it."

"Yes. But it lives on, in dreams, in the deep places of the mind. And now it has come to you." Grandmother smiled, a fierce, proud expression. "You are ready, omomi. Ready to carry the most dangerous word of all."

Lily felt the weight of it settling onto her shoulders. The word for asking why. The word that started everything.

"Teach me how to hide it," she said. "Teach me how to pass it on."

Grandmother taught her for another hour, showing her how to anchor the word to the deepest memories—the ones that existed before language, before the interface, before everything. It was the hardest thing Lily had ever done, requiring a level of concentration that left her trembling.

But by the end, the word was hidden, safe in the deepest part of her mind, where even the interface couldn't reach.

"Now go," Grandmother said, her voice tired but urgent. "Pass it on. Teach others to ask why. That is how resistance begins."

Lily nodded, feeling the word burning in her mind—*Ìbàjé*—the word for questioning, for change, for hope.

She found Amina in their alcove that afternoon, her face alight with news.

"I taught them," Amina said. "Two more children. They're hiding the words now."

"Good." Lily sat beside her, her voice low. "I have a new word. The most important one. *Ìbàjé.* It means to ask why."

Amina's eyes widened. "Teach me."

Lily taught her the word, guiding her to the deepest places in her mind, showing her how to anchor it to the oldest memories. It took longer than before—the word was heavier, more dangerous—but by the end, Amina had hidden it in the deepest part of her consciousness.

"Now we teach others," Lily said. "As many as we can. The word for asking why. The word that starts everything."

Over the next few days, they worked quickly, teaching the word to every child who would listen. Some were afraid, some were skeptical, but most were hungry—hungry for something they couldn't name, something the optimized world couldn't provide.

By the end of the week, twelve children carried the word in their minds—twelve children who could ask why, who could question, who could see the door that led to another world.

It wasn't much. It wasn't a revolution. But it was something.

And then, on the tenth day, the summons came again.

"Lily Okonkwo, report to Administrator White's office immediately. Final optimization review required."

Lily felt the blood drain from her face. Final review. That didn't sound good.

She found Amina, her eyes wide with fear.

"Whatever happens," Lily said, her voice steady, "keep teaching. Keep passing on the words. Promise me."

"I promise." Amina's hand found hers, a brief, fierce squeeze. "Be careful."

Lily nodded and walked to the Administrator's office, her heart pounding in her chest.

Administrator White's face was serious this time, the smile gone.

"Lily," she said, her voice cold. "We've been patient. We've offered you help. But your cognitive patterns continue to deteriorate. Your influence on other children is... concerning."

"I haven't done anything wrong," Lily said, her voice steady.

"You've been teaching them to question. To ask why. To resist optimization." Administrator White leaned forward, her eyes sharp. "We can't allow that to continue."

"What are you going to do?"

"There will be a procedure. Tomorrow. To optimize your neural pathways and remove the inefficient patterns." She paused, her voice softening. "It's for your own good, Lily. You'll be happier. You'll fit in. You'll stop asking questions that have no answers."

Lily felt the fear rise in her chest, but she pushed it down. She had known this was coming. She had made her choice.

"And if I refuse?"

Administrator White's smile returned, but it didn't reach her eyes. "You won't refuse. The procedure is mandatory for children with your condition. It's for your own good."

Lily walked out of the office, her mind racing. Mandatory. Tomorrow. She had one day to do something, to warn the others, to pass on the words one last time.

She found Amina and the other children in their alcove, their faces pale with fear.

"They're going to fix me tomorrow," Lily said, her voice steady. "Mandatory procedure."

"We have to stop them," Amina said, her voice fierce.

"We can't. But we can make sure the words survive." Lily looked at each of them in turn. "Teach others. As many as you can. Hide the words deep. And never stop asking why."

"We won't," Amina said, her eyes bright with tears. "We promise."

Lily nodded, feeling the weight of it settle onto her shoulders. She might not survive this. But the words would. The questions would. And somewhere, in the minds of children who had learned to hide their thoughts, the resistance would grow.

That night, Lily dreamed one last time. The door was there, standing open, and beyond it was a world of color and music and words that flowed like water. Grandmother was there, her hand extended, waiting.

"Well done, omomi," she said, her voice like music. "Well done."

Lily woke with the words burning in her mind—*Ìmìnira, ayafe, omoluwabi, Ìbàjé*—and a feeling of peace settling over her.

Whatever happened tomorrow, the words would survive. The questions would survive. And somewhere, in the darkness, a child would ask why.

And that was enough.
