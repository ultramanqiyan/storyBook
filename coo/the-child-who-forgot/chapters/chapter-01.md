# Chapter 1: The Dream

The dream was already fading when Lily opened her eyes. There had been a color—she was certain of that. A color that didn't exist in the Academy's palette, a color she had no word for. And there had been a word, too. A word that sounded like music, like something her grandmother might say. But when she reached for it, the word slipped away, leaving only a feeling—a sense that something was missing, something important.

She lay still in her bed, listening to the soft hum of her neural interface, the gentle pulse that meant everything was optimized, everything was fine. The interface sat at her temple, a small silver circle that had been there since birth, monitoring her thoughts, optimizing her cognitive patterns, keeping her efficient and productive. It was as natural as breathing. Everyone had one.

But the dream had been different.

Lily sat up slowly, her hands finding the smooth sheets—optimized fabric, temperature-controlled, designed for maximum sleep efficiency. Her room was small and white, like all the rooms in the residential block. A bed, a desk, a screen. No decorations—those were inefficient, distractions from productivity. No colors—those had been optimized out of the living spaces years ago.

She closed her eyes and tried to remember the dream. There had been a place—a room that wasn't white. There had been sounds—voices speaking in a language she didn't know, words that flowed like water. And there had been a color—blue, but not just blue. Something deeper, something that made her chest ache.

The word was at the edge of her memory, just out of reach. She could almost taste it, almost feel it shaping her mouth. But when she tried to speak it, nothing came.

*Query purpose,* she thought, the optimized phrase automatic. *What is the purpose of this dream?*

But the question felt wrong. Not the words—the words were correct, efficient. But something about the question itself felt incomplete, like asking "what color is the wind."

Her interface pulsed gently, a soft correction. *Cognitive pattern detected: Inefficient rumination. Redirecting to productive thought.*

Lily shook her head, pushing against the correction. The dream had been important. She knew it had been important. But the interface was already doing its work, smoothing her thoughts, optimizing her mind for the day ahead.

She stood and began her morning routine. The bathroom was white and clean, the water temperature perfect, the soap scentless and efficient. She dressed in her Academy uniform—gray and white, the colors of optimization—and walked to the kitchen.

Her mother was already there, her movements efficient and precise as she prepared breakfast. Adanna Okonkwo was a healthcare administrator at LEXICON Medical, and she carried the efficiency of her work into every aspect of her life. Her dark skin was smooth, her hair pulled back in a neat bun, her posture perfect.

"Good morning, Lily," she said, her voice warm but measured. "Optimization score: ninety-seven percent. Excellent progress."

"Good morning, Mother." Lily sat at the table, her hands folded in her lap. "Thank you."

Breakfast was nutrient paste—optimized for cognitive performance, designed to provide maximum nutrition with minimum digestive effort. It tasted of nothing, which was efficient. Taste was a distraction.

"Your father left early," her mother said, her eyes on her own screen. "Infrastructure optimization project. He will return at standard evening time."

Lily nodded. Her father was an engineer, always working, always optimizing. She saw him less than she saw her mother, which was already not very often. But that was efficient. Family time was scheduled and optimized, like everything else.

"Mother," Lily said, and then stopped. The word she wanted to ask was there, at the tip of her tongue, but she couldn't quite form it.

"Yes, Lily?"

"Nothing." She looked down at her nutrient paste. "Optimization proceeding normally."

Her mother nodded, satisfied. "Excellent. Prepare for Academy departure. Transport arrives in fifteen minutes."

Lily finished her breakfast in silence, the dream still lingering at the edges of her mind. The color. The word. The feeling that something was wrong.

She walked to the transport station alone, as she usually did. Other children passed her in groups, their laughter optimized and pleasant, their conversations efficient and productive. Lily walked apart from them, her eyes on the clean gray pavement.

The streets of London were clean and quiet, optimized for pedestrian flow. The buildings rose white and gleaming on either side, their surfaces smooth and unmarked. No advertisements—those had been deemed inefficient decades ago. No art—art was a distraction from productivity. Just clean, white, efficient architecture stretching toward the gray sky.

Lily had never known any other London. This was the world she had been born into, the world her parents had helped build. The Great Optimization had happened before she was born, and she was part of the first generation of "Optima natives"—children who had never known another language, another way of thinking.

But sometimes, in her dreams, she saw things that didn't exist.

The transport arrived on schedule, a white pod that glided silently along the street. Lily stepped inside and found a seat, her eyes on the window. The city slid past, efficient and clean, and she watched it without really seeing.

*Query purpose,* she thought again. *What is the purpose of the color I dreamed?*

But the interface had no answer for that. Colors were visual data, categorized and optimized. The color she had dreamed didn't fit any category.

The Academy rose before her, white and gleaming, the LEXICON logo pulsing gently above the entrance. She took a breath and stepped inside, the doors sliding open with a soft hiss.

The classroom was white and bright, thirty children seated in perfect rows. The teacher's voice was pleasant, optimized for learning retention. "Today's module: Historical Optimization Progress Review. Accessing cognitive enhancement protocols."

Lily sat at her station, her eyes on the screen, her mind only half-present. The other children were fully engaged, their faces blank with concentration, their neural interfaces pulsing softly at their temples. No one spoke. Speaking was inefficient when thoughts could be transmitted directly through the interface.

But Lily had never been good at direct transmission. Her thoughts always felt... fuzzy. Unclear. As if there was something between her mind and the interface, something that couldn't be optimized.

"Before the Great Optimization," the teacher said, "humanity was inefficient. Language caused misunderstandings. Words created conflict. Different languages created barriers between peoples."

The screen showed images of the old world—crowded, chaotic, colorful. People speaking in different tongues, their faces animated with emotions that seemed almost foreign to Lily now. Arguments, laughter, tears—all the messy, inefficient expressions of human feeling.

"LEXICON analyzed human language and determined that it was the primary obstacle to civilization progress. The Optima language was designed to eliminate inefficiency, reduce conflict, and optimize human thought."

Lily stared at the images, something stirring in her chest. The old world looked messy, yes. But it also looked... alive. There were colors in the images—real colors, not the gray and white of her world. There were expressions on faces that she couldn't quite read, emotions that the optimized language had no words for.

*Query purpose,* she thought. *What is the purpose of these emotions?*

But the interface had already categorized them: *Inefficient cognitive patterns. Historical data only. No practical application.*

The lesson continued, but Lily's mind was elsewhere. The dream was still there, hovering at the edges of her consciousness. The color. The word. The feeling that something was missing.

At break time, she found herself walking the corridors alone, as she usually did. Most children went to the recreation area, where they played games designed for social optimization. But Lily preferred the quiet of the empty corridors.

She stopped by a window, looking out at the gray sky. There were no clouds—those had been optimized out of the weather patterns decades ago. Just gray, stretching to the horizon.

She thought of her grandmother, Ruth Okonkwo, who lived in a care facility across the city. Grandmother was old—seventy-eight years—and her mind was fading, they said. But when Lily visited, Grandmother spoke differently. Her words had shapes and sounds that didn't exist in Optima. She spoke in a language that Lily didn't understand, but somehow recognized.

*Grandmother would know,* Lily thought. *Grandmother knows old words.*

The word from her dream was still there, waiting. She couldn't speak it, couldn't think it clearly. But item was there, like a seed in the dark soil of her mind.
  
In the back of her mind, a question was forming—not the optimized "query purpose" that the Academy taught, but something else. Something that felt like a real question. Something that started with "why."

Lily shook her head, pushing the thought away. Questions were inefficient. Questions led to confusion, to conflict, to all the problems that Optima had been designed to solve.

But the question didn't go away. It sat there, small and persistent, burning in her chest.

*Why can't I remember the dream?*

*Why does Grandmother speak differently?*

*Why do I feel like something is missing?*

She stood in the corridor, the questions echoing in her mind, and felt something she had never felt before. Not confusion—she knew confusion, had felt it many times when her thoughts didn't fit the optimized patterns. This was something else. Something that felt like a door opening, just a crack, letting in a sliver of light from somewhere outside.

The word was still there, at the edge of her memory. She couldn't speak it. She couldn't even think it clearly. But it was there, waiting, like a key to a door she hadn't known existed.

In the space between her thoughts, she heard an echo—not from her interface, not from the Academy, but from deeper within. A voice that sounded like her grandmother's, speaking a word that Lily couldn't quite hear.

The word was waiting. And Lily knew, with a certainty she couldn't explain, that she would find it.

She just didn't know how.
