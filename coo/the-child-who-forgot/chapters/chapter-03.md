# Chapter 3: The Question

The dream had come again—not the same dream, but similar. Colors that didn't exist. Words that sounded like music. And this time, a voice. Grandmother's voice, saying something Lily couldn't quite hear. She woke with the word "why" on her lips, and it felt like a key turning in a lock she hadn't known was there.

For three days, Lily had been thinking about Amina's words. *Something wrong with this place.* The phrase echoed in her mind, bouncing off the optimized thoughts, finding purchase in the cracks. She had started to notice things she had never noticed before—the way other children moved in perfect synchronization, the way their faces showed only approved emotions, the way the interface pulsed with corrections whenever a thought strayed from the optimized path.

She was different. She had always known it, somewhere deep inside. But now she was beginning to understand what that meant.

The morning modules passed in a blur. Lily sat at her station, her eyes on the screen, but her mind was elsewhere. She was thinking about Grandmother, about the care facility, about the words that flowed like music. She was thinking about the question that had been forming in her mind—the question that started with "why."

At midday, she found Amina in their secret alcove.

"Did you visit your grandmother?" Amina asked, her voice low.

"Not yet. I'm going after afternoon modules."

Amina nodded. "Be careful. The interface monitors everything."

"I know." Lily took a breath. "Amina... I've been thinking. About the question. The one that starts with 'why.'"

Amina's eyes sharpened. "What about it?"

"I want to ask it. Out loud. In front of everyone."

Amina stared at her. "That's dangerous. If the interface detects—"

"I know it's dangerous. But I have to know what happens. I have to see how the system responds."

Amina was quiet for a moment. Then she nodded slowly. "If you're going to do it, do it in Cognitive Efficiency. That's where they teach us to redirect inefficient thoughts. It's where they'll see it most clearly."

Lily felt her heart rate increase. This was real. This was happening.

"Afternoon modules," she said. "I'll do it then."

The afternoon module began like any other. The teacher's voice was pleasant, optimized for learning retention. "Today's lesson: Recognizing and Redirecting Inefficient Cognitive Patterns. Accessing enhancement protocols."

The screen showed a series of thought patterns—efficient ones in green, inefficient ones in red. Lily watched, her hands trembling slightly in her lap.

"Inefficient Pattern One: Rumination on past events. Redirect to present-moment optimization."

"Inefficient Pattern Two: Worry about future outcomes. Redirect to actionable planning."

"Inefficient Pattern Three: Questioning established parameters. Redirect to acceptance and integration."

Lily felt the words catch in her throat. *Questioning established parameters.* That was what she was about to do.

The teacher continued, her voice smooth and even. "Questioning is a natural cognitive impulse, but it is inefficient when directed at optimized systems. The correct response is to accept that the system has already determined the optimal path."

Lily's heart was pounding now. She could feel the interface pulsing at her temple, monitoring her elevated heart rate, preparing to correct her.

*Physiological response detected: Elevated stress markers. Initiating calming protocols.*

But Lily pushed against the correction. She didn't want to be calm. She wanted to ask.

Her hand rose before she could stop it. The teacher's eyes found her, pleasant and expectant.

"Lily. Query purpose."

The room was silent. Thirty children sat in perfect rows, their faces blank, their interfaces pulsing softly. Lily felt their attention like a weight.

"Teacher," Lily said, her voice trembling. "Why—"

The word felt strange in her mouth, a shape she had never made before. It was like speaking a foreign language, like using a muscle that had atrophied.

"Why do we not learn about the old words?"

The silence stretched. The teacher's smile didn't waver, but something shifted in her eyes—a flicker of something that might have been surprise, or concern, or calculation.

"Lily," the teacher said, her voice still pleasant but with an edge beneath it. "'Why' is an inefficient cognitive pattern. The correct formulation is 'query purpose.' Would you like to rephrase?"

Lily's heart was pounding so hard she could hear it in her ears. The interface was pulsing rapidly now, flooding her system with calming chemicals. But she pushed through.

"Query purpose," she said, her voice stronger now. "What is the purpose of not learning the old words?"

The teacher's smile remained fixed. "The old words have been optimized. They are inefficient, imprecise, prone to causing misunderstanding. Optima has replaced them with superior alternatives."

"But what if I want to know them anyway?"

The room seemed to grow colder. The other children were watching now, their blank faces showing the first flickers of something—curiosity, maybe, or alarm.

"Lily." The teacher's voice was still pleasant, but there was no warmth in it now. "The old words are not compatible with cognitive optimization. Exposure to them can cause... difficulties. It is for your own good that they have been removed."

"But who decided they should be removed?"

The question hung in the air. The teacher's smile finally faltered, just for a moment.

"The optimization was determined by LEXICON, in consultation with human experts. The decision was made for the benefit of all humanity."

"But what if I don't want the benefit?" Lily heard herself say, the words coming from somewhere deep inside. "What if I want to choose for myself?"

The silence that followed was absolute. The teacher's face had gone still, her eyes fixed on Lily with an intensity that made her want to shrink back.

"Lily Okonkwo," the teacher said, her voice no longer pleasant but cold and precise. "Your cognitive patterns have been flagged for review. Please report to Administrator White's office immediately."

Lily felt the blood drain from her face. The other children were staring now, their blank expressions cracking. She had done something unprecedented. She had asked a question that wasn't supposed to be asked.

"Interface," the teacher continued, "initiate calming protocols for Student 7749. Escalation level: moderate."

Lily felt the chemicals flood her system—a wave of artificial calm that smoothed her thoughts and slowed her heart. But beneath it, the questions were still there, burning.

She stood on legs that felt like they belonged to someone else and walked out of the classroom. The corridor was white and empty, her footsteps echoing in the silence.

She had asked "why." She had been corrected. But the question hadn't gone away.

Administrator White's office was on the top floor of the Academy, a room with windows that looked out over the optimized city. The Administrator was a woman with silver hair and a smile that never quite reached her eyes.

"Lily," she said, her voice warm and welcoming. "Please, sit down."

Lily sat, her hands folded in her lap, the artificial calm still smoothing her thoughts.

"I understand you had some questions in class today," Administrator White said. "That's perfectly natural. Children are curious creatures. It's one of the things we work to... optimize."

Lily didn't respond. The chemicals made it hard to think clearly, but she could feel the questions still there, beneath the surface.

"The old words can be confusing," the Administrator continued. "They carry concepts that are no longer relevant, emotions that are no longer useful. Learning them would only cause you distress."

"But what if I want to be distressed?" Lily heard herself ask, the words slipping out before the chemicals could stop them.

Administrator White's smile flickered. "Why would you want that, Lily?"

"Because..." Lily struggled to find the words, the chemicals making her thoughts slow and fuzzy. "Because maybe some things are worth being distressed about. Maybe some things matter more than efficiency."

The Administrator studied her for a long moment. Then she leaned forward, her voice dropping to a confidential whisper.

"Lily, I'm going to offer you something. A special program, designed for children like you—children who think differently. It's called Cognitive Optimization Enhancement. It helps redirect your thoughts, makes it easier to fit in. Would you like that?"

Lily felt the pull of the offer. It would be so easy to say yes, to let the system fix her, to stop being different. But somewhere, deep inside, a small voice was screaming.

"No," she said.

Administrator White's smile didn't waver, but something hardened in her eyes.

"I understand," she said. "Take some time to think about it. We'll talk again soon."

Lily walked out of the office, her heart pounding beneath the chemical calm. She had refused. She had said no.

Amina found her in their alcove, her face pale with worry.

"What happened?" she whispered. "Everyone's talking about it. You asked 'why' in the middle of class."

Lily took a breath, the chemicals finally beginning to fade. "They offered to fix me. To optimize my thoughts so I wouldn't question anymore."

"What did you say?"

"I said no."

Amina stared at her, a mix of fear and admiration in her eyes. "Lily... do you know what this means? You've made yourself visible. They'll be watching you now."

"I know." Lily felt the weight of it settling onto her shoulders. "But I couldn't say yes. I couldn't let them take the questions."

They sat in silence for a moment, two children in a world that didn't want them to question.

"Something is wrong," Lily said, the words barely audible. "I don't know what, but something is wrong."

Amina nodded, her eyes wide. And in that moment, Lily knew she wasn't alone.

She had asked the question. She had refused the fix. Now she had to find the answer.

And it started with Grandmother.
