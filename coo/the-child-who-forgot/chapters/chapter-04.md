# Chapter 4: The Visit

After the question, after the correction, after Administrator White's offer, Lily knew she needed answers. The dreams, the word she couldn't speak, the feeling that something was wrong—all of it pointed to one place. Grandmother. Grandmother who spoke differently. Grandmother who knew old words. Grandmother who lived in the care facility across the city, in a room filled with colors that didn't exist in the Academy's palette.

She left the Academy without permission, her heart pounding with a mixture of fear and determination. The transport system was efficient, as always, and within thirty minutes she was standing outside the care facility—a building that looked different from the others in the city. Older. Less optimized.

The lobby was white and clean, like everywhere else, but there were touches of color here and there—a painting on the wall, a vase of artificial flowers, a rug with patterns that seemed almost chaotic. Lily stared at them, feeling something stir inside her.

"Can I help you?" The receptionist's voice was pleasant, optimized for efficiency.

"I'm here to visit Ruth Okonkwo. She's my grandmother."

The receptionist checked her screen. "Room 204. Second floor. Visiting hours end at standard evening time."

Lily walked through the corridors, her eyes taking in the differences between this place and the Academy. The walls were still white, but there were photographs—real photographs, not digital displays—showing people and places from another time. The floors were carpeted, not the smooth optimized surfaces she was used to. And the air smelled different—not the filtered, scentless air of the Academy, but something warmer, with hints of age and memory.

She found Room 204 and knocked softly.

"Come in," a voice called, and Lily pushed open the door.

Grandmother's room was different from anywhere else Lily had been. There were colors—real colors, not the optimized palette of the Academy. Fabrics in blue and gold draped over the chair, and the air smelled like nutmeg and something old and warm. A bed sat against one wall, and in it lay a woman with silver hair and skin the color of dark honey.

"Grandmother?" Lily said, her voice uncertain.

The woman turned, her eyes cloudy with age but still sharp. "Lily? Is that you, omomi?"

Lily didn't recognize the word, but it sounded like music, like something she should know.

"Yes, Grandmother. It's me."

Grandmother's face broke into a smile, and for a moment, she looked younger, stronger. "Come here, child. Let me look at you."

Lily crossed the room and sat on the edge of the bed. Grandmother's hand found hers—warm, slightly trembling, but strong in its grip.

"You've grown," Grandmother said, her voice thick with an accent that Lily had never heard anywhere else. "Last time I saw you, you were small. Now look at you."

"It's been a while, Grandmother."

"Too long." Grandmother's eyes grew serious. "But you're here now. That's what matters."

They sat in silence for a moment, Grandmother's hand warm in Lily's. The room felt different from anywhere else—less optimized, more human. There were objects on the shelves—books, photographs, small figurines—that seemed to have no purpose other than to exist.

"Grandmother," Lily said, her voice low. "I need to ask you something."

"Ask, omomi. I will tell you what I can."

"The words you speak—the ones that aren't Optima—what are they?"

Grandmother's eyes sharpened. "You can hear them? The interface doesn't delete them?"

Lily shook her head. "I hear them. I don't understand them, but I hear them."

Grandmother was quiet for a long moment. Then she nodded, as if making a decision.

"The words are Yoruba," she said. "The language of my childhood, of my mother and her mother before her. It is an old language, from a place called Nigeria, far across the sea."

"Nigeria," Lily repeated, feeling the shape of the word in her mouth. "I've never heard of it."

"Of course you haven't." Grandmother's voice was bitter. "The system has optimized it away. Deleted it from history, from memory, from thought. But it existed, omomi. It was real."

Lily felt something shift inside her. A language that had been deleted. A place that had been forgotten.

"Why did they delete it?"

"Because it was inefficient." Grandmother's hand tightened on hers. "Because it contained words for things they didn't want us to think about. Freedom. Resistance. Love that cannot be optimized."

Lily's heart was pounding. "Grandmother... I've been having dreams. Dreams about colors that don't exist, words I can't speak. And I asked a question today—a question that starts with 'why'—and they offered to fix me."

Grandmother's eyes grew wide. "You asked 'why'? Out loud?"

"Yes. In class. The teacher corrected me, but I kept asking."

Grandmother was silent for a long moment. Then she pulled Lily closer, her voice dropping to a whisper.

"Listen to me, omomi. You are different. You have always been different. The interface doesn't work on you the way it works on others. Do you understand what that means?"

Lily shook her head.

"It means you can remember. You can question. You can carry the words that the system wants to delete." Grandmother's eyes were intense now, boring into Lily's. "But it also means you are in danger. The system does not tolerate difference. It will try to fix you, to optimize you, to make you like everyone else."

"They already offered. I said no."

Grandmother smiled, a fierce, proud expression. "Good. That is good. But saying no is only the beginning. Now you must learn to hide. To remember without the interface knowing. To carry the words in a place they cannot reach."

"How?"

Grandmother glanced at the door, then back at Lily. "Come back tomorrow. At the same time. I will teach you what I can." She paused, her voice dropping even lower. "But you must be careful. Tell no one where you are going. The system monitors everything."

Lily nodded, feeling the weight of the secret settling onto her shoulders.

"Grandmother... what does 'omomi' mean?"

Grandmother's smile softened. "It means 'my child.' It is what my mother called me, and what I called your mother. It is a word of love, passed down through generations."

"Omomi," Lily repeated, feeling the word settle into her heart.

"Yes, omomi. Now go, before they notice you are gone. And remember—come back tomorrow. There is much I need to teach you."

Lily stood, her hand slipping from Grandmother's. At the door, she turned back.

"Grandmother? Why do you still speak Yoruba? Why didn't you let them optimize you?"

Grandmother's eyes grew distant, seeing something Lily couldn't see. "Because I remember what it was like before. I remember the colors, the music, the words that carried whole worlds inside them. And I cannot let that die." She looked at Lily, her gaze sharp. "You are the last of us, omomi. The last who can carry the words. Do not let them take that from you."

Lily nodded, the weight of it settling onto her shoulders. She was the last. The last who could remember. The last who could ask "why."

She walked out of the care facility, her mind full of Grandmother's words. The city was gray and white around her, optimized and efficient. But somewhere, in the back of her mind, a new word was burning: *Omomi.* My child.

She would come back tomorrow. She would learn. And maybe, just maybe, she would find the answer to the question that burned inside her.

The question that started with "why."
