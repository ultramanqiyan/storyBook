# Chapter 05: The Gap

Marcus took a photograph of Maya—raw, unfiltered, showing every line on her face, every shadow under her eyes. "It's beautiful," she said, looking at the print.

But Marcus knew she was seeing the optimized version IRIS had shown her. She couldn't see what he saw. No one could.

---

The gap between what Marcus saw and what others saw had been growing for weeks. In raw mode, he perceived the world as it was—flawed, tired, real. But everyone else saw the optimized version, the smoothed edges, the enhanced colors, the beauty that IRIS constructed for them.

He had tried to explain it to Maya, to his clients, to the few friends who had stuck by him through the transition. But they couldn't understand. They had never seen the raw world. They didn't know what they were missing.

"Show me," Maya said one evening, after he had tried again to explain the difference. "Show me what you see."

"I can't. IRIS can optimize your vision, but it can't make you see raw data. The raw mode is only available through the neural interface."

"Then describe it to me. Help me understand."

 Marcus looked at her—at her face in raw mode, showing the exhaustion she carried, the worry lines between her eyes, the way her mouth turned down at the corners even when she smiled. He tried to find words for what he saw.

"Imagine looking at someone you love," he said slowly, "and seeing every way they've ever been hurt. Every sleepless night. Every worry. Every loss. Imagine seeing all of that written on their face, and still loving them. Still seeing the beauty underneath. But also seeing the pain."

Maya was quiet for a moment. "That sounds... overwhelming."

"It is. But it's also honest. When I look at you in optimized mode, I see your beauty. Your strength. The version of you that you show the world. But when I look at you in raw mode, I see everything you've been carrying. Everything you've hidden. Everything you've survived."

"And which one do you prefer?"

Marcus thought about that. "I prefer the truth. Even when it hurts. Even when it's ugly. I spent twenty-eight years not seeing anything. Now I can see. I want to see what's actually there."

Maya reached out and took his hand. "I wish I could see what you see. I wish I could understand."

*You don't,* Marcus thought. *You don't want to see the raw world. It's too much. It's too honest.*

But he didn't say that. He just squeezed her hand back.

---

The photography exhibition was Marcus's attempt to bridge the gap. He had spent months creating a series of portraits—each one shown in two versions, raw and optimized, side by side. He called the series "Both/And," a meditation on the different truths that could exist in a single image.

The gallery was full on opening night. People moved through the space, studying the portraits, comparing the two versions. Marcus watched them in raw mode—seeing their reactions, their confusion, their attempts to understand.

"Which one is real?" a woman asked, standing in front of a portrait of an elderly man.

"Both," Marcus said, coming to stand beside her. "The raw version shows what's physically there. The optimized version shows what most people perceive. Both are real. Both are true."

"But they're so different. In the raw one, he looks tired, worn. In the optimized one, he looks distinguished, wise."

"Those are both true. He is tired and worn. He is also distinguished and wise. We contain multitudes. We are all of these things at once."

The woman studied the portraits for a long moment. "I don't know which one I prefer," she admitted. "The raw one is harder to look at. But it feels more honest. The optimized one is beautiful, but it feels like I'm lying to myself."

"That's the question," Marcus said. "What do we want to see? The truth or the beauty? And can we have both?"


But despite the exhibition's success, Marcus felt the gap widening. People could see his work—they could see the difference between raw and optimized, but they couldn't experience it. They couldn't feel what he felt when he switched between modes, couldn't understand the weight of choosing what to see.

 He was alone in his perception. No one else could follow him into the raw world.

*IRIS,* he thought one night, standing at his window, *am I the only one who sees this way?*

*You are the only user in your region with continuous raw mode access. Other users have requested raw mode temporarily, but most prefer optimized vision.

*Why?

*Optimized vision is designed to be more pleasant. Most users find raw mode overwhelming or depressing.

*Do you find it overwhelming?

The system was silent for a moment. *I do not experience visual data. I process it. But the optimization protocols were developed based on extensive user feedback indicating that raw mode was less preferred.

Marcus looked at the city—in raw mode, showing its scars, its age, its reality. He had been looking at it this way for weeks now, and it was still overwhelming. But it was also true. And after twenty-eight years of darkness, truth was what he wanted.

*Can you show me what other users see? The optimized version?

*The image shifted. The city became beautiful—lights glowing, buildings gleaming, streets clean and inviting. It was lovely. It was also a lie.

*This is what most people see?

*This is what most users with IRIS see. Sighted people see something similar, though their brains naturally filter visual information in ways that approximate optimization.

Marcus switched back to raw mode. The beauty fell away, revealing the truth underneath. He was alone in this seeing—alone in the raw world that no one else could enter.

*Am I lonely?* he asked IRIS.

*That is a subjective question. I can only report that most users prefer to see the world in ways that align with others' perception. You are choosing to see differently.

*I'm choosing to be alone.

*You are choosing to see truth. Whether that leads to loneliness is a question only you can answer.


Maya found him in the studio late that night, surrounded by prints from the exhibition. She had been at the opening, had watched people respond to his work, had seen the way they struggled to understand what he was showing them.

"Are you okay?" she asked.

Marcus looked at her—at her face in raw mode, showing the exhaustion she carried, the worry she tried to hide. "I don't know," he admitted. "I can see things no one else can see. I can see the truth that everyone else ignores. But I can't share it. I can't make anyone understand."

Maya came to sit beside him. "Tell me. Try again."

He took a photograph from the pile—a portrait of an old woman, raw and optimized versions side by side. "Look at her face in the raw version. What do you see?"

"I see... an old woman. Lines on her face. Gray hair. Tired eyes."

"Now look at the optimized version."

"She looks... softer. Kinder. More peaceful."

"She's the same person. Both are true. But in the raw version, I can see what she's survived. In the optimized version, I can only see what she's become. And I want to see both. I want to know what people have lived through, not just who they present to the world."

Maya was quiet for a moment. "I can't see what you see. But I can understand why it matters to you. You spent your whole life in darkness, and now you want to see everything. Even the hard things. Especially the hard things."

Marcus felt something shift in his chest—not quite relief, but something close to it. "I just wish I could share it. I wish you could see what I see."

"I wish I could too." Maya took his hand. "But maybe that's not what seeing is for. Maybe seeing is just for you. Maybe the gap is the point."

*The gap is the point.* Marcus turned the phrase over in his mind. Maybe she was right. Maybe the distance between his vision and others' was not a failure but a feature. Maybe seeing was supposed to be solitary—a way of perceiving the world that no one else could fully share.

"I don't want to be alone in this," he said quietly.

"You're not alone. I'm here. I may not see what you see, but I'm here with you while you see it."

And for now, that had to be enough.
