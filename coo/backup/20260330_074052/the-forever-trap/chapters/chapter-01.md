# Chapter 01: The Race

Catherine woke up and checked her hands. Yesterday, they had been spotted with age. Today, the spots had spread. At twenty-nine, her body was dying of old age—progeria, the doctors called it. She called it a clock that wouldn't stop ticking, no matter how much she wanted it to slow down.

The morning light filtered through the curtains, casting long shadows across the bedroom. Catherine lay still for a moment, taking inventory. Her joints ached—the familiar stiffness that had plagued her since her teens. Her skin was thin, translucent, the veins visible beneath like rivers on a map. Her hair, once thick and dark, was now sparse and gray.

*Another day,* she thought. *Another step closer to the end.*

She had been diagnosed with Hutchinson-Gilford progeria syndrome at age four. Most children with the condition died in their early teens. Catherine had been lucky—or unlucky, depending on how you looked at it. She had survived to twenty-nine, a medical marvel, a statistical anomaly. But the price of survival was a body that aged at approximately eight times the normal rate.

By the time she was twenty, she had looked seventy. Now, at twenty-nine, her biological age was closer to two hundred. Her heart was failing. Her lungs were deteriorating. Every system in her body was breaking down, and there was nothing anyone could do to stop it.

She got up slowly, her joints protesting, and walked to the bathroom. The mirror above the sink showed a face that looked like it belonged to someone's great-grandmother—wrinkled, spotted, hollow-eyed. But behind those eyes, Catherine was still twenty-nine. Still young. Still full of thoughts and dreams and desires that her body couldn't support.

*How much longer?* she wondered, not for the first time. *Months? Weeks? Days?*

The doctors had stopped giving estimates. Every prediction had been wrong. She had outlived every prognosis, defied every expectation. But she couldn't outrun the clock forever. Eventually, it would catch her.

She dressed slowly, choosing clothes that hid the worst of her condition—long sleeves, loose pants, a scarf to cover her thinning hair. The ritual was familiar, automatic. She had been hiding her body for years, presenting a carefully curated version of herself to the world.

The phone rang as she was making breakfast. It was Dr. Webb, her primary physician.

"Catherine, I have something to discuss with you. Can you come to the office today?"

His voice was careful, measured—the voice of someone with news that might be good or might be devastating.

"I'll be there at two," she said.

The morning passed slowly. Catherine tried to read, but the words blurred together. She tried to watch television, but the shows seemed meaningless. Everything seemed meaningless when you were counting down the days.

At one-thirty, she called a car to take her to the medical center. The driver glanced at her in the rearview mirror, his expression a mixture of curiosity and pity. She was used to those looks. People couldn't help staring at someone so young who looked so old.

Dr. Webb's office was on the fifth floor of the research building. Catherine had been there dozens of times over the years, for tests, consultations, treatments that never worked. The waiting room was familiar, the magazines outdated, the other patients elderly in the normal way—people who had lived full lives before their bodies began to fail.

Catherine had never had a full life. She had had a shortened one.

"Catherine, come in," Dr. Webb said, opening the door to his office. His face was serious, but there was something else in his expression—something like excitement, or hope.

She sat in the chair across from his desk, her body settling into the familiar position of receiving bad news. But Dr. Webb didn't deliver bad news. Instead, he pulled up something on his computer screen—a diagram, complex and technical.

"I've been working on something," he said. "Something experimental. Something that might help you."

Catherine felt a flicker of the hope she had learned to suppress. "Help me how?"

"There's a treatment—a life extension protocol. It uses nanomachines to repair and maintain cellular structures. It was originally developed for age-related degeneration in normal aging, but we think it might work for your condition."

"Work how? Would it cure the progeria?"

Dr. Webb shook his head slowly. "Not cure. But it could stop the progression. Your body would stop aging. You would remain at your current biological state indefinitely."

Catherine stared at him. "Indefinitely? You mean... I wouldn't die?"

"You wouldn't die of aging. Your cells would be maintained in their current state. You would still be vulnerable to accidents, injuries, diseases. But the degeneration would stop."

The hope was growing now, a flame she had thought was extinguished. "I could live? I could have more time?"

"Yes. But there are conditions." Dr. Webb's voice was careful. "The treatment is permanent. Once started, it can't be reversed. And your body would remain at its current biological age—you'll never grow younger. You'll always look... the way you look now."

Catherine thought about that. Forever in a body that looked two hundred years old. Forever frail, forever spotted, forever aged. But forever alive.

"What's the alternative?" she asked.

"The alternative is that you continue to age. Your condition will progress. You have, in my estimation, perhaps six months. Maybe less."

Six months. Maybe less. The words hung in the air between them.

"I'll do it," Catherine said. "I'll take the treatment."

Dr. Webb nodded slowly. "I thought you might say that. But I want you to understand what you're choosing. This isn't just more time, Catherine. This is... potentially forever. You'll watch everyone you know grow old and die. You'll see generations come and go. You'll be alone in a way that most people never experience."

"I've been alone my whole life," Catherine said quietly. "Watching from the outside while everyone else lives. At least this way, I'll have time to actually live."

Dr. Webb studied her for a long moment. Then he nodded again. "The treatment can be administered tomorrow. I'll prepare the protocol."

Catherine left the office in a daze. The world outside seemed different—brighter, sharper, more present. She had been given a choice between death and forever, and she had chosen forever.

That night, she called Marcus, her childhood friend. They had grown up together, the only person who had treated her normally despite her condition.

"I'm getting a treatment tomorrow," she told him. "It's going to stop the aging."

"That's wonderful," Marcus said, his voice warm with genuine happiness. "You'll have more time."

"I'll have all the time in the world," she said. "I'll have forever."

There was a pause on the other end. "Forever is a long time, Catherine."

"I know. But the alternative is six months. I'll take forever."

She hung up the phone and looked at herself in the mirror—the wrinkled face, the thinning hair, the body that looked like it belonged to someone's great-grandmother. Tomorrow, she would begin the process of staying exactly this way. Forever.

*Forever twenty-nine,* she thought. *Forever dying. Or forever living?*

She didn't know. But she was about to find out.
