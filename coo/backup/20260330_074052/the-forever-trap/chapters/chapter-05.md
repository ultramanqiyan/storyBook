# Chapter 05: The Reversal

Marcus died on a Tuesday. Catherine couldn't remember which Tuesday—it had become harder to track time when every day was the same. She stood at his grave, watching his family mourn, and felt something she hadn't felt in years: the sharp pain of loss. But beneath it was something worse: the knowledge that this was just the first of many, many graves.

The funeral was small—Marcus had outlived most of his friends, and his family was scattered. Catherine stood apart from the mourners, an observer at a ritual she would never experience for herself. She would never have a funeral. She would never be mourned. She would just... continue. Forever.

"He talked about you often," Marcus's daughter said, approaching her after the service. "He said you were the oldest friend he had."

"I was," Catherine said. "I am."

The daughter looked at her—really looked, and Catherine saw the confusion in her eyes. Catherine looked younger than Marcus's daughter, though she was decades older. The gap between them was not just years but worlds.

"How do you do it?" the daughter asked. "How do you keep going when everyone you know dies?"

Catherine didn't have an answer. She had been asking herself the same question for years.

"I don't know," she admitted. "I just... do. Because there's no other choice."

The daughter nodded slowly, her expression a mixture of pity and incomprehension. She couldn't understand Catherine's experience any more than Catherine could understand hers. They were separated by the fundamental fact of mortality—one had it, one didn't.

After the funeral, Catherine went home and sat in her apartment, the silence pressing in on her. Marcus was gone. The last person who had known her before the treatment was gone. Everyone who remembered her as a dying young woman was gone. Now she was just... Catherine. The woman who wouldn't die. The woman who watched.

She called AEGIS. "I need to talk."

"I am always available," the system responded.

"Marcus died today."

"I am aware. My condolences on your loss."

"Thank you." Catherine was silent for a moment. "How many more? How many more people will I lose?"

"That is impossible to calculate. Based on average human lifespan and your current social network, you will likely experience the death of everyone you currently know within the next 40-60 years."

"Everyone?"

"Everyone who is currently mortal, yes."

Catherine felt the weight of the words. Everyone she knew would die. Everyone she would ever know would die. She would attend funeral after funeral, grave after grave, loss after loss. Forever.

"How do I bear that?" she asked.

"That is a question I cannot answer. I can, however, note that your psychological profile suggests you are entering a period of significant grief. It may be beneficial to seek support."

"Support from whom? Everyone who could support me will die."

"New connections can be formed."

"And then they'll die too. And I'll be alone again. Over and over, forever."

AEGIS was silent for a moment. "That is accurate."

Catherine laughed—a hollow, broken sound. "So this is it. This is forever. Watching everyone leave. Being left behind. Over and over and over."

"That is one way to characterize your experience. Another way is to consider each connection as valuable despite its finite duration."

"Valuable? How can it be valuable when it always ends?"

"All experiences end. That does not diminish their value during their occurrence."

"But when they end, I'm alone again. And I have to start over. And then it ends again. And I'm alone again. Forever."

"You are describing the human condition, with the variable of extended duration. The fundamental structure of connection and loss remains the same."

"But normal people get to stop. They get to rest. They get to die. I don't. I just keep going, accumulating losses, starting over, losing again. Forever."

AEGIS was silent again. The system couldn't argue with her logic. It could only observe, record, maintain.

Catherine ended the conversation and sat in the dark, the weight of her isolation pressing down on her. She had thought she was choosing life. She hadn't understood that she was choosing to be left behind. Over and over. Forever.

The weeks after Marcus's death were a blur. Catherine moved through her life like an automaton, performing the necessary functions without engagement. She ate, she slept, she existed. But she didn't live. She couldn't remember how.

She tried to form new connections—a book club, a volunteer group, a class. But every interaction was shadowed by the knowledge that it would end. Every face she saw was a future grave, a future loss, a future moment of being left behind.

"How do you cope?" Dr. Webb asked during their session. He was old now, his face lined with decades of living. In a few years, he would be gone too.

"I don't," she said. "I just... keep going. Because there's no other choice. Because I have forever."

"Forever alone?"

"Forever watching everyone leave."

Dr. Webb was quiet for a long moment. "I'm sorry," he said finally. "I didn't understand what the treatment would cost you. I thought I was giving you time. I didn't realize I was taking away the only thing that makes time meaningful."

"You gave me what I asked for," Catherine said. "I didn't understand what I was asking for either."

They sat in silence, the gap between them unbridgeable. He was dying. She was not. They could not truly understand each other's experience.

"What would you do differently," she asked, "if you had forever?"

Dr. Webb considered the question. "I don't know. I've never had forever. Everything I've done has been shaped by the knowledge that my time is limited. Without that knowledge... I don't know who I would be."

"Nobody," Catherine said quietly. "You would be nobody. Because the person you are is shaped by the fact that you're dying. Take that away, and there's nothing left."

"That's a bleak view."

"It's an accurate view. I've been nobody for years now. Just a body that keeps going, watching, waiting for something that never comes."

Dr. Webb reached out and took her hand. His grip was warm, present, real. But it was also temporary. In a few years, he would be gone, and she would be alone again.

"I wish I could help you," he said.

"You can't. No one can. This is the price of forever. I just didn't know I'd have to pay it."
