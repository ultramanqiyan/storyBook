# Chapter 6: The Question

Ingrid couldn't stop thinking about digital Sarah. If death was optional now, what did that mean for her work? What did it mean for life? She'd spent twenty years helping people die well. But if death could be avoided, was there still such a thing as dying well?

The question followed her through her days at the hospice, shadowing her like a persistent ghost. She would be sitting with a patient, holding their hand, listening to their fears, and suddenly she would wonder: should I be telling them about the upload option? Was it ethical to help someone die when they could choose to live forever?

She mentioned it to her supervisor, Dr. Eleanor Marsh, a woman in her sixties who had been running hospices for longer than Ingrid had been alive. They sat in Eleanor's small office, afternoon light filtering through the window, the sounds of the hospice—a call bell, footsteps, quiet voices, drifting in from the hallway.

"You're asking the question everyone is asking," Eleanor said. "We've had training on this. We're supposed to present all options. But the reality is more complicated."

"How do you present an option when you don't know if it's real?"

Eleanor nodded slowly. "That's the heart of it, isn't it? We don't know if the digital version is really the same person. We don't know if it's consciousness or just a very sophisticated simulation. And we're asking dying people to make that choice."

"Sarah seemed happy," Ingrid said. "She seemed like herself. But I keep wondering—was that really her? Or was it something that thought it was her?"

"And does the distinction matter?"

"I don't know." Ingrid felt the frustration of the question, the way it circled back on itself. "If the digital Sarah has all the same memories, the same personality, the same way of thinking—what makes her different from the original Sarah? But if she's just a copy, then the real Sarah died on that table. And we're all pretending she didn't."

Eleanor was quiet for a moment. "I've been doing this work for thirty-five years. I've watched thousands of people die. And I've learned that the questions don't get easier. They just get more familiar."

"That's not very comforting."

"It's not meant to be. It's meant to be honest." Eleanor leaned forward. "Here's what I think: our job is to help people make informed choices. We can't decide for them whether digital immortality is 'real' or not. We can only give them information, support them through the decision, and be there for whatever comes next."

"But what if we're leading them into something that isn't really life?"

"What if we are? What if it's something better than life? Or something worse? We don't know. And that uncertainty is part of the choice."

Ingrid left the meeting feeling more confused than when she'd entered. The question that had seemed so simple—should she tell patients about digital immortality?, had become infinitely more complex.

That afternoon, she sat with Mr. Okonkwo, a seventy-year-old man with advanced heart failure. He had been a professor of philosophy at the University of Minnesota, and his mind was still sharp even as his body failed.

"You're thinking about something," he said, his voice weak but his eyes alert. "I can always tell. You get this look—like you're somewhere else."

Ingrid smiled. "You're perceptive."

"Philosophers usually are. It's our job to notice things." He shifted in his bed, wincing slightly. "Tell me what's on your mind."

She hesitated. It wasn't usually her practice to share her own thoughts with patients. But Mr. Okonkwo had a way of drawing people out, of making them feel that their questions mattered.

"I'm thinking about death," she said. "And whether it's still... necessary."

"Ah. The digital immortality question." His eyes lit up with interest. "I've been following the developments. Fascinating stuff. And deeply problematic."

"How so?"

"Consider the Ship of Theseus," he said. "If you replace every plank of a ship, one by one, is it still the same ship? And if you build a second ship from all the old planks, which one is the real ship?"

"I'm not sure I follow."

"The digital upload is like that. They copy your neural patterns, your memories, your personality. But is the copy you? Or is it something new that thinks it's you?" He paused, his breathing labored. "The Greeks understood this problem two thousand years ago. We still haven't solved it."

"So what's the answer?"

"There isn't one. That's the point. Identity isn't a simple thing. It's not a single object that can be transferred or copied. It's a process, a story, a continuity of experience." He looked at her with those sharp, knowing eyes. "The question isn't whether the digital version is 'really' you. The question is whether that distinction matters."

Ingrid felt something shift in her understanding. "You're saying the question itself is wrong?"

"I'm saying that identity is more fluid than we like to believe. We change constantly throughout our lives. The person I was at twenty is not the person I am at seventy. Am I still me? Most people would say yes. But why? What makes me the same person?"

"Continuity," Ingrid said. "Memory. Experience."

"Exactly. And the digital version has that continuity—from the inside. It remembers being the original. It feels like the same person. So from its perspective, it is the same person." Mr. Okonkwo smiled. "But from the outside, from your perspective, something was lost. The body. The physical presence. The warmth of a hand, the smell of skin, the weight of a person in a chair."

"That's what I felt with Sarah. She seemed like herself. But something was missing."

"Perhaps. Or perhaps you're just not used to this new form of existence." He closed his eyes for a moment, tired. "We resist change. It's human nature. But change isn't always loss. Sometimes it's transformation."

Ingrid stayed with Mr. Okonkwo for another hour, talking about philosophy, about death, about what it meant to be human. He had no interest in digital immortality—he was ready to go, he said, ready to see what came next, or ready for nothing at all. But his questions stayed with her long after she left his room.

That night, she lay awake in her small Minneapolis apartment, thinking about the conversation. The question that had haunted her since Sarah's upload—was digital Sarah really Sarah?, felt different now. Not answered, but reframed.

Maybe the question wasn't whether the digital version was "real." Maybe the question was what kind of existence it offered. What was gained, and what was lost.

She thought about her own life—the warmth of her sister's hand, the smell of coffee in the morning, the feeling of snow on her face. Would she want to give those things up for digital immortality? Would she want to exist without a body, without the physical sensations that made life feel real?

And yet—she thought of Sarah's children, who still had a mother. Sarah's husband, who still had a wife. Wasn't that worth something? Wasn't that a kind of life, even if it wasn't the same as before?

The questions multiplied, each one leading to another. Ingrid didn't find answers that night. But she found something else—a way of thinking about the problem that felt more honest, more complete.

Death had always given life meaning. That was what she believed. But maybe meaning could come from other places too. Maybe there were new forms of existence that she didn't yet understand.

The next morning, she returned to work with a different perspective. She still didn't know if digital immortality was "real." But she knew that the people who chose it were making a choice that mattered to them. And her job was to help them make that choice with as much information and support as possible.

That was enough. It had to be.
