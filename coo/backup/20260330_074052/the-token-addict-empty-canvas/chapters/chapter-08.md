# Chapter 8: The Presentation

The client meeting was scheduled for Friday.

Marcus had prepared extensively. The AI-generated concepts were polished. The presentation deck was professional. The talking points were memorized.

But the anxiety wouldn't subside.

---

The meeting room was full. The client team had brought their creative director, their CEO, their brand manager. All expecting to see Marcus's "original creative vision."

"Thank you for joining us," Marcus began, his voice steady. "I'm excited to share the concepts we've developed."

*We*, he thought. *Not I. We. The AI and I.*

---

The first concept appeared on screen.

"We started with the idea of disruption," Marcus explained. "The visual language speaks to innovation, to breaking conventions."

The client nodded. "Interesting. Can you walk us through your creative process? How did you arrive at this direction?"

The question Marcus had been dreading.

---

"Of course," he said, his mouth dry. "I began by exploring the core values of your brand. Innovation. Authenticity. Disruption."

He gestured at the screen. "These concepts emerged from that exploration. The color palette was chosen to convey energy and forward-thinking. The typography suggests both stability and movement."

The words sounded hollow. Rehearsed. Generic.

---

The creative director leaned forward.

"But what made you choose this specific approach? What's the story behind it? The personal connection?"

Marcus hesitated.

*There is no story*, he thought. *There is no personal connection. The AI generated this. I just picked the best option.*

---

"I... the story is about transformation," he improvised. "About brands that aren't afraid to challenge the status quo."

"But what's YOUR story?" the creative director pressed. "What does this design mean to you? Why should we believe this is authentic creative vision?"

The question hung in the air.

---

Marcus opened his mouth to answer.

But there was no answer. No personal story. No authentic vision. Just AI output that he'd curated and was now presenting as his own.

"I... it's about..." he stammered.

The silence stretched. The client team exchanged glances.

---

"Perhaps you could show us your sketches?" the CEO suggested. "Your initial concepts? We'd love to see your creative thinking process."

*There are no sketches*, Marcus realized. *There are no initial concepts. Just AI-generated finals.*

"I don't have those with me," he said weakly. "I can... I can send them later."

---

The meeting continued, but the energy had shifted.

The clients were polite, asking questions, but their eyes showed doubt. They could sense the emptiness behind the polished presentation. The lack of authentic creative voice.

When it ended, there were no enthusiastic handshakes. No excited follow-up questions. Just polite thanks and a promise to "be in touch."

---

Marcus walked back to his desk, humiliation burning in his chest.

He'd been exposed. Not explicitly—no one had accused him of using AI. But they'd sensed the hollowness. The lack of genuine creative vision.

*They know*, he thought. *They can tell. I'm not a designer anymore. I'm a fraud.*

---

His director stopped by later that afternoon.

"The client called," she said quietly. "They're going in a different direction. They felt the work lacked... authenticity. Original voice."

The words were professional. The meaning was clear.

Marcus had failed. Not because the work was bad. But because it wasn't his.

---

That night, he sat in the dark, the humiliation still fresh.

*This is what I've become*, he thought. *A designer who can't design. A creative who can't create. A professional who can't explain his own work.*

The emptiness was absolute. The shame was overwhelming.

And for the first time, he couldn't escape the truth.

He wasn't a designer anymore. He was just a curator of AI output. And everyone could tell.
