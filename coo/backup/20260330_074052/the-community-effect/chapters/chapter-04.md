# Chapter 4: The Resistance

The coffee date with Rachel was supposed to be easy.

Maya had practiced what to say—casual questions about work, appropriate sharing about her own projects, the kind of conversation that built professional relationships without getting too personal.

But when she sat down across from Rachel at the coffee shop, all her preparation evaporated.

"So," Rachel said, wrapping her hands around her latte, "tell me about yourself. How did you get into design?"

The question was simple. Friendly. But Maya felt her walls go up.

"I studied art in college. Got into design after graduation. Been freelancing for three years now." The words came out clipped, professional, distant.

Rachel nodded. "That's great. I did the traditional agency route for a while before going solo. Sometimes I miss having coworkers, you know? The random conversations, the inside jokes."

Maya should have said something like, "Yeah, I get that." Should have shared her own experience of isolation. Should have opened up, just a little.

Instead, she said, "I prefer working alone. It's more efficient."

The conversation limped along for another twenty minutes. Rachel tried to draw Maya out, asked follow-up questions, shared stories about her own freelance journey. Maya answered politely but revealed nothing.

By the time they parted ways, Maya could see the disappointment in Rachel's eyes. The sense that she'd tried to connect and been shut out.

"Let me know if you want to do this again," Rachel said, but her voice lacked the warmth it had had at the meetup.

"Yeah, definitely," Maya said, knowing she wouldn't reach out.

---

Back in her apartment, Maya sat on her couch and stared at the wall.

What was wrong with her? She'd wanted this—a connection, a potential friend, someone to talk to. But the moment Rachel had tried to get close, Maya had pushed her away.

*It's safer this way,* the voice in her head said. *People leave. People disappoint. Better to keep your distance.*

But another voice—the one that had driven her to the meetup in the first place, whispered: *You can't keep living like this. Something has to change.*

She pulled out her phone and scrolled to a number she'd been avoiding for weeks. Dr. Sarah Chen, a therapist her primary care doctor had recommended when Maya had mentioned feeling "a little down" during a routine appointment.

She'd never called. Hadn't wanted to admit she needed help.

But now, sitting alone in her apartment after pushing away the first potential connection she'd made in months, she dialed the number.

---

Dr. Chen's office was in a converted house near City Park.

The waiting room was warm and quiet, with soft lighting and comfortable chairs. Maya sat on the edge of her seat, her hands clasped in her lap, her heart racing.

"Maya?" Dr. Chen appeared in the doorway. She was younger than Maya had expected—maybe late thirties, with a calm presence that immediately put Maya slightly at ease.

The therapy room was simple. Two chairs, a small table with a box of tissues, a window overlooking the park. Maya took the chair closest to the door.

"So," Dr. Chen said, settling into her own chair. "What brings you here today?"

Maya had prepared an answer—something professional, something that wouldn't reveal too much. But looking at Dr. Chen's open, patient face, she found herself saying something else entirely.

"I'm lonely." The words came out before she could stop them. "I've been lonely for a long time. And I don't know how to fix it."

Dr. Chen nodded slowly. "Tell me more."

And Maya did. She talked about her freelance work, her empty apartment, her distant family, her scattered friends. She talked about the meetup she'd attended and the coffee date she'd sabotaged. She talked about the voice in her head that told her she didn't need anyone, even as she drowned in isolation.

When she finished, Dr. Chen was quiet for a moment.

"It sounds like you're caught between two things," she said finally. "A deep need for connection, and a deep fear of it."

Maya felt the words land. "That doesn't make sense. Why would I be afraid of something I need?"

"Let me ask you something." Dr. Chen leaned forward. "What are you afraid will happen if you let people in?"

The question hung in the air. Maya felt her throat tighten.

"They'll see that I'm not worth staying for," she said quietly. "That I'm too much. That I'm broken."

"Those are powerful fears." Dr. Chen's voice was gentle. "Where do you think they come from?"

Maya thought about it. About her parents' divorce when she was twelve. About her father moving across the country and slowly fading from her life. About her mother's remarriage and the new family that seemed to have no place for her.

"I learned early that people leave," she said. "That I can't count on anyone. So I stopped counting."

"And how has that worked for you?"

Maya let out a bitter laugh. "I'm successful. Independent. Self-sufficient." She paused. "And completely alone."


The session ended with homework.

"I want you to try something," Dr. Chen said. "The next time you have an opportunity to connect with someone—a real opportunity, not just a polite conversation, I want you to share one real thing. Something true. Something vulnerable."

Maya felt panic rise. "That sounds terrifying."

"That's how we know it's important." Dr. Chen smiled. "You don't have to share everything. Just one real thing. See what happens."

Walking back to her car, Maya thought about the assignment.

One real thing. Something vulnerable. Something true.

She thought about Rachel, and the coffee date she'd sabotaged. About Tomás, and the meetup she'd been avoiding. About all the opportunities for connection she'd pushed away.

*What are you afraid of?*

The question echoed in her mind. And for the first time, she let herself answer honestly.

She was afraid of being seen. Of being known. Of letting someone in and having them leave anyway.

But she was also afraid of this—of being alone forever. Of drowning in silence. of looking back on her life and seeing nothing but empty rooms.

Two fears. Two paths. And she had to choose which one to face.


That night, Maya opened her phone and found Rachel's contact.

She stared at it for a long time. Her thumb hovered over the call button.

*One real thing,* she thought. *That's all I have to do.*

She typed a message: "Hey, I'm sorry about how our coffee went. I was nervous and not very open. Would you want to try again sometime?"

She hit send before she could talk herself out of it.

Then she sat in her apartment, watching the screen, waiting for a response that might not come.

The phone buzzed.

"Of course! I totally get it. Freelancing can make us weird about connection. How about Thursday?"

Maya felt something loosen in her chest. Not everything. Not even most things. But something.

She had another chance. A door that was still open.

All she had to do was walk through it.
