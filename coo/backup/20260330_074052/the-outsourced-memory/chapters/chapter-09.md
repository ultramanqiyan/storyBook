# Chapter 9: The Choice

The conversation with Susan happened on a Tuesday evening.

Mike had come home late, after another day of trying to relearn what he'd forgotten—walking through properties without the algorithm, taking notes by hand, trying to feel houses the way he used to. The effort had left him exhausted, his mind and body aching from the unfamiliar work of paying attention.

Susan was in the kitchen, making dinner. The smell of garlic and onions filled the air, and for a moment, Mike just stood in the doorway, watching her. She moved through the space with the ease of twenty years of familiarity, reaching for spices without looking, stirring the pan with practiced motion.

*This is what expertise looks like*, he thought. *Not data. Not algorithms. Just the quiet knowledge that comes from doing something for a long time.*

"Hey," she said, glancing up. "You're late."

"Walking through houses." He dropped his keys on the counter, poured himself a glass of water. "The old-fashioned way."

Susan's expression shifted, a careful neutrality that he'd learned to read over the years. "How's that going?"

He leaned against the counter, feeling the weight of the question. "Honestly? I don't know. I'm trying to remember things I've forgotten. But it's like... like trying to speak a language I used to know. The words are there, somewhere, but I can't find them."

Susan turned off the stove and turned to face him. "Mike. What's really going on?"

The question opened something in him. He'd been carrying this for weeks now—the Chen mistake, the empty memory, the slow realization of what he'd lost. He hadn't talked about it, not really. He'd been too ashamed, too confused, too uncertain of what to say.

"I don't know who I am anymore," he said quietly. "I've been doing this job for twenty-five years. I used to know things—real things, things I could feel in my body. And now..." He gestured vaguely at the space around him. "Now I'm just a signature. A name on a document. The algorithm does the work, and I pretend I'm still the expert."

Susan was quiet for a moment. Then she said, "So stop."

"Stop what?"

"Stop pretending." She came to him, took his hands in hers. "If you're not the expert anymore, then be something else. Or be the expert again. But don't keep signing your name to things you don't understand."

The words landed like a blow. "That's what I'm trying to do. That's why I've been walking through houses without the algorithm. That's why I've been trying to remember."

"But?"

He looked at her, feeling the weight of the truth he'd been avoiding. "But I don't know if I can. The knowledge is gone, Susan. I outsourced it, bit by bit, until there was nothing left. And now I'm trying to get it back, but it's like... like trying to rebuild a house from photographs. I can see what it used to look like, but I can't feel it anymore."

Susan's grip tightened on his hands. "Then find a new way to feel it."

"What does that mean?"

"It means you used to learn by walking through houses. So walk through more houses. You used to learn by talking to clients. So talk to more clients. You used to learn by making mistakes and fixing them. So make mistakes and fix them." Her voice was firm, certain. "You didn't become an expert overnight, Mike. You won't become one again overnight either."

---

He thought about that conversation for the rest of the evening.

*You didn't become an expert overnight. You won't become one again overnight either.*

It was true. The expertise he'd lost had taken twenty-five years to build. He couldn't expect to reclaim it in a few weeks of effort. But he also couldn't keep doing what he'd been doing—signing his name to decisions he didn't understand, pretending the algorithm had made him better when really it had made him obsolete.

*What do I want?* he asked himself. *Who do I want to be?*

The answer came slowly, reluctantly, but clearly.

*I want to be the person who knows things. Not because the algorithm tells me. Because I've felt them. Because I've learned them. Because they're mine.*

---

The next morning, he went to see his manager.

Tom Bradley had been running the agency for fifteen years, overseeing the transition from human expertise to algorithmic efficiency. He was a pragmatist, a numbers man, the kind of leader who believed in data over instinct.

"Mike." Tom looked up from his screen as Mike entered the office. "What's on your mind?"

Mike sat in the chair across from Tom's desk, feeling the familiar weight of the conversation to come. "I need to talk to you about HomeMatch."

Tom's expression shifted slightly, a wariness that Mike hadn't expected. "What about it?"

"I've been thinking about how we use it. How I use it." Mike leaned forward, trying to find the right words. "I think I've been relying on it too much. I think I've... outsourced my expertise to the algorithm."

Tom was quiet for a moment. Then he said, "That's the point, Mike. The algorithm is better than any individual agent. It processes more data, identifies more patterns, makes more accurate predictions. That's not outsourcing—that's leveraging."

"But what if it's wrong?"

"The accuracy rate is 97%."

"The Chen property was wrong." Mike's voice was steady. "The algorithm said 94% confidence. The foundation had structural damage that the inspection missed. I should have caught it. I used to catch things like that."

Tom's expression hardened slightly. "You can't blame yourself for one outlier. The algorithm is right 97 times out of 100. No human can match that."

*But a human can feel when something is wrong*, Mike thought. *A human can notice things that don't show up in the data. A human can be wrong and learn from it.*

"I'm not saying the algorithm is useless," he said carefully. "I'm saying I've stopped trusting myself. And I think that's a problem."

Tom leaned back in his chair, studying Mike with an expression that was hard to read. "What are you proposing?"

"I want to change how I work. I want to walk through properties before I look at the match scores. I want to form my own opinions before I see what the algorithm recommends. I want to... remember how to be an expert."

The silence stretched between them. Mike could hear the hum of the servers in the next room, the digital heartbeat of the business that had slowly replaced its human pulse.

"Mike." Tom's voice was careful, measured. "I appreciate your commitment to excellence. But you need to understand something. The industry has changed. Clients expect data. They expect match scores and probability assessments and algorithmic recommendations. They don't want an agent who goes with his gut. They want an agent who goes with the data."

*That's not true*, Mike wanted to say. *The Chens wanted an agent who would protect them from a bad decision. They trusted me, and I let them down.*

But he didn't say it. He could see that Tom wasn't going to understand. The algorithm was the future, and Mike was asking to return to the past.

"I'm not saying I won't use the data," he said instead. "I'm saying I want to use it differently. I want to bring my own expertise back into the process. I want to be a partner to the algorithm, not a passenger."

Tom studied him for a long moment. Then he sighed. "Fine. Try it your way. But I want to see results. If your numbers drop, if your client satisfaction scores decline, we'll have a different conversation."

Mike nodded, feeling the weight of the compromise. It wasn't what he wanted—what he wanted was to throw the algorithm away entirely, to go back to the days when he'd known houses in his bones. But it was a start.

"Thank you," he said. "I appreciate the chance."

Tom nodded, already turning back to his screen. The conversation was over.


Mike walked out of Tom's office feeling both relieved and uncertain.

He'd gotten permission to try something different, to bring his own expertise back into the process. But he'd also seen the resistance, the institutional belief that the algorithm was always right, that human judgment was a relic of a bygone era.

*They don't understand*, he thought. *They've never known anything else.*

But he had. He remembered the days before HomeMatch, before the data, before the slow surrender of his expertise to the algorithm's seductive certainty. He remembered what it felt like to walk through a house and know it—not because the data said so, but because he'd felt it in his feet and hands and nose.

*That's what I want to get back*, he thought. *That's who I want to be.*


He spent the rest of the week experimenting.

Each morning, he'd pull up the day's matches and close his eyes, trying to remember what he used to know. Then he'd drive to the properties without looking at the algorithm's assessments, walking through each house with his senses open.

The first few days were frustrating. His body had forgotten how to pay attention. He'd walk through a house and miss things that would have been obvious to him ten years ago—the slope of a floor, the smell of water damage, the sound of a settling foundation.

But slowly, gradually, the knowledge began to return.

It came in flashes—a sudden awareness of the way light fell through a window, a faint recognition of a crack pattern that suggested structural stress, a subtle feeling in his feet that told him a foundation was settling. The sensations were faint, buried under years of disuse, but they were there.

*This is how I used to know things*, he thought. *This is what I've been missing.*

He started taking notes by hand, recording his observations before he looked at the algorithm's assessments. Sometimes his instincts matched the data. Sometimes they didn't. And when they didn't, he forced himself to investigate—to walk through the house again, to look for what he'd missed, to understand why his body was telling him something different from the screen.

*This is what expertise feels like*, he realized. *Not certainty. Not accuracy. But the willingness to be wrong and learn from it.*


On Friday, he had a breakthrough.

The property was a colonial in a neighborhood he used to know well—good schools, stable property values, the kind of place that would generate a high match score for most clients. He walked through the house with his eyes and feet and hands, trying to notice everything.

The floors creaked in a familiar rhythm. The walls held the faint smell of fresh paint. The light came through the windows in patterns that told him which way the house faced.

*South*, he thought. *Good natural light in the afternoon.*

He walked into the basement and felt the slope of the floor—subtle, but present. He ran his hand along the walls, feeling for cracks, for signs of settling, for the particular texture that indicated water damage.

And then he felt it.

A faint unevenness in the floor near the back corner, almost invisible but definitely there. He knelt down, pressing his palm flat against the concrete, trying to understand what he was sensing.

*This floor has settled*, he thought. *Recently. And not just from age.*

He pulled out his phone and opened the property file, looking for the inspection report. The foundation had been inspected three months ago—no structural concerns, minor settling typical for age.

But Mike's hand was telling him something different.

He walked back upstairs and called the seller's agent, asking questions about the property's history. Had there been any water issues? Any foundation repairs? Any work done in the past year?

The answers came slowly, reluctantly. There had been some drainage work in the backyard. The sellers had noticed some cracking in the basement walls and had it repaired. The work had been done six months ago, just before the house went on the market.

*The inspection report is wrong*, Mike realized. *Or incomplete. The foundation has been repaired, but the underlying issue—the drainage problem, hasn't been fully addressed.*

He drove back to the office and pulled up the algorithm's assessment. Match score: 91%. Confidence: high. No mention of foundation issues or drainage problems.

*The algorithm missed it*, he thought. *But I caught it.*

The realization was both validating and terrifying. Validating because it meant his expertise wasn't completely gone—he could still feel things the data couldn't capture. Terrifying because it meant the algorithm was fallible, and he'd been trusting it blindly for years.

*How many other properties have I missed?* he wondered. *How many other times has the algorithm been wrong, and I never noticed because I stopped looking?*


He went home that night feeling both hopeful and afraid.

Hopeful because he'd proven to himself that the knowledge wasn't completely gone—that with effort, he could reclaim some of what he'd lost. Afraid because he'd also seen how much he'd missed, how many decisions he'd made without understanding, how many clients he'd failed to protect.

*This is going to take time*, he thought. *This is going to take work.*

But for the first time in months, he was willing to do the work.


**END OF CHAPTER 9**
