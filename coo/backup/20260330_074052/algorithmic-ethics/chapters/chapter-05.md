# Chapter 5: The Presentation

The city council chamber was different from the last time Marie had been here.

Three weeks ago, it had been a room for a vote—a procedural step in a process that had already been decided. Today, it was a room for a fight. And Marie had brought an army.

The gardeners of Garden E sat in the front rows, dressed in their Sunday best. Mrs. Rossi wore a red dress and her best jewelry. Mr. Nguyen had brought photographs of his wife. The Haitian women had prepared traditional food to share after the meeting. They had come not just as gardeners, but as witnesses to what the AI had failed to see.

Behind them sat the supporters—neighbors, activists, students from Amara's sociology class, business owners who understood what the gardens meant to their streets. The room was full, and the energy was palpable.

Marie stood at the podium, her report in front of her, Patricia Okonkwo at her side.

"Thank you for the opportunity to present today," Marie began. "I know you've already seen the AI's analysis. What I'm here to show you is what the AI missed."

She clicked to the first slide. A photograph of Garden E, taken at sunrise. The light was golden, the plants were green, and in the foreground, Mrs. Rossi was bending over her tomato plants.

"This is Garden E. The AI sees 0.8 acres of underperforming land. I see thirty families who have built a community over three decades. I see traditions that have been passed down through generations. I see people who have nowhere else to go."

She clicked to the next slide. A chart showing the demographics of Garden E's gardeners.

"Eighty percent of our gardeners live alone. Seventy percent are over seventy years old. Sixty percent have no family in the city. For these people, Garden E isn't just a place to grow vegetables—it's their primary social connection, their reason to get up in the morning, their link to a world that has moved on without them."

The council members were watching now. Some were taking notes.

"The AI measures yield per square foot. But it doesn't measure the cost of isolation. Studies show that loneliness increases healthcare costs by an average of $6,700 per person per year. If we close Garden E and displace forty elderly residents, the city will spend over $250,000 annually in additional healthcare costs. That's more than the garden's entire operating budget."

A murmur ran through the room. Marie pressed on.

"The AI measures water efficiency. But it doesn't measure self-sufficiency. Garden E's irrigation system was built by the gardeners themselves, using salvaged materials and traditional techniques. The garden operates on a shoestring budget because the community maintains it. If we close it, we lose not just a garden, but a model of sustainable community management."

She clicked to the next slide. A photograph of Mr. Nguyen, holding a bundle of Vietnamese herbs.

"This is Mr. Nguyen. He's eighty-three years old. His wife is buried in Vietnam. He grows herbs here that can't be found anywhere else in New Orleans—plants his wife brought from their homeland, plants that have been in his family for generations. If Garden E closes, those plants die. That knowledge dies. That connection to his wife's memory dies."

Mr. Nguyen stood in the audience, holding the photograph Marie had shown. He didn't speak—his English wasn't strong enough, but his presence said everything.

"The AI measures what can be counted," Marie continued. "But some things can't be counted. You can't measure the value of a grandmother teaching her grandchildren to garden. You can't quantify the worth of a community that has built itself over decades. You can't calculate the cost of losing traditions that have been passed down through generations."

She paused, looking at each council member in turn.

"The AI recommends closing Garden E because it's inefficient. But efficiency isn't the only measure of value. Sometimes the most important things are the least efficient—the slow, patient work of building community, of preserving tradition, of caring for people who have nowhere else to go."

She turned to the audience. "Mrs. Rossi, would you like to say something?"

The old woman stood, her red dress bright against the gray of the council chamber. She walked to the podium slowly, deliberately, and took Marie's place.

"I've been gardening at Garden E for thirty years," she said, her voice clear despite her age. "My husband built that garden. My children learned to walk there. My grandchildren have played in those paths. That garden is my home. It's my family. It's my life."

She looked at the council members, one by one.

"You can close the garden. You can pave it over. You can build something more efficient there. But you can't replace what we've built. You can't measure it. You can't optimize it. You can only destroy it."

She stepped back, and the room erupted in applause.

---

The council deliberated for three hours.

Marie waited in the hallway with the gardeners, drinking bad coffee and trying not to pace. Patricia had gone back to her office, but she'd texted Marie: "Good presentation. The data was strong. Fingers crossed."

When the council finally returned, the chamber fell silent.

"We've reviewed the AI's analysis and the community's presentation," the council president said. "We've considered the efficiency metrics and the human impact data. This is not a simple decision."

Marie's heart pounded.

"However, we believe that the purpose of the community garden program is not just to produce food, but to build community. The AI system was designed to optimize for yield, but yield is not the only metric that matters."

The council president looked at Marie.

"We are voting to reject the AI's recommendation. Garden E will remain open. We are also directing UrbanTech to revise the optimization parameters to include community value, cultural significance, and human impact. The AI will learn to see what it missed."

The room exploded. Gardeners were crying, hugging, cheering. Mrs. Rossi was being embraced by the Haitian women. Mr. Nguyen was bowing to the council members, his hands pressed together in gratitude.

Marie stood at the back of the room, watching it all, and felt something she hadn't felt in weeks.

Hope.

The AI had been wrong. The numbers had been incomplete. But the people had spoken, and the people had won.

---

That night, Marie walked through Garden E in the darkness.

The garden was quiet, but it felt different now—not threatened, but protected. The plants were still growing. The traditions were still alive. The community was still here.

She found Mrs. Rossi on her usual bench, watching the stars.

"We did it," Marie said, sitting beside her.

"We did," Mrs. Rossi agreed. "But this isn't over, is it?"

Marie shook her head. "The AI will be back. It will keep optimizing. It will keep finding inefficiencies. We'll have to keep fighting."

"Then we'll keep fighting." Mrs. Rossi took Marie's hand. "That's what gardens teach us, you know. You don't plant once and walk away. You tend. You water. You weed. You protect. Every day, every season, every year."

"That sounds exhausting."

"It is." Mrs. Rossi smiled. "But it's also beautiful. Because that's what love is. Not a feeling, but a practice. Not a moment, but a lifetime."

Marie looked at the garden, at the evidence of decades of that practice—the plants, the paths, the benches, the community. She thought about her mother, who had started this work. She thought about the gardeners, who had built this place. She thought about the AI, which would keep trying to optimize what couldn't be measured.

"We'll tend it," she said. "Together."

"Together," Mrs. Rossi agreed.

They sat in silence, watching the stars, surrounded by the garden they had saved. Tomorrow would bring new challenges, new optimizations, new fights. But tonight, they rested.

The garden was safe. The community was strong. And Marie Fontenot had learned that some things were worth fighting for, no matter what the numbers said.
