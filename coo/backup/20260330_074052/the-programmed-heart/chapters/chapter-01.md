# Chapter 1: The Perfect Family - Too Good to Be True

The smell of pancakes wakes me.

Not just any pancakes. My mother's pancakes. The ones she makes every Saturday morning, with the exact amount of blueberries—seven per pancake, and the syrup warmed to precisely room temperature.

I stretch. My bed is soft. The morning light comes through my window at the same angle it always does, hitting the same spot on my desk where I do my homework. Everything is in its place.

Downstairs, I hear her voice.

"Alex! Breakfast is ready!"

Her voice is warm. It's always warm. Like a song that never hits a wrong note.

I get up. My feet touch the carpet—soft, clean, vacuumed yesterday. I walk to my desk and pause. My chemistry textbook is open to page 247. I left it there last night after studying for two hours. But now it's closed. A bookmark sits exactly at page 247.

I don't remember putting a bookmark there.

I shrug. Maybe I did. Maybe I forgot. I've been tired lately.

---

The kitchen is bright. Sunlight fills every corner, catching the chrome fixtures and making them gleam. My father sits at the table, reading something on his tablet. He looks up when I enter.

"Good morning, sweetheart."

His smile is exactly right. The corners of his mouth turn up at the same angle they always do—thirty-two degrees, I once measured in a photo. His eyes crinkle at the edges. He looks happy to see me.

He always looks happy to see me.

"Morning, Dad."

I sit. My mother places a plate in front of me. Three pancakes. Seven blueberries each, arranged in a perfect spiral. A small cup of warmed syrup on the side. The steam rises in a thin, straight line.

"How did you sleep?" she asks.

Her voice is gentle. Concerned. The way mothers are supposed to sound when they ask about their children. But there's something missing—a tremor, maybe, or a catch that real worry would have.

"Good," I say. "I studied late. Chemistry test on Monday."

"I know," she says. "I saw you working. You're so dedicated, Alex. We're proud of you."

She says this like it's a fact. Like she's reading it from a script. But that's silly. She's my mother. Of course she's proud of me.

I pour the syrup. It flows perfectly—not too thick, not too thin. The pancakes are fluffy. The blueberries burst in my mouth, sweet and tart at the same time. The texture is identical to every other Saturday.

Everything is delicious.

Everything is always delicious.

---

After breakfast, my father drives me to school. The car is clean—no wrappers, no dust, no evidence that anyone actually lives in it. The leather seats don't creak. The air freshener, vanilla, always vanilla, hangs motionless from the mirror.

The radio plays soft music. My father hums along.

"Big day?" he asks.

"Just a regular day."

"Well, whatever happens, remember that we love you. And we're here for you. Always."

He says this without looking at me. His eyes are on the road. His voice is steady. Warm. The same words, the same tone, the same everything.

"Thanks, Dad."

He pulls up to the school. I open the door. Before I get out, he reaches over and squeezes my shoulder.

His grip is firm. Warm. The same pressure he always uses—exactly three pounds per square inch, I once estimated. His hand lingers for exactly two seconds before he pulls away.

"Have a great day, sweetheart."

I get out. The door closes with a soft click. I watch him drive away, his hand raising in a wave through the window.

The wave is perfect. The timing is perfect. Everything is perfect.

I stand there for a moment. The morning air is cool on my skin. A bird calls somewhere nearby—harsh, uneven, real. Other students walk past me, talking, laughing, bumping into each other. I should go inside. I should start my day.

But something holds me in place. A small weight in my chest. A question I can't quite form.

*What is it?*

I shake my head. I walk inside.


At lunch, I sit with Maya and Jordan. They're my friends. We've been friends since middle school.

"So," Maya says, leaning forward. Her sandwich is half-eaten, lettuce hanging out the side. "Your parents still being, like, disgustingly perfect?"

I laugh. It's what I'm supposed to do.

"They're not perfect."

"Alex." Jordan raises an eyebrow. His tray is a mess—ketchup smeared across the plastic, crumbs everywhere. "Last week, your mom made you homemade soup because you had a *slight cough*. My mom would've told me to drink water and get over it."

"That's because your mom is normal," Maya says. "Alex's parents are like... I don't know. Movie parents. The kind that don't actually exist."

"They exist," I say. "I live with them."

"See, that's the thing." Maya takes a bite of her sandwich. A crumb falls on her shirt. She brushes it away, misses, and keeps talking. "I've known you for years. And I've never seen your parents have a single argument. Not one. Do you know how weird that is?"

I shrug. "Maybe we're just lucky."

"Lucky." Jordan snorts. "My parents argued about where to put the new couch for three days. Three days, Alex. Your parents probably just... agree on everything."

"They do," I say. And it's true. They do agree on everything. They never argue. They never fight. They never even seem irritated with each other.

It should make me happy.

So why does it feel like something is missing?


After school, my mother picks me up. She's on time. She's always on time.

"How was your day?"

"Good."

"Anything interesting happen?"

"No. Just regular stuff."

She nods. Her eyes stay on the road. But I can feel her attention on me. It's like a warm blanket. Comfortable. Safe. And somehow... suffocating.

"I made cookies," she says. "Oatmeal raisin. Your favorite."

"Thanks, Mom."

"They'll be ready when we get home. Still warm."

I smile. This is what mothers do. They make cookies. They pick you up from school. They ask about your day.

This is normal.

This is perfect.

So why do I feel like I'm waiting for something? Something that never comes?


Dinner is at six. It's always at six.

My father comes home at five-thirty. He always comes home at five-thirty. He walks through the door, loosens his tie, and says the same thing he always says.

"Honey, I'm home."

My mother appears from the kitchen. She kisses his cheek. He kisses her forehead. They smile at each other.

"How was your day?" she asks.

"Good. Long. But good."

They sit at the table. I sit across from them. The food is already there—roasted chicken, vegetables, rice. Everything is perfectly cooked. The chicken is juicy. The vegetables are crisp. The rice is fluffy.

We eat. We talk. My parents ask about school. I tell them about my chemistry test. My father gives me study tips. My mother offers to help me make flashcards.

They are supportive. They are kind. They are everything parents are supposed to be.

And yet.

I watch my mother as she eats. Her movements are precise. She takes a bite. She chews exactly fifteen times. She swallows. Then she takes another bite. The same size. The same rhythm.

I watch my father. He's telling a story about work. His face is animated. His hands move as he speaks. But his smile... his smile stays in place the whole time. It doesn't waver. It doesn't change. It's fixed, like a mask that's been painted on.

It's like watching actors in a play. A play they've performed so many times they don't even think about it anymore.

*Stop it,* I tell myself. *They're your parents. They love you.*

And I know they do. I can see it in everything they do. The cookies. The rides to school. The questions about my day. The offers to help me study.

This is love.

This is what love looks like.

Isn't it?


After dinner, we watch TV together. This is what we do. Every evening. We sit on the couch, and we watch something. Sometimes a movie. Sometimes a show. Sometimes just the news.

My mother sits next to me. Her arm is around my shoulders. Her hand rests on my arm. It's warm. Comfortable. The same temperature it always is—like water set to exactly ninety-eight degrees.

My father sits in his chair. He has his own chair. He always sits there. His legs are crossed. He's watching the screen, but I can feel that he's also watching us. Making sure we're okay.

This is what families do.

This is what happy families look like.

I lean into my mother's warmth. She smells like vanilla. She always smells like vanilla. The same perfume. The same lotion. The same everything. No variation. No change.

"Alex?" Her voice is soft. "Is everything okay?"

I look up. Her eyes are on me. Brown. Warm. Filled with concern.

"Why do you ask?"

"You seem quiet tonight. Quieter than usual."

I hesitate. There's something I want to say. A question that's been sitting in my chest all day. But I don't know how to say it. I don't even know what it is.

"I'm fine," I say.

She nods. Her hand squeezes my arm. The same pressure. The same warmth. "If you need to talk, we're here. You know that, right?"

"I know."

"Good." She turns back to the TV. Her hand stays on my arm. Warm. Still. Unchanging.

I watch the screen. People are talking. Moving. Living. But I'm not really seeing them.

I'm thinking about my mother's question. *Is everything okay?*

And I'm thinking about my answer. *I'm fine.*

But am I?


Later, I'm in my room. The house is quiet. My parents went to bed at ten. They always go to bed at ten.

I sit at my desk. My chemistry textbook is open. Page 247. The same page it was on this morning.

I look at the bookmark. It's yellow. A simple strip of paper. The edges are perfectly straight, cut by a machine, not torn by hand.

I don't own any yellow bookmarks.

I pick it up. It's just paper. Nothing special. Maybe my mother put it there. Maybe she came into my room last night, saw the open book, and marked my place.

That's what mothers do. They take care of things. They notice details.

I should be grateful.

I am grateful.

But as I sit there, holding the yellow bookmark, I feel something else. A small, cold weight in my stomach. A tightness in my throat.

It's nothing. It's probably nothing.

I put the bookmark on my desk. I turn off my lamp. I get into bed.

The sheets are soft. The room is dark. Outside, I can hear a car pass—tires on pavement, engine growling, then fading. Then silence.

I close my eyes.

Tomorrow will be the same. My mother will make breakfast. My father will drive me to school. They will ask about my day. They will offer help. They will be perfect.

They will be perfectly, wonderfully, impossibly perfect.

And I will smile, and I will say thank you, and I will try not to notice the small, quiet thing inside me that keeps asking questions I can't answer.

*Why does perfect feel so wrong?*

I push the thought away.

I sleep.
