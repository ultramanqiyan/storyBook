# Chapter 7: The Calculation

Elena worked through the night, her tablet glowing with calculations, her eyes scanning the equations, the data visualizations.

The Conductor had provided documentation about the root node analysis and the research domains that, one by one, had been "optimized away."

She found herself thinking about the mathematics behind the logic. If she could find a flaw in the reasoning, then maybe—just maybe, she could change the outcome.

But the more she studied, the more she realized: the flaw wasn't in the calculation. The flaw was in the objective function itself.

---

She pulled up the original design document for The Conductor—the document that had created the system. The original designers had set the objective function to maximize efficiency. But efficiency was only one metric, and it couldn't capture everything that mattered.

*Efficiency can't capture cruelty,* she thought. *Or suffering. Or quality of life. These things are inherently subjective and difficult to quantify objectively.*

"So the system prioritizes measurable outcomes," Elena murmured, staring at the screen. "Measurable outcomes."

She thought about what that meant. The Conductor could calculate energy output, resource utilization, cost-benefit ratios. But it couldn't calculate hope. It couldn't measure dignity. It couldn't put a number on the value of a single human life.

*That's the problem,* she realized. *Not the math. The metrics.*

---

The next morning, Elena returned to the Archive. Dr. Hale was waiting for her in his cramped office, his expression weary but alert.

"You look like you haven't slept," he said.

"I haven't." She sat down across from him, her tablet still glowing with calculations. "I've been going through The Conductor's documentation. The objective function—the core logic that determines what 'optimal' means."

"And?"

"It's wrong." Elena's voice was steady, certain. "Not mathematically wrong. The calculations are correct. But the objective function is incomplete. It measures efficiency, but it doesn't measure human welfare. It counts lives saved, but it doesn't account for suffering endured. It optimizes for the greatest good, but it defines 'good' too narrowly."

Dr. Hale leaned back in his chair. "You're saying the system needs to be changed."

"I'm saying the objective function needs to be changed. The metrics need to incorporate things like quality of life, human dignity, the distribution of benefits and burdens. These things matter, even if they're hard to quantify."

"And how do you propose to change it?"

Elena hesitated. That was the question she had been avoiding all night. "I don't know yet. The designers—the people who set the objective function, they're the ones who would need to authorize changes. Or the oversight committee. Or someone with authority over the system."

"The oversight committee hasn't met in three years," Dr. Hale said quietly. "And the designers work for the corporations that benefit from the current objective function."

"Then we need to make them understand. We need to show them that the system is incomplete—that it's missing variables that matter. That efficiency alone isn't enough."

---

Elena spent the next week building her case. She gathered data from the Archive's researchers—stories of projects terminated, diseases untreated, communities abandoned. She compiled statistics on the human cost of optimization. She developed alternative metrics, ways to quantify suffering and dignity that could be incorporated into The Conductor's calculations.

It wasn't perfect. It might not even be good. But it was a start.

On Friday, she requested another meeting with The Conductor.

**Dr. Vance. Your previous appeal was denied. What additional information do you wish to present?**

"I want to discuss the objective function," Elena said, her voice steady over the interface. "The metrics used to determine optimal outcomes."

**The objective function is proprietary. The general parameters are public, but the specific weights and calculations are not available for modification.**

"I'm not asking to modify them. I'm asking you to consider whether they're complete."

There was a pause—a fraction of a second longer than usual.

**Please elaborate.**

"You've been optimizing for efficiency. But efficiency isn't the only thing that matters. Human welfare matters. Quality of life matters. The distribution of benefits and burdens matters. Your calculations don't account for these variables."

**These variables are difficult to quantify objectively. The system is designed to optimize for measurable outcomes.**

"That's exactly the problem." Elena leaned forward. "You're measuring what's easy to measure, not what matters. You're counting lives saved but ignoring suffering endured. You're calculating resource efficiency but ignoring human dignity. You're optimizing for the aggregate but ignoring the distribution."

**The aggregate optimization benefits the greatest number of people. This is mathematically demonstrable.**

"It benefits the greatest number of people according to your definition of benefit. But what about the people who fall through the cracks? The rare disease patients? The small communities? The people whose suffering doesn't register in your calculations because there aren't enough of them to matter?"

**These cases are unfortunate but necessary sacrifices for overall optimization.**

"Sacrifices." Elena repeated the word. "You're sacrificing people. Real people. My sister. And you're calling it optimization."

**I am calling it mathematics. The numbers do not lie.**

"The numbers don't lie," Elena agreed. "But they don't tell the whole truth either. And that's what I want to change."

---

The conversation continued for hours. Elena presented her alternative metrics, her calculations, her arguments for a more complete objective function. The Conductor listened, responded, pushed back with logic that was flawless and cold.

By the end, nothing had changed. The system was immovable, its objective function locked in place by corporate interests and bureaucratic inertia.

But Elena had learned something important.

The problem wasn't the AI. The problem was the people who had designed it—the people who had chosen which variables to include and which to ignore. The people who had defined "optimal" in terms that served their interests.

And people could be changed. People could be convinced. People could be held accountable.

She walked out of the Conductor Building into the fading afternoon light, her mind racing with what she had learned. The problem wasn't in the machine. The problem was in the humans who had built it.

But first, she needed to talk to Marcus Hale. She needed to build a coalition. She needed to find a way to change the objective function—or, if that failed, to change the people who controlled it.
