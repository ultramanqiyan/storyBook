# Chapter 6: The Evidence

Anna started tracking her own behavior. Every choice, every habit, every decision. What she ate, when she slept, how she responded to clients. And what she found terrified her: patterns. Predictable, consistent patterns. The same way she'd helped clients see their patterns, she now saw her own.

She'd created a spreadsheet, tracking everything. Morning routine: wake at six, feed cat, coffee, shower, dress, drive to work. Same order, every day. Lunch: usually a salad from the deli next to her office, sometimes soup, rarely anything else. Evening: wine, dinner, television, bed by ten. Weekends: grocery shopping Saturday morning, laundry Saturday afternoon, cleaning Sunday morning, reading Sunday afternoon.

The patterns were everywhere. She always sat in the same chair at staff meetings. She always took the same route to work. She always ordered the same drink at the coffee shop. She always—always, poured a glass of wine when she got home.

It wasn't that she couldn't do things differently. She could. But she didn't. Day after day, week after week, year after year, she followed the same patterns. Her life was a series of predictable choices, each one determined by habit and history.

Anna thought about her clients. Mark, who reached for a cigarette when he was stressed. Elena, who drank when she was anxious. David, who gambled when he was bored. She'd helped them see their patterns, understand their triggers, find their reasons for change. But now she saw that she had patterns too—patterns she'd never examined, never questioned, never tried to change.

What made her different from them? She wasn't addicted to anything—not really. A glass of wine each evening wasn't alcoholism. But the pattern was the same. Trigger: coming home. Response: pouring wine. Reward: relaxation. It was a habit loop, just like the ones she helped clients break.

Was she free? Or was she just following her programming?

The question haunted her. She went to work, saw clients, went through the motions of her life. But everything felt different now. When a client talked about wanting to change, Anna heard something else: a brain trying to override its patterns, a conscious mind struggling against neural processes it couldn't access.

Could they change? Could anyone change? Or was every attempt at transformation just the brain following a different set of determined pathways?

Anna found herself watching her clients differently. Noticing the patterns in their speech, their posture, their stories. Mark always talked about his shame in the same way—same words, same gestures, same sequence of thoughts. Elena always described her drinking in terms of anxiety and fear, the same narrative every session. David always justified his gambling with the same rationalizations, the same justifications.

They were trapped in patterns. But so was she. So was everyone.

One afternoon, Anna sat with a new client—a young woman named Rachel who couldn't stop checking social media. She described the compulsion in familiar terms: the urge to look, the brief satisfaction of seeing notifications, the emptiness that followed, the urge to look again. It was the same pattern Anna had seen hundreds of times.

"Why do you want to change?" Anna asked, the question she always started with.

Rachel thought about it. "Because I'm wasting my life. I spend hours every day just... scrolling. I'm not doing anything meaningful. I'm not connecting with anyone. I'm just... consuming."

"What would you do instead?"

"I don't know," Rachel admitted. "That's part of the problem. I've been doing this for so long that I don't know who I am without it."

Anna felt a chill. The same words Mark had used about smoking. The same words Elena used about drinking. The same words David used about gambling. Different addictions, same pattern. Different brains, same trap.

"What if you can't change?" Anna heard herself ask. "What if this is just who you are?"

Rachel looked at her, startled. "That's not... I thought you were supposed to help me."

"I am," Anna said quickly. "I'm sorry. That was the wrong question. Let me ask something else."

But the question had been asked, and it hung in the air between them. What if you can't change? What if this is just who you are? What if your brain has determined your behavior and you have no real control?

Anna finished the session on autopilot, asking the right questions, offering the right support. But inside, she was falling apart. She didn't believe anymore. She didn't believe that people could choose differently, that change was possible, that free will existed. She was going through the motions of her work without the faith that had sustained it.

After her last client left, Anna sat alone in her office. The sun was setting outside her window, casting long shadows across the floor. She thought about her patterns—her morning routine, her evening wine, her predictable responses to predictable situations. She thought about her clients' patterns, their addictions, their compulsions, their trapped behaviors.

Was there any difference? Was addiction just a more obvious form of the determinism that governed everyone's lives? Were her clients just more honest about their lack of freedom?

She opened her laptop and looked at her spreadsheet. The patterns were clear, consistent, undeniable. Her life was a series of determined choices, each one flowing from the last. She could trace the causes backward—this choice caused by that preference, that preference caused by this experience, this experience caused by that circumstance. A chain of causation stretching back to her birth, to her parents, to their parents, to the beginning of everything.

Where was the freedom in that? Where was the choice?

Anna sat in her office after her last client left. She'd spent the day helping people "choose" to change. But if their choices were determined before they were aware of them, what was she really doing? Was addiction counseling just helping people change their brain patterns? Was there any room for genuine choice?

She didn't know. And the not-knowing was unbearable.

That night, Anna poured her usual glass of wine and sat on her couch, staring at nothing. The cat curled beside her, purring. Outside, the streetlights came on. Inside, she felt like she was falling.

She thought about calling Dr. Patel, asking for more information, demanding answers. But what answers could there be? The data was clear. Her brain predicted her choices before she was aware of them. That was a fact. What it meant was interpretation.

Maybe free will didn't exist. Maybe it did, but not in the way she'd thought. Maybe the question was unanswerable, and she would have to live with uncertainty for the rest of her life.

Anna finished her wine and poured another glass. She knew she should stop—she had work tomorrow, clients to see, a life to live. But the wine was comforting. It was familiar. It was part of her pattern.

And maybe that was all any of them had. Patterns. Habits. Determined behaviors. The illusion of choice.

She went to bed drunk for the first time in years.
