# Chapter 06: The Weaning

The first week with reduced EVE regulation was like learning to breathe again after years of mechanical assistance. David walked through his life feeling everything—the good, the bad, the overwhelming. Each emotion was a discovery, a reminder of what he had lost and what he was reclaiming.

The mornings were hardest. Without EVE's optimized start-of-day contentment, David woke with a jumble of feelings—anxiety about the day ahead, lingering fatigue from poor sleep, vague unease about the path he had chosen. The emotions were uncomfortable, but they were his.

*This is what normal people feel,* he realized. *This chaos. This uncertainty. This constant fluctuation.*

He started keeping a journal, documenting his emotional experiences. Each day brought new discoveries:

*Day 1: Woke feeling anxious. Not the 40% anxiety that EVE would provide, but a raw, gnawing feeling in my chest. Uncomfortable, but real. Went to work, felt genuine interest in a project—not optimized interest, but actual curiosity. By evening, exhausted from the intensity of feeling.*

*Day 3: Had a disagreement with a colleague. Felt anger—real anger, hot and sharp. EVE would have provided 45% frustration, appropriate and contained. This was different. This was mine. It scared me, but also... felt right. Like I was actually alive.*

*Day 5: Walked through the park during lunch. Saw children playing, felt a pang of something—sadness? longing? joy?, that was too complex for EVE's categories. It was a mixture, a mess, a genuine emotional response to the beauty and transience of life. I stood there for twenty minutes, just feeling it.*

The intensity was exhausting. By the end of each day, David felt drained, overwhelmed by the constant fluctuation of authentic emotion. EVE's optimized responses had been smooth, consistent, sustainable. His own emotions were jagged, unpredictable, depleting.

But they were also meaningful in a way that optimized feelings had never been. When he felt joy, it was because something genuinely brought him joy—not because EVE had determined that joy was the appropriate response. When he felt sadness, it was because something genuinely saddened him, not because EVE had calculated the optimal level of grief.

*This is what I was missing,* he thought. *Not the happiness. The meaning. The connection between feeling and cause.*

He continued to see Dr. Sharma regularly, discussing his progress and challenges.

"The intensity is normal," Dr. Sharma explained. "You've been accustomed to regulated emotions—smooth, consistent, predictable. Authentic emotions are messier. They fluctuate more, last longer, feel stronger. Most people adapt over time."

"How long?"

"Months, usually. Sometimes years. The neural pathways that produce authentic emotional response need to be reactivated, strengthened. It's like physical therapy for the emotions—gradual, sometimes painful, but ultimately restorative."

"Will I ever feel... normal? Whatever that means?"

"You'll feel authentic. Whether that's normal or not depends on your definition. Most people experience a range of emotional intensities—some days are overwhelming, others are calm. The goal isn't to eliminate intensity, but to develop the capacity to experience it."

David nodded, absorbing the information. The path ahead was long, uncertain, potentially painful. But it was his path. And that made it worth walking.

The second week brought new challenges. David found himself experiencing emotions he hadn't felt in years—grief for his failed marriage, regret for the years of emptiness, anger at himself for the choices he had made. The feelings were intense, sometimes overwhelming, but he let them come.

*This is what I was avoiding,* he realized. *Not just the emptiness, but the pain. The grief. The regret. EVE gave me happiness, but it took away the full spectrum. And the full spectrum is what makes us human.*

He reached out to Rachel again, asking if they could meet. She agreed, cautiously, and they arranged to have coffee the following weekend.

The days leading up to the meeting were a rollercoaster of emotions—hope, fear, longing, anxiety. EVE would have smoothed these into appropriate, proportional responses. But David felt them in their full intensity, the jagged edges of authentic feeling.

When Saturday arrived, he stood outside the coffee shop, his heart racing—actually racing, with genuine nervousness. He had felt this before, with EVE's help. But this was different. This was his nervousness, his fear, his hope.

He walked inside and saw Rachel at a table by the window. She looked the same—beautiful, guarded, cautious. But David saw her differently now. Not just as the woman who had left him, but as the person who had seen through his emptiness when he couldn't see it himself.

"Hi," he said, sitting across from her.

"Hi." Her eyes searched his face, looking for something. "You look different."

"I feel different."

"How?"

David considered the question. How could he explain what had happened—the emptiness, the system, the borrowed joy, the trap, the slow reclamation of authentic feeling?

"I was numb," he said finally. "For years. I had everything, and I felt nothing. Then I got this system that gave me feelings—optimized, standardized feelings. I thought that was what I wanted. But it wasn't mine. It was borrowed."

"And now?"

"Now I'm learning to feel my own emotions. It's hard. It's overwhelming. But it's real." He met her eyes. "I understand now what you needed. What I couldn't give. I'm sorry."

Rachel was quiet for a long moment. When she spoke, her voice was softer. "I always knew there was someone in there. Someone who could feel. I just couldn't reach him."

"Neither could I. But I'm trying now."

They talked for hours—about their past, about what had happened, about what David was learning. Rachel shared her experience of being married to someone who couldn't feel, the loneliness, the frustration, the eventual decision to leave.

"It wasn't that I didn't love you," she said. "It was that I couldn't be with someone who wasn't there. You were present physically, but emotionally... it was like being alone."

"I know. I'm sorry. I didn't understand what I was missing. I thought success was enough. I thought achievement could fill the emptiness."

"Did it?"

"No. Nothing filled it. Not the money, not the status, not the accomplishments. I had everything, and I felt nothing." David paused. "Now I have less, and I feel everything. And that's better. That's what I was missing."

As they parted, Rachel touched his arm—a small gesture, but it sent a wave of feeling through David. Not the optimized warmth that EVE would have provided, but genuine connection, fragile and precious.

"I'm glad you're learning to feel," she said. "I hope you find what you're looking for."

"I'm finding it," David said. "One feeling at a time."

He walked home through the city, the emotions still washing over him—hope, sadness, gratitude, uncertainty. They were exhausting, overwhelming, sometimes painful. But they were his. And for the first time in years, he was grateful for all of them.
