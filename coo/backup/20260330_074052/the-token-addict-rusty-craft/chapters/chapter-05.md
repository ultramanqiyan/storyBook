# Chapter 5: The Interview - Exposed

The hiring request came from Jennifer.

"We're growing the team," she said. "I need you to interview candidates for a senior engineer position. Can you handle that?"

"Of course," Marcus replied. "I'll set up the interviews."

---

He'd interviewed candidates before. In the past, he'd ask technical questions, evaluate their problem-solving approach, assess their coding skills.

But that was before. Before the gap. Before the rust.

Now, the prospect of interviewing filled him with dread.

*What if they ask me questions I can't answer?* The fear was constant. *What if they realize I don't know what I'm talking about?*

---

He prepared by asking ThinkTank for interview questions.

"Generate technical interview questions for a senior software engineer position," he typed.

The AI provided a comprehensive list. System design questions. Algorithm challenges. Architecture scenarios.

He memorized the questions. Studied the expected answers. Prepared to evaluate candidates against AI-generated criteria.

---

The first interview was with a candidate named Rachel.

She had eight years of experience. Strong background in distributed systems. Impressive portfolio.

Marcus asked his prepared questions. She answered confidently, elaborating on her reasoning, showing deep technical understanding.

---

Then she asked a question of her own.

"I noticed your payment service architecture uses PostgreSQL," she said. "How did you handle the CAP theorem trade-offs? Did you consider eventual consistency?"

Marcus froze.

CAP theorem. He knew the term. He'd learned it years ago. But he couldn't remember the specifics. Couldn't explain the trade-offs. Couldn't discuss the decision.

---

"We... prioritized consistency," he said, grasping at fragments. "The business requirements demanded it."

"But what about partition tolerance?" Rachel pressed. "How does the system handle network partitions?"

The question hung in the air. Marcus's mind went blank.

---

"Let me... clarify the architecture," he said, stalling. "The system is designed for high availability within a single region."

"But single-region systems can still experience partitions," Rachel countered. "How do you handle that scenario?"

---

Marcus felt the shame burn.

He didn't know. He couldn't answer. The architecture had been generated by ThinkTank. He'd never understood it deeply. He'd just presented it.

"I think we're getting into implementation details," he said, deflecting. "Let's focus on your experience."

---

The interview continued, but the damage was done.

Rachel had seen the gap. Had watched him struggle with a fundamental concept. Had realized that the tech lead didn't understand his own architecture.

When the interview ended, Marcus sat in silence, the shame absolute.

---

Jennifer stopped by later that afternoon.

"How did it go?"

"She's... not the right fit," Marcus said, the lie tasting bitter. "Technical skills are strong, but not aligned with our needs."

Jennifer nodded. "Okay. We have more candidates tomorrow."

---

That night, Marcus stared at the ceiling, unable to sleep.

He'd failed. Not Rachel—himself. He couldn't evaluate technical candidates because he wasn't technical anymore.

*What am I doing?* The question was brutal. *How can I lead a technical team when I can't even interview engineers?*

---

The next interview, he was more careful.

He stuck to surface-level questions. Avoided technical depth. Evaluated candidates on cultural fit rather than technical skill.

It worked. The candidate passed. Marcus's secret remained safe.

But the shame grew. The gap widened. And the interviews continued, each one a reminder of what he'd lost.

*This is what I've become*, he thought. *A tech lead who can't evaluate engineers. A leader without technical credibility.*

The acceptance was bitter. But it was also real.
