# Chapter 5: The Distance

The morning passed like water through a pipe.

Mike signed his name forty-three times before noon. He knew this because the HomeMatch dashboard counted them for him, a small number in the corner of his screen, ticking up with each approval, each denial, each decision that bore his signature but not his thought. The claims moved through his queue in a blur of data points and probability scores, each one a person waiting for an answer, each one a house he would never walk through.

His coffee grew cold at his elbow, untouched.

*The Henderson file*, he thought, clicking open the next claim. *No, wait, that was last month.* He stared at the screen, trying to remember if he'd ever actually visited the property in question. The address was familiar. The data was complete. HomeMatch had already rendered its verdict: approve, 87% confidence.

He clicked approve.

The signature appeared at the bottom of the document, his name in his own handwriting, HomeMatch had learned to replicate it perfectly months ago. He didn't even need to sign manually anymore. The system did it for him.

*When did I stop signing my own name?*

The thought surfaced and sank, barely disturbing the surface of his mind. He clicked to the next claim.

---

The afternoon brought a client meeting, his first in-person showing in two weeks. Sarah Chen was looking for her first home, a young professional with a tech salary and specific requirements that HomeMatch had already processed into a neat list of properties.

Mike pulled up to the first house, a 1990s colonial in a neighborhood he used to know by heart. He stepped out of his car and walked up the driveway, his phone already open to the HomeMatch property summary.

*Four bedrooms. Three baths. Updated kitchen. Good school district. Price point: appropriate for client profile.*

He read the data as he walked, his eyes on the screen rather than the house. The front door was open by the time he looked up, the lockbox had been activated remotely, another feature he'd grown dependent on.

"Mr. Harrison?" Sarah Chen stood in the doorway, younger than he'd expected, her face bright with the particular hope of first-time buyers.

"Please, call me Mike." He extended his hand, feeling the familiar warmth of a client meeting. This, at least, was still real. "Ready to see your new home?"

She laughed nervously. "That's optimistic."

"HomeMatch is optimistic." He held up his phone, showing her the match score. "Ninety-two percent. That's the highest I've seen this month."

They walked through the house together, Mike reading from his screen while Sarah looked at the actual rooms. He pointed out features, the updated kitchen, the hardwood floors, the good school district, each one a data point he'd memorized from the summary rather than observed himself.

"The natural light is amazing," Sarah said, pausing in the living room. "Look how it comes through these windows in the afternoon."

Mike looked. The light was, indeed, beautiful, golden and warm, falling across the hardwood floors in long rectangles. He hadn't noticed. He'd been too busy checking the property specs.

"It's great," he said, and his voice sounded hollow to his own ears. "The listing mentions southern exposure."

Sarah turned to him, her expression shifting. "Have you been in this house before, Mike?"

The question landed like a stone in water. *Have I?* He searched his memory, trying to find the sensory details that would tell him yes or no. The slope of the floor. The smell of the basement. The sound the pipes made when you turned on the hot water.

Nothing came.

"I've been in many houses in this neighborhood," he said, which was true. "This particular one..." He trailed off, the lie catching in his throat.

Sarah's eyes narrowed slightly. "HomeMatch says you've sold three properties on this street in the past five years."

"It has a good memory." He tapped his phone, buying time. "Let me pull up the details."

*This is wrong*, he thought. *I should know this house. I should know every house in this neighborhood.*

But he didn't. Not anymore. The knowledge that had lived in his bones for twenty-five years had slowly, quietly, migrated to the cloud. He could access it, HomeMatch could tell him everything he needed to know, but he couldn't feel it anymore.

"The floor plan is open," he read from his screen, "which is great for entertaining. The previous owners renovated in 2019, so the systems are all up to date."

Sarah nodded slowly, but her posture had changed. The trust that had been there at the beginning was fraying, thread by thread.

"Mike," she said, and her voice was gentle but direct. "What do you think of this house? Not the data. You."

He opened his mouth to answer, but the words wouldn't come. What did he think? He'd been relying on HomeMatch's assessments for so long that he'd forgotten how to form his own. The house was... fine. The data said it was a good match. The light was nice. The floors were hardwood.

But what did he think?

"It's a solid choice," he said finally, and even he could hear the evasion in his voice. "The numbers are good. The neighborhood is stable. You'd be happy here."

"Would I?" Sarah's gaze was steady. "Or does the algorithm think I would?"

The silence stretched between them, filled with the golden light he hadn't noticed until she'd pointed it out.

---

He drove home in the fading afternoon, the houses of Austin passing in a blur of familiar streets he no longer recognized. The Millers' place with the oak tree. The Hendersons' old house. The Johnson property that had been on the market too long because of the foundation issues.

*Did I catch those foundation issues?* he wondered. *Or did HomeMatch?*

He couldn't remember.

His phone buzzed in the cupholder, a notification from HomeMatch, a new property match, a client inquiry, something that required his attention. He ignored it, watching the sunset paint the sky in shades of orange and pink.

Twenty-five years. Twenty-five years of walking into houses and knowing them before he saw them. Of feeling foundations and smelling water damage and hearing the stories in the walls. Of being the expert, the guide, the person clients trusted because he knew things they couldn't learn from an app.

And now?

Now he was just a signature. A name on a document. A warm body walking through houses while the real work happened on a screen.

*When did I become a rubber stamp?*

The question sat in his chest like a stone, heavy and cold. He didn't have an answer. He wasn't sure he wanted one.


Susan was in the kitchen when he got home, grading papers at the table. She looked up as he walked in, her face brightening and then settling into something more careful.

"Long day?"

"Client meeting." He dropped his keys on the counter, poured himself a glass of water, stood there drinking it without tasting. "First showing in two weeks."

"That's good, isn't it? Getting back out there?"

He shrugged. "The client asked me what I thought of the house. Not the data. Me."

Susan set down her pen. "And what did you say?"

"I said the numbers were good." He laughed, but there was no humor in it. "I couldn't tell her what I thought. I don't know what I think anymore. HomeMatch thinks for me."

The kitchen was quiet. Outside, the last light was fading, the sky turning from orange to purple to dark.

"Mike." Susan's voice was soft. "You used to love this job. You used to come home and tell me about every house, every client, every detail you'd noticed that no one else would see."

"I know." He set down the glass, stared at his reflection in the window. "I used to know things, Susan. Real things. Things I could feel in my feet and smell in the air. Now I just... read screens."

"Then stop."

He turned to look at her. "Stop what?"

"Stop reading screens. Start walking through houses again. Start noticing things." She stood, came to him, took his hands in hers. "You're Mike Harrison. You've sold more houses in this city than anyone I know. You didn't need an app to tell you which way the floor tilted or whether the foundation was settling. You felt it."

"I don't know if I can anymore." The admission came out barely above a whisper. "I don't know if I remember how."

"Then learn again." Her grip tightened on his hands. "You taught yourself once. You can teach yourself again."

He wanted to believe her. He wanted to believe that the knowledge was still there, buried somewhere beneath the data streams and probability scores, waiting to be excavated. But all he could feel was the hollow space where his expertise used to live, the empty place where twenty-five years of embodied knowing had been replaced by an algorithm's cold certainty.

"I'll try," he said, because it was what she needed to hear. Because it was what he needed to believe.

But even as he said it, his phone buzzed again, another notification, another match, another decision that needed his signature, and he felt his hand move toward it, automatic, helpless, like a man reaching for a drug he no longer had the will to refuse.


That night, after Susan had gone to bed, Mike sat at the kitchen table with his phone dark before him. The house was quiet. The neighborhood was quiet. His mind was quiet in a way that felt wrong, empty, like a room that had been cleared of all its furniture.

He closed his eyes and tried to remember the Henderson house.

Not the data. Not the HomeMatch summary. The actual house. The way the floorboards creaked in the hallway. The smell of the basement, must and detergent fighting for dominance. The angle of the light through the kitchen window in the morning. The slope of the driveway that always collected rainwater in the same spot.

Nothing came.

He tried another property. The Maple Street house. The Oak Creek listing. The colonial he'd shown Sarah Chen that afternoon.

Nothing.

The memories were there, he could pull them up on his phone, read the details, see the photos. But the feeling was gone. The embodied knowledge that had made him Mike Harrison, the sensory expertise that no algorithm could replicate, had faded so gradually he hadn't even noticed it leaving.

*This is what outsourcing feels like*, he thought. *Not a dramatic surrender. Just a slow, quiet emptying. A room growing darker so slowly you don't realize you've lost the light.*

He picked up his phone, his thumb finding the HomeMatch icon by muscle memory. The interface glowed in the dark kitchen, blue and clean and certain.

*One more match*, he told himself. *One more decision. Then I'll stop.*

But even as he thought it, he knew it was a lie. The same lie he'd been telling himself for months. The same lie that had brought him to this empty kitchen in this empty moment, a man who had once known houses in his bones now unable to remember a single room he'd walked through that day.

He clicked the notification.

The signature appeared on the screen, his name in his own handwriting, attached to a decision he would never remember making.


**END OF CHAPTER 5**
