# Chapter 3: The Record - New Dawn

Saturday morning. My parents leave for their weekly grocery run.

"We'll be back in an hour," my mother says. She's standing at the door, her purse over her shoulder. Her smile is perfect. "Do you need anything, sweetheart?"

"No," I say. My voice sounds normal. It doesn't shake. "I'm fine."

"Okay. Call us if you need anything."

The door closes with a soft click. The car starts. The engine fades into the distance.

I wait. One minute. Two. Three.

Then I move.

---

I start in the living room.

I don't know what I'm looking for. The card from my mother's nightstand said "MHA System." I don't know what that means. But I know it's important. I know it has something to do with the way my parents are.

I open drawers. I look through cabinets. I find nothing unusual—just the normal things you'd find in a living room. Magazines. Remote controls. Candles that smell like vanilla.

The same vanilla my mother always wears.

I move to the kitchen. The counters gleam under the overhead light. The sink is empty. The dishwasher is clean and unloaded. Everything in its place.

Then the bathroom. Then the hallway closet.

Nothing.

I go upstairs. To my parents' room.

The door is still open from last night. The room looks the same. The bed is made with hospital corners. The curtains are drawn. The nightstand where I found the card is undisturbed.

I check the closet. Clothes, neatly organized by color—white, then cream, then beige, then brown, then black. Shoes, lined up perfectly on a rack. No boxes. No hidden compartments.

I check under the bed. Nothing but dust bunnies in the corners.

I check the drawers. Socks. Underwear. A few old photographs.

I pick up one of the photographs. It's my parents, younger. My mother is holding me—I must be two or three years old. She's laughing. Actually laughing. Her head is thrown back. Her eyes are crinkled. Her mouth is open wide, showing teeth.

I've never seen her laugh like that.

I put the photograph back. My hands are trembling.

I keep searching.

---

In the back of the closet, behind a stack of old sweaters that smell like cedar, I find a box.

It's cardboard. Plain. No label.

I pull it out. It's heavier than I expected. The cardboard is thick, reinforced.

I open it.

Inside, there are files. Manila folders. Labeled with dates.

The oldest one is from twelve years ago.

I pull it out. I open it.

The first page is a letter. On letterhead from something called "Mental Health Associates."

**Dear Mr. and Mrs. Chen,**

**Thank you for choosing Mental Health Associates for your family's emotional wellness needs. We understand that parenting can be challenging, and we're here to help.**

**Based on your initial consultation, we've determined that you qualify for our Parental Care Enhancement Program. This program uses the latest in neural interface technology to help you provide consistent, loving care for your child.**

**Your first calibration session is scheduled for March 15th. Please review the attached documents for more information about the MHA System.**

**Sincerely,**
**Dr. Helen Marsh**
**Director, Family Services**

I read the letter three times. The words don't make sense.

*Neural interface technology? Parental Care Enhancement?*

I turn the page. The paper is crisp, clean, like it was printed yesterday.

The next document is a medical form. It has my parents' names at the top. Their signatures at the bottom. And in the middle, a list of symptoms.

**Patient History:**
- Sarah Chen: Postpartum depression (severe), difficulty bonding with child, emotional instability
- David Chen: Anxiety, work-related stress, difficulty managing household responsibilities

**Recommended Treatment:**
- MHA System implantation (dual)
- Emotional Pattern: Parental Care (Standard)
- Calibration frequency: Annual (recommended)

I stare at the words. My mother had postpartum depression. She had trouble bonding with me.

My father had anxiety. Stress.

They were struggling.

And then they found... this. This system.

I turn the page again.

The next document is a technical specification. It has diagrams. Charts. Words I don't understand—neural pathways, synaptic modulation, emotional pattern templates.

But one sentence stands out:

**The MHA System generates appropriate emotional responses based on situational analysis, allowing users to maintain consistent, loving relationships without the variability of natural emotional fluctuation.**

I read it again.

*Generates appropriate emotional responses.*

*Without the variability of natural emotional fluctuation.*

My hands are shaking so hard the paper rattles.

I turn to the next page. It's a status report. Dated last year.

**System Status: Active**
**Emotional Pattern: Parental Care (Standard)**
**User Satisfaction: High**
**Side Effects: Minimal**
**Notes: Users report improved family relationships. No awareness of system intervention.**

*No awareness of system intervention.*

My parents don't know.

They don't know that their love—their smiles, their patience, their perfect words—is generated by a machine.

They don't know that they're not really feeling anything.

The room spins. I grab the edge of the bed. I sit down. Hard. The mattress creaks beneath me.


I read more.

There are years of documents. Status reports. Calibration records. Notes from doctors in neat, clinical handwriting.

I learn that my parents started using the system when I was four. That they were desperate. That my mother couldn't stop crying. That my father couldn't handle the stress.

I learn that the system was supposed to be temporary. A bridge. Something to help them through a hard time.

But they never stopped using it.

The documents say that after a few years, they stopped coming to appointments. The system runs on autopilot now. It calibrates itself. It adjusts their emotional responses based on my behavior.

It learns what I need. It tells my parents how to give it to me.

Every smile. Every hug. Every "I love you."

All generated. All calculated. All fake.


I find a photograph in the back of the box.

It's my parents, before the system. My mother is holding me. Her eyes are red. Her face is tired. Dark circles under her eyes. She's not smiling.

But she's looking at me. And in her eyes, there's something raw. Something real.

Pain. Love. Fear. Hope.

All mixed together. All messy. All human.

I look at that photograph for a long time. The edges are worn, like someone held it often.

Then I look at the documents in my hands. The clinical words. The sterile descriptions.

*Emotional Pattern: Parental Care (Standard).*

*No awareness of system intervention.*

*User Satisfaction: High.*

My mother loved me. Once. Really loved me. With all the mess and pain and fear that real love brings.

And then she stopped.

Not because she wanted to. But because a machine took over. Because she was so desperate to be a good mother that she let something else do it for her.

And now she doesn't even know.


I hear a car.

The engine. Coming closer.

My parents are back.

I look at the documents scattered around me. The box. The files. The photograph.

I should put them back. I should hide the evidence. I should pretend everything is normal.

But I can't move.

I sit there, on the floor of my parents' room, surrounded by the proof that my entire life has been a lie.

The car stops. Doors open. Close.

Footsteps on the walkway. The sound is sharp in the morning air.

The front door opens.

"Alex? We're back!"

My mother's voice. Warm. Loving. Perfect.

Generated.

I close my eyes.

*What do I do?*

*What do I say?*

*How do I face them, knowing that everything they are is everything they're not?*

The footsteps come closer. Up the stairs. Each step is measured, even.

"Alex?"

My mother's voice, closer now.

I open my eyes. I look at the door.

I have seconds to decide.

Hide the truth. Or face it.

I stand up. My legs are shaking. I gather the documents. Stuff them back in the box. Push it into the closet.

The door opens.

My mother is there. Smiling. Perfect.

"Hey, sweetheart. What are you doing in here?"

I look at her face. The face I've known my whole life. The face that has always been kind. Patient. Loving.

The face of a stranger.

"I was looking for something," I say. My voice doesn't sound like mine.

"What were you looking for?" She tilts her head. The same tilt. The same angle.

I want to scream. I want to cry. I want to ask her if she remembers what it felt like to really love me.

But I don't.

"Nothing," I say. "I didn't find it."

She smiles. "Well, let me know if you need help. Your father and I are making lunch."

She turns to leave. Then she pauses.

"Is everything okay, Alex? You look... upset."

I look at her. I want to say: *How would you know? You can't feel anything.*

But I don't.

"I'm fine," I say.

She nods. She walks away.

I stand there, alone in the room.

The documents are hidden in the closet. The box is closed. The truth is buried.

I walk to my room. I close the door.

I sit on my bed. I don't move.

Outside, the sun is shining. Birds are singing. The world is going on like normal.

But my world has ended.

And I don't know what to do next.
