# Chapter 06: The Competition

The morning of the competition dawned bright and cold, the Swiss Alps visible through the windows of the venue. I stood in the athlete area, my hands chalked, my mind racing, my heart pounding in my chest.

The other competitors were easy to spot—their movements were precise, their expressions calm, their AI systems guiding every decision. They looked like I used to look: optimized, efficient, certain.

I looked different. My movements were less precise, my expression less calm. I had no AI system, no data, no guidance. Just me and the rock.

---

The first route was a speed climb—a vertical sprint up a 15-meter wall. The goal was simple: reach the top as fast as possible. The execution was anything but.

I stood at the base, studying the holds, trying to see the sequence. Without RISK-AI, I couldn't calculate the optimal path, couldn't analyze the grip strength required, couldn't predict the probability of success.

I had to figure it out myself.

The whistle blew. I launched myself at the wall, my hands finding holds, my feet placing themselves, my body moving through the sequence I had visualized. It wasn't perfect—my movements were less efficient than they would have been with AI guidance—but it was mine.

I reached the top in 8.2 seconds—not my best time, but good enough to advance.

---

The second route was technical—a complex problem that required precision and creativity. I stood at the base, studying the holds, trying to see the sequence.

The other competitors were already climbing, their AI systems guiding them through the optimal path. They moved with efficiency, their bodies flowing over the rock like water.

I moved differently. I paused at each hold, feeling the texture, judging the stability, making decisions in real time. It was slower, less efficient, but it was real.

I reached the top, my hands finding the final hold, my body settling into position. The crowd cheered, but I barely heard them. I was too focused on the feeling—the joy of actually knowing, of actually trusting, of actually climbing.


The third route was the crux—a steep overhang that required strength, technique, and judgment. I stood at the base, my heart racing, my mind working through the possibilities.

This was the kind of route that RISK-AI excelled at—the kind that required precise calculations, optimal sequences, perfect execution. Without the system, I would have to rely on intuition.

I started climbing, my hands finding holds, my body moving through the sequence. The overhang was brutal, my arms burning, my fingers aching. But I kept going, trusting my judgment, feeling the rock.

Halfway up, I made a mistake. My foot slipped, my body swung, my hands lost their grip. I fell, the rope catching me, the crowd gasping.

I hung there for a moment, my heart pounding, my mind racing. I had failed. I had fallen. I had embarrassed myself in front of the world.

But then I felt something else: relief. I had tried. I had trusted myself. I had climbed without the system.

And that was enough.


The final standings were posted that evening. I had placed seventh—not my best result, but not my worst either. The winner was a climber from Japan, his AI system guiding him to a perfect performance.

I watched him accept the trophy, his face calm and certain. He looked like I used to look: optimized, efficient, successful.

But I didn't envy him anymore. I had something he didn't: the ability to think for myself.


The after-party was crowded with athletes and sponsors and journalists. I moved through the room, accepting congratulations and answering questions.

"Alex, how does it feel to compete without AI assistance?"

"Challenging," I said honestly. "But also liberating."

"Will you go back to RISK-AI when it's repaired?"

The question hung in the air. I had been asking myself the same thing for months.

"I don't know," I admitted. "The system made me successful. But it also made me dependent. I'm still figuring out what I want."


I found Marcus in the corner of the room, his expression thoughtful.

"You did well," he said. "Not your best result, but your best performance."

"What do you mean?"

"I mean you climbed like yourself. Not like a machine, not like an optimized athlete. Like a person who trusts their own judgment."

I nodded, the words settling into my mind. He was right. I hadn't won the competition, but I had won something more important: the ability to climb without the system.


That night, I lay in my hotel bed, staring at the ceiling. The competition was over, my result was posted, my future was uncertain. But I felt something I hadn't felt in years: peace.

I had spent eight years optimizing for success, avoiding failure at all costs. But failure had found me anyway—on the Sentinel, in the hospital, in the months of recovery that followed. And somehow, through that failure, I had found something essential.

I had found myself.

The system could be repaired. RISK-AI could come back online. But I wasn't sure I wanted it to. Because the question that had haunted me for months had finally been answered:

Who was I without the system?

I was a climber. A person who trusted their own judgment. A human being who had learned to think again.

And that was enough.
