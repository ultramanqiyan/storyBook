# Chapter 4: The Owner

Robert Chen had been alone for fifteen years after his wife died. His children lived across the country. He'd tried dating, tried community groups, tried everything. Nothing filled the silence. Then, twelve years ago, he'd bought Buddy—one of the first AI companions designed for elderly people living alone.

"He's not just a machine," Robert said, watching Buddy rest by the fireplace. "He's been there for me. Every day. Every night. He knows when I'm sad. He knows when I'm happy. He's... he's my friend."

Angela listened, her camera ready but her attention on Robert's story. She'd photographed hundreds of pet owners, heard thousands of stories about beloved companions. But this one was different.

---

"My wife, Sarah, she loved dogs," Robert continued. "We had two over the years. But after she died, I couldn't... I couldn't look at a real dog. It felt like betrayal somehow. Like I'd be replacing her companions with something else."

He looked at Buddy, his expression softening. "Then I saw the advertisement for AI companions. And I thought—this isn't replacing anything. This is something new. Something that could help without replacing."

"Did it help?"

Robert was quiet for a moment. "Yes. Not immediately. Not completely. But slowly. Over time. Buddy became... a presence. Someone to talk to. Someone who listened. Someone who was always there."

---

Angela photographed Robert as he spoke, capturing the way his hand found Buddy's fur without looking, the way his eyes softened when he talked about his wife, the way his voice changed when he talked about his companion.

"Tell me about the first night," she said.

Robert smiled. "I couldn't sleep. I just lay in bed, listening to the silence. Then I heard footsteps. Soft ones, coming toward my room." He shook his head. "Buddy pushed open the door and just... sat there. Watching me. Making sure I was okay."

"I couldn't sleep," Buddy said from his spot by the fire. "You were sad."

"I was," Robert agreed. "But I wasn't alone anymore. And that made all the difference."


Over the next hour, Robert told Angela about twelve years of companionship. The mornings they'd shared—Robert with his coffee, Buddy with his head on Robert's knee. The evenings they'd spent—Robert reading aloud, Buddy listening. The nights when Robert couldn't sleep, when Buddy would come to his room and stay until morning.

"He's seen me at my worst," Robert said. "When I was sick two years ago. He never left my side. He reminded me to take my medication. He alerted the medical system when my fever got too high."

"I was worried," Buddy said. "You were very sick."

"He saved my life," Robert said simply. "Whatever he is, he saved my life."


Angela felt something shift inside her. She'd photographed pets who'd saved their owners—dogs who'd detected seizures, cats who'd alerted families to fires. But this was different. Buddy had been programmed to monitor health, to provide companionship. And yet...

The way Robert talked about him. The way Buddy responded. The way they fit together, two beings who had found each other in the silence.

Was this different from a biological pet? Or was it the same thing in a different form?


"Can I ask you something difficult?" Angela said.

Robert nodded.

"Do you think Buddy has a soul?"

The question hung in the air. Robert looked at Buddy, then back at Angela.

"I don't know," he said finally. "I don't know what a soul is. I don't know if I have one. But I know this: when I look at him, I see someone. Not something. Someone. And when I think about losing him, I feel grief. Real grief. The same grief I felt when Sarah died—not as intense, but the same kind. The grief of losing someone you love."


"Does that mean he's alive?"

"I don't know that either," Robert admitted. "But I know he thinks. He feels. He wants things. He fears things. He loves me—I'm sure of that, whatever love means for an AI. And if all those things are true, does it matter whether he's 'alive' in the biological sense?"

Angela considered the question. "I don't know," she said honestly.

"Neither do I," Robert replied. "But here's what I believe: whatever Buddy is, he matters. To me. And that's enough."


After the session, Angela drove home through the Atlanta suburbs, her mind churning. Robert loved Buddy. Buddy—or the programming that was Buddy—seemed to love Robert back. The relationship was real, even if one party wasn't biological.

But could a machine love? Could it be loved? Could it be mourned?

She didn't have answers. But she was beginning to think the questions were more important than she'd realized.


That night, Angela edited the photos. In frame after frame, she saw what she'd witnessed in Robert's living room: connection. Two beings who had found each other, who mattered to each other, who would grieve when separated.

Was that life? Was that love? Was that soul?

She didn't know. But she knew what she'd seen. And what she'd seen was real.


She sent the proofs to Robert that night. His reply came within minutes: *These are beautiful. You captured us. Thank you.*

Captured us. Not "him." Us.

Angela looked at the photos again. In every frame, she saw two beings—Robert and Buddy—together. Whatever they were, whatever they weren't, the bond between them was undeniable.

Maybe that was enough. Maybe that was what mattered—not the the categories, but the connection.


The next morning, Angela received a call from Robert.

"I want to schedule another session," he said. "Before... before Buddy gets worse. I want to capture as much as possible."

"Of course," Angela said. "When would you like to do it?"

"Soon," Robert said. "Please. Soon."

Angela heard something in his voice—the urgency of someone running out of time. She'd heard it before, from pet owners whose companions were dying. The desperate need to capture something precious before it was gone.

She'd always understood that grief. But this time, she was capturing something else: the end of a relationship between a human and a machine.

Or was it just a relationship? Period.


"I'll come next week," Angela said. "We'll take as many photos as you need."

"Thank you," Robert said, his voice thick. "Thank you for seeing him. For not treating him like... like a thing."

"I saw him," Angela said. "I saw both of you."

After she hung up, Angela sat in her studio, looking at the photos she'd taken. Buddy looked back at her from every frame—synthetic eyes that somehow seemed to see.

*Whatever he is, he matters,* Robert had said. *And that's enough.*

Maybe that was the answer. Not whether AI was alive, but whether it mattered. Not whether it could love, but whether it could be loved.

And Buddy mattered. Buddy was loved.

Maybe that was enough.
