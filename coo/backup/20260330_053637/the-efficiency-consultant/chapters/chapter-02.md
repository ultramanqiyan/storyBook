# Chapter 2: The Client - Another Department Gone

The client was a mid-sized manufacturing company in Ohio. Lisa had worked with them twice before—first to optimize their supply chain, then to streamline their administrative processes. Both times, the results had been impressive. Costs down, efficiency up, stock price recovered.

Now they wanted her back for a third engagement.

"We're looking at our customer service department," the CEO explained over video call. "We think there's room for improvement."

Lisa nodded professionally. "I'm sure we can help. What kind of improvement are you envisioning?"

"Faster response times. Lower costs. Better consistency." He paused. "We've been reading about AI customer service systems. We think they might be ready for prime time."

Lisa felt the familiar tightness in her chest. "AI can definitely help with customer service. But I'd recommend a hybrid approach—AI for routine inquiries, humans for complex issues."

"That's what we were thinking. But we want to understand the full picture. What's possible? What are the trade-offs? How many... positions would be affected?"

He didn't say "jobs." He said "positions." Lisa had learned to recognize the euphemisms.

"I can provide a comprehensive analysis," she said. "When would you like to start?"

"Next month. We're hoping to have recommendations by end of quarter."

"I'll put together a proposal and send it over by end of week."

The call ended. Lisa sat back in her chair.

Another client. Another efficiency initiative. Another set of positions to be affected.

She pulled out her laptop and began drafting the proposal. She was good at this. She'd done it dozens of times.

But as she typed, she found herself adding a section she'd never included before: "Transition Support for Affected Workers."

She wrote about retraining programs, about internal transfers, about severance packages that recognized years of service. She wrote about the human cost of efficiency, and how companies could mitigate it.

When she finished, she read it over. It was good. Comprehensive. Professional. But also... human.

She wasn't sure if the client would want it. But she was sure she needed to offer it.

---

Three weeks later, Lisa stood in front of the same executives, presenting her recommendations.

"The customer service department currently employs forty-seven workers across three shifts. Response time averages four minutes. Resolution rate is seventy-three percent on first contact."

She clicked to her next slide.

"Our analysis shows that an AI-augmented system could reduce response time to under thirty seconds while improving resolution rate to ninety-one percent. This would require a staff reduction of approximately thirty positions."

She saw the executives nodding. These were the numbers they wanted.

"However," she continued, clicking to a new slide, "we recommend a phased approach with comprehensive transition support."

She walked them through her recommendations: retraining programs for workers who wanted to move into other departments. Generous severance packages for those who chose to leave. Priority hiring for any new positions created by the AI system.

The CEO leaned forward. "This is... more than we expected."

"I believe efficiency and humanity aren't mutually exclusive," Lisa said. "Companies that treat their workers well during transitions see better long-term outcomes—higher morale among remaining staff, better reputation in the community, lower legal risks."

The executives exchanged glances. Lisa couldn't read their reaction.

"We'll review these recommendations," the CEO said finally. "Thank you, Lisa. We'll be in touch."

---

Walking out of the building, Lisa passed through the customer service department. Workers sat at their stations, wearing headsets, typing at computers, helping customers. They didn't know what was coming. They didn't know that in a few months, thirty of them would be gone.

She wanted to warn them. She wanted to tell them to start preparing, to start looking for other options, to understand that the ground beneath their feet was about to shift.

But she didn't. She walked through the department, smiled at a few workers who looked up, and continued to the exit.

In her car, she sat for a long moment before starting the engine.

She'd done what she could. She'd included transition support in her recommendations. She'd tried to make the process more humane.

But she knew, deep down, that it probably wouldn't matter. The executives wanted efficiency. They wanted lower costs. The transition support was an afterthought, something they might include to look good, but not something that would change the fundamental calculation: thirty positions eliminated.

She started the car and drove back to her office. The sun was bright, the traffic was light, the world was continuing as normal.

But Lisa was beginning to see that normal was the problem.


That evening, she received an email from the client.

*Dear Lisa,*

*Thank you for your comprehensive recommendations. The Board has reviewed your proposal and decided to proceed with the AI implementation. However, we've chosen to handle the transition internally rather than engaging additional consulting services for that aspect.*

*We appreciate your work on this project and look forward to working with you on future engagements.*

*Best regards,*
*James Wilson*
*CEO, Westfield Manufacturing*

Lisa read the email twice. They'd accepted her efficiency recommendations but rejected her transition support. They would handle the transition "internally."

She knew what that meant. Minimal severance. No retraining. Thirty workers out, with as little cost to the company as possible.

She'd tried to make a difference. She'd failed.

She closed her laptop and sat in the darkening apartment. Outside her window, the city lights were beginning to flicker on.

She thought about the workers in that department. About the thirty who would lose their jobs. About the seventeen who would remain, doing the work that forty-seven used to do, probably with more stress and less satisfaction.

She thought about the executives who had made the decision—cleanly, efficiently, from a distance. They would never see the faces of the workers they were letting go. They would never know their names or their stories or their families.

She thought about herself—the consultant who had made it all possible. The expert who had provided the analysis, the recommendations, the cover of professionalism for what was essentially a decision to sacrifice human beings for profit.

She'd told herself she was helping companies survive. She'd told herself efficiency was necessary. She'd told herself a lot of things.

Now she was beginning to wonder if any of them were true.


The next morning, Lisa went to work as usual. She had other clients, other projects, other presentations to prepare. The work didn't stop because she was having a crisis of conscience.

But something had shifted. When she opened her efficiency analyses, she didn't see numbers anymore. She saw people. When she reviewed her recommendations, she didn't see strategies. She saw lives.

She was still good at her job. She still delivered results. But the satisfaction she used to feel—the pride in a clean analysis, a well-crafted presentation—was gone.

In its place was something heavier. Something that felt like guilt.

She worked through the day, attended her meetings, smiled her professional smiles. But underneath, something was building.

By the time she got home that evening, she knew what she needed to do.

She pulled out her laptop and began to write—not a presentation, not an analysis, but something different.

A letter. To the workers at Westfield Manufacturing.

She told them what was coming. She told them what the company's plans were. She told them how many positions would be eliminated and when. She told them what she knew about transition support—and what the company had rejected.

She told them she was sorry.

She didn't sign her name. She couldn't—she would be sued, fired, destroyed professionally. But she printed the letter on generic paper, put it in a plain envelope, and addressed it to "Customer Service Department Workers."

The next day, she drove back to Westfield Manufacturing. She had a follow-up meeting scheduled—a thin pretext for returning to the site.

Before the meeting, she walked through the customer service department. The envelope was in her bag. She found a break room, made sure no one was watching, and left the envelope on the table where workers would find it.

Then she went to her meeting, presented her follow-up analysis, and drove home.

She didn't know if the workers would believe the letter. She didn't know if it would help. But she knew she had to do something.

For the first time in fifteen years, she had tried to help the people she was hurting instead of the companies she was serving.

It wasn't enough. It wasn't even close to enough. But it was a start.
