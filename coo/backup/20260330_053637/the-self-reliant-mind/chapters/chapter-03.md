# Chapter 3: The Independent Path

David hadn't planned to stop at the coffee shop.

He'd been walking back from a client meeting—another disaster, another moment of paralysis when a decision was needed—and somehow found himself standing in front of the familiar storefront. The rain had started, a cold Chicago drizzle that seeped through his coat, and the warm glow from inside looked like sanctuary.

He ordered a black coffee and found a corner table, pulling out his phone to check his email for the hundredth time that day. Nothing from Jennifer. Of course not. She was gone, and he was alone with his incompetence.

"Is this seat taken?"

David looked up. A woman stood beside the empty chair across from him, a laptop bag slung over her shoulder. She was maybe his age, early thirties, with an easy confidence that made him immediately aware of his own slumped posture.

"No, go ahead," he said.

She sat down and pulled out a tablet, settling in with the comfortable efficiency of someone who worked in coffee shops often. David watched her over the rim of his cup, noticing how she didn't immediately check her phone. How she seemed completely at ease in her own skin.

"You look like you're carrying the weight of the world," she said, not looking up from her tablet.

David blinked. "Excuse me?"

She met his eyes. "The hunched shoulders. The death grip on your phone. The thousand-yard stare." She smiled slightly. "I've been there."

David felt a flash of defensiveness. "I'm fine. Just... work stuff."

"Ah, work stuff." She set down her tablet. "Let me guess. You're waiting for someone to tell you you're doing okay. Checking your phone every five minutes for validation that you haven't screwed up."

The accuracy of her observation stung. "I don't know what you're talking about."

"Sure you do." Her tone was gentle but direct. "I used to do the same thing. Spent years waiting for someone else to tell me I was allowed to trust myself."

David shifted in his seat. "Who are you?"

"Rachel Kim. Independent consultant." She extended her hand. "And you are?"

"David Chen. I'm a consultant too. At Mercer."

"Mercer." She nodded. "Big firm. Lots of structure. Lots of people telling you what to do."

"It's not like that."

"Isn't it?" She tilted her head. "How many decisions have you made today without checking with someone first?"

The question landed like a punch. David opened his mouth to respond, then closed it. How many? He tried to remember. The client meeting—he'd deferred every question. The emails—he'd drafted responses but not sent them. The team questions—he'd said he'd "check on that."

"I make decisions," he said finally, but the words sounded hollow.

"You make recommendations," Rachel corrected. "Then you wait for someone to approve them. There's a difference."

David felt his face heat. "You don't know anything about me."

"You're right. I don't." She picked up her coffee. "But I know what I see. And I see someone who's built his entire professional identity on other people's approval. Someone who doesn't know who he is without someone telling him he's okay."

The words hung in the air between them. David wanted to argue, to defend himself, to prove her wrong. But he couldn't. Because she was right.

"Jennifer," he said quietly. "My manager. She's out indefinitely. And I'm... I'm falling apart without her."

Rachel nodded slowly. "That's the thing about building on other people. When they leave, you collapse."

"So what am I supposed to do?" The desperation in his voice surprised him. "I've always worked this way. Always had someone to check with. I don't know how to... to trust myself."

"You learn." Rachel leaned forward. "Self-reliance isn't about being alone. It's not about never asking for help or never seeking feedback. It's about being the final authority on your own choices. It's about knowing that your judgment matters—even when it's wrong."

"But what if I am wrong? What if I make a mistake?"

"Then you make a mistake." She shrugged. "And you learn from it. That's how you build confidence. Not by never being wrong, but by being wrong and surviving it."

David stared at her. The concept was so simple, yet so foreign. His entire career had been built on avoiding mistakes, on getting approval before acting, on never being exposed as incompetent.

"What if I can't do it?" he asked. "What if I try to trust myself and I fail?"

"Then you'll have learned something valuable." Rachel smiled. "But here's what I think: you've been failing your whole career by never really trying. At least this way, you'd be failing forward."

She stood up, gathering her things. "I'm in this coffee shop most Tuesday mornings. If you want to talk more."

"Wait." David stood too. "Why are you telling me this? You don't know me."

Rachel paused at the door. "Because someone told me once, when I needed to hear it. And it changed my life." She smiled. "Besides, independent consultants need to stick together. Even the ones who don't know they're independent yet."

Then she was gone, disappearing into the rainy Chicago street.

---

David walked back to the office slowly, Rachel's words echoing in his mind.

*Being the final authority on your own choices.*

He'd never thought of himself that way. He'd always seen his role as executing someone else's vision, following someone else's lead, getting someone else's approval. The idea that he could be the authority—on anything—felt foreign. Impossible.

But also... intriguing.

He thought about the Meridian client. The questions he'd deferred. The recommendations he'd been too afraid to make. What would have happened if he'd just... answered? If he'd trusted his own analysis and made the call?

Maybe he would have been wrong. Maybe the client would have pushed back. But at least he would have been standing on his own feet.

His phone buzzed. An email from Patricia at Meridian: "David, we need to reschedule. Can you do Thursday at 3 PM? We need concrete recommendations this time."

David stared at the message. Thursday. Three days away. Three days to figure out how to be the consultant he was supposed to be.

He started typing a response to Marcus, asking if he could review the recommendations. Then he stopped.

*You're waiting for someone to tell you you're allowed to trust yourself.*

He deleted the draft. Opened a new document. Started writing.

The recommendations came slowly at first. His instinct was to hedge, to qualify, to add disclaimers. But with each sentence, he felt something shift. A small voice inside him, quiet but persistent, saying: *You know this. You've done the work. Trust it.*

By the time he finished, he had a plan. Not a perfect plan. Not a guaranteed-to-succeed plan. But a plan that was his. A plan he could stand behind.

He saved the document and closed his laptop.

It wasn't much. It wasn't transformation. But for the first time in days, David felt something other than fear.

He felt possibility.
