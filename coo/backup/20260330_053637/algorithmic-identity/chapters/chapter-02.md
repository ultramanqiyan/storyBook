# Chapter 2: The Diagnosis - When Sickness Comes

The doctor's words came from far away. "Stage four. Pancreatic cancer. I'm sorry." Maria held Elena's hand, the way she'd held it through every crisis since they were born. But this crisis was different. This one had no solution.

The examination room was white and cold, the fluorescent lights casting harsh shadows. Dr. Williams sat across from them, her face professionally sympathetic. She'd been Elena's doctor for years, had seen her through minor illnesses and routine checkups. But this wasn't routine. This was the kind of news that divided time into before and after.

"How long?" Elena asked. Her voice was steady, but Maria could feel the tremor in her hand.

"Without treatment, perhaps three months. With treatment... perhaps six. Maybe a little more." Dr. Williams paused. "I'm sorry. I know this isn't what you were hoping to hear."

Maria couldn't speak. The words echoed in her mind—stage four, pancreatic cancer, three months—as if they were in a language she didn't understand. This couldn't be happening. Elena was healthy. Elena was strong. Elena was her other half, the person who had always been there, who would always be there. People like Elena didn't get terminal illnesses. They didn't die at forty.

"What are the options?" Elena asked. Still calm. Still practical. The one who always thought clearly in a crisis.

"Chemotherapy might extend your time. It won't cure the cancer, but it might give you more months. There are also clinical trials, experimental treatments. I can give you information about those." Dr. Williams handed Elena a packet of papers. "Take some time to think. Talk to your family. This is a lot to process."

Maria took the packet automatically. Her hands were shaking. She looked at Elena—really looked at her—and saw what she'd been trying not to see for months. The weight loss. The pallor. The fatigue that Elena had dismissed as stress. The signs had been there all along. Maria just hadn't wanted to see them.

They left the doctor's office in silence. The hallway was crowded with patients and nurses and the ordinary business of a hospital, but Maria felt like she was walking through a dream. Nothing seemed real. Nothing seemed to matter except the words that kept repeating in her mind: stage four, pancreatic cancer, three months.

Elena drove them home. Maria sat in the passenger seat, staring out the window at the streets of Boston, the city they'd lived in their whole lives. The same streets they'd walked as children, the same buildings they'd passed a thousand times. Everything looked the same, but everything was different now.

"Say something," Elena said quietly.

Maria turned to look at her sister. Elena's eyes were bright with unshed tears, but her jaw was set in that familiar expression of determination. She was being strong. She was being practical. She was being Elena.

"I don't know what to say," Maria admitted. "I don't know how to... I can't..."

"I know." Elena reached over and took Maria's hand. "I know."

They drove in silence for a while longer. The city gave way to their neighborhood—the narrow streets of the North End, the old brick buildings, the familiar storefronts. Elena parked in front of the bakery, but neither of them moved to get out.

"What do you want to do?" Maria asked finally.

"I want to keep working. I want to keep living. I want..." Elena's voice broke. "I want more time."

Maria squeezed her sister's hand. "We'll do whatever you want. Whatever you need. I'm here. I'll always be here."

"I know." Elena wiped her eyes. "That's what makes this so hard. I'm not just leaving my life. I'm leaving you."

The words hit Maria like a physical blow. She'd been thinking about Elena's death as an abstract horror, a terrible future she couldn't imagine. But Elena was right. She wasn't just losing her sister. She was losing the person who had been part of her since before she was born. The person who knew her better than anyone. The person who made her who she was.

"You're not leaving yet," Maria said fiercely. "You have months. Maybe more. We'll fight this. We'll try everything. We'll�?

"Maria." Elena's voice was gentle. "I've done the research. I know what pancreatic cancer means. I know the survival rates. I'm not going to spend my last months in hospitals, making myself sick with treatments that won't work. I want to spend them here. With you. At the bakery. Living my life."

Maria wanted to argue, to fight, to demand that Elena try everything. But she knew her sister. Elena had made her decision. And Maria would respect it, even if it broke her heart.

They sat in the car for a long time, holding hands, not speaking. The afternoon light faded into evening. The streetlights came on. The neighborhood settled into the quiet rhythm of night.

Finally, Elena said, "We should tell Marco."

Maria nodded. Their brother deserved to know. But the thought of calling him, of explaining what was happening, felt impossible. How could she put this into words? How could she make him understand?

"I'll call him tomorrow," Elena said. "Tonight, I just want to be normal. I want to pretend, for a few more hours, that everything is okay."

They got out of the car and walked to their separate homes. At the corner where they always parted, Elena turned to Maria.

"Thank you for coming with me today," she said.

"Where else would I be?"

Elena smiled, but her eyes were sad. "I love you, you know. I've never said it enough, but I love you. You're the best thing in my life."

"I love you too." Maria's voice cracked. "I don't know how to do this without you."

"You'll figure it out. You're stronger than you think."

"I don't want to be strong. I want you to be okay."

Elena hugged her, holding on tight. "I know. I want that too."

They stood there for a long moment, two sisters who had shared everything, facing the one thing they couldn't share. Then Elena pulled away, smiled one more time, and walked across the street to her apartment.

Maria watched her go, then went inside her own home. The house felt emptier than it ever had before. Every room held memories of Elena—childhood games, teenage secrets, adult conversations over wine. The walls seemed to echo with the ghost of their shared past.

That night in bed, Maria couldn't sleep. Across the street, she could see the light in Elena's window. Her twin was awake too. They'd shared everything for forty years—a womb, a childhood, a business, a life. And now Maria would have to learn to live without her other half.

She thought about all the things they'd never done. The trips they'd talked about taking. The recipes they'd meant to try. The conversations they'd postponed for another day. There was never enough time. There was never enough of anything, really. You just didn't realize it until it was almost gone.

Maria closed her eyes and tried to pray, the way her grandmother had taught her. But the words felt empty. What was the point of praying to a God who would take Elena away? What was the point of faith when it couldn't save the person you loved most in the world?

The light in Elena's window went out. Maria lay in the darkness, feeling more alone than she'd ever felt in her life. Tomorrow would bring new challenges—telling Marco, telling the staff at the bakery, beginning the slow process of saying goodbye. But tonight, she just let herself feel the grief. The terrible, overwhelming grief of knowing that her sister was dying.

She cried until she had no tears left. Then she lay in the silence, waiting for morning, waiting for the next day of a life that would never be the same.
