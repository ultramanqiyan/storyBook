# Chapter 3: The Test

The lab was nothing like Anna had imagined. She'd expected something clinical—white walls, cold equipment, the sterile atmosphere of a hospital. Instead, it looked almost cozy, with warm lighting and comfortable chairs. The researcher, Dr. Patel, explained what would happen. "We'll show you two images. You choose the one you prefer. Simple as that."

Anna sat in the preparation room, filling out last-minute paperwork while a research assistant explained the MRI process. The scanner was a large machine in the next room, and Anna could hear its hum through the wall. She'd never had an MRI before, and she felt a flutter of nervousness.

"The most important thing is to stay still," the assistant said. "Any movement can blur the images. We'll give you a cushion and some padding to help you stay comfortable."

"What if I need to move?"

"There's a panic button in your hand," the assistant said. "Squeeze it and we'll stop the scan immediately. But most people find it's not as bad as they expect. Some even fall asleep."

Anna smiled at that. She doubted she'd be falling asleep.

Dr. Patel appeared in the doorway. "Ready?"

"As I'll ever be."

The scanner was smaller than Anna had expected—or maybe it just seemed that way once she was inside. She lay on her back, staring up at the curved ceiling of the machine, a mirror positioned above her eyes reflecting a screen at the far end. The research assistant placed padding around her head to keep it still, then handed her a small button box with two buttons, one for each hand.

"Left button for the left image, right button for the right image," Dr. Patel's voice came through headphones. "Just choose whichever one you prefer. There are no wrong answers."

The scan began with a loud series of clicks and hums—sounds Anna had been warned about but still found startling. She focused on the screen reflected in the mirror, waiting for the first pair of images.

They appeared: two abstract patterns, swirls of color and shape. Anna looked at them for a moment, then pressed the left button. The patterns disappeared, replaced by a new pair. More swirls. She pressed right.

This continued for what felt like hours but was probably only minutes. Pairs of images appeared, and Anna chose between them. Sometimes they were abstract patterns. Sometimes they were photographs—landscapes, faces, objects. Sometimes they were words—positive versus negative, active versus passive. Always the same task: choose the one you prefer.

Anna found herself falling into a rhythm. Look, decide, press. Look, decide, press. The decisions felt random—she didn't have strong preferences between most of the images—but she made them anyway. Left. Right. Left. Left. Right.

The scanner hummed around her, and Anna's mind began to wander. She thought about her clients, about the decisions they struggled with. They would have found this task absurdly easy—just choose, no consequences, no stakes. But their real decisions were nothing like this. Their decisions involved cravings and fears and years of accumulated habit. Their decisions were hard.

Was that the difference? Was free will something that only mattered when choices were difficult? When the brain had to struggle, to deliberate, to fight against itself? Maybe these simple choices weren't really choices at all—just automatic responses, like breathing or blinking. Maybe the real test of free will was in the hard decisions, the ones that required effort.

The scan paused, and Dr. Patel's voice came through the headphones. "You're doing great. Take a short break, then we'll continue."

The research assistant helped Anna out of the scanner for a few minutes of stretching and water. Anna's head felt slightly swimmy—probably from lying still for so long—but otherwise she was fine.

"How many more?" she asked.

"About an hour of scanning," Dr. Patel said. "We need a lot of data points to train the prediction algorithms. The more choices you make, the better our predictions."

"And you're really able to predict which button I'll press before I press it?"

"We'll see," Dr. Patel said. "The technology is getting better all the time. But every brain is different."

Anna climbed back into the scanner. The second half of the session was similar to the first—pairs of images, choices, button presses—but with a twist. Sometimes, before the images appeared, a small symbol would flash on the screen. Anna was told to ignore it, but she noticed it anyway. A circle. A triangle. A square. What did they mean?

She didn't ask. She just kept choosing. Left. Right. Left. Right.

By the end of the session, Anna had made hundreds of choices. Her fingers were tired from pressing buttons, her neck was stiff from lying still, and her mind was foggy from the repetitive task. But she'd done it. She'd completed the first part of the study.

"One more session next week," Dr. Patel said as Anna prepared to leave. "Same time, same place. Then we'll analyze your data and share the results."

"When will I know what you found?"

"Give us about a week after the second session," Dr. Patel said. "We'll contact you when the analysis is complete."

Anna drove home through the darkening evening, her mind still processing the experience. The test had been simple—too simple, almost. Just choose between images, over and over. What could that possibly reveal about free will?

But the researchers seemed confident. They believed they could predict her choices before she was aware of making them. And if they were right...

Anna shook her head. She was getting ahead of herself. She'd know the results soon enough. For now, she should focus on her work, her clients, her life. The study was just a study. It didn't change anything.

But even as she thought this, she knew it wasn't entirely true. The questions the study raised—the questions about choice and consciousness and free will—had already taken root in her mind. She couldn't un-ask them. She could only wait and see what the answers would be.

The test lasted two hours. Anna made hundreds of choices—left or right, this image or that, this word or that one. By the end, she was tired but curious. "When do I find out the results?" she asked. Dr. Patel smiled. "We'll have them analyzed by next week. I think you'll find them interesting."

The following week, Anna returned for her second session. This time, the scanning felt more familiar—she knew what to expect, knew how to position herself, knew the rhythm of the task. She made more choices, pressed more buttons, watched more images flash past.

And then it was over. The research assistant helped her out of the scanner for the last time. Dr. Patel shook her hand.

"Thank you for participating," she said. "Your data will be valuable."

"When will I hear from you?"

"Sometime next week. We'll email you with the results."

Anna walked out of the neuroscience building into the bright October afternoon. The campus was bustling with students, the leaves turning gold and red on the trees. It all looked the same as before—nothing had changed. But Anna felt different. She'd participated in something that might challenge everything she believed about choice and free will.

She didn't know what the results would show. But she knew, whatever they were, they would change how she thought about her work. About her clients. About herself.

And that was both exciting and terrifying.
