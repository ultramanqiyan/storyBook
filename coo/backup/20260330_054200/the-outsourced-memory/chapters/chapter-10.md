# Chapter 10: The Remembering

The morning of the Chen follow-up arrived like any other—gray light filtering through the curtains, the familiar sounds of the neighborhood waking up, Susan moving through her morning routine in the bathroom.

Mike lay in bed, staring at the ceiling, feeling the weight of what he was about to do.

*Today I find out if I can still do this*, he thought. *Today I find out if I'm still Mike Harrison.*

He'd been preparing for weeks. Walking through houses without the algorithm. Taking notes by hand. Trying to relearn the sensory expertise he'd outsourced to the data. Some days it felt like progress—small moments of recognition, flashes of embodied knowledge that surfaced from somewhere deep. Other days it felt like failure, the empty space where his expertise used to live echoing with the absence of what he'd lost.

But today was different. Today he would face David Chen again, walk through a property with the family he'd failed, and try to be the agent they'd trusted him to be.

*Can I do this?* he wondered. *Can I be who I used to be?*

He didn't have an answer. But he was about to find out.

---

The property was a colonial in a neighborhood he'd shown dozens of times over the years. Good schools. Stable property values. The kind of place that would have generated a high match score for the Chens if they'd trusted the algorithm again.

But they hadn't. They'd trusted him.

*Why?* Mike wondered. *After everything, why would they trust me?*

He'd asked David Chen that question when the young father had called to schedule the showing. The answer had been simple, devastating.

"Because you called us back," David had said. "Because you admitted the mistake. Because you didn't hide behind the algorithm."

*That's not expertise*, Mike thought. *That's just honesty.*

But maybe, in a world of algorithmic certainty, honesty was its own kind of expertise.

---

He arrived at the property early, before the Chens, before the algorithm's assessment could influence his perception. He stood in the driveway, trying to see the house with fresh eyes.

The exterior was clean, well-maintained—a 1990s colonial with updated windows and a new roof. The yard was neat, the landscaping professional. Everything looked right.

But Mike had learned that looking right and being right were different things.

He walked up the driveway, feeling the slope of the concrete beneath his feet. It was even, solid—no settling, no cracks. Good sign. He stepped onto the front porch, testing the boards for softness, for the give that would indicate rot or water damage. They were solid too.

*So far, so good*, he thought.

He opened the front door—the lockbox had been activated remotely, another feature he'd grown to resent—and stepped into the house.

The first thing he noticed was the light. It came through the windows in long rectangles, falling across hardwood floors that gleamed with recent refinishing. The living room was open, spacious, the kind of space that would look good in photographs and generate high match scores.

But Mike wasn't looking at the space. He was feeling it.

He walked through the living room, his feet reading the floor. The boards creaked in a familiar rhythm—not the random creaking of age, but the specific pattern that indicated solid construction. Good sign. He ran his hand along the walls, feeling for unevenness, for the texture that would indicate repairs or water damage. Smooth. Even. Good sign.

He moved through the kitchen, the dining room, the family room, trying to notice everything. The smell of fresh paint—too fresh, suggesting a recent update before sale. The sound of the HVAC system—quiet, efficient, recently serviced. The feel of the countertops—granite, modern, probably installed within the last five years.

*This house has been updated*, he thought. *Recently. And professionally.*

But updates could hide problems. He'd learned that the hard way.


He descended into the basement, the space where the Chen property had hidden its fatal flaw.

The basement was finished—drywall, carpeting, recessed lighting. The kind of space that would show well in photographs and add value to the listing. But Mike had learned to be suspicious of finished basements. They could hide things.

He knelt on the carpet, pressing his palm flat against the floor. The padding was thick, luxurious, but beneath it he could feel the solid hum of concrete. No softness. No dampness. Good sign.

He ran his hand along the walls, feeling for the texture that would indicate water damage or foundation repairs. The drywall was smooth, even, but he could feel the faint outline of seams—evidence of recent work.

*This basement was finished recently*, he thought. *Within the last year, maybe.*

He pulled out his phone and opened the property file, looking for the disclosure statement. *Basement finished 2023. No known water issues. Foundation inspected and cleared.*

*Foundation inspected*, he thought. *By whom?*

He made a note to find out.


He spent an hour in the house, walking through each room, trying to notice everything. The slope of the floors. The sound of the pipes. The smell of the spaces. The quality of the light.

By the time the Chens arrived, he'd formed an opinion—not a match score, not a probability assessment, but a genuine feeling about the property.

*This is a good house*, he thought. *Not perfect. But solid. The updates are recent and professional. The foundation feels right. The neighborhood is stable.*

*But I need to verify the foundation inspection.*


David Chen parked in the driveway at 10 AM, his wife Mei Ling in the passenger seat, their two young children in the back. The family looked tired, worn down by months of house hunting and the lingering stress of the failed purchase.

Mike met them at the car, feeling the weight of their trust.

"Mr. Harrison." David's voice was formal, guarded. "Thank you for meeting us."

"Please, call me Mike." He extended his hand, feeling the firmness of David's grip. "I know this has been a difficult process. I appreciate you giving me another chance."

David studied him for a moment. Then he nodded, something shifting in his expression. "Let's see the house."


They walked through the property together, Mike pointing out features he'd noticed during his pre-showing inspection.

"The floors are original hardwood," he said, leading them through the living room. "Recently refinished. You can see the quality in the grain—this is old-growth wood, the kind they don't use anymore."

Mei Ling ran her hand along the floor, feeling the smoothness. "It's beautiful."

"The kitchen was updated in 2021," Mike continued. "New appliances, granite countertops, custom cabinets. The work is professional—you can see it in the details." He opened a cabinet, showing them the dovetail joints. "This is quality construction."

David was watching him, his expression thoughtful. "You seem different, Mike. More... present."

The observation landed like a gift. "I'm trying to be," Mike said. "I'm trying to remember how to pay attention."


They reached the basement, and Mike felt David tense beside him.

"I know this is hard," Mike said quietly. "After what happened with the last property."

David nodded, his jaw tight. "We almost lost our savings. The foundation repairs would have cost more than we could afford."

"I know. And I'm sorry." Mike met his eyes, holding the gaze. "I should have caught it. I should have been paying attention."

"Why weren't you?" Mei Ling asked, her voice soft but direct.

The question sat in the space between them, demanding an answer. Mike took a breath.

"Because I stopped trusting myself," he said. "I started relying on the algorithm—the data, the match scores, the probability assessments. I thought it was making me better. But really, it was just making me... absent. I stopped noticing things. I stopped feeling houses. I became a signature instead of an agent."

David and Mei Ling exchanged a look. Then David said, "That's honest."

"It's the truth." Mike gestured around the basement. "This basement was finished last year. The disclosure says no water issues, foundation inspected and cleared. But I want to verify that inspection before we proceed. I want to make sure this house is what it appears to be."

"That's... different," Mei Ling said. "From how you were before."

"I'm trying to be different." Mike met her eyes. "I'm trying to be the agent you deserved the first time."


They finished the walkthrough, and Mike gathered his observations.

"The house is solid," he said. "The updates are professional, the neighborhood is stable, the price is fair. But I want to do two things before we proceed. First, I want to verify the foundation inspection—find out who did it, when, and what they found. Second, I want you to get an independent inspection, someone you choose, someone who isn't connected to the seller or the agency."

David frowned. "That will cost extra."

"Yes. But it's worth it." Mike held his gaze. "I'd rather you spend a few hundred dollars on an inspection than thousands on repairs you didn't expect."

The silence stretched between them. Then David nodded slowly.

"Okay," he said. "Let's do it your way."


The independent inspection happened three days later.

Mike stood beside David as the inspector—a woman named Patricia Chen, no relation—walked through the house with her tools and her expertise. She tested the foundation, checked the plumbing, examined the electrical system, assessed the roof.

Mike watched her work, trying to learn, trying to remember what he'd forgotten.

*This is what expertise looks like*, he thought. *Not data. Not algorithms. Just a person paying attention.*


The inspection came back clean.

"Foundation is solid," Patricia said, her voice matter-of-fact. "No water issues. The updates were done professionally. This is a good house."

Mike felt the relief wash through him—partly for the Chens, partly for himself. He'd been right. His senses had been right. The knowledge he'd thought was gone had been there, buried under years of disuse, waiting to be excavated.

*Maybe I can do this*, he thought. *Maybe I can be who I used to be.*


The closing happened a month later.

Mike stood beside David and Mei Ling as they signed the papers, their children playing in the corner of the lawyer's office. The process was familiar, routine—the same documents he'd signed hundreds of times before.

But this time felt different.

This time, he'd earned the signature.


After the closing, David pulled him aside.

"Mike." His voice was quiet, serious. "Thank you. For doing it right this time."

Mike nodded, feeling the weight of the words. "I should have done it right the first time."

"Maybe. But you did it right this time." David extended his hand. "That matters."

Mike took the hand, feeling the grip, the connection, the human contact that no algorithm could replicate.

"Thank you for trusting me," he said. "I know you didn't have to."

David smiled slightly. "You were honest. That's why I trusted you."

*Honest*, Mike thought. *Not expert. Not accurate. Just honest.*

Maybe that was enough. Maybe that was what he'd been missing all along—not the data, not the efficiency, but the simple willingness to be present, to pay attention, to tell the truth.


He drove home in the fading afternoon light, the houses of Austin passing in a blur of familiar streets he was learning to see again.

The Millers' place with the oak tree. The Hendersons' old house. The Johnson property that had been on the market too long because of the foundation issues.

*Foundation issues*, he thought. *I caught those. I remembered how to feel them.*

The knowledge was coming back. Slowly, imperfectly, with gaps and uncertainties that the algorithm had never had. But it was coming back. And with it, something else—a sense of self that he'd lost somewhere along the way.

*This is who I am*, he thought. *Not the signature. Not the algorithm. This. The person who notices things. The person who feels houses. The person who makes mistakes and learns from them.*


Susan was in the garden when he got home, kneeling in the dirt, her hands covered with soil. She looked up as he walked toward her, and something in his face must have shown, because she smiled.

"Good day?"

He knelt beside her, feeling the earth under his knees, the sun on his back, the simple physical presence of being in his own body.

"I helped a family buy a house today," he said. "A good house. A solid house. And I did it without the algorithm telling me what to think."

Susan's smile widened. "How did it feel?"

He considered the question, trying to find the right words.

"Terrifying," he said finally. "And right. Like remembering something I'd forgotten. Like coming home after being away for a long time."

Susan reached over, took his hand, squeezed it. "Welcome back, Mike."

He squeezed back, feeling the connection, the warmth, the simple human contact that no algorithm could replicate.

*This is what I've been missing*, he thought. *Not the data. Not the efficiency. This. The feeling of being present. The knowledge that comes from paying attention. The self that I outsourced and then forgot how to find.*


That night, after Susan had gone to bed, Mike sat at the kitchen table with his laptop open.

The HomeMatch icon glowed on the screen, blue and certain, waiting for him to click it, to let the algorithm decide, to surrender his judgment to the data.

He stared at it for a long time.

Then he closed the laptop and went to bed.


The next morning, he arrived at the office early, before the algorithms woke up, before the data streams started flowing. He sat at his desk in the quiet dark, feeling the uncertainty of a future he couldn't predict.

*What now?* he wondered. *Do I keep using the algorithm? Do I throw it away? Do I find some middle ground?*

He didn't have an answer. But for the first time in months, he was asking the question.

And that, he thought, was a start.


He pulled out a legal pad and a pen—the old tools, the analog tools, the ones that required him to think rather than process. He wrote:

*What I've learned:*
*The algorithm is a tool, not a replacement.*
*Expertise comes from paying attention, not from data.*
*Mistakes are how we learn, not what we avoid.*
*Honesty matters more than accuracy.*
*The self is something you have to keep choosing.*

He stared at the words, feeling the weight of them.

*This is what I want to remember*, he thought. *This is what I don't want to outsource.*


The office began to fill around nine, colleagues arriving with coffee and conversation, the familiar rhythms of a workday. Mike stayed at his desk, watching the algorithm wake up, watching the data streams start flowing, watching the matches appear on his screen.

*Welcome back, Mike*, the interface said. *You have 8 new matches to review.*

He stared at the matches for a long moment. Then he closed the window, stood up, and walked out of the office.

He had houses to walk through.
