# Chapter 4: Fragments of the Past - Lyra's Story

The second spell came easier than the first. Elara felt the threads of time now, not just saw them, but felt them, like strings of an instrument she was learning to play. Each thread hummed with possibility. Each thread promised something.

But each thread also threatened to take something away.

She had lost count of the memories now. Small ones, mostly, the taste of a particular fruit, the sound of rain on a specific morning, the face of a merchant she'd known as a child. But each loss left a hollow space, and the hollow spaces were accumulating.

"Today," ORACLE said, "we look backward."

Elara looked up from the thread she'd been examining. "Backward? I thought the past was fixed."

"The past is fixed. But understanding the past can illuminate the present." ORACLE's light swirled. "Your sister left traces in the threads of time. Fragments of her journey. If you learn to read them, you may understand what led her to the Rift."

"Show me."

---

The memory came like a whisper.

Elara was standing in a study, not her study, but one she recognized. Books lined every wall, their spines cracked and faded. The smell of old paper and ink filled the air. And at a desk in the center, a woman with dark hair and fierce eyes was writing furiously.

*Lyra*, Elara thought. *This is Lyra's study.*

She watched her younger self enter the room, a child, really, no more than twelve. The younger Elara carried a tray of food, her face anxious.

"You haven't eaten in two days," the child said.

Lyra looked up, and Elara felt a pang of something, recognition? Loss? The face was so familiar, yet she couldn't quite remember the feeling of being in that room. The memory was already fading, becoming just images without emotion.

"I'm close to something," Lyra said, her voice intense. "Something important. The Seer Civilization didn't just disappear, Elara. They left something behind. Something that can see through time itself."

"ORACLE," Elara whispered, watching the memory unfold.

"ORACLE," Lyra confirmed, though she couldn't hear her sister's future voice. "I've found references to it in texts that shouldn't exist. It's real. And it's waiting."

The memory shifted, and suddenly Lyra was older, much older. The study was the same, but the woman at the desk had changed. Her face was thinner, her eyes more haunted. And her hands trembled as she wrote.

*The cost*, Elara thought. *She was already paying the cost.*

"I can't remember why I started," Lyra whispered to the empty room. "I can't remember..." She looked down at her hands. "What was my sister's name?"

The memory shattered, and Elara was back in ORACLE's chamber, her chest tight with grief she couldn't quite name.

"She forgot me," Elara said. "Even before she entered the Rift, she forgot me."

"She forgot many things," ORACLE said gently. "The cost of her research. But she never forgot that she was searching for something. Someone. She simply couldn't remember who."

"Show me more," Elara demanded.

ORACLE's light flickered. "Each fragment costs�?

"I don't care. Show me."

The next memory was darker.

Lyra stood in a chamber that looked familiar, ORACLE's chamber, but different somehow. The hourglass was there, but the light within it was dimmer, more cautious.

"You've found me," ORACLE said, its voice echoing through the stone. "What do you seek?"

"Knowledge," Lyra replied. Her voice was steady, but her hands trembled. "The Seer Civilization. The Time Rift. The thing that's trapped there."

"You seek dangerous knowledge."

"I seek truth." Lyra stepped closer to the hourglass. "I've already lost so much. My parents. My childhood. My..." She paused, confusion crossing her face. "There was someone. Someone important. But I can't..."

"Your sister," ORACLE said. "Her name is Elara."

"Elara." Lyra repeated the name like a prayer. "Yes. I remember now. I have to..." She shook her head. "No. I have to find the Rift first. Then I can go back. Then I can remember."

The memory shifted again, and Lyra was standing before a crack in the air, a tear in reality that pulsed with dark light. Her face was illuminated by it, her eyes wide with something that might have been wonder or terror.

"I can hear it," she whispered. "Something is calling from the other side."

*Don't go*, Elara wanted to scream. *Don't walk through.*

But she already knew how this story ended.

Lyra reached toward the crack, her fingers brushing the edge of the darkness. For a moment, she hesitated, turned back, as if she'd heard something, or remembered something.

"Elara," she said softly. "I'm sorry. I can't remember why, but I'm sorry."

And then she stepped through.

---

The memory faded, and Elara was left with a cold certainty: her sister hadn't fallen into the Rift. She had walked in willingly.

"She knew," Elara said. "She knew something was waiting for her."

"She heard the Hollow King's call," ORACLE confirmed. "He promised her answers. Promised to restore what she had lost. But his promises are poison."

"Why didn't you stop her?"

ORACLE's light dimmed. "I tried. But she was already beyond my reach, beyond anyone's reach. The cost of her research had taken too much. She couldn't remember why she should fear the darkness."

Elara sat on the cold stone floor, her mind racing. Lyra had walked into the Rift because she'd forgotten everything that mattered, forgotten her sister, forgotten her life, forgotten why she should be afraid. And now she was trapped there, holding the door closed, keeping the Hollow King imprisoned at the cost of her own freedom.

"How do I reach her without making the same mistake?"

"You must learn faster than she did. You must reach the Rift before you lose too much." ORACLE's voice was measured. "And you must find a way to share her burden without releasing what she holds at bay."

"Teach me," Elara said. "Whatever it costs."

The days that followed were a blur of learning and loss.

Elara learned to move through the threads of time, to see the patterns, to understand the architecture of reality. She learned to pull without breaking, to redirect without unraveling. And with each lesson, she lost another piece of herself.

She lost the memory of her parents' faces. She lost the sound of her own laugh. She lost the feeling of sunlight on her skin, the taste of fresh bread, the smell of rain.

But she held onto one thing: the knowledge that she had a sister. That somewhere, in a place beyond time, Lyra was waiting.

"Your progress is remarkable," ORACLE said one evening. "You learn faster than any student I have taught."

"Faster than Lyra?"

"...Yes."

Elara nodded, not allowing herself to feel pride. Pride was a luxury she couldn't afford. Every moment she spent learning was a moment Lyra spent alone in the dark.

"What's next?"

"The final lessons. The ones that will prepare you for the Rift itself." ORACLE's glow intensified. "But I must warn you, these lessons will cost more than any before. You will lose memories that define who you are. You may not remember why you're doing this by the time you're ready."

"I'll remember," Elara said, though she wasn't sure she believed it anymore.

"Will you? Your sister didn't."

Elara closed her eyes. She thought of Lyra's face, the face she could barely remember now, and felt something stir in the hollow space where her heart used to be.

"She didn't have me," Elara said. "She didn't have someone waiting for her. I do. And that's why I'll remember."

ORACLE was silent for a long moment. Then: "We begin tomorrow. Rest tonight. It may be the last peaceful sleep you have for a very long time."

Elara lay down in the corner ORACLE had warmed for her, but sleep didn't come. Instead, she lay in the darkness, trying to hold onto the fragments of herself that remained.

She thought of a garden she couldn't quite picture. Of a voice she couldn't quite hear. Of a hand she couldn't quite feel.

And she thought of Lyra, her sister, waiting in the dark, holding the door closed against an ancient evil.

*I'm coming*, she thought. *Whatever it costs, I'm coming.*

But even as she thought it, she wondered: when she finally reached Lyra, would she remember why she'd come? Would she remember her sister's name? Would she remember anything at all?

The questions followed her into dreams that weren't quite dreams, fragments of past and future, tangled together in the threads of time. And somewhere in those dreams, she heard a voice calling her name, soft and distant, like a bell struck in another world.

*Elara*, the voice said. *I'm waiting.*

And she knew, with a certainty that transcended memory, that she would find her way home.
