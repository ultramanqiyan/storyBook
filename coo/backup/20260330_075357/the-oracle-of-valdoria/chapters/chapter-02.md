# Chapter 2: The Oracle - First Contact

Elara didn't sleep. She couldn't. Every time she closed her eyes, she saw ORACLE's light, heard its layered voice saying her sister's name. *Lyra lives. She waits. She is trapped.* The words echoed through her mind like bells that wouldn't stop ringing.

She emerged from the ruins at dawn, blinking against the light she'd forgotten existed. The village was waking behind her, smoke rising, voices calling, the ordinary rhythm of ordinary lives. But Elara had left ordinary behind the moment she'd squeezed through that crack in the earth.

She needed to find Master Theron.

---

The old mage lived at the edge of the village, in a cottage that leaned precariously to one side like a drunk leaning on a friend. Books spilled from every window, and the smell of old paper and stranger things drifted through the cracked door.

Theron had been Lyra's teacher, once. Before the forbidden research. Before the disappearance. He had warned her, Elara knew, warned her about the Rift, about the cost of time magic, about the dangers of seeking knowledge that had been buried for good reason.

Lyra hadn't listened.

Elara knocked, and the door swung open on its own, revealing a room cluttered with artifacts, scrolls, and the detritus of a lifetime spent studying things that most people preferred to forget.

"Elara Vance." Theron's voice came from somewhere deep in the clutter. "I wondered when you would come."

She found him in a chair by the window, looking older than she remembered. His hands trembled slightly as he turned a page in an ancient book, and his eyes, when they met hers, were clouded with something that might have been fear.

"You know what I found," she said.

"I know what lies beneath the ruins." He closed the book carefully. "I know what your sister found there, five years ago. And I know what it cost her." His voice cracked on the last word. "I warned her, Elara. I told her the price."

"What price?" Elara stepped closer. "ORACLE said... it said time magic costs memories. Is that true?"

Theron's hands trembled harder. "It is true. Every working of significant time magic takes a piece of who you are. A memory, yes, but more than that. A piece of your self. Your identity." He looked at her with ancient, sorrowful eyes. "I have seen time mages who forgot their own names. Who forgot why they studied magic in the first place. Who forgot the faces of everyone they ever loved."

Elara's throat tightened. "Lyra�?

"Lyra was brilliant. Brilliant and stubborn and so certain that knowledge was worth any price." Theron shook his head slowly. "She learned quickly. Faster than any student I've ever seen. But each spell took something from her. By the time she found the Rift, she had already lost..." He trailed off.

"Lost what?"

"Her memories of you."

The words hit Elara like a physical blow. "She forgot me?"

"She forgot she had a sister. She forgot your childhood together, your parents, everything that connected her to the world above." Theron's voice was barely a whisper. "But she remembered something was missing. She remembered she had to find something, someone. She just couldn't remember who."

Elara sank into a chair, her legs suddenly unable to hold her. All those years, wondering why Lyra had never sent word, never tried to come back. She hadn't forgotten Elara on purpose. She had simply... forgotten.

"Can I save her?" Elara asked. "If I learn time magic, if I pay the price, can I bring her back?"

Theron was silent for a long moment. When he spoke, his voice was heavy with the weight of terrible knowledge.

"You can try. But you must understand, Lyra walked into the Rift willingly. She wasn't pulled in. She wasn't trapped by accident. She chose to go."

"Why?"

"Because something was calling her." Theron's eyes grew distant. "Something ancient. Something that has been trapped in the Rift since before the Seer Civilization fell. Something that has been waiting for someone to open the door."

---

Elara returned to the ruins at dusk, her mind churning with everything Theron had told her.

The Hollow King. That was what he called it, an ancient entity that had been imprisoned in the Rift by the Seer Civilization, trapped in the space between moments. It had been calling to Lyra, whispering promises through the cracks in time. And Lyra, already half-emptied by her use of time magic, had walked willingly into its prison.

"She didn't know," Elara whispered to herself as she descended into the darkness. "She couldn't remember why she shouldn't trust it."

ORACLE's chamber was just as she'd left it, the crystal hourglass turning slowly in its column of light, the air humming with possibility.

"You spoke with Master Theron," ORACLE said. It wasn't a question.

"He told me about the Hollow King."

The light within ORACLE swirled, and Elara thought she detected something like unease in its glow.

"He told you true. The Hollow King has been trapped in the Rift for a thousand years. He seeks freedom, and he will say anything, promise anything, to obtain it."

"Can I save Lyra without freeing him?"

"That is... a difficult question." ORACLE's voice was measured, careful. "Lyra is in the Rift. To reach her, you must open a door. To open a door is to risk releasing what lies beyond."

"Then how�?

"There may be a way. A path between the moments. A way to reach her without fully opening the door." ORACLE's glow brightened slightly. "But it will require great power. Great sacrifice. You will lose much of yourself before this is over, Elara Vance. Are you prepared for that?"

Elara thought of Lyra, her sister's laugh, her fierce intelligence, the way she'd always protected Elara when they were children. She thought of five years of empty rituals, of a memorial stone that marked a grave that wasn't real.

"I'm prepared," she said.

ORACLE was silent for a moment. Then: "Show me your hands."

Elara held them out, palms up. They were trembling.

"You are afraid," ORACLE observed.

"Yes."

"Good. Fear means you understand the stakes." The light within ORACLE pulsed. "We begin tonight. The first lesson is the hardest, not because the magic is difficult, but because the cost is immediate. Are you ready to lose something precious?"

Elara's hands clenched, then opened. "What will I lose?"

"That depends on what you value most." ORACLE's voice was soft. "The first working always takes something you love. Not your most precious memory, that comes later, but something that matters. Something that will show you the true weight of this path."

Elara closed her eyes. She thought of her parents, long dead. Of her childhood home. Of the village that had moved on without her. Of Lyra, always Lyra, at the center of everything.

"I'm ready," she said.

"Then look." ORACLE's light brightened, filling the chamber. "Look at the threads of time. See how they weave together, how they tangle and part and weave again. This is the fabric of reality, and you will learn to pull it."

Elara opened her eyes and saw.

The air was filled with light, not the light of ORACLE, but something else. Threads of gold and silver and deepest black, weaving through each other in patterns too complex to follow. Each thread hummed with its own note, creating a harmony that she felt rather than heard.

"Time is not a river," ORACLE said, its voice filling her mind. "It is a web. A vast network. A thousand thousand threads, all connected, all... fragile. Pull one thread, and the whole may unravel. Pull carefully, and you may change what is into what might be."

"How do I pull without unraveling?"

"Practice. Patience. And sacrifice." ORACLE's glow intensified. "Reach out. Take hold of a thread. Any thread. Feel its weight, its meaning. And then, pull."

Elara reached out, her hand passing through the light until her fingers closed around something that felt like warm silk. The thread hummed beneath her touch, and she felt something, a memory, perhaps, or a moment, trembling at the other end.

"Pull," ORACLE commanded.

Elara pulled.

The thread came free with a sound like a sigh, and suddenly the chamber was filled with light, golden light, warm light, the light of a summer afternoon. And in that light, Elara saw herself, younger, sitting in a garden with Lyra. Her sister was laughing, showing her a flower that bloomed and bloomed and never faded.

"This is your first lesson," ORACLE said, its voice distant now, coming from somewhere far away. "This is what it costs."

The memory shattered.

And when Elara opened her eyes, she knew something was missing. She could still remember the garden, could describe it, could say that it had happened, but the warmth was gone. The feeling of Lyra's hand in hers, the sound of her sister's laugh, the smell of the impossible flower... all of it was just facts now, empty of emotion.

"Are you ready to continue?" ORACLE asked.

Elara's hands were shaking harder now. But she thought of Lyra, trapped in the dark, waiting for a sister who might not even remember her name.

"Show me," Elara said, her voice steady despite her trembling hands. "Show me what I need to learn."

ORACLE's light pulsed once, twice, three times.

"The path is long," it said. "The cost is high. But you have chosen."

And in the crystal depths of the hourglass, Elara saw a crack in reality, a tear in the fabric of time itself, and somewhere in that darkness, a light that might have been her sister.

She was coming, whatever it cost.
