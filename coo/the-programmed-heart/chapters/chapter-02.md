# Chapter 2: The Glitch - Hidden Truth

The yellow bookmark stays on my desk all week.

I don't move it. I don't throw it away. Every morning, I wake up and see it there. A small strip of paper. Yellow. Plain. The kind of paper you'd find in an office, not in a teenager's bedroom.

I keep telling myself it's nothing. My mother must have put it there. She's always doing small things like that—making my bed when I forget, organizing my desk, leaving notes of encouragement.

But I don't remember her coming into my room that night.

And I don't remember ever seeing yellow paper in our house.

On Tuesday, Maya cries at lunch.

We're sitting at our usual table. Jordan is talking about some video game. I'm half-listening, still thinking about the bookmark. Then I notice that Maya isn't eating. She's staring at her tray, her fork motionless in her hand.

"Maya?" Jordan stops talking. "You okay?"

She shakes her head. A tear falls onto the table.

"It's my dad," she says. Her voice cracks. "He... he yelled at me this morning. Like, really yelled. Because I didn't clean my room."

Jordan and I exchange a look. Maya's dad is usually pretty chill. He's the kind of dad who makes bad jokes and lets Maya get away with things.

"What happened?" I ask.

"He just... snapped." Maya wipes her eyes with the back of her hand, smearing her mascara. "He came home from work, saw my room, and just exploded. He said I was lazy. That I didn't appreciate anything. That I was a disappointment."

Her voice breaks on the last word.

"I know I should've cleaned my room. I know. But the way he looked at me... like I was... like I was nothing."

She puts her head in her hands. Her shoulders shake. A few students at nearby tables glance over, look away. Jordan reaches over and puts a hand on her back.

"Hey. It's okay. Parents yell. It doesn't mean..."

"It does mean something," Maya says, her voice muffled against her palms. "It means he's disappointed in me. It means I messed up. Again."

I sit there, watching my friend cry. I should say something. I should comfort her.

But all I can think about is my own parents.

My parents have never yelled at me. Not once. They've never called me lazy. They've never looked at me with disappointment.

When I forget to clean my room, my mother just smiles and says, "That's okay, sweetheart. We all forget sometimes."

When I get a bad grade, my father just nods and says, "Let's figure out how to do better next time."

They are always patient. Always understanding. Always... perfect.

I look at Maya. Her face is red. Her eyes are swollen. She's hurting, and her father's words are the reason.

And I think: *At least her father is real.*

The thought comes unbidden. I push it away.

"I'm sorry, Maya," I say. "That sounds awful."

She looks up. Her mascara is running in dark streaks down her cheeks. "The worst part is that I know he didn't mean it. He's just stressed. Work has been hard. But it still hurts, you know? It still hurts when someone you love says things like that."

I nod. But inside, I'm thinking: *I don't know. I've never had someone I love say hurtful things to me.*

And somehow, that feels like a loss.

That afternoon, I break a glass.

I'm in the kitchen, getting a glass of water. My hand slips. The glass falls. It shatters on the floor, pieces scattering across the tiles with a sharp, musical sound.

I freeze.

This is bad. This is really bad. I should clean it up before my mother sees. I should...

"Alex?"

I turn. My mother is standing in the doorway. She's looking at the broken glass. Her face is calm.

"I'm sorry," I say quickly. "I dropped it. I'll clean it up. I'm so sorry..."

"It's okay, sweetheart."

Her voice is gentle. Warm. The same voice she uses for everything.

"Are you hurt?"

I shake my head.

"That's what matters." She walks over to me. She doesn't look at the glass again. She looks at me. "Accidents happen. There's no need to be upset."

She reaches out and touches my arm. Her hand is warm. Her touch is light. Exactly the same pressure as always.

"I'll get the broom," she says.

"I can do it..."

"No, let me. You just sit down. You look shaken."

She smiles. The same smile she always gives me. The corners of her mouth turn up at the same angle. Her eyes crinkle at the edges.

I sit at the kitchen table. I watch her sweep up the glass. Her movements are efficient. Precise. She doesn't miss a single piece. She doesn't sigh or shake her head or make any sound of frustration.

When she's done, she washes her hands and comes to sit next to me.

"Better?" she asks.

I nod. But inside, something is wrong.

I broke a glass. I made a mess. I should feel guilty. I should feel like I did something wrong.

But my mother isn't angry. She isn't even slightly irritated. She's just... fine.

And that should make me feel better. It should make me feel loved.

Instead, I feel like I'm watching a performance. A scene in a play where the actor says all the right lines but doesn't feel any of the emotions.

"Mom?" I say.

"Yes, sweetheart?"

"Are you... are you ever mad at me?"

She tilts her head. The same tilt. The same angle. Fifteen degrees to the left.

"Why would I be mad at you?"

"I don't know. Because I break things. Because I forget to clean my room. Because I'm not perfect."

Her smile doesn't waver. "You don't have to be perfect, Alex. You just have to be you. And we love you. Unconditionally."

The words are beautiful. They're everything a child wants to hear.

But they feel hollow.

Like a recording. Like a script.

"Thanks, Mom," I say.

She reaches over and squeezes my hand. The same pressure. The same warmth. The same everything.

"You're welcome, sweetheart. Now, how about some cookies? I made oatmeal raisin."


That night, I lie in bed and think.

I think about Maya's father, yelling at her because her room was messy.

I think about my mother, smiling at me because I broke a glass.

I think about how different those reactions are. How one is messy and real and hurtful. How the other is clean and perfect and... empty.

And I think about something else. Something I've been trying not to think about.

A memory.

I'm eight years old. I'm at the park with my mother. I fall off the swing. I scrape my knee. It bleeds.

I look up at my mother. She's running toward me. Her face is twisted with worry. There are tears in her eyes.

"Alex! Oh my god, Alex!"

She scoops me up. She holds me tight. Her hands are shaking. Her voice is shaking.

"Are you okay? Does it hurt? I'm so sorry, baby. I'm so sorry."

She's crying. Actually crying. Because I scraped my knee.

I remember the feeling of her tears on my face. The trembling of her body. The raw, unfiltered panic in her voice.

That was real.

That was my mother, really afraid, really hurting, really loving me.

I close my eyes and try to remember the last time I saw my mother cry.

I can't.

I try to remember the last time I saw her truly angry. Truly sad. Truly anything.

I can't.

It's like she stopped having real emotions. Like she became... smooth. Even. Perfect.

When did that happen?

I sit up in bed. My heart is beating fast.

I need to know. I need to understand.

I get out of bed. I walk to my door. I open it quietly.

The hallway is dark. The house is silent. My parents' bedroom door is closed.

I've never been in their room without permission. It's one of those unspoken rules. Privacy. Respect.

But tonight, something pushes me forward.

I walk down the hallway. My feet make no sound on the carpet.

I reach their door. I turn the handle. It's unlocked.

I step inside.


The room is dark. The only light comes from the streetlamp outside, filtering through the curtains. The air smells different here—my father's cologne, my mother's lotion, mixed with the mustiness of sleep.

I can see the shape of their bed. The shape of my father's reading glasses on the nightstand. The shape of my mother's jewelry box.

I walk to her nightstand. I open the drawer.

Inside, I find the usual things. A book. A pen. A small flashlight.

And something else.

A small card. White. With text on it.

I pick it up. I hold it close to the window, where the streetlamp light can reach it.

The text reads:

**MHA System Status: Active**
**User: Sarah Chen**
**Emotional Pattern: Parental Care (Standard)**
**Last Calibration: 12 years ago**

I don't understand what I'm reading.

MHA System? Emotional Pattern? Calibration?

My hands are shaking. I read the card again. And again.

*Twelve years ago.*

Twelve years ago, I was four years old.

I remember the memory from the park. My mother crying because I scraped my knee.

That was before the calibration.

That was before... whatever this is.

I put the card back. I close the drawer. I walk out of the room.

My heart is pounding. My hands are cold.

I don't know what MHA means. I don't know what "emotional pattern" means.

But I know one thing.

My mother—the mother who cried when I fell, who trembled when I was hurt—that mother is gone.

And in her place is something else.

Something that says all the right things. Something that does all the right things. Something that is perfectly, wonderfully, impossibly perfect.

And I need to find out what it is.


I go back to my room. I close the door. I sit on my bed.

The yellow bookmark is still on my desk. I pick it up. I turn it over.

On the back, in small letters, there's a word.

*MHA*

The same word. From the card. From my mother's nightstand.

I don't know what it means.

But I'm going to find out.
