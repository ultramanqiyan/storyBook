# Chapter 05: The Escape

The night is cold.

I walk down the street, my bag over my shoulder. The houses around me are dark. Everyone is sleeping. Everyone but me.

My feet carry me away from my house. Away from my street. Away from everything I've ever known.

I don't have a plan. I don't have a destination. I just know I can't go back.

Not to that house. Not to those people.

Not to the lie I've been living.

---

The bus station is three miles from my house.

I walk there in the dark. My phone says it's 11:47 PM. The last bus of the night leaves at 12:15.

I have twenty-eight minutes.

The streets are empty. A few cars pass. Their headlights cut through the darkness, then disappear. I keep my head down. I don't want anyone to see me.

I don't want anyone to ask questions.

The night air smells different than the air in my house. It smells like exhaust and wet pavement and something green—grass, maybe, or trees. It smells like the world.

Real. Messy. Alive.

I breathe it in. It's not pleasant. But it's real.

---

The bus station is small. A few benches. A ticket counter. A vending machine.

A woman sits behind the counter. She's reading a magazine. She doesn't look up when I enter.

I walk to the counter. My hands are shaking.

"One ticket," I say. "To... to the city."

She looks at me. Her eyes are tired. Bored. "Which city?"

I hesitate. I don't know. I didn't think this far ahead.

"The nearest one," I say.

She sighs. "That would be downtown. Twelve dollars."

I pull out my money. Crumpled bills. Coins. I count them out on the counter.

The woman watches me. Her expression doesn't change.

"Bus leaves in ten minutes," she says. "Platform two."

I take the ticket. I walk to the bench. I sit.

The station is quiet. A man sleeps on the other side of the room. His mouth is open. He snores.

I watch him. His face twitches in his sleep. His hand moves. He's dreaming.

Real dreams. Real sleep. Real life.

I've never seen anything so beautiful.

---

The bus arrives on time.

I climb aboard. The driver looks at my ticket. He nods. He doesn't ask questions.

I find a seat near the back. The bus is almost empty. An old woman in the front. A man in a suit, sleeping. A teenager with headphones, staring out the window.

I put my bag next to me. I lean my head against the window.

The bus starts moving.

I watch my town pass by. The streets I've walked a thousand times. The school where I've spent the last four years. The park where my mother took me when I was little.

The park where she cried when I fell.

That was real. That was her.

But that was twelve years ago.

The bus turns a corner. The town disappears behind us.

I don't look back.

---

The city is an hour away.

I don't sleep. I watch the road. The lights. The other cars.

The world is still out there. Moving. Living. Being.

And I'm part of it now. Really part of it.

For the first time in my life.

When the bus arrives, it's 1:30 AM. The station is bigger. Brighter. More people.

I get off. I stand on the platform. I look around.

The city is loud. Even at this hour. Cars. Voices. Music from somewhere nearby.

I smell food. Grease. Smoke. Something sweet.

My stomach growls. I haven't eaten since lunch.

I walk out of the station. The street is bright with neon signs. Hotels. Restaurants. Stores.

I find a cheap hotel. The sign says "VACANCY." I walk in.

The man at the counter is old. His face is lined. He's watching a small TV behind the counter.

"Room for one?" he asks.

I nod.

"Forty dollars a night. Cash up front."

I pay. He hands me a key. "Room 204. Second floor. Checkout's at eleven."

I take the key. I walk up the stairs.

---

The room is small.

A bed. A nightstand. A lamp. A window that looks out onto the street.

The carpet is worn. The walls are thin. I can hear someone talking in the next room.

It's not perfect.

It's not clean.

It's not comfortable.

It's real.

I sit on the bed. The mattress sags in the middle. The sheets are rough. The pillow is flat.

I lie down. I stare at the ceiling.

There's a crack in the plaster. A water stain. A cobweb in the corner.

Imperfections. Flaws. Evidence of life.

I've never seen anything so beautiful.

---

I don't sleep.

I lie in the bed and think. About my parents. About the system. About myself.

I think about the mother who cried when I fell. The father who laughed when I told a joke. The parents who were messy and real and human.

They're gone.

In their place are... what? Machines? Puppets?

No. They're still my parents. They just... can't feel anymore. They don't know they can't feel.

They think everything is fine.

They'll wake up tomorrow and find my note. They'll be worried. They'll call the police. They'll search for me.

And when they find me, they'll say all the right things. They'll be patient. Understanding. Loving.

Because that's what the system tells them to do.

I close my eyes.

*What do I do now?*

I don't know. I don't have a plan. I don't have a goal.

I just know I can't go back.

Not until I understand.

Not until I find something real.

---

Morning comes.

I wake up to the sound of traffic. Horns. Voices. The city coming alive.

I sit up. My neck hurts. The bed was terrible.

It's the best I've felt in years.

I wash my face in the small bathroom. The water is cold. The towel is scratchy.

I look at myself in the mirror.

My eyes are tired. My hair is messy. My face is pale.

I look like someone who didn't sleep. Someone who ran away. Someone who's lost.

But I also look like someone who's free.

I walk out of the hotel. The street is busy. People everywhere. Walking. Talking. Living.

I stand on the sidewalk. I watch them.

Are they real? Are they feeling things? Or are some of them like my parents—running on programs, thinking they're happy?

I don't know.

But I'm going to find out.

I start walking. No destination. Just forward.

The city is big. Loud. Messy. Real.

And somewhere in it, there has to be something true.

Something that isn't programmed.

Something that isn't fake.

I don't know if I'll find it.

But I have to try.
