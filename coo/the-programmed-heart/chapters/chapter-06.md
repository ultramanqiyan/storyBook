# Chapter 06: The Search

I spend three days in the city.

I walk. I watch. I listen.

I'm looking for something. Someone. Anyone who feels real.

But the more I look, the more confused I become.

---

On the first day, I go to a coffee shop.

It's small. Crowded. The kind of place where people sit with laptops and work, or stare at their phones, or talk in low voices.

I order a coffee. The cheapest one. I sit in a corner.

I watch the people around me.

A woman at the next table is crying. Silent tears running down her face. She's looking at her phone. Maybe a text. Maybe a photo.

Her pain is real. I can see it in the way her shoulders shake. In the way she wipes her eyes and then cries again.

I want to go to her. I want to ask if she's okay.

But I don't. I just watch.

A man comes in. He walks to her table. He sits down. He takes her hand.

"Hey," he says. "It's going to be okay."

His voice is calm. Soothing. Perfect.

I freeze.

The same tone. The same rhythm. The same words my parents would use.

Is he using the system? Or is he just... a good person?

I can't tell.

---

On the second day, I go to a park.

It's a nice park. Green grass. Trees. A playground where children run and scream.

I sit on a bench. I watch a mother with her child.

The child falls. Scrapes his knee. He starts to cry.

The mother runs to him. She picks him up. She holds him.

"Oh, baby, are you okay? Let me see. Let me see."

Her voice is panicked. Worried. Her face is twisted with concern.

She's not using the system. She can't be. Her reaction is too messy. Too real.

I feel a pang of something. Hope? Envy?

I watch her hold her child. Rock him. Kiss his knee.

"It's okay. It's okay. Mommy's here."

The boy stops crying. He sniffles. He looks up at her.

"I want ice cream," he says.

The mother laughs. A real laugh. Messy. Tired.

"Okay. Ice cream. But just one scoop."

They walk away. Hand in hand.

I sit on the bench. I feel hollow.

That's what I wanted. That's what I had. Once.

Before the system.

---

On the third day, I meet someone.

I'm at a library. Using the computers to search for information about MHA. The public computers are slow. The connection is bad.

A man sits next to me. He's young. Maybe twenty. His hair is messy. His clothes are wrinkled.

He glances at my screen.

"MHA?" he says. "You looking into that too?"

I look at him. His eyes are tired. But alert. Curious.

"You know about it?"

He nods. "Yeah. I know about it."

He points to a small device behind his ear. A thin metal strip. Almost invisible.

"I have one," he says. "Not the full system. Just... a little help."

I stare at him. "You use it?"

"Yeah." He doesn't look ashamed. Just tired. "I have depression. Bad. Without this, I can't get out of bed. Can't work. Can't live."

"But..." I struggle to find the words. "It's not real. The feelings. They're generated."

He shrugs. "Maybe. But I'd rather have fake feelings than no feelings at all. I'd rather be able to function than be paralyzed by sadness."

I don't know what to say.

He looks at me. His eyes are kind. "You're young. You've probably got some idea about what's real and what's not. But let me tell you something. When you've been in the dark for years, you don't care if the light is artificial. You just want to see."

He stands up. He gathers his things.

"Good luck," he says. "Whatever you're looking for."

He walks away.

I sit there. Staring at the screen.

---

That night, I go back to my hotel room.

I lie on the bed. I stare at the ceiling.

I think about the mother in the park. Her messy, real love.

I think about the man in the library. His artificial light.

I think about my parents. Their perfect, hollow care.

Which is better? Real pain or fake comfort? Messy love or programmed care?

I don't know.

I thought I knew. I thought the answer was simple. Real is good. Fake is bad.

But it's not simple.

The man in the library—he needs the system. Without it, he can't live.

Is that wrong? Is he less real because he uses it?

And the mother in the park—she's real. But she's also tired. Stressed. Overwhelmed.

Is that better? Just because it's natural?

I don't know.

I close my eyes.

---

On the fourth day, I meet Marcus.

I'm at a diner. Eating breakfast. The cheapest thing on the menu—pancakes. They're not as good as my mother's. They're lumpy. The syrup is cold.

They're perfect.

A man sits at the counter. He's talking to the waitress. Laughing. Joking.

His laugh is loud. Real. It fills the room.

I watch him. There's something about him. Something that draws my eye.

He finishes his coffee. He stands up. He turns.

Our eyes meet.

He smiles. A real smile. Crooked. Warm.

"Hey," he says. "You look like you're thinking too hard."

I blink. "What?"

"Your face." He points. "That furrow between your eyebrows. You're thinking about something heavy."

I touch my forehead. I didn't realize I was frowning.

He sits down across from me. He doesn't ask permission. He just does it.

"I'm Marcus," he says.

"Alex."

"Nice to meet you, Alex." He leans back. "So. What's the heavy thinking about?"

I hesitate. I don't know this person. I shouldn't trust him.

But I'm tired of being alone. Tired of thinking in circles.

"The MHA system," I say. "Do you know about it?"

His expression changes. Just for a second. A flicker of something. Recognition? Pain?

Then it's gone. He smiles again.

"Yeah," he says. "I know about it."

He reaches up. He touches his ear. The same place where the man in the library had his device.

"I use it," he says. "Not all the time. Just... when I need it."

I stare at him. "But you seem so... real."

He laughs. That loud, real laugh.

"I am real," he says. "The system doesn't make me fake. It just... helps me be more of who I am."

I don't understand.

He leans forward. His eyes are serious now.

"Let me tell you something, Alex. The system isn't good or bad. It's a tool. Like a wheelchair. Like glasses. Some people need it all the time. Some people need it sometimes. Some people don't need it at all."

"But the feelings," I say. "They're not real."

"What's real?" He tilts his head. "If I feel happy because the system helps me feel happy, is that happiness fake? Or is it just... assisted?"

I don't have an answer.

He reaches across the table. He takes my hand. His grip is warm. Firm. Real.

"I used to think like you," he says. "That real was better. That anything artificial was wrong. But then I realized something."

"What?"

"Real isn't always good. And fake isn't always bad. What matters is whether it helps you live. Whether it helps you connect. Whether it helps you be human."

He lets go of my hand. He stands up.

"I gotta go. But if you want to talk more, I'm usually here. Same time. Same place."

He walks out.

I sit there. My pancakes are cold now.

But I'm not thinking about them.

I'm thinking about what he said.

*What matters is whether it helps you live.*

Is that true?

Or is it just another lie I want to believe?

---

That night, I go back to the library.

I search for more information about MHA. I find articles. Studies. Testimonials.

Some people love it. They say it saved their lives. Their marriages. Their families.

Some people hate it. They say it stole their souls. Their humanity. Their freedom.

Some people are like Marcus. They use it sometimes. When they need it. They call it "emotional assistance."

There's no clear answer. No right or wrong.

Just... choices.

I close the browser. I walk out of the library.

The city is dark. The streetlights are on. People pass me on the sidewalk.

I look at their faces. Some are smiling. Some are frowning. Some are blank.

I can't tell who's using the system and who's not.

And maybe that doesn't matter.

Maybe what matters is something else.

Something I haven't figured out yet.

I walk back to my hotel. I lie on the bed.

Tomorrow, I'll talk to Marcus again.

Maybe he can help me understand.

Maybe he can help me find what I'm looking for.

Or maybe I'll never find it.

Maybe there's nothing to find.

I close my eyes.

I sleep.
