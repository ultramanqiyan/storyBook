# Chapter 04: The Confrontation

I don't eat lunch.

I sit at the table. My parents sit across from me. They're eating sandwiches. Talking about the grocery store. The traffic. The weather.

Normal things.

I watch my mother's mouth as she speaks. The way her lips move. The way her voice rises and falls in all the right places.

It's like watching a dub. The words match the movement, but something is off. Something is wrong.

"Alex?" My father's voice cuts through. "You haven't touched your food."

I look down. My sandwich is untouched. The bread is perfect. The lettuce is crisp. The turkey is sliced thin.

Everything is perfect.

"I'm not hungry," I say.

My mother tilts her head. Concern appears on her face like a light being switched on.

"Are you feeling okay? You've been quiet all morning."

*I've been quiet my whole life,* I think. *I just didn't know it.*

"I'm fine," I say.

The lie tastes bitter on my tongue.

---

That night, I try to talk to them.

We're in the living room. The TV is on, but no one is watching. My mother is knitting. My father is reading something on his tablet.

Normal. Everything is normal.

Except nothing is normal.

"Mom?" I say.

She looks up. Her needles pause. Her face arranges itself into attention. "Yes, sweetheart?"

My heart is pounding. I've been thinking about this all afternoon. What to say. How to say it.

"Do you remember... when I was little? Before I started school?"

She smiles. The same smile. "Of course I remember. You were such a sweet child."

"Do you remember... do you remember being sad? After I was born?"

Her smile doesn't change. But something flickers in her eyes. A shadow. Gone before I can be sure.

"I remember it was a difficult time," she says. "But we got through it."

"How?"

She tilts her head. "What do you mean?"

"How did you get through it? What helped?"

My father looks up from his tablet. He's watching now too.

"We had help," my mother says. "Support. From doctors. From the program."

"The program?"

"The MHA program," my father says. His voice is calm. Even. "It helped us be better parents. For you."

My hands are shaking. I clench them in my lap.

"What... what is the MHA program?"

My mother puts down her knitting. She leans forward. Her face is filled with concern.

"Sweetheart, are you sure you're okay? You're asking strange questions."

"I just want to understand," I say. My voice is tight. "What does the MHA program do?"

"It helps us love you," she says. Simply. Like it's the most natural thing in the world.

The words hit me like a physical blow.

*It helps us love you.*

"But you already loved me," I say. "Before the program. You loved me."

"Of course we did," my father says. "The program just... helped us show it better. More consistently."

"More consistently."

"Yes." He nods. "Parenting is hard, Alex. Every parent struggles sometimes. The program helped us be the parents you deserved."

I stare at him. At his calm face. His reasonable voice.

He believes it. He actually believes it.

"But what if..." I swallow. "What if the program isn't really you? What if it's... something else? Making you feel things that aren't real?"

My parents exchange a look. The same look they always exchange when I ask something they don't quite understand.

"Alex," my mother says. Her voice is gentle. "I think you might be overthinking this. The program is just a tool. Like... like a hearing aid, or glasses. It helps us be our best selves."

"But you're not being yourselves," I say. My voice cracks. "You're being what the program tells you to be."

"Sweetheart—"

"I found the documents," I say. The words come out before I can stop them. "The files in your closet. I know what the MHA system does. I know it generates your emotions. I know you're not really feeling anything."

Silence.

The TV murmurs in the background. A commercial for something I don't care about.

My parents look at me. Their faces are calm. Concerned. Patient.

Like they're waiting for me to finish a tantrum.

"Alex," my mother says. "I think you've been under a lot of stress. The chemistry test. School. Maybe you need some rest."

"I'm not making this up," I say. My voice is rising now. "I saw the documents. The system status. 'Emotional Pattern: Parental Care.' It's all there. You're not really feeling anything. You're just—"

"Alex." My father's voice is firm but kind. "I think we should talk about this when you're feeling better. You seem upset."

"I am upset!"

My mother stands up. She walks over to me. She kneels in front of me. She takes my hands.

Her hands are warm. Soft. The same as always.

"Listen to me," she says. Her voice is low. Soothing. "Whatever you think you found, whatever you're worried about... I want you to know that we love you. That's real. That's always been real."

"How would you know?" I whisper. "How would you know if it's real or not?"

She tilts her head. Her eyes are filled with... something. Concern? Love?

Or just the pattern the system told her to display?

"I know because I'm your mother," she says. "And a mother's love is the most real thing there is."

I want to believe her. I want to believe it so badly.

But I can't.

Because I know the truth.

---

I go to my room.

I close the door. I sit on my bed.

I can hear my parents downstairs. Their voices are low. They're talking about me.

"She's been acting strange lately."
"Maybe it's teenage stuff. Hormones."
"We should keep an eye on her. Make sure she's okay."

Their words are filled with concern. With love.

With nothing.

I lie down. I stare at the ceiling.

The yellow bookmark is still on my desk. I pick it up. I turn it over.

*MHA.*

The same word. The same system.

My entire life, I've been loved by a machine.

My parents don't know. They can't know. The documents said so. *No awareness of system intervention.*

They think they're feeling things. They think they're making choices.

But they're not.

They're just... running a program.

And I'm the one it's running on.

---

I don't sleep.

I lie in bed and think. About my parents. About the system. About myself.

I think about the mother who cried when I scraped my knee. The mother who trembled with fear. The mother who was messy and real and human.

She's gone.

In her place is this. This perfect, patient, loving shell.

And the worst part?

She doesn't even know.

She thinks she's happy. She thinks she loves me. She thinks everything is fine.

Because the system tells her to think that.

I sit up. I look around my room. My perfect room. My perfect house. My perfect life.

It's all a lie.

Every smile. Every hug. Every "I love you."

Generated. Calculated. Fake.

And I can't stay here.

I can't live in this house, with these people who aren't really people anymore.

I need to get out.

I need to find something real.

---

I pack a bag.

Just the essentials. Clothes. My phone. The charger. A little money I've saved.

I write a note.

*I need some time. This isn't your fault. I love you—or at least, I want to believe I do. I need to find out what's real.*

I leave the note on my desk.

I open my window. The night air is cool. Real.

I climb out.

I walk away from the house.

I don't look back.
