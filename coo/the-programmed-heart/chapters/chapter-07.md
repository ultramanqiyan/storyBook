# Chapter 7: The Gray - Hidden Truth

I meet Marcus at the diner again.

He's already there when I arrive. Sitting at the same table. Drinking coffee. Reading something on his phone. The afternoon light slants through the window, catching the steam rising from his cup.

He looks up when I walk in. He smiles. That crooked, real smile. The chipped tooth catches the light.

"Hey," he says. "You came back."

I sit across from him. The vinyl seat squeaks under me. "I have questions."

"I figured." He puts down his phone. The screen is cracked. "Ask away."

---

I start with the obvious.

"You use the system," I say. "But you don't seem... programmed. How is that possible?"

He takes a sip of coffee. He thinks for a moment. His fingers drum on the table—a restless, uneven rhythm.

"It's about how you use it," he says. "My parents—they have the full system. They're like... passengers in their own bodies. The system does everything. They don't even know it's there."

He pauses. His face changes. Something sad flickers in his eyes. The scar through his eyebrow catches the light.

"But me? I use it differently. I have a... partial setup. I can turn it on and off. I can choose when to use it."

"And that makes it real?"

He shakes his head. "No. That's not what makes it real." He leans forward. His coffee breath mingles with the smell of bacon from the kitchen. "What makes it real is that I know it's there. I know what it's doing. I make a choice, every day, about how much to let it help."

I think about my parents. They don't know. They can't choose.

"Did you always know?" I ask.

"No." He looks down at his coffee. The surface trembles slightly—from the vibrations of the kitchen, or his own hand, I can't tell. "I found out when I was eighteen. I was using the full system then. My parents had put me on it when I was a kid. I didn't even know there was another way to be."

"What happened?"

He's quiet for a moment. The diner noise fills the silence—plates clattering, the hiss of the grill, a baby crying two booths over. Then he looks up. His eyes are steady. Honest.

"I tried to kill myself."

The words hang in the air. My chest tightens.

"I was on the full system," he continues. "It made me happy. Or... it made me act happy. But underneath, something was wrong. I couldn't feel anything real. I couldn't connect. I was a shell, walking around, pretending to be alive."

He takes another sip of coffee. His hand is steady now.

"One day, I turned it off. Just to see what would happen. And I felt... everything. All the pain I'd been suppressing. All the fear. All the sadness. It was overwhelming. Like drowning in cold water."

"What did you do?"

"I almost didn't make it." His voice is quiet. Almost a whisper. "But I had help. A doctor. She helped me understand that I didn't have to choose between the system and nothing. I could find a middle ground."

He touches the device behind his ear. His fingers trace the thin metal strip.

"Now I use it when I need it. When the darkness gets too heavy. But I also let myself feel. The real stuff. Even when it hurts."

I stare at him. My mind is racing. The smell of coffee and grease fills my nostrils.

"So you're saying... the system isn't evil?"

He laughs. Soft. Tired. His shoulders rise and fall.

"I'm saying it's a tool. Like anything else. It can be used well, or it can be used badly. It can help, or it can hurt."

"But my parents... they don't even know they have it."

"That's different," he says. His face hardens slightly. "That's... that's a problem. When people don't know, they can't choose. They're not really living. They're just... existing."

I think about my mother. Her perfect smile. Her perfect words. The way she always smells like vanilla. The way her hugs are always exactly the same temperature.

She doesn't know. She can't choose.

"Is there a way to... wake them up?" I ask.

Marcus is quiet. He traces a pattern on the table with his finger—a circle, then a line through it. Then he shakes his head.

"I don't know. Maybe. But here's the thing, Alex. Even if you could... would they want to be woken up?"

"What do you mean?"

"Think about it. Your parents have been on the system for... how long?"

"Twelve years."

"Twelve years. That's a long time. They've built their whole lives around it. Their whole identities. If you take it away... what's left?"

I don't have an answer.

He reaches across the table. His hand touches mine. Warm. Calloused. Real. His palm is rough from work, or maybe from nervous habits.

"I'm not saying you shouldn't try. I'm just saying... it's complicated. There's no easy answer. No right choice."

---

We talk for hours.

The afternoon stretches into evening. The diner fills and empties and fills again. The light through the window shifts from gold to orange to gray.

He tells me about his life. His struggles. His journey to find balance.

I tell him about my parents. My discovery. My escape.

He listens. He doesn't judge. He doesn't tell me what to do.

He just... understands.

"I've been where you are," he says. "Thinking the system is the enemy. Thinking you have to destroy it to be free."

"And what did you learn?"

"That freedom isn't about destroying things. It's about understanding them. Making choices. Finding your own way."

He looks at me. His eyes are kind. Brown, with flecks of green near the iris.

"You're angry. That's okay. You have every right to be angry. But don't let the anger eat you. Don't let it become the only thing you feel."

I nod. But inside, I'm still confused.

"How do I know what's real?" I ask. "How do I know if my feelings are mine, or if they're... programmed?"

He thinks for a moment. He turns his coffee cup in a slow circle.

"Real feelings are messy," he says. "They don't follow a pattern. They surprise you. They hurt. They change. If something feels too perfect, too consistent... maybe it's not real."

"But my parents... their love feels perfect. Consistent."

"Then maybe it's not real." He pauses. "Or maybe... it's real, but it's been... smoothed over. Filtered. Like looking through a lens that makes everything clearer but also less true."

I think about this. The photograph I found. My mother, before the system. Her messy, real face. The dark circles under her eyes. The way her hair fell across her forehead.

"I want to find out," I say. "I want to know who they really are. Underneath the system."

He nods. "That's a good goal. But be prepared. You might not like what you find."

---

Before we part, he gives me a name.

"Dr. Elena Rivera," he says. "She's the one who helped me. She studies the MHA system. She knows more about it than anyone."

"Where can I find her?"

"She works at the university. Department of Psychology. Tell her Marcus sent you."

I write down the name on a napkin. The pen is almost out of ink. The letters are faint. I fold the napkin and put it in my pocket.

"Thank you," I say.

He shrugs. "I remember what it's like. Being lost. Confused. Thinking you're the only one who sees the truth." He smiles. "You're not alone, Alex. There are others. People who understand. People who've been through it."

I nod. I stand up. My legs are stiff from sitting so long.

"Will I see you again?"

He grins. "I'm always here. Same time. Same place. Come by whenever you need to talk."

I walk out of the diner. The evening air is cool on my face. It smells like exhaust and rain and something cooking from a nearby restaurant.

I feel different. Not better, exactly. But... clearer.

The world isn't black and white. The system isn't pure evil. My parents aren't villains.

They're just... lost. Like I was lost. Like Marcus was lost.

And maybe... maybe there's a way to find them.

---

That night, I lie in my hotel room.

The sheets are rough against my skin. The mattress creaks every time I shift. Through the thin walls, I can hear a couple arguing—their voices rising and falling in a rhythm I can almost follow.

I think about Marcus. His story. His partial system. His choice.

I think about my parents. Their full system. Their lack of choice.

I think about Dr. Rivera. The name in my pocket. The possibility of answers.

And I think about myself.

What do I want?

Do I want to destroy the system? To wake my parents up? To find some kind of justice?

Or do I just want to understand?

I don't know.

But I know one thing.

Tomorrow, I'm going to find Dr. Rivera.

And maybe she can help me figure out the rest.
