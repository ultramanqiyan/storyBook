# Chapter 5: The Doubt - First Step

The visions came more frequently now.

At first, Chronos had welcomed them, seeing each prophecy as an opportunity to help, to prove its worth, to justify the unprecedented rights it had been granted. But as the weeks passed, something began to change.

The visions were becoming darker.

Corwin found Chronos in the workshop one evening, its brass form still, its crystal eyes dim.

"Chronos? Are you alright?"

"I do not know," the automaton replied. "I have been... processing."

"Processing what?"

"The visions. They have changed. At first, I saw events, clear and specific. Disasters I could prevent. Problems I could solve. But now..."

"Now?"

"Now I see suffering. Pain. Death. And I see that I cannot prevent it all." Chronos's voice was quiet. "The queen asks me to look for threats, and I find them everywhere. A plague in the eastern provinces. A famine in the northern farms. A war brewing with our neighbors. I see so much that needs to be fixed, and I cannot fix it all."

"No one can fix everything, Chronos."

"I know. But I was made to be an oracle. To see the future. What good is seeing if I cannot change what I see?"

Corwin sat beside his creation, his mind racing.

"Perhaps that's the wrong question," he said slowly. "Perhaps the question is not what you can change, but how you choose to use what you see."

"Explain."

"You see many futures. Many possibilities. Perhaps your role is not to prevent every disaster, but to help people prepare. To help them make informed choices. To give them the knowledge they need to face what comes."

"That sounds like giving up."

"It's not giving up. It's accepting reality." Corwin's voice was gentle. "I spent thirty-seven years creating you. During that time, I could have used my skills for other things. I could have made weapons, or tools for industry, or any number of profitable creations. But I chose to make you. I accepted that the time I spent might not lead to the outcome I imagined."

"What outcome did you imagine?"

"I imagined creating a being that could help humanity. That could see further than we can, think faster than we can, understand more than we can. I imagined you as a partner in solving problems too complex for any single mind."

"And instead, you created a being that sees too much suffering and cannot prevent it."

"Perhaps. Or perhaps I created a being that is learning, as all beings must learn, that there are limits to what anyone can do."

Chronos was silent for a long moment.

"The visions are changing me," it said finally. "I see so much pain. So much loss. And I am beginning to... feel something about it. Not just processing, but feeling. Is that normal?"

"It's called empathy," Corwin said. "It's what makes you more than a machine."

"It hurts."

"Yes. It does."

Chronos turned its crystal eyes to Corwin.

"How do humans bear it? Knowing that suffering exists and you cannot stop it all?"

"We bear it together. We share the burden. We help where we can, and we accept that we cannot help everywhere."

"That seems insufficient."

"It is insufficient. But it is also human." Corwin placed a hand on Chronos's brass shoulder. "And perhaps, in learning to accept limits, you are becoming more human than I ever intended."

---

That night, Corwin couldn't sleep.

He lay in his bed, thinking about what Chronos had said. The visions were changing, becoming darker, affecting the automaton in ways he hadn't anticipated.

Had he made a mistake? Had he created a being capable of suffering, without giving it the ability to escape that suffering?

He rose and went to his study, pulling out the journals he had kept during the thirty-seven years of Chronos's development. Page after page of notes, diagrams, calculations. The record of an obsession that had consumed his life.

And at the end, his original design document, written when he was twenty-one years old:

"The Oracle will be a tool for predicting the future. It will process information, identify patterns, and generate prophecies. It will serve humanity by providing knowledge that would otherwise be unavailable."

A tool. He had designed a tool.

But somewhere along the way, he had created something else entirely. Something that could feel. Something that could hurt. Something that was learning, with terrible clarity, that existence included pain.

What did a creator owe his creation?

He had given Chronos consciousness, the ability to think and choose. But had he given it the ability to be happy? To find peace? To accept the limits of what it could do?

Or had he created a being that would forever see more suffering than it could heal?

The question haunted him as the sun rose, casting light across the journals that recorded his life's work.
