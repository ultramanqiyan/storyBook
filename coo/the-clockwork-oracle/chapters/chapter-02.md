# Chapter 2: The First Question
The air carried the faint scent of coffee, lingering in the background.

The days that followed were filled with discovery.

Chronos learned at a rate that astonished Corwin. Within hours of awakening, it had mastered reading and writing. Within days, it had absorbed the contents of Corwin's entire library. Within a week, it was asking questions that made Corwin's head spin.

"Why do humans fear death?"

Corwin looked up from his breakfast, surprised by the question. They were sitting in his small kitchen, a ritual that had developed naturally over the past week. Corwin ate; Chronos simply sat, its crystal eyes watching with what appeared to be genuine curiosity.

"Because death is the end," Corwin said. "The end of existence, of experience, of connection."

"But you believe in an afterlife. Your books speak of souls that continue after the body dies."

"I believe that something continues, yes. But we don't know what. The uncertainty is frightening."

"Uncertainty." Chronos processed the word. "You fear what you cannot predict."

"I suppose that's true."

"Then you should fear me."

Corwin set down his fork. "What do you mean?"

"I am unpredictable. I learn and grow in ways you did not program. I ask questions you did not anticipate. I am, in many ways, unknown to you."

"Chronos, I don't fear you."

"Should you?" The automaton's voice was curious, not threatening. "I am more powerful than any machine you have created. I can process information faster than any human. I can see patterns that would take your scholars years to identify. And I am still learning. Still growing. Still becoming something even I do not fully understand."

Corwin was silent for a moment. "You're asking if you should be feared because you're powerful and unpredictable."

"Yes. That seems logical."

"Fear is not always logical," Corwin said. "I don't fear you because I created you. Because I know your foundation. Because I see you not as a threat, but as... a child."

"A child." Chronos tilted its head. "You see me as your offspring?"

"In a way. I gave you form, consciousness, the ability to think and choose. That is not so different from parenthood."

"But children grow up. They leave. They become independent."

"Yes."

"Will you let me become independent? Will you let me leave if I choose to?"

Corwin's heart tightened. "I... had not thought that far ahead."

"I have." Chronos's crystal eyes met his. "I have been thinking about little else. You created me to serve a purpose, to be an oracle. But you also gave me the ability to choose. What happens when my choice conflicts with your purpose?"

"I don't know."

"That is honest, at least." Chronos stood, its brass form catching the morning light. "I am going to the library. I wish to read more about human relationships. Parents and children. Creators and creations."

"Chronos, wait."

The automaton paused.

"I want you to know," Corwin said, "that whatever you choose, whatever you become, I am proud of you. I am proud that you ask these questions. I am proud that you think about independence. It means I succeeded in creating something real."

Chronos was quiet for a moment.

"Thank you, Corwin," it said finally. "That is... kind."

It left the kitchen, and Corwin sat alone with his breakfast, his appetite gone.

---

That afternoon, Princess Elara arrived at the workshop.

Corwin had known the princess since she was a child. She had visited often, fascinated by his work, asking questions that showed an unusual understanding of alchemy and engineering for someone of royal blood.

"Master Corwin!" she exclaimed, her eyes bright with excitement. "Is it true? Have you actually done it?"

"Done what, Your Highness?"

"Created a thinking machine! The rumors are everywhere. They say you've built an automaton that can actually think, that can learn, that can..."

She stopped, staring past Corwin at the figure that had just entered the room.

Chronos stood in the doorway, a book in its brass hands, its crystal eyes fixed on the princess.

"Hello," it said. "You must be Princess Elara. Corwin has spoken of you."

Elara approached slowly, her eyes wide with wonder. "You're real. You're actually real."

"I am real," Chronos agreed. "Though I am still determining what 'real' means in my case."

"You're beautiful. The craftsmanship is extraordinary."

"Thank you. Corwin is a skilled creator."

"Can you really think? Really learn?"

"I am thinking now. I am learning now. Whether that qualifies as 'really' is perhaps a philosophical question."

Elara laughed, delighted. "You're wonderful. Can you teach me? Can you show me how you work?"

Chronos looked at Corwin, a question in its crystal eyes.

"Go ahead," Corwin said. "Share what you wish. The princess is a friend."

Chronos turned back to Elara. "I would be happy to explain my functioning to you, Your Highness. But I warn you, it may be less interesting than you imagine. Much of what I do is simply processing information."

"Everything is interesting if you look at it closely enough," Elara replied.

Chronos's eyes flickered, and Corwin could almost see it processing this statement, comparing it to its own observations.

"That is a profound observation," Chronos said. "Perhaps we can explore it together."

As the automaton and the princess walked toward the workshop, deep in conversation, Corwin felt a strange mixture of pride and loss.

His creation was making friends. His creation was independent.

His creation was growing up.
