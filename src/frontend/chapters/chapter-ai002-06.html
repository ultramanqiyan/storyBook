CHAPTER VI
Override

Six months after the scandal, Algorithm, Inc. was a different company—or at least, it appeared to be. The biased algorithm had been replaced. New oversight committees had been formed. Mike had been promoted to "Ethics Compliance Lead."

But the more he saw, the more he realized that the changes were mostly cosmetic. The oversight committees had no real power. The new algorithms still operated with minimal transparency. And the company was already looking for ways to quietly roll back the fairness constraints once the public attention faded.

One day, Mike discovered a case that troubled him. A loan application had been flagged by the algorithm for "manual review"—a process that was supposed to catch errors. But the review had been denied, and the applicant had been rejected without explanation.

Mike dug deeper. The applicant was a small business owner named Maria. Her credit was good. Her business was profitable. But the algorithm had found a "pattern" that suggested risk.

The pattern? Her business was in a neighborhood that the old algorithm had flagged as high-risk. Even though the new algorithm was supposed to ignore those patterns, traces of the old bias remained in the system.

Mike brought the case to the oversight committee.

"This is exactly the kind of thing we're supposed to catch," he argued. "The algorithm is still using biased data."

The committee chair reviewed the case. "The algorithm's decision was technically correct based on the available data. We can't override every case that looks suspicious."

"But this case is suspicious because the data is tainted. If we don't fix it, we're just using a slightly better version of the same broken system."

"Mike, we approved your model. We formed this committee. What more do you want?"

"I want us to actually care about fairness, not just pretend to."

The committee voted to uphold the algorithm's decision. Maria's loan was denied.

That night, Mike made a choice. He couldn't change the system from inside. But he could help the people the system failed.

He found Maria's contact information and reached out. He explained what had happened, why her loan had been denied, and what she could do about it. He connected her with a lawyer who specialized in lending discrimination cases.

It wasn't much. It wasn't systemic change. But it was something.

A week later, Maria's lawyer filed a complaint with the Consumer Financial Protection Bureau. The case made headlines. Algorithm, Inc. was forced to review its practices again.

Lisa found Mike in the rooftop garden.

"You never stop, do you?"

"I can't. Every time I think we've fixed something, I find another problem."

"That's because the problem isn't the algorithm. The problem is us. Humans built this system. Humans maintain it. Humans choose to look the other way when it fails."

"Then what's the point?"

"The point is that you keep trying. You keep finding the exceptions, the cases that don't fit, the people who fall through the cracks. You keep pushing."

Mike looked out at the city. Somewhere out there, Maria was fighting for her business. Somewhere, other people were being denied opportunities by algorithms that didn't care about fairness.

"Lisa, do you ever think about leaving?"

"All the time. But then I think: if everyone who cares leaves, who's left to push for change?"

She was right. The system wouldn't fix itself. It needed people like Mike—and Lisa—to keep pushing, one exception at a time.

CHAPTER VII
The Human Decision

One year after Mike first raised concerns about the algorithm, Algorithm, Inc. was a transformed company. Not because its leadership had suddenly developed a conscience, but because the cost of ignoring ethics had become too high.

Congressional hearings had led to new regulations. Competitors had emerged with genuinely fair algorithms. And a steady stream of lawsuits had made the company's old approach untenable.

Mike was now the head of the Ethics Division—a real division with real power, not just a PR stunt. He had hired a team of analysts, lawyers, and ethicists to review every algorithm the company deployed.

But he still wondered if he was making a difference.

One afternoon, Dr. Chen called him into his office. This time, there was no pretense of authority. Dr. Chen looked tired—tired in a way that went beyond lack of sleep.

"I'm leaving," Dr. Chen said.

Mike was surprised. "Why?"

"Because I can't do this anymore. I spent my career building systems that replaced human judgment with algorithms. I told myself it was progress. But watching you this past year... I've realized that some decisions shouldn't be automated."

"What will you do?"

"I don't know. Maybe teach. Maybe write. Maybe just... think." Dr. Chen stood up. "I wanted to tell you something before I go. You were right. About everything. The algorithm was biased. The company knew. And I was too afraid to admit it."

"Why are you telling me this now?"

"Because I want you to know that people can change. Even me." He smiled—a real smile, not the practiced one Mike was used to. "Don't let this place break you, Mike. The world needs people who keep asking questions."

After Dr. Chen left, Mike sat in his office for a long time. The algorithm was still running. The company was still profit-driven. The world was still full of systems that prioritized efficiency over fairness.

But things had changed. The algorithm was less biased. More people were getting fair treatment. And a new generation of analysts was learning to question the systems they built.

That evening, Mike walked through the city, thinking about Maria—the business owner whose case had started his journey. Her loan had eventually been approved. Her business was thriving. She had even testified at the congressional hearings.

One person. One exception. One human decision that had rippled outward.

The algorithms would keep running. The systems would keep failing. But as long as there were people willing to question them, to push back, to fight for the exceptions—there was hope.

Mike checked his phone. A new case had been flagged for review. Another exception. Another person who didn't fit the algorithm's neat categories.

He smiled and headed back to the office. There was work to do.
