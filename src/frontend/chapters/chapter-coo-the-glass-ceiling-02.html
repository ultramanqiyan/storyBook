CHAPTER II
The Warning - Whispers in the Hallway

The onboarding was unlike anything Alex had experienced. New hires were required to complete "Meridian University"—a week-long immersion program designed to teach the company's culture, values, and ways of working. Alex sat in a conference room with thirty other new employees—engineers, designers, marketers, product managers. They were from different backgrounds, different countries, different life experiences. But they all shared one thing: they'd made it to Meridian. The conference room was climate-controlled to perfection—cool without being cold, comfortable without being casual. Glass walls looked out onto the campus, where employees walked purposefully between buildings. The air smelled of fresh coffee from the station in the corner, where a barista prepared custom drinks for anyone who wanted them. Everything about the space whispered success, whispered belonging, whispered that they had arrived somewhere special. The facilitator, a perky woman named Lisa, began with a video. The lights dimmed, and the screen came to life. On the screen, Meridian's CEO appeared. He was young—maybe forty—with the kind of casual confidence that came from being a billionaire. He wore a simple black t-shirt and sat on the edge of a desk, leaning toward the camera like he was sharing a secret. "Welcome to Meridian," he said. "You're here because you're the best. But more importantly, you're here because you believe in what we're building. We're not just a company. We're a movement. A family. And now, you're part of that family." Family. The word stuck with Alex. It settled somewhere in their chest, warm and heavy. They'd never been part of a family like this—not at school, not at their previous jobs, not anywhere. The taste of the complimentary latte lingered on their tongue - rich, expensive, like everything else in this place. Lisa led the group through Meridian's core values. Each one appeared on the screen behind her, accompanied by inspiring music and images of happy employees working together, celebrating together, living their best lives. 1. We Put People First - Employees are our greatest asset 2. We Dream Big - No idea is too ambitious 3. We Move Fast - Speed is a competitive advantage 4. We're Transparent - Information should flow freely 5. We're a Family - We take care of our own "These aren't just words on a wall," Lisa said, her voice earnest. "These are how we operate every day. When you're here, you're not just an employee. You're a Meridian person." A Meridian person. Alex liked the sound of that. They'd spent their whole life feeling like they didn't belong—too smart for Millbrook, too poor for the university, too inexperienced for every job they'd applied to. But here, they were a Meridian person. Here, they belonged. The other new hires nodded along. Alex could see the same expression on their faces—hope, excitement, the feeling that they'd finally made it. They were the chosen ones. They were special. Lisa continued. "At Meridian, we believe that work should be meaningful. You shouldn't have to choose between a career and a life. That's why we offer unlimited PTO, flexible hours, and the freedom to work from anywhere. We trust you to manage your own time." Alex thought about their previous job—the teaching assistant position where every bathroom break had to be logged, where arriving five minutes late meant a meeting with HR, where taking a sick day required a doctor's note. This was different. This was what work was supposed to be. On Day 3, Alex met their team. There were six product managers in Marcus's group, and they gathered in a glass-walled conference room for introductions. The afternoon light streamed through the windows, casting long shadows across the table. Someone had ordered coffee and pastries from the cafe downstairs, and the room smelled of espresso and butter. "Team, I want you to meet Alex," Marcus said, his voice warm. "They're joining us from Illinois, and I'm really excited about what they're going to bring to the table." He turned to Alex. "Tell us a little about yourself." Alex gave their prepared introduction—background, interests, why they wanted to work at Meridian. They talked about Millbrook, about being the first in their family to go to college, about wanting to build something that mattered. They talked about the long hours they'd spent teaching themselves to code, the projects they'd built in their spare time, the dreams they'd had since they were a kid staring at a computer screen in the public library. When they finished, the team applauded. Marcus beamed. And Alex felt, for the first time in their life, like they were exactly where they were supposed to be. "Welcome to the family," Marcus said, and Alex believed him. After the formal introductions, Alex found themselves alone with Sarah Kim, one of the senior PMs. She was older than Alex expected—maybe mid-thirties—with tired eyes that seemed to hold secrets. "Can I give you some advice?" Sarah asked, her voice low. "Of course." "Be careful here." She glanced around, as if checking for eavesdroppers. "This place... it's not what it seems. The 'family' thing? It's a trap." Alex blinked. "What do you mean?" "I mean..." Sarah hesitated, her fingers tracing the rim of her coffee cup. "Just... watch your back. Document everything. And don't trust everything you hear." Before Alex could ask more, Sarah walked away, leaving them with more questions than answers.
